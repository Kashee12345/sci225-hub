Renal & Urinary

The kidneys are the body’s quiet housekeepers — filtering waste, balancing water and electrolytes, steadying blood pressure, and even making blood. This chapter starts at the nephron and works outward through the ways kidneys fail suddenly or slowly, the stones and infections of the urinary tract, and what the labs are telling you.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Describe the nephron and what GFR, creatinine, and BUN mean.
  • Classify acute kidney injury as prerenal, intrarenal, or postrenal.
  • Explain chronic kidney disease, uremia, and its complications.
  • Distinguish nephrotic from nephritic syndrome.
  • Tell cystitis from pyelonephritis and recognize renal colic.

Section 1The nephron: how the kidney cleans blood

Every minute, more than a liter of blood pours into your kidneys, and by the time it leaves it has been cleaned, balanced, and fine-tuned. The kidney pulls off this trick using millions of tiny units called nephrons - roughly one million per kidney. The nephron is the kidney's functional unit, meaning it is the smallest structure that can do the whole job of making urine. When we talk about kidney disease, we are really talking about nephrons being damaged, overworked, or dying.

The nephron in three steps: filter, reabsorb, secrete

Think of the nephron as a factory with three stations. First comes filtration. Blood enters a tuft of leaky capillaries called the glomerulus, which sits inside a cup called Bowman's capsule. Blood pressure pushes water and small molecules - sodium, glucose, urea, potassium - through the filter into the tubule. This early fluid is called filtrate. Big things like blood cells and proteins are too large to pass, so normally they stay in the blood. That is why finding protein or blood in the urine is a red flag: it usually means the filter is leaking.

Next comes reabsorption along the tubule (the proximal tubule, loop of Henle, and distal tubule). The body cannot afford to dump everything it just filtered, so about 99% of the water and nearly all the glucose and needed sodium are pulled back into the blood. Finally comes secretion, where the tubule actively adds unwanted substances - certain drugs, extra hydrogen ions, extra potassium - into the filtrate to be peed out. What is left after all three steps is urine.

Filter, then fine-tune. Filtration is fast and unfussy; reabsorption and secretion are where the kidney does its precise, life-sustaining accounting. A drug like furosemide works by blocking reabsorption in the loop, so more sodium and water leave as urine.

GFR: the number that tells you how well the kidney works

The single best measure of kidney function is the glomerular filtration rate (GFR) - how many milliliters of filtrate all the glomeruli produce per minute. A normal GFR is usually around 90-120 mL/min. When GFR falls, the kidney is filtering less, and wastes start backing up in the blood. GFR is the basis for staging chronic kidney disease (CKD); a GFR under 60 for three or more months defines CKD, and under 15 signals kidney failure. We usually estimate it (eGFR) from a blood test rather than measuring it directly.

The kidney's other jobs (it is not just a filter)

Losing kidney function causes trouble far beyond waste buildup, because the kidney moonlights in several roles:

  • Blood pressure - through the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS). When blood flow or pressure drops, the kidney releases renin, which sets off a cascade that constricts vessels and retains sodium and water to raise pressure.
  • Red blood cells - the kidney makes erythropoietin (EPO), the hormone that tells bone marrow to build red cells. This is why patients with failing kidneys often develop anemia.
  • Activating vitamin D - the kidney converts vitamin D to its active form, which lets the gut absorb calcium. Kidney disease therefore weakens bones and disturbs calcium balance.
  • Acid-base balance - the kidney excretes hydrogen ions and reclaims bicarbonate, keeping blood pH steady. Failing kidneys tend toward metabolic acidosis.
Why kidney patients feel awful all over. Fatigue (anemia from low EPO), high blood pressure (RAAS), and bone pain (inactive vitamin D) are not separate diseases - they are one failing organ showing up in many systems.

Reading the labs: creatinine and BUN

Two blood tests reveal how the nephrons are doing. Creatinine is a waste product from normal muscle breakdown. The body makes it at a steady rate and the kidney clears it, so when creatinine rises, it usually means filtration is falling - it is our everyday marker of GFR. BUN (blood urea nitrogen) measures urea, a waste from protein breakdown. BUN also rises when the kidney struggles, but it is more easily thrown off by dehydration, bleeding, and high-protein intake, so nurses read it alongside creatinine rather than alone. Section 2Acute kidney injury

Your kidneys are the body's quiet, constant filters. Every minute, about a fifth of the blood your heart pumps flows through them. Inside each kidney sit roughly a million tiny filtering units called nephrons, and each nephron starts with a knot of capillaries called the glomerulus. Blood pressure pushes fluid and waste across that filter to form urine, while the tubules that follow reclaim the water, sodium, and other things your body wants to keep. Working normally, the kidneys clear metabolic garbage, balance fluid and electrolytes, and help control blood pressure and red blood cell production. When they suddenly stumble, waste backs up fast.

Acute kidney injury (AKI) is a sudden drop in kidney function, unfolding over hours to days. The key word is sudden (unlike chronic kidney disease, which creeps along for months to years). The good news: because it is abrupt, AKI is often reversible if the underlying cause is caught and fixed before the nephrons are permanently damaged.

How we know the kidneys are failing

Two blood tests tell the story. Creatinine is a waste product from muscle that the kidneys normally clear at a steady rate; when filtering drops, creatinine rises. BUN (blood urea nitrogen) is a nitrogen waste from protein breakdown, and it climbs too. A rising creatinine means the glomerular filtration rate (GFR), the amount of blood filtered per minute, is falling. At the same time, the kidneys usually make less urine. Oliguria means putting out less than about 400 mL of urine in 24 hours (often described as under roughly 0.5 mL/kg/hr). Put simply: labs go up, urine goes down.

The three categories: where the problem lives

AKI is sorted by where the trouble sits relative to the kidney itself. This matters because the fix is completely different for each.

Core problemPoor blood flow to the kidneyDirect damage inside the kidneyObstruction of outflow after the kidney
Typical causesDehydration, hemorrhage, heart failure, shockAcute tubular necrosis, nephrotoxins, prolonged ischemiaKidney stones, enlarged prostate, tumors, blocked catheter

Prerenal AKI is the most common. Anything that drops blood flow (low volume from vomiting or bleeding, a weak heart, or widespread vasodilation in sepsis) starves the glomerulus of the pressure it needs to filter. The kidney tissue is still healthy, so if you restore perfusion quickly (fluids, treat the heart, stop the bleeding), function bounces back. Ignored, though, prolonged low flow becomes ischemic damage and tips into the intrarenal category.

Intrarenal (intrinsic) AKI means the nephron itself is injured. The classic form is acute tubular necrosis (ATN), where the delicate tubule cells die, either from prolonged ischemia or from toxins like certain antibiotics (aminoglycosides), IV contrast dye, or myoglobin released in rhabdomyolysis. Dead tubule cells slough off, clog the tubules, and can't reabsorb properly.

Postrenal AKI is a plumbing problem downstream: urine can't get out. A stone, an enlarged prostate, a tumor, or even a kinked catheter blocks flow. Pressure backs up into the kidney and stops filtration. This type is often the most quickly reversible, relieve the obstruction and urine pours out, but only if caught before back-pressure scars the tissue.

Find the category, fix the cause. Prerenal needs perfusion, intrarenal needs the toxin or insult removed and time to heal, and postrenal needs the blockage relieved. Treating the wrong one wastes the reversible window.

The phases of recovery

When AKI does progress, recovery classically moves through phases. During the oliguric phase (often lasting days to a couple of weeks), urine output is low, and waste, potassium, and fluid pile up. This is the dangerous stretch, watch closely for hyperkalemia (high potassium, which can trigger deadly heart arrhythmias), fluid overload, and metabolic acidosis.

As the tubules heal, the kidney enters the diuretic phase. The nephrons can filter again but haven't regained their concentrating ability, so urine output surges, sometimes several liters a day. Now the risk flips: patients can become dehydrated and lose too much sodium and potassium. Careful fluid and electrolyte replacement is essential. A slower recovery phase follows as function normalizes, though some patients never fully return to baseline.

The pendulum swings. Oliguric phase, worry about too much (potassium, fluid). Diuretic phase, worry about too little (volume, electrolytes). The same patient needs opposite monitoring a week apart.

Key points
  • AKI is a sudden, often reversible drop in kidney function, marked by rising creatinine and BUN with falling GFR and urine output (oliguria).
  • Prerenal AKI is poor blood flow to the kidney, intrarenal is direct damage (classically acute tubular necrosis), and postrenal is obstruction of urine outflow.

Section 3Chronic kidney disease

Your kidneys are quiet workhorses. Every day, about a million tiny filtering units called nephrons in each kidney strain your blood, pulling out waste and extra fluid to make urine while keeping the good stuff (protein, blood cells, most electrolytes) in circulation. They also do jobs that have nothing to do with peeing: they make erythropoietin (EPO) to tell your bone marrow to build red blood cells, activate vitamin D so your intestines can absorb calcium (which then strengthens bone), and balance the blood's acid level. In chronic kidney disease (CKD), nephrons are slowly and permanently destroyed, so all of those jobs start to fail at once.

What CKD is and how we stage it

CKD is a gradual, irreversible loss of nephrons over months to years. Because kidneys have huge reserve, a person can lose a lot of function before feeling sick, so we measure the damage with the glomerular filtration rate (GFR) - roughly, how many milliliters of blood the kidneys clean per minute. A normal GFR is usually around 90-120 mL/min. CKD is defined as kidney damage or a GFR below 60 for at least 3 months, and it is staged from 1 (mild) to 5 (kidney failure).

Why it happens: diabetes and hypertension

The two leading causes are the same two conditions nurses see everywhere. In diabetes, chronically high blood glucose damages the delicate glomerular capillaries (diabetic nephropathy), and one of the earliest warning signs is small amounts of protein leaking into the urine (albuminuria). In hypertension, the constant high pressure batters those same tiny vessels, thickening and scarring them. Cruelly, failing kidneys also raise blood pressure by retaining salt and water and firing the renin-angiotensin system - so hypertension both causes CKD and gets worse because of it, a vicious cycle.

Uremia: when wastes build up

As filtering fails, nitrogen wastes like urea and creatinine climb in the blood. This toxic buildup is called uremia, and because the blood reaches every organ, the symptoms are body-wide. Patients often feel exhausted, nauseated, and itchy (pruritus from waste deposited in skin); they may lose appetite, taste metal, and think slowly. In severe uremia, wastes irritate the heart lining (pericarditis) and the brain (uremic encephalopathy, causing confusion and even seizures). Uremia is why "the kidneys" become a whole-body problem.

The complications and why each one happens

Understanding CKD means tracing each problem back to a lost kidney job:

  • Anemia - fewer working cells make less EPO, so the marrow builds fewer red cells. Patients are pale, tired, and short of breath.
  • Bone disease (renal osteodystrophy) - kidneys can no longer activate vitamin D or excrete phosphate. Low calcium and high phosphate drive the parathyroids to pump out PTH, which pulls calcium out of bones, leaving them weak.
  • Hyperkalemia - the kidneys normally dump excess potassium. When they can't, potassium rises, threatening dangerous cardiac arrhythmias. This is often the most urgent lab.
  • Fluid overload - unexcreted salt and water back up, causing edema, hypertension, and pulmonary congestion.
  • Metabolic acidosis - kidneys normally excrete acid and regenerate bicarbonate. As that fails, acid accumulates, contributing to fatigue, bone loss, and rapid breathing.
Watch the potassium. In late CKD, hyperkalemia can be silent until it triggers a lethal arrhythmia. Peaked T waves on the ECG are a red-flag clue to act fast.

When the kidneys fail

At stage 5, often called end-stage renal disease (ESRD), the kidneys can no longer keep the body's chemistry safe. The two options are dialysis - a machine (hemodialysis) or the abdominal lining (peritoneal dialysis) that filters the blood artificially - or a kidney transplant. Dialysis manages the disease but does not cure it; it replaces only a fraction of true kidney function, which is why prevention and slowing progression matter so much. Section 4Glomerular disease: nephrotic vs nephritic

Every minute, your kidneys pull a huge amount of blood through roughly a million tiny filters called glomeruli. Picture each glomerulus as a wadded-up tuft of capillaries wrapped in a cup. Its filtering wall has three layers: the capillary lining (endothelium), a middle mesh called the glomerular basement membrane (GBM), and specialized cells with interlocking foot processes called podocytes. This wall is a smart sieve. It lets water, salts, and small wastes pass into the urine, but it holds back big things the body wants to keep, especially albumin (the main blood protein) and red blood cells. Two features make that possible: the pores are size-selective, and the wall carries a negative charge that repels negatively charged albumin. When this filter breaks, it tends to fail in one of two patterns, and telling them apart is one of the most testable ideas in renal nursing.

Nephrotic syndrome: a very leaky filter

In nephrotic syndrome, the filter becomes too permeable without much inflammation. The podocytes and the charge barrier are damaged, so albumin pours out. The hallmark is massive proteinuria, usually defined as more than 3.5 grams of protein in the urine per day. Losing that much protein drops the blood albumin, producing hypoalbuminemia. Because albumin normally holds fluid inside vessels by oncotic (pulling) pressure, losing it lets fluid leak into tissues, causing the heavy, generalized edema classic for this syndrome, often puffy eyes in the morning and swollen legs and belly by evening. The liver, sensing low protein, ramps up production of many proteins including lipoproteins, so cholesterol and triglycerides climb (hyperlipidemia), and fatty casts can appear in urine. Patients also lose clotting-regulator proteins and immunoglobulins, raising risks of thrombosis and infection. A classic cause in children is minimal change disease; in adults, membranous nephropathy and diabetic or lupus-related disease are common.

Nephritic syndrome: an inflamed filter

In nephritic syndrome, the problem is inflammation, usually immune complexes lodging in the glomerulus and triggering an immune attack. That inflammation physically breaks the capillary wall, so red blood cells escape into the urine. The hallmark is hematuria, often tea- or cola-colored, sometimes with red blood cell casts (tiny cell-molds shaped in the tubules) that are essentially diagnostic of glomerular bleeding. Protein leaks too, but usually in smaller amounts (typically less than 3.5 g/day). Inflammation also swells and clogs the filter, so filtration drops; the kidney retains salt and water, driving hypertension and a milder, often facial edema, plus a rising creatinine (azotemia) and reduced urine output. The teaching classic is post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis (PSGN): a child develops cola-colored urine, puffy eyes, and high blood pressure roughly 1 to 3 weeks after a strep throat or skin infection, as immune complexes deposit in the glomeruli.

Color tells the story. Frothy, foamy urine hints at heavy protein (nephrotic), while cola- or tea-colored urine points to blood (nephritic). It is a quick bedside clue, not a diagnosis.

How to keep the two straight

A simple anchor: nephrOtic = prOtein (huge protein loss), nephrItic = Inflammation and hematurIa (blood). Nephrotic is a quiet, leaky sieve; nephritic is an angry, bleeding, high-pressure one. Real patients can overlap, but the dominant pattern usually declares itself.

Section 5Kidney stones

Your kidneys are constantly filtering your blood and dissolving the leftover minerals and salts into your urine. Think of urine as a river carrying dissolved cargo. As long as there is enough water flowing, the minerals stay dissolved and wash out harmlessly. Kidney stones (nephrolithiasis) form when that balance tips: the urine becomes supersaturated, meaning it holds more mineral than it can keep dissolved. The excess starts to crystallize, and those tiny crystals clump together into a hard stone. It is the same physics as making rock candy, except it happens inside the collecting system of the kidney, and nobody wants it.

Why the urine becomes supersaturated

Two things push urine past its saturation point. First, too little water (dehydration) concentrates the minerals, so the same amount of solute is packed into less fluid. Second, too much mineral, from diet, genetics, or metabolic conditions, overloads the urine. When calcium, oxalate, uric acid, or other salts climb high enough, crystals precipitate out. Sluggish, concentrated, standing urine is the perfect environment for a stone to grow, which is why chronic low fluid intake is the single biggest modifiable risk factor.

The pain: renal colic

A stone sitting quietly in the kidney may cause nothing at all. The trouble starts when it drops into the ureter, the narrow tube draining urine to the bladder. The stone lodges and blocks flow, urine backs up, and the ureter stretches and spasms trying to squeeze the stone along. That produces renal colic: sudden, excruciating, wave-like flank pain that classically radiates to the groin as the stone travels downward. Patients are famously restless, unable to find any position that helps, which contrasts with the still, guarded posture of peritonitis. The stone also scrapes the ureteral lining, so you often see hematuria (blood in the urine, sometimes visible, sometimes only on dipstick). Nausea, vomiting, and urinary urgency are common companions because the kidney and gut share nerve pathways.

Restless, not still. A patient writhing and pacing with severe flank pain that shoots to the groin is the picture of renal colic. Someone who lies rigidly still is more worried about their abdomen.

Types of stones

Calcium stones, usually calcium oxalate, are by far the most common, making up roughly 75 to 80 percent of cases. Knowing the type matters because prevention differs.

FeatureCalcium stonesUric acid stones
FrequencyMost common (~75-80%)Less common
Urine conditionOften normal to alkalineFavored by acidic urine
Key contributorsHigh calcium/oxalate, dehydrationHigh purine intake, gout

A common point of confusion: cutting dietary calcium usually backfires. Normal calcium intake binds oxalate in the gut so it leaves in the stool instead of the urine, which actually lowers calcium-oxalate stone risk. The real fix is fluid.

Nursing priorities

Management rests on three pillars while the stone passes or is treated:

  • Pain control. Renal colic is genuinely one of the worst pains people report. NSAIDs like ketorolac are often first-line because they reduce ureteral spasm and inflammation; opioids may be added. Treat pain aggressively and early.
  • Hydration. Encourage generous oral or IV fluids to keep urine dilute and flowing, which helps flush the stone along and prevents new crystals from forming. This is prevention and treatment in one.
  • Strain all urine. Have the patient void through a fine mesh strainer to catch the stone. This confirms passage and, crucially, lets the lab analyze the stone's composition so prevention can be targeted.

Watch for warning signs that need escalation: fever (a blocked, infected kidney is an emergency), signs of complete obstruction, or intractable pain and vomiting. Larger stones that will not pass may need lithotripsy (shock waves that fragment the stone) or surgical removal. Section 6UTIs & incontinence

Your urinary tract is basically a plumbing system with a one-way flow and a built-in cleaning stream. The kidneys make urine, the ureters carry it down, the bladder stores it, and the urethra lets it out. Normally that flow is sterile from the bladder up, and every time you empty your bladder you flush out any stray bacteria trying to swim upstream. The lining also fights back with a slightly acidic pH and mucus that makes it hard for microbes to stick. A urinary tract infection (UTI) happens when bacteria, most commonly Escherichia coli from the bowel, colonize the urethra and climb upward faster than the body can rinse them away.

Lower UTI (cystitis) vs upper UTI (pyelonephritis)

Where the bacteria land decides how sick the patient looks. In a lower UTI (cystitis), the infection stays in the bladder and urethra. The inflamed bladder wall becomes irritated and hypersensitive, so patients feel dysuria (burning with urination), frequency, and urgency, often with cloudy or foul-smelling urine and low suprapubic discomfort. Because the infection is localized, patients usually stay afebrile and don't feel systemically ill.

An upper UTI (pyelonephritis) means bacteria have ascended the ureters into one or both kidneys. Now the infection reaches the bloodstream-rich renal tissue, so the body mounts a full systemic response: fever, chills, nausea, and the classic flank pain with costovertebral angle (CVA) tenderness when you percuss over the kidney. Pyelonephritis is the more dangerous picture because it can progress to urosepsis and can scar the kidney over time.

FeatureCystitis (lower)Pyelonephritis (upper)
LocationBladder / urethraKidney(s)
Hallmark symptomsDysuria, frequency, urgencyFever, chills, flank pain

Why women get more UTIs, and the catheter problem

Anatomy is the main reason women get far more UTIs than men. The female urethra is much shorter (roughly 4 cm versus about 20 cm in men), so bacteria have a shorter climb to the bladder. The urethral opening also sits close to the vagina and anus, giving perineal E. coli an easy route. Sexual activity mechanically pushes bacteria toward the urethra, and after menopause, lower estrogen thins the tissue and shifts the protective vaginal flora, raising risk again.

An indwelling (Foley) catheter is a major risk because it bypasses every one of those natural defenses at once. The tube is a direct highway into the bladder, bacteria form a biofilm along its surface where antibiotics and immune cells can't reach, and continuous drainage removes the flushing effect of a normal void. This is why catheter-associated UTIs (CAUTIs) are among the most common hospital-acquired infections, and why the single best prevention is removing the catheter as early as possible.

Culture, don't assume. Bacteria in the urine without symptoms (asymptomatic bacteriuria) usually is NOT treated except in pregnancy or before urologic surgery. Treating it needlessly breeds resistance.

The older adult who just gets confused

Older adults often skip the textbook burning-and-fever story entirely. Instead, a UTI may show up as new-onset confusion, delirium, or a sudden functional decline such as new incontinence, falls, or refusing to eat. This happens partly because a blunted immune response mutes fever and partly because an aging brain is very sensitive to the systemic inflammation and dehydration that infection brings. The teaching point for nurses: an acute change in mental status in an elder always deserves a workup that includes a urinalysis.

Delirium is a vital sign. In frail older adults, new confusion can be the first and only clue to infection. Trust the change in baseline even when the temperature looks normal.

Urinary incontinence at a glance

Incontinence is the involuntary leakage of urine, and it is a symptom, not a normal part of aging. The main types:

  • Stress incontinence — leakage with coughing, laughing, or lifting because weak pelvic floor support lets pressure overcome the sphincter.
  • Urge incontinence — a sudden, uncontrollable need to void from an overactive detrusor muscle contracting on its own.
  • Overflow incontinence — constant dribbling from a bladder that never fully empties, often from obstruction (enlarged prostate) or a weak, underactive detrusor.
  • Functional incontinence — a normal urinary tract, but a physical or cognitive barrier (mobility, dementia) keeps the person from reaching the toilet in time.

Key points
  • Cystitis (lower UTI) causes dysuria, frequency, and urgency without fever; pyelonephritis (upper UTI) adds fever, chills, and CVA/flank tenderness and can progress to sepsis.
  • Women get more UTIs because of a short urethra near the perineum; E. coli is the usual culprit.
  • Indwelling catheters bypass natural defenses and cause CAUTIs, so remove them as early as possible.
  • In older adults, new confusion or delirium may be the only sign of a UTI, even without fever.
  • Incontinence types: stress (leak with pressure), urge (overactive detrusor), overflow (incomplete emptying), and functional (a barrier to reaching the toilet).

The chapter in one breath

  • The nephron filters ~180 L/day at the glomerulus; GFR (usually ~90-120 mL/min) is the single best marker of kidney function, and it falls before symptoms appear.
  • Acute kidney injury (AKI) is a rapid, often reversible rise in creatinine and drop in urine output, sorted as prerenal (low perfusion, e.g. dehydration/shock), intrarenal (tubule damage like ATN), or postrenal (obstruction).
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is irreversible nephron loss over months to years (GFR <60 for 3+ months), driving anemia (low erythropoietin), bone disease (low active vitamin D), and metabolic acidosis.
  • In CKD the kidney stops excreting potassium and phosphate, so watch for hyperkalemia (peaked T waves, lethal arrhythmias) as the most urgent electrolyte threat.
  • Nephrotic syndrome is a leaky filter: massive proteinuria (>3.5 g/day), low albumin, generalized edema, and high cholesterol - think holes letting protein out.
  • Nephritic syndrome is an inflamed filter: hematuria (often tea-colored urine with RBC casts), hypertension, and mild-to-moderate proteinuria - think inflammation letting blood out.
  • Kidney stones (nephrolithiasis), most often calcium oxalate, cause sudden severe flank pain radiating to the groin (renal colic) with hematuria; the patient writhes and cannot get comfortable.
  • UTIs usually start as cystitis (dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain) and can ascend to pyelonephritis (flank pain, fever, CVA tenderness); confusion may be the only sign in older adults.
  • Urinary incontinence has distinct types - stress (leak with cough/sneeze from weak pelvic floor) versus urge (sudden strong need from an overactive detrusor) - and each is managed differently.

ReferenceWords worth knowing

A quick reference for the terms in this chapter — skim it now, or circle back whenever a word trips you up.

Acute kidney injury (AKI)
A sudden, often reversible drop in kidney function (over hours to days) that lets waste products like creatinine and BUN build up in the blood, usually with reduced urine output.
Acute tubular necrosis (ATN)
The most common intrarenal cause of AKI, in which the tubule cells are injured and die, most often from prolonged ischemia (low blood flow) or nephrotoxins such as certain antibiotics or contrast dye.
BUN (blood urea nitrogen)
A blood test measuring urea, a nitrogen waste from protein breakdown that the kidneys normally clear, so a rising BUN often signals reduced kidney function or dehydration.
Chronic kidney disease (CKD)
A gradual, permanent loss of kidney function lasting three months or longer, staged by the GFR and usually caused over time by diabetes or hypertension.
Creatinine
A waste product from normal muscle metabolism that the kidneys filter and excrete steadily, so a rising serum creatinine is one of the most reliable markers that kidney function is declining.
Dialysis
A treatment that artificially filters wastes, excess fluid, and electrolytes from the blood (by hemodialysis or peritoneal dialysis) when the kidneys can no longer do the job adequately.
Erythropoietin (EPO)
A hormone made mostly by the kidneys that tells the bone marrow to produce red blood cells, which is why failing kidneys often cause anemia.
GFR (glomerular filtration rate)
The volume of blood the glomeruli filter each minute (normally about 90–120 mL/min), used as the best overall measure of how well the kidneys are working.
Glomerulus
The tiny tuft of capillaries at the start of each nephron where blood is first filtered, allowing water and small wastes through while normally holding back blood cells and large proteins.
Nephron
The functional filtering unit of the kidney (about one million per kidney) that filters blood and fine-tunes the urine through its glomerulus and connected tubules.
Nephritic syndrome
A pattern of glomerular inflammation marked by blood in the urine (hematuria), mild-to-moderate protein loss, hypertension, and often reduced GFR.
Nephrotic syndrome
A pattern of glomerular damage marked by heavy protein loss in the urine (usually more than 3.5 g/day), low blood albumin, widespread edema, and high blood lipids.
Oliguria
Abnormally low urine output, usually defined as less than about 400 mL in 24 hours, which is a key warning sign of acute kidney injury.
Pyelonephritis
A bacterial infection of the kidney and its collecting system (often from a bladder infection that ascended upward), typically causing fever, chills, and flank pain.
Renal colic
Severe, cramping, wave-like flank pain that radiates toward the groin, classically caused by a kidney stone obstructing and stretching the ureter.
Uremia
The toxic clinical syndrome of advanced kidney failure in which retained nitrogen wastes cause symptoms such as nausea, fatigue, itching, and confusion.

Check yourself

1. A nursing instructor asks students to describe the basic filtering unit of the kidney. Which structure should the students identify, and what drives filtration there?
The nephron, driven by hydrostatic pressure in the glomerulus. Each kidney holds roughly a million nephrons. Blood enters the glomerulus under pressure, and that hydrostatic push forces water and small solutes across the filtration membrane into Bowman capsule, forming the glomerular filtrate. The GFR (glomerular filtration rate, normally about 90-120 mL/min) is the amount filtered per minute and is the single best index of overall kidney function.
2. A patient with chronic kidney disease is fatigued, short of breath, and has a hemoglobin of 8.5 g/dL. The patient asks why kidney disease is making them anemic. What is the nurse's best explanation of the underlying mechanism?
Diseased kidneys make too little erythropoietin (EPO), so the bone marrow makes fewer red blood cells. Beyond filtering, the kidney is an endocrine organ. Specialized cells sense oxygen and release erythropoietin, the hormone that tells bone marrow to produce red cells. When kidney tissue is damaged, EPO output falls and the patient develops a normocytic, normochromic anemia of CKD. This is why many CKD patients need synthetic EPO (epoetin) rather than just iron.
3. Which laboratory value is generally the more reliable indicator of GFR and kidney function, and why can the other value be misleading?
Creatinine is usually more reliable than BUN. Creatinine (normal about 0.6-1.2 mg/dL) is a steady waste product of muscle metabolism that the kidney filters at a fairly constant rate, so a rising level fairly directly reflects a falling GFR. BUN (blood urea nitrogen, normal about 10-20 mg/dL) is swayed by many non-kidney factors, dehydration, high-protein intake, GI bleeding, and steroids can all raise it, so it is a softer, less specific marker.
4. A patient becomes severely dehydrated after a bout of vomiting and diarrhea. Labs show a BUN:creatinine ratio of about 25:1 and a rising creatinine. Which category of acute kidney injury does this pattern most suggest?
Prerenal AKI from decreased kidney perfusion. In prerenal AKI the kidney tissue itself is healthy, but blood flow to it drops (from volume loss, hemorrhage, heart failure, or shock). The nephrons respond by avidly reabsorbing sodium and water, dragging urea back with it, which pushes the BUN:creatinine ratio above 20:1. Restoring perfusion (usually IV fluids here) often reverses it before tubular damage sets in.
5. Match each AKI category to its typical cause: prerenal, intrarenal (intrinsic), and postrenal.
Prerenal = poor perfusion; intrarenal = direct kidney tissue damage; postrenal = obstruction to outflow.
FeaturePrerenalIntrarenal
Problem siteBefore the kidney (blood flow in)Inside kidney tissue (nephron)
Classic causeHypovolemia, shock, heart failureAcute tubular necrosis, nephrotoxins, contrast, glomerulonephritis
Reversible?Yes, if perfusion restored quicklySlower; depends on tissue recovery
Postrenal AKI comes from blockage of urine outflow, kidney stones, an enlarged prostate (BPH), or tumors, causing back-pressure that eventually harms the nephrons.
6. A patient with end-stage CKD is confused, nauseated, itchy, and has a uremic frost. The nurse recognizes uremia. In plain terms, what is uremia and why does it cause these symptoms?
Uremia is the buildup of nitrogenous wastes (like urea) in the blood when kidneys can no longer clear them. As GFR falls in chronic kidney disease, waste products the kidney normally excretes accumulate systemically. These toxins irritate nearly every organ, producing encephalopathy (confusion), nausea and anorexia, pruritus (itching), and pericarditis. Uremic frost, white urea crystals on the skin, is a late, ominous sign. Uremia is a key indication that dialysis is needed.
7. A CKD patient's potassium returns at 6.8 mEq/L. What is the greatest immediate danger, and why do failing kidneys cause this?
Life-threatening cardiac dysrhythmias, because the kidneys can no longer excrete potassium. The kidney is the main route for removing potassium, so as function declines, potassium climbs (hyperkalemia, normal 3.5-5.0 mEq/L). High extracellular potassium destabilizes cardiac cell membranes, producing peaked T waves, widened QRS, and risk of ventricular fibrillation or asystole. This is a medical emergency, expect calcium gluconate to protect the heart plus insulin/dextrose to shift potassium into cells.
8. Two patients have glomerular disease. One has massive proteinuria and edema; the other has hematuria, hypertension, and only mild proteinuria. Which has nephrotic syndrome and which has nephritic syndrome?
Massive proteinuria + edema = nephrotic; hematuria + hypertension = nephritic.

9. A patient arrives in the ED writhing, unable to sit still, with sudden severe flank pain radiating to the groin and gross hematuria. What condition is most likely, and what causes the pain pattern?
Renal colic from a kidney stone (urolithiasis). A stone lodged in the ureter causes renal colic, classically excruciating, colicky (waves) flank pain that radiates toward the groin as the stone moves. The pain comes from ureteral spasm and obstruction with back-pressure. Unlike peritonitis, these patients cannot find a comfortable position and are restless. Hematuria occurs as the stone abrades the ureteral lining. Care focuses on pain control, hydration, and straining urine to catch the stone.
10. Compare cystitis and pyelonephritis. Which findings tell the nurse the infection has ascended from the bladder to the kidney?
Fever, flank/CVA tenderness, and systemic illness signal pyelonephritis rather than simple cystitis.
FeatureCystitis (bladder)Pyelonephritis (kidney)

Costovertebral angle (CVA) tenderness is a hallmark that the infection has reached the kidney.
11. Besides erythropoietin, the kidney performs several non-filtering jobs. List the kidney's roles in vitamin D, blood pressure, and acid-base balance.
The kidney activates vitamin D, helps regulate blood pressure via renin, and maintains acid-base balance by handling bicarbonate and hydrogen ions.
  • Vitamin D: the kidney performs the final activation step, converting vitamin D to its active form (calcitriol) so the gut can absorb calcium, this is why CKD causes bone disease.
  • Blood pressure: the kidney releases renin, triggering the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) to retain sodium and water and raise BP.
  • Acid-base: the kidney excretes hydrogen ions and reabsorbs/regenerates bicarbonate, so kidney failure often causes a metabolic acidosis.
12. An 84-year-old nursing home resident becomes suddenly confused and falls, but denies burning with urination. The nurse suspects a UTI. Why does a UTI often look so different in older adults?
Older adults frequently present with new confusion or functional decline instead of classic dysuria. In elderly patients a UTI may show up as acute confusion (delirium), agitation, new incontinence, falls, or decreased appetite rather than the textbook burning and urgency. Blunted immune and inflammatory responses mean fever may be low or absent, and cognitive changes can mask symptom reporting.
Assessment pearl. In any older adult with sudden mental status change, always consider infection, especially a UTI, on the differential.
