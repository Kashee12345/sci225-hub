Hematology: The Blood

Blood is a liquid organ — it carries oxygen, stops leaks, and defends the body — so when it goes wrong, the whole body feels it. This chapter covers how blood cells are made, the anemias that starve tissues of oxygen, the clotting and bleeding disorders, the rules of blood typing and transfusion, and the cancers of the blood itself.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Describe hematopoiesis and the role of erythropoietin.
  • Classify the common anemias and match each to its cause.
  • Explain hemostasis and the major bleeding and clotting disorders.
  • Apply ABO/Rh compatibility and respond to a transfusion reaction.
  • Distinguish leukemia, lymphoma, and multiple myeloma.

Section 1Blood, marrow & how cells are made

Think of blood as a river that touches every cell you own. It looks like a simple red liquid, but it is actually a connective tissue - living cells suspended in fluid. Blood delivers oxygen and nutrients, hauls away carbon dioxide and waste, carries hormones and immune defenders, helps clot a wound, and even balances your temperature and pH. When something goes wrong with blood, the effects show up everywhere at once, because everything depends on it.

What blood is made of

If you spin a tube of blood in a centrifuge, it separates into layers. The top ~55% is plasma - a straw-colored fluid that is mostly water plus proteins (albumin, which holds fluid inside vessels; clotting factors; and antibodies), electrolytes, glucose, and wastes. The bottom ~45% is packed red cells; that percentage is the hematocrit. A thin middle layer, the buffy coat, holds white cells and platelets.

  • Red blood cells (erythrocytes) - carry oxygen. By far the most numerous.
  • White blood cells (leukocytes) - the immune army (neutrophils, lymphocytes, monocytes, eosinophils, basophils).
  • Platelets (thrombocytes) - cell fragments that plug leaks and start clotting.

Hematopoiesis - where blood cells are born

Every blood cell traces back to one ancestor: the hematopoietic stem cell (HSC) living in the red bone marrow (in adults, mainly the pelvis, sternum, vertebrae, and ends of long bones). This process is called hematopoiesis. A stem cell is pluripotent - it can become any blood cell - and it makes copies of itself so the factory never runs out. As daughter cells mature, they commit down one of two big lines: the myeloid line (red cells, platelets, and most white cells) or the lymphoid line (B and T lymphocytes). Because the marrow constantly replaces cells that wear out, anything that damages the marrow - chemotherapy, radiation, leukemia crowding it out, or aplastic anemia - can drop all the cell lines at once, a state called pancytopenia.

One factory, many products. When you see a low red cell, white cell, AND platelet count together, think marrow, not three separate problems. The common thread is the stem cell nursery.

Erythropoietin - the kidney's oxygen alarm

How does the body know to make more red cells? The kidneys act as oxygen sensors. When they detect low oxygen reaching their own tissue (hypoxia) - from blood loss, high altitude, or lung disease - they release a hormone called erythropoietin (EPO). EPO travels to the bone marrow and tells it to ramp up red cell production. This is a beautiful negative-feedback loop: more red cells means more oxygen delivery, which quiets the alarm and lowers EPO again.

This explains a classic clinical picture. In chronic kidney disease, the damaged kidneys stop making enough EPO, so the marrow never gets the signal - the patient develops anemia of chronic kidney disease even though the marrow itself is healthy. That is why these patients are often treated with synthetic EPO. It also explains why athletes and high-altitude dwellers naturally run higher red cell counts.

Hemoglobin - how red cells carry oxygen

A red cell is essentially a bag stuffed with hemoglobin (Hgb), the iron-containing protein that actually binds oxygen. Each hemoglobin molecule has four heme groups, and each heme holds one iron atom that grabs one oxygen molecule - so one hemoglobin carries up to four oxygen molecules. The red cell's flexible biconcave disc shape gives it lots of surface area and lets it squeeze through tiny capillaries.

This is why the pieces have to line up. You need enough iron (to build heme), enough vitamin B12 and folate (to build the cell), and a healthy marrow responding to EPO. Take away any one and oxygen delivery falls. Low hemoglobin is anemia, and its symptoms flow directly from the mechanism: fatigue and pallor because tissues are starved of oxygen, and tachycardia and shortness of breath because the heart speeds up to move the remaining oxygen faster. Nurses usually watch the hemoglobin and hematocrit together as the fastest read on a patient's oxygen-carrying capacity.

Symptoms follow oxygen, not just numbers. A patient who bleeds fast can feel terrible before their hemoglobin fully drops, while someone with slow chronic anemia may compensate and feel okay at a surprisingly low value. Always treat the patient, not only the lab.
Key points
  • Blood is a connective tissue: ~55% plasma (water plus albumin, clotting factors, antibodies) and ~45% cells - red cells, white cells, and platelets.
  • Hematopoiesis makes all blood cells from pluripotent stem cells in the red bone marrow, splitting into myeloid and lymphoid lines; marrow damage can cause pancytopenia.
  • The kidneys sense low oxygen and release erythropoietin to drive red cell production - so chronic kidney disease causes anemia from lost EPO.
  • Hemoglobin is the iron-based protein that binds oxygen; each molecule carries up to four O2, and building it needs iron, B12, and folate.
  • Low hemoglobin (anemia) starves tissues of oxygen, producing fatigue, pallor, and compensatory tachycardia - watch Hgb and hematocrit together.

Section 2Anemias

Every one of your cells needs a steady supply of oxygen to make energy, and oxygen does not travel through blood on its own. It rides on hemoglobin, the iron-rich protein packed inside your red blood cells (RBCs). Healthy bone marrow constantly builds new RBCs, stuffs them with hemoglobin, and releases them to circulate for about 120 days. When there is not enough functional hemoglobin to carry the oxygen your tissues need, we call that anemia. Notice the wording: anemia is not simply "low blood." It is low oxygen-carrying capacity, whether from too few RBCs, too little hemoglobin per cell, or hemoglobin that does not work.

Because the problem is tissue oxygen delivery, the classic symptoms make sense once you trace the mechanism. Tissues starved of oxygen produce less energy, so patients feel fatigue and weakness. With fewer red cells reflecting light through the skin and mucous membranes, you see pallor (pale skin, conjunctiva, nail beds). The heart tries to compensate by pumping faster to move what little oxygen there is, causing tachycardia. And because the blood is oxygen-poor, patients breathe faster and feel short of breath, especially with exertion, which is dyspnea. These are the shared symptoms of almost every anemia; the differences lie in why the hemoglobin is missing.

Iron-deficiency anemia (microcytic)

Iron is the core of the heme molecule that binds oxygen. Without enough iron, the marrow makes red cells that are small (microcytic, low MCV) and pale (hypochromic) because they simply cannot fill with hemoglobin. This is the most common anemia worldwide. Causes usually fall into three buckets: blood loss (heavy menstrual periods, or slow GI bleeding from ulcers or colon cancer, which quietly drains iron), increased demand (pregnancy, infancy, rapid growth), and poor intake or absorption (diets low in iron, or gut conditions). Lab clues include low ferritin (the body's iron storage form) and low serum iron.

Trace the bleed. New iron-deficiency anemia in an older adult is considered a GI bleed (and possible colon cancer) until proven otherwise. Iron pills treat the number, but the source must be found.

B12 and folate deficiency (macrocytic)

Vitamin B12 and folate are required to build DNA. When they run short, developing red cells cannot divide properly, so they grow large and fragile instead, producing macrocytic (high MCV) megaloblastic anemia. Folate deficiency comes from poor diet (few leafy greens), alcohol use, or pregnancy demand. B12 deficiency has a special cause worth memorizing: pernicious anemia. Absorbing B12 from food requires intrinsic factor, a protein made by the stomach's parietal cells. In pernicious anemia, an autoimmune attack destroys those cells, intrinsic factor disappears, and B12 cannot be absorbed no matter how much is eaten, so it must be replaced by injection. One key distinction: because B12 also maintains the nervous system, its deficiency adds neurologic signs (numbness, tingling, balance problems) that folate deficiency does not.

Hemolytic anemias and sickle cell disease

Hemolytic anemias occur when red cells are destroyed faster than the marrow can replace them (hemolysis means RBC breakdown). Because dying cells spill their contents, you often see jaundice from the released bilirubin. The classic inherited example is sickle cell disease. A single genetic change produces abnormal hemoglobin called HbS. When oxygen levels drop, or with dehydration, infection, or cold, HbS molecules stick together and warp the soft round RBC into a rigid, crescent (sickle) shape. These stiff cells jam small vessels, blocking blood flow in what is called a vaso-occlusive crisis - episodes of severe, sudden pain as tissues downstream are starved of oxygen. The sickled cells are also fragile and are destroyed early, driving chronic anemia.

Crisis care is oxygen, fluids, and pain control. Hydration and oxygen help unsickle cells and prevent further vaso-occlusion; pain is often severe and real. Treat it.

Finally, aplastic anemia is a marrow-failure state where the bone marrow stops producing blood cells altogether, so RBCs, white cells, and platelets all fall together (pancytopenia). Explore the different anemia types in the figure below.

Key points
  • Anemia is low functional hemoglobin, reducing oxygen delivery; shared signs (fatigue, pallor, tachycardia, dyspnea) all stem from tissue hypoxia and cardiac compensation.
  • Iron-deficiency anemia is microcytic and most common; a new case in an older adult means rule out GI bleeding.
  • B12 and folate deficiencies are macrocytic; only B12 deficiency causes neurologic signs, and pernicious anemia is loss of intrinsic factor blocking B12 absorption.
  • Sickle cell disease (HbS) sickles under low oxygen, causing painful vaso-occlusive crises; manage with oxygen, hydration, and pain control.
  • Aplastic anemia is bone marrow failure producing pancytopenia.

Section 3Bleeding & clotting disorders

Every time you get a paper cut, a beautifully choreographed emergency response kicks in to stop the bleeding within minutes. This process is called hemostasis (literally "blood standing still"), and it happens in overlapping steps. Understanding the normal sequence is the key to understanding what goes wrong in bleeding and clotting disorders.

Normal hemostasis: how the body plugs a leak

The moment a blood vessel is injured, three things happen in quick succession. First, vasoconstriction: the vessel clamps down to slow blood flow to the area. Second, the platelet plug forms. Platelets are tiny cell fragments that normally glide past smooth vessel walls, but when they touch exposed collagen at an injury, they stick, activate, change shape, and clump together, helped by a bridging protein called von Willebrand factor (vWF). This soft plug is a quick fix. Third comes the coagulation cascade, a chain reaction of clotting proteins (factors) that activate one another in sequence. The cascade has an intrinsic and extrinsic pathway that converge, ultimately turning prothrombin into thrombin. Thrombin then converts fibrinogen into fibrin, tough protein threads that weave through the platelet plug like rebar in concrete, creating a stable clot. Later, the clot is dissolved by fibrinolysis once healing is underway.

Two clues, two problems. Platelet or vWF problems cause immediate, superficial bleeding like petechiae, bruising, and mucosal ooze. Clotting-factor problems cause delayed, deep bleeding into joints and muscles. The pattern tells you where the defect is.

Thrombocytopenia and ITP: too few platelets

Thrombocytopenia means a low platelet count (usually below 150,000/mm3, with serious bleeding risk often under 20,000). With too few platelets, the first plug is weak. Patients show petechiae (pinpoint red spots), purpura, easy bruising, nosebleeds, and gum bleeding. In immune thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP), the immune system makes antibodies that tag the patient's own platelets, and the spleen destroys them. It often follows a viral illness in children and is more chronic in adults. Treatment targets the immune attack, using corticosteroids, IVIG, and sometimes splenectomy.

Hemophilia and von Willebrand disease: the factor problems

Hemophilia is an inherited deficiency of a clotting factor, so the platelet plug forms fine but the fibrin mesh never sets properly. Both common types are X-linked recessive, which is why they classically affect males, whose mothers are carriers. Hemophilia A is a deficiency of factor VIII; hemophilia B (Christmas disease) is a deficiency of factor IX. Clinically they look identical: hemarthrosis (deep bleeding into joints), muscle bleeds, and prolonged bleeding after surgery or trauma. Lab work shows a prolonged aPTT with a normal platelet count. Treatment is replacing the missing factor.

Von Willebrand disease is the most common inherited bleeding disorder and is usually autosomal (affecting both sexes equally). Missing or defective vWF means platelets cannot stick well, and because vWF also carries factor VIII, that factor drops too. So it blends both worlds: mucosal, platelet-type bleeding (heavy menstrual periods, nosebleeds) plus a mild factor picture.

DIC and DVT: when clotting itself becomes the disease

Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) is a dangerous paradox. Triggered by sepsis, trauma, obstetric complications, or cancer, the cascade fires everywhere at once, throwing tiny clots throughout the microcirculation. This consumes platelets and clotting factors faster than the body can replace them. The result is a patient who is both clotting and bleeding: microclots damage organs while the depleted supply leaves them oozing from IV sites, gums, and incisions. Labs show low platelets, prolonged PT/aPTT, low fibrinogen, and elevated D-dimer. DIC is always secondary, so treatment means fixing the underlying cause.

Clotting can also go wrong in a single vessel. A deep vein thrombosis (DVT) is a clot in a deep vein, usually of the leg. Virchow's triad names the three ingredients that promote it: venous stasis (immobility), endothelial injury (vessel-wall damage), and hypercoagulability (blood that clots too readily). The feared complication is a piece breaking off and traveling to the lungs as a pulmonary embolism.

Key points
  • Hemostasis runs in order: vasoconstriction, platelet plug (needs vWF), then the coagulation cascade making thrombin and fibrin.
  • Platelet/vWF problems cause immediate superficial bleeding (petechiae, mucosal); factor problems cause delayed deep bleeding (joints, muscles).
  • ITP is autoimmune platelet destruction; hemophilia A (factor VIII) and B (factor IX) are X-linked; von Willebrand disease is the most common inherited bleeding disorder.
  • DIC consumes platelets and factors, so the patient clots AND bleeds at once, with a high D-dimer; it is always secondary to another problem.
  • DVT forms via Virchow's triad (stasis, endothelial injury, hypercoagulability) and can embolize to the lungs.

Section 4Blood types & transfusion

Every drop of blood carries a kind of ID badge. On the surface of your red blood cells sit tiny protein and sugar markers called antigens, and floating in your plasma are antibodies, immune proteins that attack any antigen your body does not recognize as its own. Your immune system learns early in life to leave your own red cells alone while staying armed against foreign ones. This self-versus-nonself recognition is the whole reason transfusions must be matched. Give someone blood carrying an antigen their plasma has antibodies against, and those antibodies clamp onto the donor cells and destroy them, sometimes with life-threatening speed.

The ABO system

The ABO system sorts blood into four types based on which antigens sit on the red cells. Type A cells carry the A antigen, type B carry B, type AB carry both, and type O carry neither. Here is the elegant, slightly counterintuitive part: your plasma automatically makes antibodies against whatever antigen you lack. So type A blood has anti-B antibodies, type B has anti-A, type O has both anti-A and anti-B, and type AB has neither.

Because type O cells carry no A or B antigen, there is nothing on them for a recipient's antibodies to attack, which is why O-negative is called the universal donor, the go-to in a trauma emergency before typing is done. Because type AB plasma carries no anti-A or anti-B antibodies, an AB person can receive any ABO type, making AB-positive the universal recipient.

The Rh factor and hemolytic disease of the newborn

On top of ABO sits a second marker, the Rh factor (also called the D antigen). If your red cells carry it, you are Rh-positive; if not, Rh-negative. Unlike ABO, Rh-negative people do not come with ready-made anti-Rh antibodies. They only build them after being exposed to Rh-positive blood, a process called sensitization.

This matters most in pregnancy. If an Rh-negative mother carries an Rh-positive baby, a small leak of fetal blood, often during delivery, can sensitize her. The first baby is usually fine, but in a later Rh-positive pregnancy her anti-Rh antibodies can cross the placenta and destroy the fetal red cells. This is hemolytic disease of the newborn (HDN), also called erythroblastosis fetalis, causing fetal anemia, jaundice, and heart failure.

The fix is RhoGAM (Rh immune globulin), given around 28 weeks and again within 72 hours of delivery. RhoGAM works by mopping up any stray fetal Rh-positive cells before the mother's immune system can notice them and start making its own permanent antibodies, so sensitization never happens.

RhoGAM prevents, it does not treat. Once a mother is already sensitized, RhoGAM cannot undo it. Its whole job is to stop that first alarm from ever sounding.

Type, screen, and crossmatch

Before any transfusion, the lab types the blood (identifies ABO and Rh), screens the plasma for unexpected antibodies, and crossmatches by mixing donor cells with recipient plasma to confirm they do not clump. Clumping, called agglutination, is the visible warning that antibodies are attacking, and it means that unit is unsafe. This careful matching is why transfusion is normally so safe.

When a transfusion goes wrong

Reactions still happen, and telling them apart guides your response.

  • Acute hemolytic reaction is the feared one, usually from an ABO mismatch (often a clerical or ID error). Recipient antibodies destroy donor cells, releasing hemoglobin that can trigger fever, chills, flank or back pain, dark urine, hypotension, and kidney failure.
  • Febrile non-hemolytic reaction is the most common: fever and chills from the recipient reacting to donor white blood cells, uncomfortable but usually not dangerous.
  • Allergic reaction is a response to donor plasma proteins, ranging from hives and itching up to anaphylaxis.

For any suspected reaction, the priority is the same: stop the transfusion immediately. Keep the IV line open with normal saline (using fresh tubing so no more blood enters), then check vital signs, notify the provider and blood bank, and send the blood bag and tubing back for analysis.

Stop first, assess second. Every extra milliliter of mismatched blood adds to the damage. Stopping the infusion is always the first nursing action, before vitals, before the phone call.

Section 5Blood cancers

Your bone marrow is a busy blood factory. Deep inside your big bones, hematopoietic stem cells divide and mature into three great families of blood cells: red cells that carry oxygen, platelets that plug leaks, and white cells that fight infection. Normally this factory is tightly controlled. Every cell knows when to grow, when to stop, and when to die. Blood cancers happen when one line of blood cells loses that control and multiplies without a brake. The trouble is not just the abnormal cells themselves, but the fact that they take up space, resources, and function that healthy cells need. Understanding which cell went rogue and where it piles up explains almost every symptom your patient will have.

Leukemia: the marrow gets crowded out

Leukemia is cancer of the white-cell-forming lines in the bone marrow. Immature, useless white cells (often called blasts) reproduce so fast that they physically crowd out normal production. Picture a factory floor jammed with defective units so nothing else can be built. That single mechanism explains the classic triad: too few red cells means anemia (fatigue, pallor, shortness of breath), too few platelets means thrombocytopenia (easy bruising, petechiae, bleeding gums), and too few functional white cells means dangerous infection risk, even though the total white count on the lab report may look high.

Leukemias are sorted two ways. Acute vs chronic describes tempo: acute leukemias explode with immature blasts and make patients sick in days to weeks, while chronic leukemias smolder for months to years with more mature-looking cells. Myeloid vs lymphocytic describes the parent line: myeloid cells give rise to red cells, platelets, and granulocytes, while lymphocytic cells give rise to lymphocytes. Combine them and you get the four big names.

Lymphoma: cancer that builds tumors in lymph tissue

Where leukemia floods the marrow and blood, lymphoma is a solid cancer of lymphocytes that grows into masses inside lymph nodes and lymphoid organs like the spleen. The hallmark sign is painless lymphadenopathy: a firm, swollen, non-tender node in the neck, armpit, or groin. Patients often also have B symptoms, which is the textbook name for the trio of drenching night sweats, unexplained fever, and weight loss.

Lymphomas split into two branches. Hodgkin lymphoma is defined by a specific giant abnormal cell, the Reed-Sternberg cell, on biopsy. It usually starts in one node region and spreads in an orderly, predictable pattern, which helps make it one of the more curable cancers. Non-Hodgkin lymphoma is a large, diverse group without Reed-Sternberg cells; it tends to be more scattered and unpredictable at diagnosis and is far more common overall.

Multiple myeloma: rogue plasma cells attack bone and kidney

Plasma cells are the mature antibody-factory version of B lymphocytes. In multiple myeloma, one plasma cell line turns malignant and churns out a single useless antibody, called a monoclonal (M) protein, while crowding the marrow. Its damage spells out neatly with the mnemonic CRAB: Calcium elevated, Renal (kidney) injury, Anemia, and Bone lesions.

  • Bone lesions: myeloma cells activate osteoclasts that eat away bone, creating punched-out lytic lesions, deep bone pain, and pathologic fractures.
  • Hypercalcemia: the dissolving bone dumps calcium into the blood, causing confusion, constipation, and thirst.
  • Kidney injury: the flood of M protein and calcium clogs and damages the renal tubules.
  • Anemia and infection: marrow crowding plus antibodies that do not actually work.
One presentation, three cancers. Notice that fatigue (anemia), frequent infections, and easy bruising show up again and again. Any patient with that trio plus swollen nodes, bone pain, or an odd CBC deserves a closer hematologic look.
The high white count that still means infection risk. In acute leukemia the WBC number can be sky-high, but those cells are non-functional blasts. Treat the patient as immunocompromised regardless of what the count says.

  • Lymphoma forms solid masses in lymph nodes: Hodgkin shows Reed-Sternberg cells and spreads predictably; non-Hodgkin is more common and more scattered. Watch for painless lymphadenopathy plus B symptoms.

The chapter in one breath

  • Hematopoiesis from a single bone-marrow stem cell makes all blood cells; erythropoietin from the kidney drives red-cell production, so kidney failure often causes anemia.
  • Blood splits into plasma (fluid, proteins, clotting factors) and formed elements: RBCs carry oxygen via hemoglobin, WBCs fight infection, and platelets plug leaks.
  • Iron-deficiency anemia makes RBCs small and pale (microcytic, hypochromic) with low ferritin, causing fatigue, pallor, and pica.
  • Vitamin B12/folate deficiency causes large immature cells (macrocytic, megaloblastic anemia); only B12 loss adds neuro signs, and B12 needs stomach intrinsic factor to absorb.
  • Sickle cell anemia is inherited hemoglobin S that warps RBCs when deoxygenated, triggering painful vaso-occlusive crises, while hemolytic anemias destroy cells early and raise bilirubin.
  • Clotting fails from too few platelets (thrombocytopenia), missing factors (hemophilia), or widespread clotting-then-bleeding (DIC); watch for petechiae, bruising, and prolonged bleeding.
  • Transfusion safety hinges on ABO and Rh typing: type O is the universal RBC donor, AB the universal recipient, and a mismatch triggers a life-threatening hemolytic reaction.
  • Leukemias flood the marrow with abnormal white cells (crowding out normal lines), lymphomas grow in lymph nodes (Hodgkin vs non-Hodgkin), and multiple myeloma is malignant plasma cells eating bone.

ReferenceWords worth knowing

A quick reference for the terms in this chapter — skim it now, or circle back whenever a word trips you up.

ABO system
The main red-cell blood-group classification (A, B, AB, O) based on which sugar antigens sit on the red-cell surface, so a patient's plasma carries antibodies against the antigens they lack and a mismatched transfusion triggers a dangerous hemolytic reaction.
Anemia
A reduced number of red blood cells or amount of hemoglobin, which lowers the blood's oxygen-carrying capacity and usually causes fatigue, pallor, and shortness of breath.
DIC (disseminated intravascular coagulation)
A life-threatening emergency in which widespread clotting is switched on throughout the small vessels, using up clotting factors and platelets so fast that the patient paradoxically both clots and bleeds at the same time.
Erythropoietin
A hormone released mainly by the kidneys in response to low oxygen that signals the bone marrow to make more red blood cells.
Hematopoiesis
The ongoing process, occurring mostly in red bone marrow, by which stem cells mature into all the formed elements of blood: red cells, white cells, and platelets.
Hemoglobin
The iron-containing protein inside red blood cells that binds oxygen in the lungs and releases it to the tissues, and also gives blood its red color.
Hemophilia
An inherited (usually X-linked) bleeding disorder caused by a missing or deficient clotting factor (factor VIII in type A, factor IX in type B), leading to prolonged bleeding, often into joints and muscles.
Hemostasis
The body's coordinated process of stopping bleeding, moving from blood-vessel spasm to a platelet plug and finally a stable fibrin clot.
Intrinsic factor
A protein made by the stomach's parietal cells that binds vitamin B12 so it can be absorbed in the small intestine, without which B12 deficiency develops.
Leukemia
A cancer of the blood-forming tissue in which the marrow overproduces abnormal, immature white blood cells that crowd out healthy cells, leaving the patient prone to infection, bleeding, and anemia.
Macrocytic
Describing red blood cells that are abnormally large, a pattern often seen in vitamin B12 or folate deficiency.
Microcytic
Describing red blood cells that are abnormally small, a pattern classically seen in iron-deficiency anemia.
Pernicious anemia
A type of macrocytic anemia caused by loss of intrinsic factor (often from autoimmune damage to the stomach lining), which prevents vitamin B12 absorption and impairs red-cell maturation.
Sickle cell disease
An inherited disorder in which abnormal hemoglobin S makes red cells stiffen into a crescent shape under low-oxygen stress, so the rigid cells block small vessels and cause painful crises, hemolysis, and organ damage.
Thrombocytopenia
An abnormally low platelet count, which raises the risk of easy bruising and prolonged or spontaneous bleeding.
Virchow's triad
The three conditions that together promote abnormal clot formation: sluggish blood flow (stasis), vessel-wall injury, and increased blood clottability (hypercoagulability).

Check yourself

1. A patient with chronic kidney disease has a hemoglobin of 8.2 g/dL. The nurse understands the anemia is most directly caused by which mechanism?
Decreased erythropoietin production by the diseased kidneys. The kidneys sense oxygen levels and normally release erythropoietin (EPO), the hormone that tells red bone marrow to make more red blood cells. When kidney tissue is damaged, it makes less EPO, so hematopoiesis slows and the patient becomes anemic despite having enough iron.
2. A 32-year-old woman with heavy menstrual periods reports fatigue and pale skin. Labs show low hemoglobin, low MCV, and low ferritin. Which type of anemia is most consistent with these findings?
Iron-deficiency anemia (microcytic, hypochromic). Iron is the core of hemoglobin, so when iron runs low the marrow builds red cells that are small (low MCV, microcytic) and pale (hypochromic). A low ferritin confirms depleted iron stores, and chronic blood loss such as heavy menses is the classic cause in menstruating women.
3. A patient with a macrocytic anemia asks why their red blood cells are larger than normal. What is the nurse's best explanation of the underlying mechanism?
Lack of B12 or folate impairs DNA synthesis, so cells grow large but divide poorly. Vitamin B12 and folate are needed for DNA replication during cell division. Without them the developing red cells keep growing but cannot divide on schedule, producing the large, immature megaloblasts that show up as a high MCV (macrocytic) anemia.
4. A patient with pernicious anemia asks why oral vitamin B12 pills did not work and they now need injections. What is the nurse's best response?
Pernicious anemia lacks intrinsic factor, so B12 cannot be absorbed from the gut. The stomach's parietal cells normally make intrinsic factor, which binds B12 so it can be absorbed in the terminal ileum. In pernicious anemia an autoimmune attack destroys these cells, so oral B12 passes through unabsorbed and lifelong intramuscular (or high-dose) B12 is usually required.
5. A patient in sickle cell crisis is admitted with severe bone and joint pain. Which set of nursing priorities best addresses the underlying pathophysiology?
Hydration, oxygen, and aggressive pain management (often opioids). In a vaso-occlusive crisis, abnormal hemoglobin S causes red cells to sickle and clump, blocking small vessels and starving tissue of oxygen (ischemia), which triggers the pain. IV fluids lower blood viscosity to restore flow, and oxygen (when the patient is hypoxic) helps limit further sickling, while pain is real and usually needs opioids, not undertreatment.
6. When explaining hemostasis, the nurse describes the step immediately after a blood vessel is injured and before the clotting cascade fully activates. Which event occurs first?
Platelet plug formation (primary hemostasis). After vasoconstriction narrows the injured vessel, platelets stick to the exposed collagen and to each other, forming a temporary platelet plug. Then the coagulation cascade (secondary hemostasis) lays down fibrin to reinforce that plug into a stable clot.
7. A patient with hemophilia A is missing which specific component of the coagulation cascade?
Clotting factor VIII. Hemophilia A is an X-linked deficiency of factor VIII, a protein in the coagulation cascade. Without it, the fibrin-building steps stall, so patients form platelet plugs but cannot stabilize them, leading to prolonged bleeding and hallmark hemarthrosis (bleeding into joints).
8. A critically ill septic patient is simultaneously oozing from IV sites and forming small clots throughout the body. Labs show low platelets, low fibrinogen, and elevated D-dimer. Which condition does the nurse suspect?
Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC). In DIC the cascade is switched on everywhere at once, forming tiny clots that consume platelets and clotting factors. Once those are used up, the patient paradoxically bleeds; the consumption pattern shows as low platelets, low fibrinogen, and high D-dimer from clot breakdown.
9. A patient has type O-negative blood. The nurse recognizes this blood type is known as the universal what, and why?
Universal donor, because O-negative red cells have no A, B, or Rh antigens. ABO and Rh typing is based on antigens on the red cell surface. O-negative cells carry none of the A, B, or D (Rh) antigens, so a recipient's antibodies find nothing to attack, making these red cells safe to give to almost anyone in an emergency.
10. Which blood type is considered the universal recipient, and what makes it able to receive red cells from any ABO/Rh type?
Type AB-positive, because the plasma has no anti-A or anti-B antibodies. An AB-positive person has A, B, and Rh antigens on their cells, so their immune system never made antibodies against them. With no anti-A, anti-B, or anti-Rh antibodies circulating, donor red cells of any ABO/Rh type are not attacked.
11. Fifteen minutes into a blood transfusion, a patient develops fever, chills, low back pain, and dark urine. What is the nurse's FIRST action?
Stop the transfusion immediately. These are signs of an acute hemolytic transfusion reaction, usually from ABO incompatibility where recipient antibodies destroy donor cells. Stopping the blood halts further antigen-antibody destruction; the nurse then keeps the IV line open with normal saline, notifies the provider and blood bank, and monitors for shock and renal injury.
12. A nursing instructor asks a student to distinguish leukemia, lymphoma, and multiple myeloma. Which statement is correct?
Leukemia arises in blood/bone marrow (WBCs), lymphoma in lymph nodes/lymphatic tissue, and myeloma in plasma cells. Leukemia is a cancer of white blood cells that crowds the bone marrow; lymphoma forms solid tumors in lymph nodes and lymphoid tissue (often painless lymphadenopathy); multiple myeloma is a malignancy of antibody-producing plasma cells that damages bone and produces abnormal proteins.
