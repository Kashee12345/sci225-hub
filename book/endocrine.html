The Endocrine System

The endocrine system is the body's chemical messaging service — slower than nerves, but with reach that lasts hours to days. When one gland makes too much or too little of a hormone, the ripple effects show up everywhere: blood sugar, heart rate, blood pressure, mood, growth, and water balance. This chapter gives you the one idea that unlocks all of it — feedback — and then walks each major gland from healthy to what goes wrong.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Trace how the hypothalamus and pituitary command the other glands.
  • Use negative feedback to predict a lab (e.g., what TSH does when T4 is low).
  • Contrast the too-much vs too-little state for the adrenal, thyroid, and ADH.
  • Explain why Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes are different diseases, and tell DKA from HHS.
  • Recognize growth-hormone excess by timing (before vs after the growth plates close).

Section 1The chain of command

Think of the endocrine system as a company. The hypothalamus is the executive — it reads the body's status and decides what needs to change. It rarely acts directly; instead it sends orders to its manager, the pituitary gland, which sits just beneath it.

The pituitary has two very different halves. The anterior pituitary is a factory — it makes six hormones, and several of them are "tropic," meaning their whole job is to tell another gland what to do: TSH → thyroid, ACTH → adrenal cortex, FSH & LH → ovaries and testes, plus GH (growth) and prolactin (milk). The posterior pituitary is a storage locker — it doesn't make hormones, it just stores and releases two the hypothalamus made: ADH (holds onto water) and oxytocin (labor and milk let-down).

Here is the single most important idea in this chapter. The system regulates itself with negative feedback, exactly like a thermostat. When the final hormone rises high enough, it switches off the signals that told it to rise. Turn this around and it becomes a lab-reading superpower: if a target gland fails and its hormone falls, the pituitary senses the shortage and cranks its stimulating hormone up to compensate. Low thyroid hormone → high TSH. You'll use that in Section 3.

Key points
  • Hypothalamus commands; anterior pituitary makes (GH, PRL, ACTH, TSH, FSH, LH); posterior pituitary stores (ADH, oxytocin).
  • Negative feedback shuts the signal off when the end hormone is high — and ramps it up when the end hormone is low.

Section 2Adrenal glands — the cortisol problem

The adrenal glands sit on top of the kidneys. The outer cortex makes the corticosteroids: glucocorticoids (cortisol), mineralocorticoids (aldosterone), and a small amount of androgens. The inner medulla makes the "fight-or-flight" catecholamines (epinephrine, norepinephrine). Two classic diseases are simply too much and too little cortisol.

Cushing's syndrome — too much cortisol

Most often the cause is taking steroid medication (like prednisone) long-term; less often an adrenal or pituitary tumor. Sustained high cortisol produces a recognizable picture: high blood sugar, weight gain that is central (round "moon" face, a "buffalo hump," and a heavy trunk with thin arms and legs), thin, fragile skin that bruises easily, high blood pressure, and a suppressed immune system that raises infection risk.

Addison's disease — too little cortisol

Here the adrenal gland is destroyed, usually by an autoimmune attack, so both cortisol and aldosterone fall. Low aldosterone drops blood pressure and blood volume; the person is hypotensive, fatigued, and losing weight, often craving salt. A classic early clue is bronze, hyperpigmented skin — because the pituitary, sensing low cortisol, pours out ACTH (feedback again), and ACTH stimulates skin pigment.

Never stop steroids abruptly. Long-term steroid medication suppresses the body's own cortisol production. Quit cold-turkey and there's a dangerous gap before natural production restarts — an Addisonian crisis. Steroids are always tapered down.

A third, rarer adrenal problem lives in the medulla: a pheochromocytoma, a tumor that dumps catecholamines in surges — producing episodic spikes of high blood pressure, pounding headache, palpitations, and sweating.

Key points
  • Cushing's = hypercortisol: central obesity, hyperglycemia, thin skin, hypertension, infection risk.
  • Addison's = hypocortisol/aldosterone: hypotension, weight loss, fatigue, salt craving, bronze skin.
  • Feedback check: an adrenal tumor making its own cortisol drives ACTH down; a failed adrenal gland drives ACTH up.

Section 3Thyroid — the metabolic thermostat

Thyroid hormone sets the body's metabolic speed — heart rate, temperature, weight, and energy all track with it. So thyroid disease comes in two mirror-image forms.

Hypothyroidism — everything slows down

Too little thyroid hormone means a sluggish metabolism: fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, a slow heart rate (bradycardia), dry skin, and constipation. The most common cause is Hashimoto's thyroiditis, an autoimmune condition; iodine deficiency and congenital cases also occur.

Hyperthyroidism — everything speeds up

Too much thyroid hormone revs the metabolism: weight loss, heat intolerance, a fast heart (tachycardia), anxiety, and tremor. The most common cause is Graves' disease (also autoimmune), which can push the eyes forward (exophthalmos). A goiter — a visibly enlarged thyroid — can appear in either direction, so a goiter alone just tells you to look closer.

The TSH trick (a favorite exam question). Remember negative feedback: if thyroid hormone (T4) is low, the pituitary senses the shortage and pumps out more TSH to push the gland. So in primary hypothyroidism, T4 is low and TSH is high. When you see low T4, expect high TSH.
Watch the parathyroids. The four tiny parathyroid glands sit right behind the thyroid. After thyroid surgery, they can be bumped or removed — so watch for hypocalcemia (numbness, tingling, and carpal spasm — a positive Trousseau or Chvostek sign).

Key points
  • Hypothyroid = slow (tired, cold, heavy, bradycardic) — often Hashimoto's. Low T4, high TSH.
  • Hyperthyroid = fast (thin, hot, anxious, tachycardic) — often Graves' with exophthalmos. High T4, low TSH.
  • A goiter can occur in either state; after thyroid surgery, watch for hypocalcemia.

Section 4Diabetes mellitus

Insulin is the key that lets glucose into cells. In diabetes that fails, so glucose piles up in the blood while the cells starve. There are two very different reasons the key stops working.

Type 1 — no insulin

An autoimmune attack destroys the insulin-making beta cells of the pancreas, so the body makes essentially no insulin. Glucose can't enter cells, so it spills into the urine and drags water with it — producing the classic triad of polyuria (peeing a lot), polydipsia (thirst), and polyphagia (hunger), plus weight loss and blurry vision. With no insulin at all, the body burns fat for fuel, and fat breakdown releases ketones (acids) — this is how Type 1 tips into diabetic ketoacidosis.

Type 2 — insulin resistance

Here insulin is present, but the cells have become resistant to it, so glucose still can't get in efficiently. It develops gradually, is strongly linked to obesity and older age, and often has few symptoms early. The dangerous acute complication is HHS (below). Gestational diabetes is a related insulin-resistance problem of pregnancy that can lead to an oversized baby (macrosomia).

The two emergencies — DKA vs HHS

Both are hyperglycemic crises, but they belong to different types and look different:

  • DKA (Type 1): blood sugar usually > 250 mg/dL, ketones present causing a metabolic acidosis, and deep, rapid Kussmaul breathing as the lungs try to blow off acid.
  • HHS (Type 2): blood sugar very high — often > 600 mg/dL — with profound dehydration and thickened blood, but little or no ketosis.

Key points
  • Type 1 = autoimmune, no insulin, prone to DKA (ketones, acidosis, Kussmaul).
  • Type 2 = insulin resistance, gradual, prone to HHS (very high glucose, severe dehydration, no ketones).
  • Both crises are hyperglycemic emergencies; the presence of ketones and acidosis is what separates DKA from HHS.

Section 5Growth hormone — timing is everything

Growth hormone (GH, also called somatotropin) comes from the anterior pituitary and drives the growth of bone, muscle, and tissue. Too little GH in a child causes short stature with normal, proportional body features (and metabolic problems in adults). Too much GH is more interesting, because when it happens changes the disease entirely.

The deciding factor is whether the growth plates — the growing ends of the long bones — are still open. Excess GH before the plates close (in childhood) lets the whole skeleton keep lengthening, producing gigantism — a person who grows abnormally tall. Excess GH after the plates have fused (in adulthood) can no longer lengthen bone, so bone instead thickens: this is acromegaly, with enlarged hands and feet, a protruding jaw and brow, and coarsening features. Both are usually caused by a GH-secreting pituitary tumor.

Key points
  • Too little GH in kids → short, proportional stature.
  • Too much GH before plates close → gigantism (tall all over); after they close → acromegaly (thickened hands, feet, jaw).

Section 6ADH — the body's water valve

Antidiuretic hormone (ADH) does one job: it tells the kidneys to hold on to water. Its two disorders are exact opposites, which makes them easy to keep straight.

SIADH — too much ADH

In the syndrome of inappropriate ADH, the body makes too much ADH, so the kidneys reabsorb too much water. The blood becomes diluted: sodium falls (hyponatremia), the person is fluid-overloaded and may gain weight, and the urine is small in volume and concentrated. Memory hook: SIADH → Soaked, Sodium low.

Diabetes insipidus — too little ADH

In diabetes insipidus there's too little ADH (or the kidney can't respond to it), so the body can't hold water. The result is huge volumes of dilute urine, dehydration, a rising sodium (hypernatremia), and intense thirst. Despite the name, it has nothing to do with blood sugar — "insipidus" means tasteless urine, as opposed to the sweet urine of diabetes mellitus.

Key points
  • SIADH = too much ADH → retain water → low sodium, fluid overload, concentrated urine.
  • DI = too little ADH → lose water → high sodium, dehydration, large volumes of dilute urine.

The chapter in one breath

  • Feedback is the master key. A low target hormone drives its pituitary signal up (low T4 → high TSH); a high one shuts it off.
  • Adrenal: Cushing's is too much cortisol (round, high sugar, thin skin, HTN); Addison's is too little (low BP, weight loss, bronze skin). Never stop steroids abruptly.
  • Thyroid: hypo = slow, hyper = fast; a goiter can be either. After thyroid surgery, watch calcium.
  • Diabetes: Type 1 = no insulin → DKA; Type 2 = resistance → HHS. Ketones separate them.
  • Growth hormone: excess before plates close = gigantism; after = acromegaly.
  • ADH: too much = SIADH (retain water, low Na); too little = DI (lose water, high Na).

Check yourself

1. A client is tired, gaining weight, always cold, and has a heart rate of 55. Labs show a low T4. What do you expect the TSH to be?
High (above normal). This is hypothyroidism. By negative feedback, the pituitary senses the low T4 and releases more TSH to push the gland — so a low T4 pairs with a high TSH.
2. Which hyperglycemic emergency features ketones, a metabolic acidosis, and Kussmaul breathing — and which type of diabetes is it?
DKA — diabetic ketoacidosis, in Type 1. With no insulin, the body burns fat and makes ketoacids, dropping the pH; deep, rapid Kussmaul breathing blows off CO₂ to compensate. HHS (Type 2) has very high glucose but little or no ketosis.
3. A client has low blood pressure, weight loss, salt cravings, and bronze patches on the skin. What is the likely diagnosis?
Addison's disease (adrenal insufficiency). Low cortisol and aldosterone cause the hypotension and weight loss; the high ACTH driving the low cortisol also stimulates skin pigment, giving the bronze appearance.
4. An adult develops enlarging hands and feet and a more prominent jaw. Excess growth hormone is found. Gigantism or acromegaly — and why?
Acromegaly. Because the growth plates have already closed in adulthood, bone can no longer lengthen — so it thickens instead, enlarging the hands, feet, jaw, and brow. The same excess GH before the plates close would cause gigantism.
5. A client with too much ADH is admitted. Which way will the serum sodium move, and what is this syndrome called?
Sodium goes down (hyponatremia) — this is SIADH. Excess ADH makes the kidneys retain water, diluting the blood. The opposite, diabetes insipidus, loses water and raises the sodium.
