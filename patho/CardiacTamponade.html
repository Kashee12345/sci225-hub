Cardiac Tamponade

Pericardium under pressure · Pathophysiology · Cardiovascular Part 2
The heart sits inside a tough pericardial sac holding only ~15–50 mL of lubricating fluid. When fluid builds up faster than the sac can stretch, it squeezes the heart from the outside — the chambers can't fill, cardiac output falls, and the patient slides into obstructive shock. Why the rate matters

The pericardium is stiff. How the pressure responds to fluid depends entirely on how fast it accumulates.
A rapid effusion (e.g. stab wound, aortic rupture) overwhelms the stiff sac — as little as ~150 mL can be fatal. A gradual effusion (e.g. cancer, uremia) lets the sac slowly stretch, tolerating >800 mL with little pressure rise. Same volume, opposite outcome.

Beck's Triad

The three classic bedside signs of tamponade — they appear together as pressure climbs.
HypotensionThe compressed heart pumps less blood each beat, so blood pressure falls.
Jugular venous distention (JVD)Blood can't return into the squeezed right atrium, so it backs up into the neck veins.
Muffled heart soundsThe surrounding fluid dampens the sound of the valves closing.
All three appear together in tamponade — a medical emergency.
Clinical pearl: tamponade is one cause of obstructive shock (along with tension pneumothorax and massive PE) — the pump is fine, but something outside it blocks filling. Fix the obstruction and the patient recovers within seconds.
