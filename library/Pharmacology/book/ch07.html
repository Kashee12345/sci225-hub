Autonomic Drugs

Your patient's heart rate, blood pressure, pupils, bladder, gut, and airways are all being quietly managed — second by second — by a nervous system nobody chose to switch on. Autonomic drugs work by pushing on that system: turning up the gas, stepping on the brake, or cutting a wire. Once you understand which receptor a drug lands on and what that receptor does to the organ underneath it, the adverse effects stop being a list to memorize and start being something you can predict from first principles. This is the chapter that makes the rest of pharmacology click, because so many drugs — for hypertension, asthma, glaucoma, overactive bladder, shock, and anesthesia — are simply autonomic drugs wearing a different name badge.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Contrast the sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions and predict each one's effect on the major organs.
  • Match alpha-1, alpha-2, beta-1, beta-2, beta-3, muscarinic, and nicotinic receptors to their locations and responses.
  • Explain how adrenergic agonists work, and describe the dose-dependent behavior of epinephrine.
  • Describe the nursing implications of alpha blockers and beta blockers, including first-dose hypotension and abrupt-withdrawal rebound.
  • Compare cholinergic agonists, acetylcholinesterase inhibitors, and anticholinergics, and recognize crisis states of each.
  • Distinguish depolarizing from non-depolarizing neuromuscular blockade and explain why only one is reversible.
  • Name the antidotes for cholinergic crisis, anticholinergic toxicity, and non-depolarizing blockade.
  • Identify the high-alert autonomic drugs and the monitoring a nurse owns for each.

Section 1The autonomic nervous system

Two divisions, one body

The autonomic nervous system (ANS) runs everything you do not consciously decide: heart rate, vessel tone, breathing passages, digestion, sweating, pupil size, bladder emptying. It has two divisions that mostly oppose each other, and the classic shorthand is worth keeping because it is genuinely predictive. The sympathetic division is fight or flight — the body preparing to run from something. The parasympathetic division is rest and digest — the body settling down to housekeeping. Ask yourself what a person sprinting from danger needs, and sympathetic effects fall out on their own: faster heart, higher pressure, open airways, dilated pupils to see the threat, glucose released for fuel, blood shunted to muscle and away from gut and bladder. Ask what a person napping after a meal needs, and the parasympathetic list appears: slower heart, constricted pupils, active gut, a bladder that empties.

The two divisions differ in their chemistry, and that is where drugs get their leverage. Every autonomic pathway uses two neurons in series with a ganglion (a synapse) between them. At every ganglion, in both divisions, the transmitter is acetylcholine (ACh) acting on nicotinic receptors. What changes is the last step, at the organ itself. Parasympathetic nerves release acetylcholine onto muscarinic receptors — these are the cholinergic synapses. Most sympathetic nerves release norepinephrine onto adrenergic receptors. Two important sympathetic exceptions are worth memorizing now, because they explain odd drug effects later: the adrenal medulla is a modified ganglion that dumps epinephrine straight into the blood as a hormone, and the sweat glands are sympathetic but use acetylcholine on muscarinic receptors — which is exactly why anticholinergic drugs make patients hot and dry.

Two memory hooks pay for themselves all semester. “One heart, two lungs” puts beta-1 in the heart and beta-2 in the lungs and never lets you mix them up — and it instantly explains why a nonselective beta blocker is dangerous in asthma. SLUDGE (Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI upset, Emesis) is the muscarinic picture; the anticholinergic picture is simply its mirror image, which students remember as dry as a bone, red as a beet, blind as a bat, mad as a hatter, hot as a hare.

Four things a drug can do

Every drug in this chapter falls into one of four buckets, and the vocabulary is worth pinning down now. A sympathomimetic (adrenergic agonist) mimics the sympathetic system. A sympatholytic (adrenergic antagonist, or blocker) blocks it. A parasympathomimetic (cholinergic agonist) mimics the parasympathetic system. A parasympatholytic (anticholinergic) blocks it. Notice the elegance hiding in there: blocking one division looks a great deal like stimulating the other. A beta blocker slows the heart, and so does a cholinergic drug — different receptor, same bradycardia on your monitor. Do not let the clinical picture alone tell you which drug your patient took.

Predict, don't memorize. Before you look up an autonomic drug's side effects, name its receptor and ask what that receptor's organ does. Alpha-1 agonist? Vasoconstriction — so expect hypertension, reflex bradycardia, and tissue damage if it infiltrates an IV site. You will be right far more often than not, and on an exam you can reconstruct a fact you never memorized.

Key points
  • The sympathetic division is fight-or-flight (mostly norepinephrine on adrenergic receptors); the parasympathetic is rest-and-digest (acetylcholine on muscarinic receptors).
  • All ganglia in both divisions use acetylcholine on nicotinic receptors; the divisions differ at the organ, not the ganglion.
  • One heart (beta-1), two lungs (beta-2); alpha-1 constricts vessels; alpha-2 is presynaptic negative feedback that lowers BP.
  • Sympathetic sweat glands are cholinergic — the reason anticholinergics cause dry, hot skin.

Section 2Adrenergic agonists

Turning up the sympathetic gas

An adrenergic agonist, or sympathomimetic, produces the effects the sympathetic nervous system would produce. Some do it directly, by binding the receptor themselves (epinephrine, norepinephrine, phenylephrine, albuterol). Others do it indirectly, by causing the nerve terminal to dump its own stored norepinephrine or by blocking its reuptake (ephedrine, which is mixed direct and indirect; amphetamines; cocaine). Either way the receptor sees more signal, and the organ responds.

What makes this class confusing at first is that a single drug can hit several receptors at once, so its effect depends on which receptors it reaches and at what dose. Selectivity is the whole game. A drug that hits only beta-2 opens airways without racing the heart; one that hits everything opens airways and races the heart and clamps down the vessels. Selectivity is also dose-dependent — push the dose high enough and a “selective” drug starts spilling onto its neighbors. This is precisely why an asthmatic patient who overuses albuterol develops tachycardia and tremor: at high doses the beta-2 selectivity leaks onto beta-1.

Epinephrine: the drug that changes personality with dose

Epinephrine is the prototype and the one to know cold. It is a nonselective agonist — alpha-1, alpha-2, beta-1, and beta-2 — but its receptors have different sensitivities, so the drug behaves differently at different doses. At low doses, beta effects dominate: the heart speeds up, contractility rises, and airways open while skeletal-muscle vessels actually dilate, which can drop the diastolic pressure. At high doses, alpha-1 effects take over and vasoconstriction wins, driving blood pressure up hard. This dose-dependent split is why the same molecule is used for anaphylaxis, for cardiac arrest, for shock, and (in dental and local anesthetic mixtures) to keep lidocaine from washing away.

In anaphylaxis, epinephrine is doing three jobs simultaneously, and the nurse should be able to name all three: alpha-1 constricts vessels to reverse the catastrophic drop in blood pressure and reduce airway edema; beta-1 supports cardiac output; beta-2 dilates the bronchi and stabilizes mast cells against further mediator release. No other drug does all of that. Antihistamines and steroids are adjuncts and never the first move. The first-line route for anaphylaxis is intramuscular into the vault of the lateral thigh (vastus lateralis), because that muscle's rich blood supply absorbs the drug faster and more reliably than subcutaneous tissue or the deltoid.

Look again at clonidine, because it trips up nearly everyone. It is an agonist, yet it lowers blood pressure. The reason is where its receptor sits: alpha-2 receptors are presynaptic brakes. Watch the IV site like it's the patient. Norepinephrine, dopamine, and epinephrine constrict the vessels they sit in. If one infiltrates, the tissue loses its blood supply and dies. Blanching, coolness, or pain at the site means stop the infusion, aspirate what you can, leave the catheter in, and call for phentolamine to infiltrate the area — an alpha blocker that reopens the strangled vessels. Key points
  • Sympathomimetics mimic the sympathetic system directly (receptor binding) or indirectly (transmitter release/reuptake block).

Section 3Adrenergic antagonists

Alpha blockers: releasing the vessels

An alpha-1 blocker stops norepinephrine from constricting vascular smooth muscle. The vessels relax, resistance falls, and blood pressure comes down. The same receptor sits in the bladder neck and prostatic smooth muscle, so blocking it also relaxes the outflow tract — which is why the “-osin” drugs are workhorses in benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), letting men empty a bladder that a big prostate had been squeezing shut. Tamsulosin is relatively uroselective and drops pressure less; doxazosin, terazosin, and prazosin lower blood pressure more and are sometimes used for that purpose (prazosin is also used off-label for PTSD-related nightmares, though the trial evidence is mixed).

The signature hazard is first-dose orthostatic hypotension. The first dose — or the first dose after any increase — can drop a patient's pressure sharply enough to cause syncope, and a syncopal fall in an older adult is a hip fracture waiting to happen. The nursing response is concrete: give the first dose at bedtime, teach the patient to rise slowly and to sit on the edge of the bed before standing, warn them not to drive after the first dose, and check orthostatic vitals. Phentolamine is a nonselective alpha blocker used differently — as the antidote for vasopressor extravasation and in the management of catecholamine excess such as pheochromocytoma.

Beta blockers: the “-olol” family

A beta blocker occupies beta receptors so catecholamines cannot. Blocking beta-1 slows the heart rate, weakens contraction, slows AV conduction, and suppresses renin release — the combination that lowers blood pressure, quiets angina by cutting myocardial oxygen demand, controls rate in atrial fibrillation, and, most importantly, improves survival after myocardial infarction and in chronic heart failure. That last point deserves emphasis because it is counterintuitive: a drug that weakens contraction helps the failing heart, because it protects it from years of catecholamine flogging. In heart failure the beta blocker is started at a low dose and titrated up slowly — and never started during an acute decompensation.

The critical distinction in this class is cardioselectivity. Cardioselective agents (metoprolol, atenolol, bisoprolol, esmolol — the ones that start with A through M) block mainly beta-1 and largely spare the lungs. Nonselective agents (propranolol, nadolol, timolol, sotalol — N through Z) block beta-2 as well, which means they can constrict bronchi. That is the mechanism behind the caution in asthma and COPD. The A–M letter trick is a study aid, not a rule — carvedilol and labetalol are the classic exceptions, since both begin in the first half of the alphabet yet block beta-1, beta-2, and alpha-1. Cardioselectivity is also relative, not absolute, and is lost at higher doses, so even a “cardioselective” blocker gets used cautiously in reactive airway disease.

The three cautions a nurse must own

First, never stop a beta blocker abruptly. Chronic blockade upregulates beta receptors, and yanking the drug away exposes a hypersensitized heart to circulating catecholamines — rebound tachycardia, severe hypertension, angina, and infarction have all been documented. Taper over one to two weeks, and teach the patient this explicitly, because a patient who feels fine and stops on their own is the one who ends up in the emergency department.

Second, beta blockers mask hypoglycemia. The trembling, racing heart, and anxiety that warn a diabetic patient their glucose is falling are beta-mediated sympathetic signals — block beta and the warning goes silent. Sweating is the one sign that survives, because sweat glands are cholinergic. Teach patients on beta blockers with diabetes to check their glucose rather than trust how they feel, and never assume a patient without tremor is safe.

Third, hold parameters are yours to enforce. Take an apical pulse for a full minute before every dose. If the heart rate is under 60 or the blood pressure is below the ordered threshold, hold the dose and notify the prescriber — do not simply give it and chart the vitals. Beta blockers also blunt the compensatory tachycardia of hypovolemia, so a bleeding patient on metoprolol may not mount the heart rate you would otherwise expect. In beta blocker overdose — profound bradycardia and hypotension unresponsive to atropine — the specific antidote is glucagon, which raises cardiac cAMP by a route that bypasses the blocked receptor entirely.

Eye drops count as drugs. A patient with glaucoma on timolol who becomes bradycardic or wheezy is not a mystery — the drop drained through the tear duct into systemic circulation. Teach punctal occlusion (gentle finger pressure at the inner corner for a minute or two after instilling), and list ophthalmic drops on the medication reconciliation. Patients almost never think of them as “medications.”

  • Alpha-1 blockers (“-osin”) relax vessels and the bladder outlet — hypertension and BPH — and cause first-dose orthostatic syncope; dose at bedtime.
  • Cardioselective beta blockers (A–M: metoprolol, atenolol) spare beta-2; nonselective ones (N–Z: propranolol) risk bronchospasm in asthma/COPD.

Section 4Cholinergic and anticholinergic drugs

Cholinergic agonists: turning up rest-and-digest

A cholinergic agonist (parasympathomimetic) reproduces what acetylcholine does at muscarinic receptors, and the resulting picture is SLUDGE: salivation, lacrimation, urination, defecation, GI cramping, emesis — plus bradycardia, bronchoconstriction, and miosis. Written out that way it sounds purely like a poisoning, and at high doses it is. Used deliberately at therapeutic doses, though, two of those effects are useful. Bethanechol selectively stimulates the bladder detrusor and gut, which makes it the drug for urinary retention that is not caused by an obstruction — a distinction that matters enormously, because squeezing a bladder against a blocked outlet risks rupture. Pilocarpine, given as eye drops, constricts the pupil and opens the drainage angle in glaucoma, and orally it stimulates saliva in xerostomia. Cholinergic agonists are contraindicated in asthma (bronchoconstriction), in bradycardia or heart block, in peptic ulcer disease, and in bowel or bladder obstruction.

AChE inhibitors: making the body's own acetylcholine last longer

Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) is the enzyme that chews up acetylcholine in the synapse within milliseconds. Block the enzyme and acetylcholine lingers, so the same signal hits harder — an indirect cholinergic agonist. This is the mechanism behind three quite different therapies. In myasthenia gravis, where autoantibodies have destroyed nicotinic receptors at the muscle end plate, pyridostigmine keeps acetylcholine around long enough for the surviving receptors to work, restoring strength; the timing of doses around meals matters so patients can chew and swallow safely. In Alzheimer disease, donepezil, rivastigmine, and galantamine raise central acetylcholine — they modestly improve or stabilize symptoms for a time, but they neither cure the disease nor halt the underlying neurodegeneration. And in anesthesia, neostigmine reverses non-depolarizing neuromuscular blockade, which Section 5 takes up.

Push cholinergic stimulation too far and you get cholinergic crisis: profound SLUDGE with bradycardia, bronchoconstriction, copious secretions, and — the part that kills — muscle weakness and respiratory failure from a depolarized, overwhelmed end plate. This is the mechanism of organophosphate poisoning (insecticides and nerve agents), which irreversibly inactivates AChE. The treatment is two drugs, not one: atropine to block the muscarinic effects and dry the secretions, and pralidoxime to pull the organophosphate off the enzyme before the bond “ages” and becomes permanent. Atropine alone will not fix the nicotinic muscle weakness — a fact worth understanding rather than memorizing.

The cruel clinical trap in myasthenia is that myasthenic crisis (too little drug) and cholinergic crisis (too much) both present as severe weakness and respiratory failure. The differentiator is the company the weakness keeps: cholinergic crisis brings SLUDGE — drooling, tearing, cramping, tiny pupils, bradycardia — while myasthenic crisis does not. Either way the airway comes first.

Anticholinergics: blocking rest-and-digest

An anticholinergic (antimuscarinic, parasympatholytic) blocks muscarinic receptors, so the picture is SLUDGE in reverse: dry mouth, no sweat, blurred near vision and dilated pupils, tachycardia, constipation, and urinary retention, with confusion when the drug reaches the brain. Atropine is the prototype — the drug you push for symptomatic bradycardia, use to dry secretions preoperatively, and reach for in organophosphate poisoning. Ipratropium and tiotropium are inhaled anticholinergics that bronchodilate in COPD by a route completely different from albuterol's, which is why the two are often combined. Oxybutynin, tolterodine, and solifenacin quiet an overactive bladder. Scopolamine patches prevent motion sickness. Benztropine and trihexyphenidyl rebalance the dopamine–acetylcholine seesaw in Parkinson disease and in drug-induced extrapyramidal symptoms.

Every one of those anticholinergic effects is predictable from the table in Section 1, and so is the danger. Anticholinergic burden is the additive load of every drug with antimuscarinic activity a patient is taking — and it is not just the drugs above. Many antihistamines, tricyclic antidepressants, and antipsychotics carry hidden antimuscarinic activity. Stack three or four in an 80-year-old and you get confusion, falls, retention, and constipation that get misread as “dementia” or “just getting older.” Counting up anticholinergic burden on a medication list is one of the highest-yield things a nurse can do for an older patient.

Dry as a bone, red as a beet, blind as a bat, mad as a hatter, hot as a hare. That old rhyme is the complete anticholinergic toxidrome, and every line is mechanistic: no secretions (dry), flushed skin shunting heat because sweating is blocked (red, hot), paralyzed accommodation and dilated pupils (blind), central muscarinic blockade (mad). Because sweating fails, these patients cannot cool themselves — heat stroke is a genuine risk, so teach every patient on an anticholinergic to avoid heat and exertion. The antidote for severe toxicity is physostigmine.

Section 5Neuromuscular blockers

A different synapse, a much higher stake

Neuromuscular blocking agents (NMBAs) are technically somatic, not autonomic, but they belong here because they work on the same molecule — acetylcholine — at a nicotinic receptor. They act at the neuromuscular junction, on the NM receptor of the skeletal muscle end plate, to produce flaccid paralysis for intubation, surgery, and mechanical ventilation.

Three facts must be absolutely fixed before any of the pharmacology matters. These drugs do not sedate. They do not relieve pain. They do not cause amnesia. A paralyzed patient who has not been given adequate sedation and analgesia is fully awake, feeling everything, and unable to move a finger or make a sound to tell you — among the most terrifying experiences medicine can inflict. NMBAs are high-alert medications, stored separately with auxiliary warnings, and they are never given except where the airway can be secured and mechanical ventilation is immediately available. Paralysis always includes the diaphragm. The nurse's obligations are non-negotiable: confirm deep sedation and analgesia are running first, never allow paralysis without a secured airway, provide eye lubrication and taping (the blink is gone), reposition to prevent pressure injury, provide DVT prophylaxis, and keep talking to the patient because hearing is unaffected.

Depolarizing blockade: succinylcholine

Succinylcholine is the only depolarizing agent in common use, and it works by an odd trick: it is an agonist that paralyzes. It binds the nicotinic receptor and opens it — producing an initial wave of disorganized contractions called fasciculations — but unlike acetylcholine it is not broken down by acetylcholinesterase, so it sits on the receptor and holds the end plate depolarized. A depolarized membrane cannot fire again, and the muscle goes flaccid. Onset is under a minute and the effect wears off in five to ten minutes as plasma pseudocholinesterase metabolizes it, which is exactly the profile you want for rapid sequence intubation.

Three hazards define it. First, there is no reversal agent — you wait it out, ventilating the patient the whole time. Giving an AChE inhibitor would only prolong the block by adding more acetylcholine to an already depolarized plate. Second, the depolarization leaks potassium out of every muscle cell in the body, so succinylcholine is contraindicated in patients with existing hyperkalemia, and in burns, crush injury, denervation, spinal cord injury, or prolonged immobility beyond roughly 24–48 hours, where receptor upregulation can produce a potassium surge large enough to cause cardiac arrest. Third, succinylcholine is a leading trigger of malignant hyperthermia in genetically susceptible patients — a hypermetabolic crisis whose earliest reliable sign is a rising end-tidal CO2, with masseter rigidity, tachycardia, and a late temperature spike. The antidote is dantrolene, which must be immediately available wherever the drug is used. A rare patient with atypical pseudocholinesterase stays paralyzed for hours; the management is simply continued ventilation and sedation until it clears.

Non-depolarizing blockade: the “-curium” and “-curonium” drugs

Rocuronium, vecuronium, cisatracurium, and atracurium take the more intuitive route: they are competitive antagonists that occupy the nicotinic receptor without activating it, so acetylcholine cannot get in. There are no fasciculations, onset is slower (though a large dose of rocuronium approaches succinylcholine's speed), and the block lasts much longer. Because the blockade is competitive, it is reversible — flood the junction with enough acetylcholine and it outcompetes the drug. That is what neostigmine does, and it is always given with glycopyrrolate or atropine, because the extra acetylcholine spilling onto muscarinic receptors would otherwise cause bradycardia and drowning secretions. Sugammadex is the newer and cleaner option: it physically encapsulates rocuronium or vecuronium molecules and drags them out of circulation, reversing even deep blockade in minutes without touching the cholinergic system at all. Cisatracurium earns a special mention because it degrades spontaneously in plasma (Hofmann elimination) rather than through liver or kidney, making it the agent of choice in organ failure.

Monitoring is where the nurse lives with these drugs. Train-of-four (TOF) peripheral nerve stimulation delivers four small shocks and counts the resulting twitches; the depth of blockade is titrated to a target, commonly one to two twitches out of four for a continuous infusion. Fewer twitches means deeper block and a longer, harder recovery. Before extubation, recovery is judged by objective criteria — sustained head lift, adequate tidal volume and grip, the ability to protect the airway — and never by appearance alone, because a patient can look awake while still too weak to breathe. Remember too that several common drugs deepen and prolong blockade: aminoglycoside antibiotics, magnesium sulfate, calcium channel blockers, inhaled anesthetics, hypothermia, and acidosis. A postpartum patient on a magnesium drip is a different pharmacologic problem than the same patient without it.

Paralysis is not sedation, and the patient can hear you. Sedation and analgesia go in first and stay running — check the pumps and the volume in the bag before you check anything else, and treat an unexplained rise in heart rate or blood pressure in a paralyzed patient as pain or awareness until proven otherwise, because it is the only signal they have left. Then keep speaking to them by name and explaining what you are doing. Hearing is the last sense standing, and it costs you nothing.

Key points
  • NMBAs act at the nicotinic (NM) receptor of the motor end plate; they are high-alert drugs that paralyze the diaphragm.
  • They provide no sedation, no analgesia, no amnesia — sedation and pain control must run first and continuously.
  • Succinylcholine depolarizes: fasciculations, ultra-short action, no reversal; risks hyperkalemia and malignant hyperthermia (antidote dantrolene).
  • Non-depolarizers compete: no fasciculations, longer action, reversible with neostigmine + glycopyrrolate or sugammadex.
  • Monitor depth with train-of-four; aminoglycosides and magnesium prolong blockade; confirm real strength before extubation.

The chapter in one breath

  • The autonomic nervous system has a sympathetic (fight-or-flight, adrenergic) and a parasympathetic (rest-and-digest, cholinergic) division; all ganglia are nicotinic.
  • The receptor map does the work: alpha-1 constricts, alpha-2 feeds back to lower BP, beta-1 is the heart, beta-2 is the lungs, muscarinic is SLUDGE.
  • Epinephrine is dose-dependent (beta low, alpha high) and is the first-line, IM-lateral-thigh drug in anaphylaxis; vasopressor extravasation is reversed with phentolamine.
  • Beta-2 agonists rescue airways at the price of tremor, tachycardia, and hypokalemia; long-acting agents are maintenance only.
  • Alpha blockers cause first-dose syncope (dose at bedtime); beta blockers save lives post-MI and in heart failure but must never be stopped abruptly and mask hypoglycemia except sweating.
  • AChE inhibitors amplify the body's own acetylcholine (myasthenia, Alzheimer, reversal); overshoot is cholinergic crisis, treated with atropine + pralidoxime in organophosphate poisoning.
  • Anticholinergics are dry, hot, red, blurred, tachycardic, and confused — and their burden stacks dangerously in older adults; the antidote is physostigmine.
  • Neuromuscular blockers paralyze without sedating: succinylcholine depolarizes and cannot be reversed (hyperkalemia, malignant hyperthermia → dantrolene); non-depolarizers compete and reverse with neostigmine + glycopyrrolate or sugammadex.

ReferenceWords worth knowing

A quick reference for the terms in this chapter — skim it now, or circle back whenever a word trips you up.

Acetylcholine (ACh)
The transmitter at all autonomic ganglia, at parasympathetic organ synapses, at sympathetic sweat glands, and at the skeletal muscle end plate.
Acetylcholinesterase (AChE)
The enzyme that breaks down acetylcholine in the synapse; blocking it amplifies and prolongs cholinergic signaling.
Alpha-1 receptor
Receptor on vascular smooth muscle, pupil, and bladder neck; stimulation causes vasoconstriction, mydriasis, and urinary retention.
Alpha-2 receptor
Presynaptic and central receptor whose stimulation reduces norepinephrine release, lowering blood pressure (clonidine).
Anticholinergic (antimuscarinic)
A drug that blocks muscarinic receptors, producing dry mouth, blurred vision, tachycardia, constipation, retention, and confusion.
Anticholinergic burden
The cumulative antimuscarinic load from all of a patient's drugs, a major cause of confusion and falls in older adults.
Autonomic nervous system
The involuntary nervous system controlling heart, vessels, airways, gut, bladder, glands, and pupils.
Beta-1 receptor
Cardiac (“one heart”) and renal receptor; stimulation raises rate, contractility, conduction, and renin release.
Beta-2 receptor
Pulmonary (“two lungs”), vascular, uterine, and hepatic receptor; stimulation causes bronchodilation and vasodilation.
Cardioselective
Describing a beta blocker that acts mainly on beta-1 and largely spares beta-2; selectivity is relative and lost at high doses.
Cholinergic crisis
Excess cholinergic stimulation causing SLUDGE plus muscle weakness and respiratory failure; the mirror trap of myasthenic crisis.
Depolarizing blocker
A neuromuscular blocker (succinylcholine) that activates and then holds the end plate depolarized; has no reversal agent.
Extravasation
Leakage of a vesicant such as a vasopressor into surrounding tissue, risking necrosis; reversed locally with phentolamine.
Malignant hyperthermia
A hypermetabolic crisis triggered by succinylcholine or volatile anesthetics; earliest sign is rising end-tidal CO2; antidote is dantrolene.
Muscarinic receptor
The acetylcholine receptor on parasympathetic target organs; stimulation produces the SLUDGE picture.
Neuromuscular blocking agent (NMBA)
A high-alert drug that paralyzes skeletal muscle at the nicotinic end plate without sedating or relieving pain.
Nicotinic receptor
The acetylcholine receptor at autonomic ganglia (NN) and at the skeletal muscle end plate (NM).
Non-depolarizing blocker
A competitive nicotinic antagonist (rocuronium, vecuronium, cisatracurium) reversible with neostigmine or sugammadex.
Parasympathetic division
The rest-and-digest division; acetylcholine on muscarinic receptors slows the heart and activates gut and bladder.
SLUDGE
Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI upset, Emesis — the muscarinic (cholinergic excess) picture.
Sympathetic division
The fight-or-flight division; mostly norepinephrine on adrenergic receptors, plus adrenal epinephrine release.
Train-of-four (TOF)
Peripheral nerve stimulation delivering four impulses to gauge the depth of neuromuscular blockade by counting twitches.

Check yourself

1. A patient asks why their asthma inhaler makes their hands shake and their heart pound if it is “just for the lungs.” Explain in receptor terms.
Beta-2 selectivity is relative, not absolute — at higher doses albuterol spills over onto beta-1 receptors in the heart, and beta-2 receptors in skeletal muscle cause tremor. Also watch for hypokalemia, since beta-2 stimulation drives potassium into cells. Escalating rescue-inhaler use signals poor asthma control and should be reported.
2. Why is epinephrine, and not an antihistamine or a steroid, the first drug in anaphylaxis — and by what route and site?
Epinephrine does three things at once: alpha-1 vasoconstriction reverses hypotension and airway edema, beta-1 supports cardiac output, and beta-2 dilates bronchi and stabilizes mast cells. Give it IM into the vault of the lateral thigh (vastus lateralis). Antihistamines and steroids are adjuncts that work far too slowly to be first-line.
3. Clonidine is an alpha-2 agonist, yet it lowers blood pressure. Resolve the apparent contradiction, and state the teaching point that keeps a patient out of the ED.
Alpha-2 receptors are presynaptic and central brakes — stimulating them tells the nerve terminal to release less norepinephrine, so the net effect is sympatholytic. Teaching: never stop clonidine abruptly — rebound hypertensive crisis. Receptor location matters as much as receptor type.
4. A norepinephrine infusion site is pale, cool, and painful. What are your actions, and what is the antidote?
This is extravasation of a vasoconstrictor. Stop the infusion, aspirate what you can, leave the catheter in place, notify the prescriber, and infiltrate the area with phentolamine (an alpha blocker that reopens the constricted vessels). Restart the vasopressor at another site — ideally central access, which is preferred precisely for this reason.
5. Your patient with COPD is prescribed propranolol for migraine prophylaxis. What is your concern, and what would you ask about?
Propranolol is nonselective — it blocks beta-2 in the lungs and can cause bronchospasm in reactive airway disease. Clarify with the prescriber whether a cardioselective agent (metoprolol, atenolol) would be safer, and note that even cardioselectivity is dose-dependent and relative.
6. A patient with type 1 diabetes is started on metoprolol. What specific hypoglycemia teaching is required, and why?
Beta blockade masks the beta-mediated warning signs of hypoglycemia — tremor, palpitations, anxiety. Sweating still occurs because sweat glands are cholinergic. Teach the patient to check blood glucose on a schedule rather than relying on how they feel, and to treat diaphoresis as a warning sign.
7. Your patient's apical pulse is 52 and metoprolol is due. What do you do, and what should you never advise the patient to do?
Hold the dose and notify the prescriber — standard hold parameters are a heart rate under 60 or a systolic BP below the ordered threshold. Take the apical pulse for a full minute. Never advise stopping a beta blocker abruptly: chronic blockade upregulates receptors, and abrupt withdrawal causes rebound tachycardia, hypertension, angina, or infarction. Taper over 1–2 weeks.
8. Why is the first dose of doxazosin given at bedtime, and what else do you teach?
Because of first-dose orthostatic hypotension and syncope — alpha-1 blockade abruptly relaxes vessels, and the resulting fall can fracture a hip. Teach rising slowly, sitting on the edge of the bed before standing, no driving after the first dose, and expect the same risk after any dose increase. Check orthostatic vitals.
9. A patient with myasthenia gravis on pyridostigmine develops severe weakness, drooling, tearing, abdominal cramping, and pinpoint pupils. Which crisis is this, and how do you know?
Cholinergic crisis — too much drug. Both crises cause weakness and respiratory failure, but only cholinergic crisis brings the SLUDGE picture (salivation, lacrimation, cramping, miosis, bradycardia). Myasthenic crisis (too little drug) has weakness without SLUDGE. Either way, airway and ventilation come first.
10. A farmworker arrives with copious secretions, bradycardia, pinpoint pupils, and muscle weakness after pesticide exposure. Name the mechanism and both antidotes, and explain why one is not enough.
Organophosphate poisoning irreversibly inhibits acetylcholinesterase, so acetylcholine accumulates. Give atropine to block the muscarinic effects and dry the secretions, and pralidoxime to reactivate the enzyme before the bond “ages.” Atropine alone cannot fix the nicotinic effects — the skeletal muscle weakness and respiratory failure — because atropine only blocks muscarinic receptors.
11. An 82-year-old on oxybutynin, diphenhydramine, and amitriptyline becomes confused and cannot urinate. Name the phenomenon, and explain why she is also at risk in hot weather.
This is anticholinergic burden — antimuscarinic activity stacked across three drug classes, causing confusion and urinary retention. Because sympathetic sweat glands are cholinergic, blocking muscarinic receptors abolishes sweating, so she cannot dissipate heat — heat stroke is a genuine risk. Recognize the toxidrome: dry, red, blind, mad, hot. Severe toxicity is treated with physostigmine.
12. Why can neostigmine reverse rocuronium but not succinylcholine, and what must always accompany neostigmine?
Rocuronium is a competitive antagonist, so raising acetylcholine with neostigmine outcompetes it at the receptor. Succinylcholine is a depolarizing agonist — adding more acetylcholine only deepens and prolongs the block, so there is no reversal; you ventilate and wait. Neostigmine is always paired with glycopyrrolate or atropine to block the muscarinic spillover (bradycardia, secretions). Sugammadex is an alternative that encapsulates rocuronium or vecuronium directly.
