Pharmacology · Chapter 5

Mental Health, Antiepileptic & Anesthetic Drugs

Every drug in this chapter works in the same place: the tiny gap between two nerve cells. Speed a chemical signal up, slow it down, or silence it entirely, and you change mood, thought, movement, awareness, and pain. That is enormous power, and it comes with enormous responsibility — these are the drugs that cause serotonin syndrome, that stop a seizure, that stop breathing. This chapter walks you from the synapse outward: what the neurotransmitters do, what each drug class does to them, and what you — the nurse holding the syringe and watching the monitor — do about it.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Describe how a chemical synapse works and name the four neurotransmitters most psychotropic drugs target.
  • Compare SSRIs, SNRIs, TCAs, and MAOIs by mechanism, adverse effects, and teaching priorities.
  • Recognize serotonin syndrome early and describe the nursing response.
  • Explain why lithium is a high-alert drug and state its therapeutic range, toxicity signs, and monitoring plan.
  • Distinguish typical from atypical antipsychotics, and identify EPS, tardive dyskinesia, and neuroleptic malignant syndrome.
  • Describe how benzodiazepines work, why they are habit-forming, and what to do in an overdose.
  • Explain the three mechanisms by which antiepileptic drugs stop seizures, and monitor the major agents safely.
  • Compare general and local anesthesia and describe nursing priorities in each stage of anesthesia.

Section 1Neurotransmitters & drug targets

The synapse is the whole story

Neurons do not touch. Where one ends and the next begins there is a microscopic gap — the synaptic cleft — and the message crosses it as a puff of chemical called a neurotransmitter. The sending (presynaptic) neuron packages the transmitter into vesicles, dumps them into the cleft when an electrical impulse arrives, and the transmitter drifts across and binds receptors on the receiving (postsynaptic) neuron. Then it must be cleared, or the signal never turns off. Clearance happens two ways: the transmitter is vacuumed back into the sending neuron by a reuptake pump, or it is chopped up by an enzyme.

That handful of steps is your entire map of psychiatric pharmacology. There is no drug that "fixes depression." There are drugs that block a reuptake pump, drugs that inhibit a degrading enzyme, drugs that occupy a receptor so the real transmitter cannot, and drugs that make a receptor more responsive than it would otherwise be. Once you know which step a drug touches, its effects — wanted and unwanted — stop being a list to memorize and start being something you can reason out.

Two vocabulary words earn their keep here. An agonist binds a receptor and turns it on, imitating the natural transmitter. An antagonist binds the receptor and turns nothing on — it just sits there, blocking the real transmitter from docking. Most antipsychotics are antagonists. Most antidepressants are neither: they leave more transmitter in the cleft by blocking its removal.

The four transmitters that matter most

Serotonin (5-HT) is the mood, sleep, appetite, and anxiety transmitter — and, importantly, it is not confined to the brain. Most of the body's serotonin lives in the gut, which is exactly why serotonergic drugs cause nausea and diarrhea, and why too much serotonin produces the gastrointestinal storm you will read about shortly. Dopamine runs four different circuits: reward and motivation, fine motor control (the basal nuclei), hormone regulation (it suppresses prolactin), and a brainstem trigger zone for vomiting. Block dopamine and you will hit all four, which is why antipsychotics cause both the effect you want and the movement disorders you do not.

Norepinephrine (NE) is alertness, drive, energy, and blood pressure — the internal accelerator. Boost it and depressed patients gain energy; boost it too much and they get tachycardic, tremulous, and hypertensive. GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid) is the brain's brake pedal, the main inhibitory transmitter. When GABA binds its receptor, chloride flows into the neuron and makes it harder to fire. Nearly every sedative, anxiolytic, many antiepileptics, and most general anesthetics work by pushing on that brake — which is also why they all stack on top of one another and why combining them can stop a patient's breathing.

Notice that last row. Acetylcholine is not usually the target of these drugs — it is the innocent bystander. Older antidepressants and older antipsychotics block acetylcholine receptors by accident, and that accident produces the anticholinergic effects your patient will actually complain about: the cotton mouth, the constipation, the inability to urinate, and in an older adult, the confusion that gets mistaken for dementia.

Ask "which step?" before you memorize anything. Every drug in this chapter blocks a pump, blocks an enzyme, blocks a receptor, or modulates an ion channel. Fluoxetine blocks a pump. Phenelzine blocks an enzyme. Haloperidol blocks a receptor. Lorazepam modulates a channel — it does not open the chloride channel itself, it makes GABA better at opening it. Name the step and you can predict the side effects without a flashcard.

Key points
  • Transmitters cross the synaptic cleft, bind receptors, and are cleared by a reuptake pump or an enzyme — every psychotropic drug touches one of those steps.
  • Agonists turn a receptor on; antagonists block it. Antidepressants mostly do neither — they block clearance.
  • Serotonin = mood & gut; dopamine = reward, movement, prolactin, vomiting; norepinephrine = drive & blood pressure; GABA = the brake.
  • Anticholinergic effects (dry mouth, constipation, retention, blurred vision, confusion) are usually an accident of older drugs, not their purpose.

Section 2Antidepressants & mood stabilizers

Four generations of the same idea

All four antidepressant classes raise the amount of serotonin and/or norepinephrine hanging around in the synapse. They differ almost entirely in how selectively they do it — and selectivity is safety. The classes came in the order they did because each generation learned to hit the target and miss the bystanders.

SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors) — fluoxetine, sertraline, escitalopram, paroxetine, citalopram — block only the serotonin pump. They are first-line because they are hard to die from, not because they work better than the old drugs. Expect nausea and headache in the first week (the gut is full of serotonin receptors), insomnia or drowsiness, and, in the long run, sexual dysfunction — the single most common reason patients quietly stop taking them. Ask about it directly and without embarrassment; a patient who feels able to say "this is ruining my sex life" is a patient who can be switched to something else instead of relapsing in silence.

SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine) block the serotonin pump and the norepinephrine pump. That second target adds energy and blunts neuropathic pain — duloxetine is genuinely useful in diabetic neuropathy and fibromyalgia — but it also adds hypertension. Check a blood pressure at every visit on venlafaxine.

TCAs (amitriptyline, nortriptyline, imipramine) block both pumps too, but they also blunder into histamine, acetylcholine, and alpha-1 receptors. So: sedation, anticholinergic misery, and orthostatic hypotension. Their real danger is cardiac — in overdose they block sodium channels in the heart, widen the QRS, and cause fatal dysrhythmias. A widening QRS is the signal to act, and the treatment is IV sodium bicarbonate, which overcomes the sodium-channel blockade. A one-week supply can kill, which makes them a poor choice for a patient at risk of suicide. MAOIs (phenelzine, tranylcypromine, selegiline) take the enzyme route: they disable monoamine oxidase, the enzyme that destroys serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine. Effective, and hemmed in by a diet.

The MAOI diet, and why it is not optional

Monoamine oxidase in your gut wall exists to destroy tyramine, an amine in aged and fermented food. Disable that enzyme and tyramine walks into the bloodstream, floods nerve terminals, and dumps stored norepinephrine all at once. The result is a hypertensive crisis: a pounding occipital headache, blood pressure that can exceed 180/120, stiff neck, sweating, and the real possibility of a stroke. Teach the list — aged cheeses, cured and smoked meats, tap and craft beer, red wine, soy sauce, sauerkraut, fava beans, overripe fruit, yeast extracts — and teach it as though it were an allergy list, because functionally it is.

The two-week lie, and the black box

Every antidepressant raises transmitter levels within hours, yet mood does not lift for two to six weeks — the therapeutic effect requires slow receptor adaptation downstream. Patients do not know this. They take a pill for eight days, feel nothing but nausea, and quit. Telling them up front — "the side effects come first and fade, the benefit comes later and lasts" — is one of the highest-yield things a nurse says all shift.

The gap is also dangerous. Antidepressants carry a boxed warning for increased suicidal thinking in patients under 25, and there is a mechanical reason the risk is front-loaded: energy returns before mood does. A profoundly depressed patient may lack the drive to act on suicidal thoughts; two weeks in, the drive is back and the hopelessness is not. Assess suicidality at every contact during that window, not just at intake.

Two more habits. Do not stop an SSRI or SNRI abruptly — discontinuation syndrome (dizziness, flu-like aches, electric-shock "brain zaps," irritability) is miserable and easily avoided by tapering; paroxetine and venlafaxine are the worst offenders because of short half-lives. And screen for bipolar disorder before starting: an antidepressant given alone to a patient with undiagnosed bipolar disorder can flip them into mania.

Serotonin syndrome

Serotonin syndrome is too much serotonergic activity, usually from combining agents — an SSRI plus tramadol, plus triptans for migraine, plus linezolid, plus St. John's wort, plus an MAOI. It arrives fast, typically within hours of the new drug or dose increase, and it is recognized by a triad: mental status change (agitation, confusion), autonomic instability (fever, tachycardia, diaphoresis, diarrhea), and neuromuscular hyperactivity (tremor, hyperreflexia, and clonus — the most specific sign, often worst in the legs). Stop the serotonergic drugs, call the provider, support with cooling and IV fluids, benzodiazepines for agitation and rigidity, and cyproheptadine as the serotonin-blocking antidote in severe cases.

Lithium is salt, and the kidney cannot tell the difference. Lithium is reabsorbed by the same tubular pathway as sodium, so anything that makes the body hoard sodium makes it hoard lithium — dehydration, vomiting, a low-salt diet, a new thiazide, an NSAID, an ACE inhibitor, a hot day with heavy sweating. Teach a consistent intake: 2–3 liters of fluid and a steady, normal salt intake every day. "I started a diet" or "I'm on ibuprofen for my back" is a lithium level waiting to climb.

Lithium levels track a narrow road: 0.6–1.2 mEq/L for maintenance, with anything over 1.5 mEq/L counting as toxic and over 2.0 mEq/L a medical emergency. Draw levels as troughs, 12 hours after the evening dose. Expected effects at therapeutic levels are a fine hand tremor, mild thirst, and increased urination. Early toxicity is GI — nausea, vomiting, diarrhea — plus a coarse tremor and slurred speech; later comes ataxia, confusion, seizures, and cardiovascular collapse. There is no antidote; treatment is fluids and, at high levels, hemodialysis. Because lithium is cleared renally and affects the thyroid, baseline and periodic renal function, TSH, and pregnancy status belong in every monitoring plan. Valproate and lamotrigine also serve as mood stabilizers — both carry their own warnings, covered next section over.

Key points
  • All antidepressants raise synaptic serotonin and/or norepinephrine; selectivity equals safety, which is why SSRIs are first-line.
  • Mood lifts in 2–6 weeks while side effects come first — and the boxed warning for suicidality in patients under 25 exists because energy returns before mood.
  • Serotonin syndrome = mental status change + autonomic instability + neuromuscular hyperactivity (clonus); stop the drug, cool, hydrate, benzodiazepines, cyproheptadine.
  • Lithium is high-alert: range 0.6–1.2 mEq/L, trough draws, steady salt and fluid, watch NSAIDs, thiazides, and dehydration; monitor renal function and TSH.

Section 3Antipsychotics & anxiolytics

Typical antipsychotics: dopamine blockade, and the price of it

Typical (first-generation) antipsychotics — haloperidol, fluphenazine, chlorpromazine — are dopamine D2 antagonists, and they are good at exactly one thing: the positive symptoms of psychosis. Hallucinations, delusions, and disorganized thought are the "added" symptoms, and blocking dopamine strips them away. What these drugs barely touch are the negative symptoms — the flat affect, poverty of speech, social withdrawal, and lost motivation. That distinction matters at the bedside, because a patient whose voices are gone but who still cannot get out of bed is not treatment-resistant; they are inadequately treated.

The price is movement. Dopamine blockade in the basal nuclei — the circuit in the reference figure above — produces extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS), and they arrive in a predictable order that is worth knowing because the timing tells you what you are looking at.

Two other consequences follow straight from the dopamine map. Dopamine normally suppresses prolactin, so blocking it raises prolactin: expect galactorrhea, amenorrhea, gynecomastia, and sexual dysfunction — embarrassing effects that patients hide and then stop the drug over. And neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS) is the rare, life-threatening reaction: fever (often above 40 °C), lead-pipe rigidity, altered consciousness, autonomic instability, and a sky-high creatine kinase from muscle breakdown. Stop the antipsychotic, cool aggressively, hydrate, and expect dantrolene or bromocriptine. NMS and serotonin syndrome look alike; the tell is that NMS is rigid and slow (days, lead-pipe rigidity, hyporeflexia), while serotonin syndrome is fast and jumpy (hours, clonus, hyperreflexia, diarrhea).

Atypicals: fewer movements, more metabolism

Atypical (second-generation) antipsychotics — risperidone, olanzapine, quetiapine, aripiprazole, ziprasidone, clozapine — block dopamine D2 and serotonin 5-HT2A receptors (aripiprazole is the exception: it is a D2 partial agonist, which is why it is less sedating and rarely raises prolactin), and that second action buys them two advantages: less EPS and some genuine effect on negative symptoms. They did not, however, come free. They traded movement problems for metabolic syndrome: weight gain, new-onset type 2 diabetes, and dyslipidemia. Olanzapine and clozapine are the worst; ziprasidone and aripiprazole are gentler. That means metabolic monitoring is nursing work — baseline and periodic weight, waist circumference, blood pressure, fasting glucose or A1C, and a lipid panel.

Clozapine is the one to know cold. It is the most effective antipsychotic that exists, reserved for treatment-resistant schizophrenia, and it can cause severe neutropenia (agranulocytosis) — which is why it is dispensed only through a REMS program with mandatory absolute neutrophil count monitoring. A fever or a sore throat in a patient on clozapine is not a cold until an ANC proves it. It also causes myocarditis, seizures at higher doses, severe constipation that can progress to ileus, and, oddly, drooling. All antipsychotics carry a boxed warning against use in elderly patients with dementia-related psychosis because of increased mortality.

Benzodiazepines and the GABA brake

Benzodiazepines — lorazepam, diazepam, alprazolam, midazolam, clonazepam — do not open the chloride channel themselves. They are positive allosteric modulators: they sit alongside the GABA receptor and make GABA's own effect stronger, increasing how frequently the chloride channel opens when GABA binds. That mechanism has a built-in ceiling, and it is the reason a benzodiazepine alone is fairly hard to die from — without GABA present, the drug does nothing. Add an opioid or alcohol, which push on the same brake by other routes, and the ceiling disappears. Most benzodiazepine deaths are combination deaths.

They are excellent short-term drugs: rapid anxiety relief, sedation, muscle relaxation, seizure termination, and alcohol-withdrawal management. They are poor long-term drugs: tolerance develops, dependence follows, and abrupt withdrawal after chronic use can cause seizures and death — a benzodiazepine taper is not a courtesy, it is a safety measure. In older adults they are on the Beers list for a reason: falls, hip fractures, and confusion. Flumazenil reverses them, but use it cautiously — in a chronic user or a mixed overdose it can precipitate intractable seizures. The safer non-benzodiazepine option for chronic anxiety is buspirone: no sedation, no dependence, no reversal needed — but it takes weeks to work and is useless as a rescue.

Restlessness on an antipsychotic is a side effect until proven otherwise. Akathisia gets misread as agitation, the dose goes up, the akathisia gets worse, and the cycle repeats. Ask the patient where the restlessness lives: psychotic agitation is in the mind, akathisia is in the legs — "I have to move, I can't explain it." That one question prevents a great deal of harm, and akathisia is linked to suicidality.

Key points
  • Typicals block dopamine — good for positive symptoms, heavy on EPS: dystonia (hours) → akathisia (days) → parkinsonism (weeks) → tardive dyskinesia (months, often irreversible).
  • Atypicals block dopamine and serotonin — less EPS, more metabolic syndrome; monitor weight, glucose, and lipids.
  • Clozapine is the most effective and the most dangerous — REMS-mandated ANC monitoring for agranulocytosis; treat fever or sore throat as an emergency.
  • NMS = fever + lead-pipe rigidity + altered mental status; it is slow and stiff, while serotonin syndrome is fast and jumpy with clonus.
  • Benzodiazepines amplify GABA (frequency of channel opening); dangerous with opioids/alcohol, never stop abruptly, reversal is flumazenil with caution.

Section 4Antiepileptic drugs

Three ways to stop a seizure

A seizure is a neuronal riot: a population of neurons firing too fast, too synchronously, and spreading that firing to their neighbors. There are only three fundamental ways a drug can quiet it, and essentially every antiepileptic drug (AED) — the word anticonvulsant is used interchangeably — uses at least one of them.

Slow the sodium channels. A neuron cannot fire without opening voltage-gated sodium channels, and after each firing those channels need a moment to reset. Phenytoin, carbamazepine, lamotrigine, and others prolong that inactive state, so a neuron can still fire at a normal rate but cannot fire at a runaway rate. That is elegant: normal traffic passes, the riot does not. Slow the calcium channels. The T-type calcium channels in the thalamus drive the rhythmic 3-per-second discharge of absence seizures; ethosuximide blocks them, which is why it works for absence seizures and nothing else. Boost GABA. More inhibition raises the seizure threshold for the whole brain — the mechanism of benzodiazepines, phenobarbital, and part of what valproate does.

Two nursing consequences fall out of this. First, most AEDs are broad brakes on brain excitability, so sedation, dizziness, and ataxia are class effects, and fall precautions and a no-driving-until-stable conversation belong in the plan. Second, AEDs are prevention, not rescue — missed doses are the single most common reason a controlled patient seizes. Never abruptly discontinue an AED; abrupt withdrawal can trigger status epilepticus in a patient with epilepsy, and withdrawal seizures even in a patient taking the drug for pain or mood who has never had a seizure in their life.

Monitoring, teaching, and the seizure itself

Phenytoin and valproic acid have narrow therapeutic ranges and get serum levels; lamotrigine and levetiracetam generally do not. Draw levels as troughs, just before the next dose, and remember that phenytoin is heavily protein-bound — in a patient with low albumin, the total level can look normal while the free (active) drug is toxic. If the number says fine and the patient has nystagmus, ataxia, and slurred speech, believe the patient.

Teaching carries real weight here. Take doses at the same times every day. Do not stop, even if seizure-free for years, without a supervised taper. Carry medical identification. Know your state's driving restrictions. Alcohol lowers the seizure threshold and stacks sedation. Many AEDs — carbamazepine, phenytoin, phenobarbital — are enzyme inducers that can render oral contraceptives ineffective, and valproate and carbamazepine are teratogenic, so contraception counseling is not optional for anyone of childbearing potential. AEDs also carry a class warning for suicidal thoughts; screen for mood changes at follow-up.

And when a seizure happens: your job is not to stop it with your hands. Stay, protect the head, turn the patient to the side, loosen tight clothing, clear the area, time it, and never force anything into the mouth. A generalized seizure lasting more than five minutes, or repeated seizures without a return to baseline, is status epilepticus — a true emergency treated with IV benzodiazepines first, then a loading agent such as fosphenytoin, valproate, or levetiracetam.

A rash on lamotrigine or carbamazepine is not "just a rash." These are the two AEDs most associated with Stevens–Johnson syndrome and toxic epidermal necrolysis, which begin as an unremarkable rash days to weeks in and end in sloughing skin and an ICU. Teach every patient to report any new rash the day it appears, and never let anyone speed a lamotrigine titration to "catch up" on missed doses — a missed run means restarting the titration from the beginning.

Key points
  • AEDs stop seizures three ways: prolong sodium channel inactivation, block T-type calcium channels (absence), or boost GABA.
  • Sedation, dizziness, and ataxia are class effects; never stop an AED abruptly — it can trigger status epilepticus.
  • Phenytoin 10–20 mcg/mL, valproate 50–100 mcg/mL, drawn as troughs; check albumin before trusting a phenytoin level.

  • Status epilepticus = >5 minutes or no return to baseline — IV benzodiazepine first.

Section 5Anesthetics

Two entirely different jobs

The words sound related, but general and local anesthesia are opposites in strategy. General anesthesia takes away the brain: unconsciousness, amnesia, analgesia, and immobility, delivered systemically, with the patient's protective reflexes — including breathing — deliberately abolished. Local anesthesia leaves the brain entirely alone and cuts the wire instead: the drug blocks sodium channels in a specific nerve so the pain signal never leaves the tissue. The patient is awake and knows exactly what is happening. One takes the receiver offline; the other cuts the phone line.

Modern general anesthesia is almost never one drug — it is balanced anesthesia, a deliberate combination: an IV induction agent to go to sleep fast, an inhaled agent to stay asleep, an opioid for pain, and a neuromuscular blocker for immobility. Combining lets each drug be given at a lower, safer dose. Inhaled agents (sevoflurane, desflurane, isoflurane, nitrous oxide) are titrated breath by breath and eliminated by the lungs. IV agents include propofol — fast, smooth, antiemetic, and a potent respiratory depressant and vasodilator; ketamine — a dissociative that preserves airway reflexes and respiratory drive, valuable in trauma, with emergence delirium as its signature; etomidate — hemodynamically kind, so favored in unstable patients, but it suppresses adrenal function; and midazolam — for anxiolysis and amnesia.

Neuromuscular blockers deserve special fear. Succinylcholine (depolarizing, ultra-short, with fasciculations, hyperkalemia risk, and a link to malignant hyperthermia) and rocuronium or vecuronium (non-depolarizing, reversed with neostigmine or sugammadex) cause paralysis without any sedation or analgesia whatsoever. A paralyzed patient who is not adequately sedated is fully awake, in pain, and unable to signal it. These are high-alert drugs, stored separately, and given only where an airway can be secured immediately.

The stages of anesthesia

Guedel's four stages were described in the era of ether, when induction was slow enough to watch. Modern agents blow through the first three in under a minute — but the stages are still taught because they name what you are watching for, and because Stage II is where a patient can be hurt.

Emergence reverses the sequence, so the patient passes back through Stage II on the way out — which is why the PACU is quiet, why side-lying with suction at hand is standard, and why an emerging patient who thrashes is not "being difficult." Two emergencies live in this territory. Malignant hyperthermia is a genetic reaction to volatile agents and succinylcholine: uncontrolled calcium release in muscle produces masseter rigidity, a fast-rising end-tidal CO₂ (the earliest sign), rigidity, tachycardia, and a temperature that climbs alarmingly — treated with dantrolene, immediate cessation of the trigger, cooling, and treatment of hyperkalemia. Ask about family history of anesthesia problems before surgery; it is a question that saves lives. And aspiration is why NPO status exists — anesthesia strips the gag reflex.

Local anesthetics: the wire cut

Locals come in two chemical families and the difference is clinical. Esters (procaine, benzocaine, tetracaine) are broken down in plasma and cause more allergic reactions; benzocaine sprays also cause methemoglobinemia — suspect it when a patient is cyanotic with a normal PaO₂ and chocolate-brown blood, and treat with methylene blue. Amides (lidocaine, bupivacaine, ropivacaine — note the tell-tale second "i" in the name) are metabolized by the liver and rarely cause true allergy. They are given topically, by local infiltration, as a nerve block, epidurally, or spinally.

Epinephrine is often added to a local. It constricts vessels, which keeps the drug where you put it: longer block, less bleeding, less systemic absorption. The old teaching to avoid epinephrine-containing locals in fingers, toes, nose, ears, and penis has been substantially revised for the digits by modern evidence, but it remains a caution worth knowing and honoring wherever facility policy says so.

The danger of locals is systemic absorption — too much drug, or an accidental intravascular injection. Local anesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST) announces itself in the brain first: circumoral (around-the-mouth) numbness, a metallic taste, tinnitus, dizziness, and agitation, then seizures, then cardiac arrest. Bupivacaine is the most cardiotoxic and can go straight to arrest. Treatment is airway support, benzodiazepines for seizures, and IV lipid emulsion — which every unit doing blocks keeps at hand. After a spinal or epidural, watch for hypotension from sympathetic blockade, monitor the level of the block, keep the patient flat per protocol, and remember that the block recedes in the reverse of how it came on: the level drops from the top down, so the highest dermatomes wake up first and the toes come back last. A patient with moving toes and a wearing-off block is exactly the patient who tries to stand and falls — sensation and motor strength in the feet are the last things to return, and the fall risk lasts until they are fully back.

The first sign of local anesthetic toxicity is a taste and a tingle. Circumoral numbness, ringing ears, and a metallic taste come before the seizure and long before the arrest. A patient during a block who says "my lips feel funny" is telling you the drug is in the bloodstream. Stop injecting, get help, get the lipid emulsion. That sentence is the whole warning you get.

Key points
  • General anesthesia abolishes consciousness and reflexes systemically; local anesthesia blocks sodium channels in one nerve and leaves the patient awake.
  • Balanced anesthesia combines induction agent + inhaled agent + opioid + neuromuscular blocker so each can be dosed lower and safer.
  • Neuromuscular blockers paralyze without sedating — high-alert, airway-ready use only.
  • Stage II (excitement) is the dangerous stage — vomiting and laryngospasm; the patient passes back through it on emergence.
  • Malignant hyperthermia (volatiles, succinylcholine) → dantrolene; LAST (circumoral numbness, metallic taste, tinnitus → seizures → arrest) → IV lipid emulsion.

The chapter in one breath

  • Every drug here acts at the synapse — blocking a reuptake pump, an enzyme, a receptor, or an ion channel. Name the step and you can predict the effects.
  • Serotonin drives mood and the gut; dopamine drives reward, movement, prolactin, and vomiting; norepinephrine is drive and blood pressure; GABA is the brake.
  • Antidepressants take 2–6 weeks to lift mood while side effects come first — and suicide risk rises early because energy returns before mood does.
  • Serotonin syndrome is fast, jumpy, and clonic; NMS is slow, rigid, and febrile. Telling them apart is a bedside skill.
  • Lithium is high-alert: 0.6–1.2 mEq/L, steady salt and fluid, beware NSAIDs, thiazides, and dehydration; monitor renal function and TSH.
  • Typicals cause EPS in a predictable timeline ending in irreversible tardive dyskinesia; atypicals trade that for metabolic syndrome; clozapine demands ANC monitoring.
  • AEDs slow sodium or calcium channels or boost GABA, are never stopped abruptly, and carry signature toxicities — and any rash on lamotrigine or carbamazepine is reported today.
  • General anesthesia removes the brain, local anesthesia cuts the wire; the emergencies to know are malignant hyperthermia (dantrolene) and LAST (lipid emulsion).

ReferenceWords worth knowing

The vocabulary of this chapter is the vocabulary of the report you will give and the order you will question — skim it now, and come back whenever a term stops you.

Agonist
A drug that binds a receptor and activates it, imitating the body's own transmitter.
Akathisia
An extrapyramidal effect of intolerable inner restlessness and an inability to sit still; commonly mistaken for worsening psychosis.
Antagonist
A drug that binds a receptor without activating it, blocking the natural transmitter from acting.
Antiepileptic drug (AED)
A drug that raises the seizure threshold by slowing sodium or calcium channels or enhancing GABA; also called an anticonvulsant.
Balanced anesthesia
Combining an induction agent, an inhaled agent, an opioid, and a neuromuscular blocker so each can be given at a lower, safer dose.
Discontinuation syndrome
Dizziness, flu-like aches, irritability, and "brain zaps" after abruptly stopping an SSRI or SNRI; prevented by tapering.
Dystonia
Sudden sustained muscle spasm — neck twisting, oculogyric crisis, or laryngospasm — occurring within hours to days of an antipsychotic; an emergency.
Extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS)
Movement disorders caused by dopamine blockade in the basal nuclei: dystonia, akathisia, parkinsonism, and tardive dyskinesia.
Flumazenil
The benzodiazepine reversal agent; used cautiously, as it can precipitate seizures in chronic users or mixed overdoses.
GABA
Gamma-aminobutyric acid, the brain's main inhibitory neurotransmitter; opening its chloride channel makes neurons harder to fire.
Local anesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST)
Toxicity from systemic absorption of a local anesthetic: circumoral numbness, metallic taste, and tinnitus progressing to seizures and cardiac arrest; treated with IV lipid emulsion.
Malignant hyperthermia
A genetic, life-threatening reaction to volatile anesthetics or succinylcholine causing rigidity, rising end-tidal CO₂, and hyperthermia; treated with dantrolene.
Monoamine oxidase (MAO)
The enzyme that degrades serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine — and dietary tyramine in the gut wall.
Negative symptoms
The "subtracted" symptoms of psychosis — flat affect, poverty of speech, withdrawal, lost motivation — poorly treated by typical antipsychotics.
Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS)
A rare antipsychotic reaction of high fever, lead-pipe rigidity, altered consciousness, autonomic instability, and elevated creatine kinase.
Neuromuscular blocker
A high-alert drug that paralyzes skeletal muscle without producing any sedation or analgesia; requires a secured airway.
Positive symptoms
The "added" symptoms of psychosis — hallucinations, delusions, disorganized thought — that respond best to dopamine blockade.
Reuptake pump
The transporter that vacuums a neurotransmitter back into the presynaptic neuron; the target of SSRIs, SNRIs, and TCAs.
Serotonin syndrome
Excess serotonergic activity producing mental status change, autonomic instability, and neuromuscular hyperactivity with clonus; treated by stopping the agents, supportive care, and cyproheptadine.
Status epilepticus
A seizure lasting more than five minutes, or repeated seizures without a return to baseline; treated first with an IV benzodiazepine.
Tardive dyskinesia
Late-onset involuntary lip-smacking, tongue-thrusting, and grimacing after months to years of dopamine blockade; often irreversible.
Tyramine
An amine in aged, fermented, and cured foods that triggers a hypertensive crisis in a patient taking an MAOI.

Check yourself

1. A drug blocks a reuptake pump, another blocks an enzyme, and a third blocks a receptor. Give a prototype for each and explain why identifying "the step" is more useful than memorizing side effects.
Pump: fluoxetine (SSRI). Enzyme: phenelzine (MAOI). Receptor: haloperidol (dopamine antagonist). Every effect — wanted and unwanted — follows from which synaptic step the drug touches and which transmitters that step controls, so the step lets you reason out the side effects rather than recall them.
2. Your patient started sertraline eight days ago, feels no better, and is nauseated. She wants to stop. What do you teach?
Side effects come first and usually fade; the mood benefit takes 2–6 weeks. And she should not stop abruptly — SSRIs are tapered to avoid discontinuation syndrome. Nausea reflects serotonin acting on the gut, where most of the body's serotonin lives, and it typically settles within a week or two. Set that expectation at the first dose, not the tenth.
3. Why do antidepressants carry a boxed warning for suicidality, and when in therapy is the risk highest?
Because energy and motivation return before mood does — typically in the first weeks of therapy, and especially in patients under 25. A severely depressed patient may lack the drive to act on suicidal thoughts; once the drug restores drive while hopelessness persists, the risk peaks. Assess suicidality at every contact during that window.
4. A patient on an SSRI is started on tramadol. Hours later he is agitated and diaphoretic, temperature 39.4 °C, with hyperreflexia and clonus in both legs. What is happening, and what do you do?
Serotonin syndrome — mental status change + autonomic instability + neuromuscular hyperactivity, with clonus as the most specific sign. Stop all serotonergic drugs, notify the provider, support with cooling and IV fluids, give benzodiazepines for agitation and rigidity, and expect cyproheptadine (a serotonin blocker) in severe cases.
5. How would you distinguish serotonin syndrome from neuroleptic malignant syndrome at the bedside?
Serotonin syndrome is fast and jumpy: onset within hours, clonus, hyperreflexia, diarrhea. NMS is slow and stiff: onset over days, lead-pipe rigidity, hyporeflexia, very high fever and creatine kinase. The drug history clinches it — a new serotonergic agent versus a dopamine-blocking antipsychotic. Treatment differs: cyproheptadine versus dantrolene/bromocriptine.
6. Your patient on lithium started a low-sodium diet and is taking ibuprofen for back pain. Why does this worry you, and what teaching follows?
Both make the kidney retain sodium — and lithium is reabsorbed by the same pathway, so the lithium level will climb toward toxicity. Teach a consistent normal salt intake and 2–3 L of fluid daily, avoid NSAIDs (and thiazides, and ACE inhibitors) unless the prescriber approves, and report vomiting, diarrhea, or heavy sweating. Therapeutic range is 0.6–1.2 mEq/L; over 1.5 is toxic.
7. A patient on a MAOI reports a pounding occipital headache and stiff neck after dinner out. BP is 196/118. What happened and what was on the plate?
A tyramine-induced hypertensive crisis — likely aged cheese, cured or smoked meat, red wine, draft beer, soy sauce, or sauerkraut. With MAO inhibited, dietary tyramine reaches the circulation and causes a massive norepinephrine release. This is an emergency with stroke risk; teach the tyramine-free diet like an allergy list, and honor the 2-week washout between MAOIs and serotonergic drugs.
8. Two days after starting haloperidol, your patient's neck twists to one side and his eyes roll upward. What is this, and what is your first action?
Acute dystonia with oculogyric crisis — an EPS emergency because it can progress to laryngospasm. Assess and protect the airway first, then give IM/IV diphenhydramine or benztropine as ordered and notify the provider. Dystonia appears within hours to days; akathisia follows in days to weeks; parkinsonism in weeks; tardive dyskinesia in months to years.
9. Why is akathisia so often mismanaged, and what question sorts it out?
It looks like agitation, so the antipsychotic dose gets increased — which makes it worse. Ask where the restlessness is: psychotic agitation lives in the mind, akathisia lives in the legs ("I have to move and I can't explain why"). Treat with a beta blocker or benzodiazepine, or switch agents. Akathisia is distressing and has been linked to suicidality.
10. Your patient on clozapine calls with a sore throat and a fever of 38.6 °C. Why is this not "just a cold"?
Clozapine causes severe neutropenia (agranulocytosis) — fever and sore throat are the classic presentation of a patient with no neutrophils to fight with. This needs an urgent absolute neutrophil count; clozapine is dispensed only through a REMS program precisely because of this risk. Also watch for myocarditis, seizures at higher doses, severe constipation, and drooling.
11. A patient on phenytoin has a total level of 14 mcg/mL but shows nystagmus, ataxia, and slurred speech. Albumin is 2.1 g/dL. Explain.
Phenytoin is highly protein-bound; with low albumin, more drug is free and active, so the total level looks therapeutic while the patient is clinically toxic. Believe the patient over the number, report it, and expect a free phenytoin level. Also remember: dilute IV phenytoin in normal saline only (it precipitates in dextrose) and push it slowly to avoid hypotension and dysrhythmias.
12. During a nerve block a patient says her lips feel numb, she has a metallic taste, and her ears are ringing. What is this, and what happens next?
Early local anesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST) — the drug has reached the bloodstream. Stop the injection immediately, call for help, support the airway, give benzodiazepines if she seizes, and get the IV lipid emulsion. These early neurologic signs precede seizures and cardiac arrest — bupivacaine is the most cardiotoxic and can go straight to arrest, so this sentence from the patient is the entire warning you get.
