Pharmacology · Chapter 3

Immunomodulators & Antineoplastic Agents

Most drugs help a patient by turning a body system up or down a little. The drugs in this chapter do something far more consequential: they rewrite the terms on which the immune system operates, or they poison cell division itself. That power is exactly why they work — a transplanted kidney survives, a lupus flare quiets, a lymphoma melts away — and exactly why they hurt. An immunosuppressed patient cannot mount a fever the way you expect. A chemotherapy patient loses the lining of the gut and the blood-forming marrow along with the tumor. This chapter walks you through the drugs that quiet immunity, the drugs that wake it up, and the drugs that kill dividing cells — and, at every step, what the nurse watches, teaches, and refuses to let slide.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Name the major immunosuppressant classes, their prototypes, and their signature adverse effects.
  • Explain why infection is the defining risk of immunosuppression and how that changes your assessment.
  • Distinguish immunostimulants, biologics, and the main vaccine types, and state who must not receive a live vaccine.
  • Describe the cell cycle and contrast cell-cycle–specific with cell-cycle–nonspecific antineoplastics.
  • Explain why chemotherapy damages normal rapidly dividing tissues, and predict which ones.
  • Identify the five chemotherapy classes with their prototypes and class-defining toxicities.
  • Manage myelosuppression, nausea and vomiting, mucositis, alopecia, and extravasation at the bedside.
  • Apply hazardous drug handling precautions to protect yourself, the patient, and the family.

Section 1Immunosuppressants

Why we deliberately weaken a defense system

The immune system is superb at one job: telling self from not-self and destroying the not-self. That instinct becomes a problem in exactly two situations. In transplantation, the donated organ is not-self, and an untreated immune system will reject it within days. In autoimmune disease — rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, Crohn disease, multiple sclerosis, psoriasis — the system has misfiled the patient's own tissue as foreign and is attacking it. In both cases we give drugs to turn the response down. There is no free lunch here: every bit of immunity we take away is immunity that was also guarding against bacteria, viruses, fungi, and cancer cells.

Transplant regimens are usually built in two stages. Induction therapy is heavy and brief, given around the time of surgery — basiliximab (an interleukin-2 receptor antagonist) or antithymocyte globulin. Maintenance therapy is lifelong and usually combines three drugs from different classes at lower doses each: a calcineurin inhibitor, an antiproliferative, and a corticosteroid. Using three mechanisms at modest doses beats using one at a huge dose, because the toxicities do not stack in the same organ.

The classes worth knowing cold

Calcineurin inhibitors — cyclosporine and tacrolimus — are the backbone of transplant care. They block the calcineurin signal that a T cell needs to make interleukin-2, so the T cell never proliferates. Both have a narrow therapeutic index: too little and the graft rejects, too much and the kidneys fail. Trough levels are drawn immediately before the next dose, and the whole regimen lives or dies on the patient taking it at the same times every day. Both are metabolized by CYP3A4, which is why grapefruit juice, azole antifungals, and clarithromycin raise levels dangerously, while rifampin, phenytoin, and St. John's wort drop them toward rejection.

Antiproliferatives starve lymphocytes of the DNA building blocks they need to divide. Mycophenolate is the modern standard; azathioprine is older and carries one of the most famous interactions in pharmacology — giving allopurinol alongside it blocks xanthine oxidase, the enzyme that clears azathioprine's active metabolite, so drug levels climb and can cause catastrophic marrow failure. mTOR inhibitors (sirolimus, everolimus) spare the kidney but impair wound healing, so they are typically held in the immediate post-op period. Corticosteroids blunt nearly every arm of inflammation and are effective, cheap, and toxic in a dozen directions; they are tapered, never stopped abruptly, because chronic use suppresses the adrenal axis.

On the autoimmune side, low-dose methotrexate is the anchor drug for rheumatoid arthritis, and here is the fact that saves lives: it is dosed once weekly, not daily. Daily dosing of a weekly methotrexate order has killed patients. Folic acid is given on the other days to reduce mucositis and marrow toxicity. Biologic DMARDs — the TNF inhibitors etanercept, adalimumab, and infliximab — are extraordinarily effective and require latent tuberculosis screening before the first dose, because knocking out TNF lets walled-off TB break loose.

Infection is the whole risk

An immunosuppressed patient does not present the way your textbook patient presents. Steroids blunt fever. Neutropenia removes the cells that make pus, so a wound infection may show no drainage and pneumonia may show a nearly clear chest film. The classic signs of inflammation are, in part, the immune system working — and you have turned it off. This means the threshold for concern drops through the floor: a low-grade temperature, a new cough, a sore throat, a small area of redness, or simply "I don't feel right" all get taken seriously and reported.

Prophylaxis is standard rather than optional in this population — typically trimethoprim–sulfamethoxazole against Pneumocystis jirovecii, an antiviral against cytomegalovirus, and an antifungal in high-risk patients. Live vaccines are contraindicated in significant immunosuppression, and they should be brought up to date before transplant or before starting a biologic. Long-term, these patients also carry a real excess risk of malignancy — skin cancer above all — so sunscreen, protective clothing, and annual skin checks are legitimate nursing teaching, not filler.

A fever in an immunosuppressed patient is never "just a fever." In someone on tacrolimus, prednisone, or a TNF inhibitor, a temperature that you would watch overnight in anyone else may be the only sign of an infection that is already systemic. Report it, culture it, and treat it as an emergency until proven otherwise.

Section 2Immunostimulants and vaccines

Drugs that wake the immune system up

If immunosuppressants turn the dial down, immunostimulants turn it up — either by rebuilding cells the immune system has lost, or by releasing a brake that a tumor has been exploiting. The most common ones you will hang are the colony-stimulating factors. Filgrastim and its long-acting cousin pegfilgrastim are manufactured versions of G-CSF; they tell the marrow to pump out neutrophils and are given after chemotherapy to shorten the window of dangerous neutropenia. The classic side effect is deep, aching bone pain — the marrow is literally working overtime — and it is usually managed with acetaminophen or an NSAID rather than by stopping the drug. Timing matters: these are given at least twenty-four hours after chemotherapy, never alongside it, because you do not want to hand freshly stimulated dividing marrow cells to a drug that kills dividing cells.

Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (epoetin alfa, darbepoetin) raise hemoglobin but carry a boxed warning: pushed too high, they shorten survival in some cancers and cause thrombosis and stroke. They are used at the lowest dose that avoids transfusion, not to make the number look pretty. Interferons (interferon alfa-2b; interferon beta for multiple sclerosis) reliably produce flu-like symptoms — fever, chills, myalgia — and can cause depression severe enough to require screening. Interleukin-2 (aldesleukin) is powerful and dangerous, with capillary leak syndrome as its defining toxicity.

The most important recent class is the immune checkpoint inhibitors — pembrolizumab and nivolumab (anti–PD-1), atezolizumab (anti–PD-L1), and ipilimumab (anti–CTLA-4). Tumors survive partly by pressing the immune system's own "stand down" buttons; these drugs cut that wire. The result is a reawakened immune system that sometimes attacks the patient too, producing immune-related adverse events: colitis, pneumonitis, hepatitis, thyroiditis, dermatitis, hypophysitis. Any new diarrhea, cough, or shortness of breath in a patient on a checkpoint inhibitor is reported — these are treated with corticosteroids, and delay does real harm.

A quick decoder for biologic names, because it genuinely helps: -mab means monoclonal antibody, -cept means a receptor fusion protein, -nib means a small-molecule kinase inhibitor, and -kin or -grastim flags a cytokine or growth factor.

Vaccines: borrowing the memory without the disease

A vaccine is the most cost-effective drug there is. It shows the adaptive immune system a harmless preview of a pathogen so that memory B and T cells are already standing by when the real thing arrives — active immunity, slow to build but long-lasting. Contrast that with passive immunity, where we simply inject someone else's ready-made antibodies: immune globulin, Rho(D) immune globulin, rabies or tetanus immune globulin. Passive protection works within minutes and fades within weeks, because the patient made no memory of their own. Use active for prevention, passive for a threat that is already here.

Vaccine administration is a nursing skill with a few non-negotiables. Give the Vaccine Information Statement before the injection and document that you did. Have epinephrine immediately available and observe for at least fifteen minutes afterward, longer if there is any history of reaction. Document lot number, expiration, site, and route — this is one of the few places where charting is itself a public health function. Teach that soreness, a low-grade temperature, and fatigue for a day mean the vaccine is working, and treat them with acetaminophen; do not give aspirin to a child. And know the real contraindications from the false ones: a serious allergy to a vaccine component or a previous anaphylactic reaction is a genuine contraindication; a mild cold, a runny nose, breastfeeding, or a family history of reactions is not.

Ask the immune-status question before every live vaccine. "Are you pregnant, could you be pregnant, are you on steroids or a biologic, or does anyone in your household have a weakened immune system?" A live vaccine given to the wrong person — or occasionally to someone living with the wrong person — can cause the disease it was meant to prevent.

Section 3Antineoplastic principles

The cell cycle, and why it is the target

Cancer is, at bottom, a disease of division that will not stop. So the oldest and still most widely used strategy against it is to attack division itself. To understand where each drug lands, you need the cycle. G1 is growth: the cell enlarges and manufactures the enzymes it will need. S is synthesis: DNA is copied, and the cell is at its most chemically vulnerable here. G2 is the second growth phase, building the spindle apparatus and proofreading the new DNA. M is mitosis, when the chromosomes are pulled apart and the cell splits in two. And G0 is the resting state — the cell is alive and working but not dividing. G0 is the hiding place. Cell-cycle–specific drugs cannot touch a cell in G0 at all, and even the nonspecific ones work on it poorly — a resting cell is not copying DNA and has time to repair the damage it does take. That sanctuary is a large part of why cancers relapse years later.

That geography splits antineoplastics into two groups. Cell-cycle–specific (CCS) drugs only work on a cell that is in a particular phase: antimetabolites act in S, mitotic inhibitors act in M, bleomycin acts in G2. Because they need to catch the cell in the act, they work best against fast-growing tumors and are often given as a continuous infusion or in divided doses to widen the window. Cell-cycle–nonspecific (CCNS) drugs — alkylating agents, antitumor antibiotics, platinum compounds — damage DNA whenever they find it, including in G0. They are typically given as a single large bolus, because the damage is dose-dependent rather than timing-dependent.

Log kill, growth fraction, and why chemo comes in cycles

Here is the idea that explains the entire structure of a chemotherapy regimen: a given dose of a drug kills a constant percentage of tumor cells, not a constant number. This is the log kill hypothesis, sometimes called fractional kill. If a dose kills 99.9 percent of a billion cancer cells, a million are still alive. The next cycle kills 99.9 percent of the million, leaving a thousand. You can see immediately why one round is never enough, why the schedule is a schedule and not a suggestion, and why a patient who feels wonderful after two cycles still needs the other four.

The gaps between cycles are equally deliberate. Normal marrow recovers from chemotherapy faster than tumor does, so a rest period of two to three weeks lets the patient's blood counts climb back while the tumor gets comparatively less benefit. Combination chemotherapy follows the same logic from a different angle: drugs are chosen with different mechanisms, different resistance patterns, and — crucially — non-overlapping toxicities, so that no single organ takes the whole hit. Dosing is calculated by body surface area in mg/m2 rather than by weight, and carboplatin is dosed by target AUC using the patient's renal function. Small errors in these calculations are not small; this is why chemotherapy orders require an independent double-check by a second qualified clinician.

Why chemotherapy hurts the patient too

Traditional chemotherapy cannot tell a cancer cell from a healthy cell. It can only tell a dividing cell from a resting one. That is the entire tragedy of the drug class in one sentence — and it lets you predict every major side effect before you ever open the drug book. Ask yourself which normal tissues in the body divide fast, and you have your answer.

Two more principles round out the picture. Growth fraction — the proportion of a tumor's cells actively cycling — predicts responsiveness: leukemias and lymphomas with high growth fractions melt away, while slow, bulky solid tumors respond far less. And drug resistance develops, most notoriously through multidrug resistance proteins such as P-glycoprotein, which sit in the cell membrane and pump the drug straight back out. Resistance is another reason we combine drugs and another reason we do not stretch out the schedule.

You can predict chemo toxicity without memorizing it. "Which of my patient's normal tissues divide fastest?" Marrow, gut lining, hair, gonads, skin. That single question gives you myelosuppression, mucositis and diarrhea, alopecia, infertility, and rash — the whole list, derived rather than memorized.

Key points
  • The cycle is G1 → S → G2 → M, with G0 as the resting phase that cell-cycle–specific drugs cannot reach at all and nonspecific drugs reach poorly — the source of relapse.
  • CCS drugs hit one phase (antimetabolites in S, mitotic inhibitors in M); CCNS drugs damage DNA in any phase, including G0.
  • The log kill hypothesis — each dose kills a percentage, not a number — is why treatment comes in repeated cycles and why finishing the regimen matters.
  • Chemo cannot distinguish cancer from healthy; it distinguishes dividing from resting, which is why marrow, gut, hair, and gonads suffer.

Section 4Chemotherapy classes

Damaging the DNA: alkylating agents and antitumor antibiotics

Alkylating agents are the oldest class — descendants of the sulfur mustard gas of the First World War, whose habit of destroying the bone marrow of exposed soldiers was the clue that led to the nitrogen mustards being tried against lymphoma in the 1940s. They attach an alkyl group to DNA, cross-linking the strands so the double helix can no longer unzip and copy itself. They are cell-cycle–nonspecific and broadly effective, and each subgroup carries a signature toxicity. Cyclophosphamide breaks down into acrolein, which sits in the bladder and burns it — hemorrhagic cystitis, prevented with aggressive hydration, frequent voiding, and the rescue agent mesna. Ifosfamide does the same and adds neurotoxicity. The nitrosoureas such as carmustine cross the blood–brain barrier (useful for brain tumors) and cause a notoriously delayed myelosuppression that nadirs four to six weeks out. The platinum compounds are grouped here by behavior: cisplatin is nephrotoxic, ototoxic, and one of the most emetogenic drugs in medicine; carboplatin trades that for marrow suppression; oxaliplatin produces a strange cold-triggered neuropathy — patients must be taught not to drink cold liquids or touch cold metal for days after a dose.

Antitumor antibiotics come from soil bacteria and intercalate into DNA, generating free radicals that shred it. The anthracyclines — doxorubicin, daunorubicin, epirubicin — are among the most useful drugs in oncology and among the most feared, because their cardiotoxicity is cumulative and largely irreversible. A lifetime dose ceiling (roughly 550 mg/m2 for doxorubicin) is tracked across the patient's entire history, a baseline ejection fraction is measured by echocardiogram or MUGA, and dexrazoxane may be given as a cardioprotectant. Doxorubicin is also a potent vesicant and turns urine red-orange for a day or two — harmless, but terrifying if nobody warned the patient. Bleomycin is the exception that proves the rule: it causes almost no myelosuppression, and instead causes pulmonary fibrosis, with its own cumulative lifetime limit and a baseline pulmonary function test.

Starving and stalling: antimetabolites and mitotic inhibitors

Antimetabolites are molecular impostors. They look enough like the natural building blocks of DNA that the cell picks them up and jams its own machinery — so they act in the S phase and are strongly cell-cycle–specific. Methotrexate blocks dihydrofolate reductase and starves the cell of folate; high-dose regimens are followed by leucovorin rescue, which restores folate to normal cells. Never give NSAIDs or salicylates with high-dose methotrexate — they delay its clearance and can cause fatal toxicity. 5-fluorouracil and its oral prodrug capecitabine cause mucositis, diarrhea, and the palms-and-soles redness of hand–foot syndrome. Cytarabine adds a distinctive cerebellar toxicity and a chemical conjunctivitis prevented with corticosteroid eye drops.

Mitotic inhibitors attack the spindle in the M phase, and they do it from opposite directions. The vinca alkaloids (vincristine, vinblastine) prevent microtubules from assembling; the taxanes (paclitaxel, docetaxel) do the reverse, freezing microtubules so they cannot disassemble. Either way, mitosis stops. Vincristine's dose-limiting problem is peripheral neuropathy — numb fingertips, foot drop, and an autonomic constipation that can progress to ileus, which is why a bowel regimen goes out with the drug. And vincristine carries the most absolute rule in oncology nursing: it is labeled "For intravenous use only — fatal if given by other routes," because intrathecal administration causes ascending paralysis and death. Taxanes cause hypersensitivity reactions from their solvent, so patients are premedicated with a corticosteroid, an antihistamine, and an H2 blocker, and paclitaxel is infused through non-PVC tubing with an in-line filter.

Aiming instead of carpet-bombing: targeted and hormonal therapy

Everything above kills dividing cells indiscriminately. Targeted therapy asks a better question: what is broken in this tumor specifically? Imatinib shuts down the BCR-ABL kinase that drives chronic myeloid leukemia and turned a fatal disease into a manageable one. Trastuzumab targets HER2-positive breast cancer and causes cardiotoxicity that is, unlike doxorubicin's, usually reversible — but the two together are a bad combination. Rituximab targets CD20 on B cells and can reactivate hepatitis B, so screening comes first. Bevacizumab starves tumors of blood supply by blocking VEGF, at the cost of hypertension, bleeding, impaired wound healing, and rare GI perforation. Targeted drugs are gentler on the marrow but not gentle — they simply trade one toxicity profile for another. Hormonal agents work the same logic on hormone-driven tumors: tamoxifen blocks estrogen at the breast (and raises the risk of endometrial cancer and clots), the aromatase inhibitors anastrozole and letrozole cut estrogen production in postmenopausal women (at the cost of joint pain and bone loss), and leuprolide suppresses testosterone in prostate cancer after an initial tumor flare.

Section 5Chemotherapy toxicities and nursing care

Myelosuppression: the toxicity that limits the dose

For most regimens, the marrow decides how much drug a patient can have. Counts fall after each cycle, hit bottom — the nadir — usually seven to fourteen days later, and recover over the following two to three weeks. The three cell lines fail on their own schedule, and each produces its own emergency.

Neutropenia is the deadliest. The absolute neutrophil count, not the white count, is what matters: an ANC below 1,000/mm3 is serious and below 500/mm3 is severe. Febrile neutropenia is an oncologic emergency — a single oral temperature of 38.3°C (101°F), or 38.0°C (100.4°F) sustained for an hour, in a neutropenic patient means blood cultures now and broad-spectrum antibiotics within one hour. Not after rounds. Not after the culture results. Within the hour, because there are no neutrophils to hold the line and a patient can go from a low fever to septic shock in a few hours. Teach the patient that fever at home means calling immediately and going to the emergency department, not waiting until morning.

Neutropenic precautions are largely about removing sources of organisms and removing breaks in the skin: meticulous hand hygiene by everyone, no fresh flowers or standing water, no unpasteurized or raw foods, no crowds or sick visitors, and — a favorite of exam writers because it is genuinely important — no rectal temperatures, suppositories, or enemas, since a tear in the rectal mucosa in a neutropenic patient is a direct line to the bloodstream. Thrombocytopenia brings bleeding precautions below roughly 50,000/mm3 and spontaneous bleeding risk below 20,000/mm3: electric razor, soft toothbrush, no aspirin or NSAIDs, no IM injections, no flossing, and fall prevention. Anemia is the least dramatic and the most complained about — profound fatigue, dyspnea on exertion, and a life shrunk down to the couch. Take it seriously; it is what patients remember.

Nausea, mouth, hair, and the things patients actually fear

Chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting comes in three flavors and they are managed differently. Acute emesis begins within a few hours. Delayed emesis starts more than twenty-four hours out and is the specialty of cisplatin and cyclophosphamide — patients get home, feel fine, and then are ambushed. Anticipatory nausea is conditioned: the patient vomits in the parking lot before the drug ever hangs, and the answer is a benzodiazepine such as lorazepam plus better control of the first cycle. The single most important principle is that antiemetics are given prophylactically and on a schedule, before the chemotherapy — never PRN, never chasing. Modern regimens combine a 5-HT3 antagonist (ondansetron — watch the QT interval), an NK-1 antagonist (aprepitant), dexamethasone, and often olanzapine.

Mucositis makes eating a misery and is a portal for infection. Assess the mouth every day, and teach a soft toothbrush, saline or sodium bicarbonate rinses several times daily, and no alcohol-based mouthwash — it is like pouring astringent on a burn. Oral cryotherapy (ice chips during a short 5-FU infusion) genuinely helps. Alopecia is not medically dangerous and is often the thing patients grieve most; it typically starts one to three weeks in, involves brows, lashes, and body hair as well as scalp, and it grows back — sometimes a different color or texture. Encourage choosing a wig or head covering before the hair falls, because matching it afterward is harder; scalp cooling reduces loss with some regimens. Teach scalp sun protection and gentle care.

Extravasation and the drugs you never take your eyes off

A vesicant is a drug that causes tissue necrosis if it leaks out of the vein. The anthracyclines, the vinca alkaloids, mechlorethamine, and mitomycin are the classic offenders, and the damage from a leak can go to tendon and bone and end in grafting or amputation. Prevention is the entire strategy: a central line for vesicants whenever possible, a fresh site with confirmed blood return, no site over a joint or in an antecubital fossa, and a nurse who stays and watches rather than starting it and stepping out.

If the patient reports burning or stinging, or you see swelling, redness, resistance to injection, or loss of blood return — stop the infusion immediately. Do not pull the needle out. Disconnect the tubing, attempt to aspirate residual drug through the catheter, notify the provider, mark and photograph the area, and elevate the limb. Then the antidote depends on which drug leaked, and the temperature is part of the antidote: for anthracyclines, apply cold and give dexrazoxane or topical DMSO; for vinca alkaloids, apply warm and give hyaluronidase. Cold for anthracyclines localizes the drug; warm for vincas disperses it. Reversing them makes the injury worse.

Protecting yourself and the family

Chemotherapy agents are hazardous drugs: they are carcinogenic, mutagenic, or teratogenic to the people who handle them as well as to the patient. Preparation happens in a biological safety cabinet by trained pharmacy staff, never on a unit counter. Administration requires chemotherapy-rated double gloves and a lint-free, back-closing impermeable gown, eye protection when splashing is possible, and closed-system transfer devices. A spill kit stays where the drug is given, and all chemotherapy waste goes in the designated hazardous stream, not the regular sharps box. Nurses who are pregnant, trying to conceive, or breastfeeding should not handle these agents.

The precautions follow the patient home, because the drug leaves in body fluids. For roughly 48 hours after a dose, teach the family to wear gloves when handling urine, stool, vomit, or soiled linens; to close the toilet lid and flush twice; to wash contaminated laundry separately from the household's; and to wash hands thoroughly afterward. Say this out loud rather than handing over a pamphlet, and say why — families who understand the reason follow it, and families who think they are being told their loved one is "toxic" often feel shame instead. Finally, chemotherapy is a high-alert medication class: two qualified clinicians independently verify the order, the drug, the dose against the body surface area calculation, the route, and the patient's cumulative lifetime totals before anything is hung.

Cold for anthracyclines, warm for vincas. If doxorubicin leaks, cold compresses keep it where it is until dexrazoxane or DMSO can work. If vincristine leaks, warm compresses plus hyaluronidase spread and dilute it. Swap them and you turn a bad night into a surgical one — this is one of the few pairs genuinely worth memorizing as a pair.

Key points
  • The nadir arrives 7–14 days after a cycle; febrile neutropenia means cultures and antibiotics within one hour, and no rectal temps, suppositories, or enemas.
  • Antiemetics are scheduled before chemotherapy, not PRN; delayed emesis (cisplatin) ambushes patients at home and anticipatory emesis responds to lorazepam.
  • For extravasation: stop, leave the needle in, aspirate, notify — then cold for anthracyclines and warm for vinca alkaloids.
  • Chemotherapy drugs are hazardous and high-alert: double gloves and gown, independent double-check, and body-fluid precautions at home for 48 hours.

The chapter in one breath

  • Immunosuppressants keep a graft alive and quiet autoimmune disease by turning down the immune system — and every bit of immunity removed was also guarding against infection and cancer.
  • Calcineurin inhibitors need trough levels and avoid grapefruit; methotrexate is weekly; azathioprine plus allopurinol is marrow failure; TNF inhibitors need TB screening first.
  • Immunosuppressed patients hide infection — blunted fever, no pus — so any fever is an emergency, and live vaccines are contraindicated.
  • Immunostimulants rebuild counts (filgrastim — bone pain) or release the brake (checkpoint inhibitors — immune-related colitis, pneumonitis, thyroiditis).
  • Vaccines give slow, lasting active immunity; immune globulin gives immediate, temporary passive immunity; live vaccines are off-limits in pregnancy and immunosuppression.
  • Chemotherapy targets the cell cycle; it cannot tell cancer from healthy, only dividing from resting — which predicts myelosuppression, mucositis, alopecia, and infertility.
  • The log kill hypothesis — each dose kills a percentage, not a number — explains repeated cycles, rest periods, and combination therapy with non-overlapping toxicities.
  • The class signatures: cyclophosphamide → hemorrhagic cystitis (mesna); doxorubicin → cumulative cardiotoxicity; bleomycin → pulmonary fibrosis; vincristine → fatal if intrathecal; cisplatin → nephro/ototoxicity.
  • At the bedside: febrile neutropenia = antibiotics within one hour; antiemetics scheduled before the dose; for extravasation stop and leave the needle in, then cold for anthracyclines, warm for vincas; handle every agent as hazardous and high-alert.

ReferenceWords worth knowing

A quick reference for the terms in this chapter — skim it now, or circle back whenever a word trips you up.

Absolute neutrophil count (ANC)
The number of infection-fighting neutrophils per mm3; below 1,000 is serious, below 500 is severe neutropenia. The number that drives every precaution.
Alkylating agent
A cell-cycle–nonspecific drug that cross-links DNA so it cannot replicate; prototype cyclophosphamide.
Antimetabolite
A drug that mimics a normal DNA building block and jams synthesis in the S phase; prototypes methotrexate and 5-fluorouracil.
Calcineurin inhibitor
An immunosuppressant that blocks interleukin-2 production so T cells cannot proliferate; tacrolimus, cyclosporine.
Cell-cycle–nonspecific (CCNS)
Acts on cells in any phase, including resting G0; alkylating agents, antitumor antibiotics, platinums.
Cell-cycle–specific (CCS)
Acts only on cells in a particular phase; antimetabolites (S), mitotic inhibitors (M).
Checkpoint inhibitor
A monoclonal antibody that releases the immune system's brakes on a tumor (pembrolizumab, nivolumab, ipilimumab); causes immune-related adverse events.
Extravasation
Leakage of a drug out of the vein into surrounding tissue; with a vesicant, this causes necrosis.
Febrile neutropenia
Fever in a neutropenic patient — an oncologic emergency requiring cultures and broad-spectrum antibiotics within one hour.
G0 phase
The resting state, where a cell is alive but not dividing; cell-cycle–specific drugs cannot reach it and nonspecific drugs reach it poorly, which is why cancers relapse.
Hazardous drug
An agent carcinogenic, mutagenic, or teratogenic to handlers as well as patients; requires double gloves, gown, closed systems, and dedicated waste.
Immune-related adverse event (irAE)
Autoimmune injury — colitis, pneumonitis, hepatitis, thyroiditis — caused by a checkpoint inhibitor; treated with corticosteroids.
Leucovorin rescue
Giving reduced folate after high-dose methotrexate to restore normal cells while the tumor stays starved.
Live attenuated vaccine
A weakened but replicating organism (MMR, varicella, rotavirus, LAIV, yellow fever); contraindicated in pregnancy and significant immunosuppression.
Log kill hypothesis
Each chemotherapy dose kills a constant percentage of tumor cells, not a constant number — the reason treatment comes in repeated cycles.
Mesna
A rescue agent that binds acrolein in the bladder, preventing the hemorrhagic cystitis caused by cyclophosphamide and ifosfamide.
Mucositis
Inflammation and ulceration of the GI lining, mouth to anus, from loss of rapidly dividing mucosal cells.
Myelosuppression
Chemotherapy-induced shutdown of bone marrow production → neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, anemia; the dose-limiting toxicity of most regimens.
Nadir
The lowest point blood counts reach after a chemotherapy cycle, typically 7–14 days out.
Passive immunity
Protection from borrowed, ready-made antibodies (immune globulin); immediate but temporary, with no memory formed.
Tumor lysis syndrome
Massive cell breakdown releasing potassium, phosphate, and uric acid, dropping calcium and injuring the kidney; prevented with hydration and allopurinol or rasburicase.
Vesicant
A drug that causes tissue necrosis if it extravasates — anthracyclines, vinca alkaloids, mechlorethamine, mitomycin.

Check yourself

1. A kidney transplant patient is on tacrolimus, mycophenolate, and prednisone. Why three drugs instead of one large dose of the most effective agent?
Because three different mechanisms at lower doses suppress rejection effectively while spreading the toxicity across different organs instead of stacking it in one. A calcineurin inhibitor blocks the IL-2 signal, an antiproliferative starves lymphocytes of the purines they need to divide, and a corticosteroid blunts inflammation broadly — three separate angles on the rejection response, so no single drug has to be pushed to a nephrotoxic or marrow-toxic dose.
2. A patient on cyclosporine mentions he has started drinking grapefruit juice every morning. Why does this matter, and what will happen?
Grapefruit juice inhibits CYP3A4, so cyclosporine levels rise — risking nephrotoxicity, hypertension, tremor, and hyperkalemia. Calcineurin inhibitors have a narrow therapeutic index, so even a modest level increase is clinically dangerous. Teach him to stop the juice and report tremor or decreased urine output.
3. A new order reads "methotrexate 15 mg PO daily" for rheumatoid arthritis. What do you do, and why?
Hold the drug and clarify the order immediately — low-dose methotrexate for rheumatoid arthritis is dosed once weekly, and daily dosing has been fatal. Daily administration causes profound mucositis and marrow failure. Folic acid is given on the non-methotrexate days to reduce those toxicities.
4. Before a patient's first dose of adalimumab, what screening is mandatory and why?
Screening for latent tuberculosis (and hepatitis B). TNF is what walls off latent TB in granulomas; neutralizing it lets dormant TB reactivate and disseminate. The same logic applies to hepatitis B reactivation with rituximab.
5. Why is an immunosuppressed patient's fever more alarming than the same fever in a healthy patient, and why might a serious infection show almost no local signs?
Because the drugs have removed the defenses that would normally contain the infection, so it spreads fast — and because the classic signs of inflammation are the immune response you have suppressed. Steroids blunt fever, and without neutrophils there is no pus, so a wound may look clean and a pneumonia film may look nearly normal while sepsis is already underway.
6. Your patient is starting pembrolizumab and calls a week later reporting new watery diarrhea six times a day. What is your concern and why is this urgent?
Immune-mediated colitis — an immune-related adverse event from checkpoint inhibition. Releasing the immune brake can turn the immune system against the patient's own bowel, lung, liver, or thyroid. It is treated with corticosteroids, and delay risks perforation, so this is reported the same day rather than watched.
7. A 4-year-old is due for MMR. Her mother is currently receiving chemotherapy. A father asks whether his own varicella vaccine is safe while on prednisone 60 mg daily, which he has taken for the past month. Sort out who can get what.
The child can receive MMR — living with an immunosuppressed person is not a contraindication for MMR. The father must not receive live varicella vaccine. The steroid threshold is worth knowing precisely: prednisone at 20 mg or more per day for two weeks or longer counts as significant immunosuppression, and he is well past both marks — he can be vaccinated once he has been off it for a month. Live vaccines are contraindicated in the significantly immunosuppressed person themselves; household contacts are generally still vaccinated (which protects the vulnerable person), with special care around live rotavirus stool handling and any vaccine rash.
8. Explain the log kill hypothesis and use it to answer a patient who says, "My scan is clear after two cycles — why do I need four more?"
Each dose kills a fixed percentage of tumor cells, not a fixed number — so surviving cells always remain, far below what a scan can detect. A clear scan means the tumor is below the resolution of imaging, not that it is gone. Stopping early lets the survivors regrow, often with resistance.
9. Without looking anything up, predict the major side effects of a new cytotoxic chemotherapy drug. What single question gets you there?
"Which of the patient's normal tissues divide fastest?" — bone marrow, GI mucosa, hair follicles, gonads, skin, and a fetus. That yields myelosuppression, mucositis and diarrhea, alopecia, infertility, rash, and teratogenicity. Cytotoxic chemo cannot distinguish cancer from healthy tissue, only dividing cells from resting ones.
10. Your patient is receiving cyclophosphamide and reports pink-tinged urine. What is happening, and what should have been in place to prevent it?
Hemorrhagic cystitis from acrolein, a bladder-toxic metabolite — report it now. Prevention is aggressive hydration, frequent voiding (including overnight), and the rescue agent mesna, which binds acrolein in the bladder. Note this is different from doxorubicin's harmless red-orange urine, which needs teaching, not treatment.
11. A neutropenic patient with an ANC of 300 has an oral temperature of 38.4°C. Name your first three actions and the time limit you are working against.
This is febrile neutropenia — an oncologic emergency. Notify the provider immediately, draw blood cultures, and get broad-spectrum antibiotics started within one hour. Do not wait for culture results. With no neutrophils to contain the organism, the patient can progress to septic shock in hours. Also: no rectal temperatures, suppositories, or enemas in a neutropenic patient.
12. Mid-infusion, a patient receiving doxorubicin through a peripheral IV reports burning and you note swelling and no blood return. Walk through your response — and state how it would differ if the drug were vincristine.
Stop the infusion immediately, but leave the needle in place, disconnect the tubing, aspirate residual drug through the catheter, notify the provider, mark/photograph and elevate the limb. For an anthracycline like doxorubicin, apply cold compresses and give dexrazoxane or topical DMSO to localize the drug. For a vinca alkaloid like vincristine, do the opposite — apply warm compresses and give hyaluronidase to disperse and dilute it. Reversing cold and warm makes the tissue injury worse.
