Pharmacology · Chapter 1

Pharmacological Principles & Herbal Supplements

Every drug you will ever give is a bargain. You accept a wanted effect and you accept, in the same breath, the unwanted ones that ride along with it. Pharmacology is the study of that bargain — what the body does to a drug, what the drug does to the body, and how a nurse tips the odds toward benefit and away from harm. This chapter builds the vocabulary and the reasoning you will use for every medication in the rest of the book: how drugs are named and grouped, how they move through a patient, how they act once they arrive, how to give them safely, and why the bottle of ginkgo in your patient's purse belongs on the medication list too.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Distinguish a drug's chemical, generic, and trade names, and explain why nurses work in generic names.
  • Define a drug class and a prototype drug, and use a prototype to predict a whole class's effects.
  • Describe the four pharmacokinetic processes — absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion — and the first-pass effect.
  • Use half-life to reason about dosing intervals, steady state, and trough levels.
  • Explain pharmacodynamics: receptors, agonists and antagonists, dose–response, potency versus efficacy.
  • Interpret the therapeutic index and name the common narrow-therapeutic-index drugs.
  • Apply the rights of medication administration, match a drug to an appropriate route, and name concrete error-prevention practices.
  • Identify common herbal supplements and their clinically important drug interactions.

Section 1Drugs, names & classes

One drug, three names

A drug is any chemical that changes the way the body works. Every approved drug carries three names, and confusing them is one of the quiet sources of medication error. The chemical name describes the molecule — N-acetyl-para-aminophenol — and you will essentially never use it. The generic name (also called the nonproprietary or United States Adopted Name) is the drug's one true name: acetaminophen. It belongs to no company, it never changes, and it is what the order, the chart, and the reference book use. The trade name (brand or proprietary name) is the marketing name the manufacturer owns — Tylenol, and a dozen others.

Here is why this matters at the bedside. One generic drug can wear many trade names, and a patient arriving from home may know only the brand. A patient taking Tylenol, Percocet, and a nighttime cold remedy is taking acetaminophen three times and may sail past the maximum daily dose without ever recognizing the drug. Nurses work in generic names precisely because the generic name is the only name that cannot hide a duplicate.

Generic names also carry built-in clues. Most classes share a stem — a fragment of the name that tells you what the drug is before you look it up. If it ends in -olol it is a beta blocker; -pril, an ACE inhibitor; -statin, an HMG-CoA reductase inhibitor; -prazole, a proton pump inhibitor; -cillin, a penicillin; -sartan, an angiotensin II receptor blocker. Learning stems is one of the highest-yield hours you can spend in this book.

Classes: sorting drugs by what they do

A drug class (or pharmacologic class) is a group of drugs that share a mechanism of action, and therefore share effects, adverse effects, and nursing implications. This is the whole reason pharmacology is learnable. There are thousands of drugs and only a few dozen mechanisms that matter. You are not memorizing a list; you are learning a small number of stories and then recognizing which story a new drug belongs to.

Drugs can be grouped two ways, and both appear in practice. A therapeutic classification says what the drug is used for — antihypertensive, anticoagulant, analgesic. A pharmacologic classification says how it works — calcium channel blocker, vitamin K antagonist, opioid receptor agonist. One drug lives in both systems at once: metoprolol is therapeutically an antihypertensive and antianginal, and pharmacologically a beta1-selective adrenergic blocker.

Prototype drugs

Because a class shares a mechanism, each class has a prototype — the well-studied, representative member you learn cold, then use as the template for every other drug in the family. Learn morphine and you have most of hydromorphone, fentanyl, and oxycodone. Learn furosemide and bumetanide and torsemide hold few surprises. When you meet an unfamiliar drug, your first question is not "what does this do?" but "what class is this, and what is the prototype?"

Section 2Pharmacokinetics — what the body does to the drug

The four-step journey

Pharmacokinetics is the study of drug movement: how a drug gets in, where it goes, how it is chemically changed, and how it leaves. Four processes, remembered as ADME, run at once from the moment of administration: Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion. Everything that determines how much drug reaches its target and for how long lives in these four steps — which is why the same 5 mg tablet behaves very differently in a young athlete and in an 88-year-old with heart failure and kidney disease.

Absorption is the movement of drug from the site of administration into the bloodstream. It depends on the route (IV is instant and complete; oral is slow and partial), on blood flow to the site (a shocked patient absorbs almost nothing from an intramuscular injection), on the drug's ability to cross membranes (lipid-soluble and un-ionized crosses easily), and on the presence of food. The fraction of a dose that actually reaches the systemic circulation intact is its bioavailability. By definition an IV dose is 100% bioavailable; an oral dose is always less.

Distribution is the drug's travel from blood into tissue. Blood flow decides who gets it first — heart, brain, liver, and kidney are perfused richly; fat and resting muscle far less. Two barriers matter clinically: the blood–brain barrier, which only lipid-soluble drugs cross easily (a reason some antihistamines sedate and others do not), and the placenta, which stops almost nothing. Distribution is also governed by protein binding: many drugs ride on albumin, and only the unbound (free) fraction is active. A patient with low albumin — malnourished, cirrhotic, elderly — has more free drug from the same dose and is at risk for toxicity at a "normal" dose.

Metabolism and the first-pass effect

Metabolism (biotransformation) chemically alters a drug, usually in the liver, usually via the cytochrome P450 (CYP450) enzyme family. Most of the time metabolism inactivates the drug and makes it water-soluble enough to be excreted. Sometimes it does the opposite: a prodrug is given inactive and must be metabolized to work — codeine must be converted to morphine by CYP2D6, and a patient who is a poor metabolizer gets little relief while an ultrarapid metabolizer can be dangerously overdosed.

The CYP450 system is where most drug–drug interactions live. An enzyme inducer (rifampin, phenytoin, carbamazepine, St. John's wort) speeds metabolism, so partner drugs fall to subtherapeutic levels — the classic disaster is an oral contraceptive or warfarin failing. An enzyme inhibitor (many azole antifungals, clarithromycin, amiodarone, grapefruit juice) slows metabolism, so partner drug levels climb toward toxicity.

The first-pass effect is the reason a route change is never a straight swap. Anything absorbed from the stomach or intestine drains into the portal vein and is carried through the liver before it reaches the rest of the body. The liver may destroy most of the dose on that first trip. Oral morphine is the textbook case: roughly three times the IV dose is needed by mouth for the same effect. Routes that bypass the portal circulation — IV, sublingual, transdermal, rectal (partially), inhaled — escape the first pass, which is exactly why nitroglycerin is placed under the tongue and not swallowed.

Excretion, half-life, and steady state

Excretion removes the drug, overwhelmingly through the kidneys. Reduced renal function — from age, dehydration, or kidney disease — means drug accumulates, so renally cleared drugs are dose-adjusted to creatinine clearance or eGFR. Smaller routes matter for specific drugs: bile and feces (some antibiotics), lungs (inhaled anesthetics), and breast milk, which is trivial in quantity but never trivial for the nursing infant.

The single most useful number in kinetics is the half-life (t½): the time for plasma concentration to fall by half. Half-life sets the dosing interval, and it drives two rules worth memorizing. It takes about four to five half-lives to reach steady state — the plateau where the amount going in equals the amount coming out, and where the drug is finally doing its full job. It also takes about four to five half-lives to clear a drug after the last dose. That is why a loading dose exists: when a patient cannot wait days for steady state, a large first dose fills the tank immediately, and smaller maintenance doses keep it there.

  • Bioavailability is the fraction reaching circulation intact; IV is 100%, oral is always less.

Section 3Pharmacodynamics — what the drug does to the body

Receptors: the lock and the key

Pharmacodynamics is the other half of the story: once the drug arrives, what does it actually do? Almost always, it binds something. Most drugs act on a receptor — a protein, usually on a cell membrane, that the body built for its own signaling molecule. Drugs do not invent new functions. They borrow existing ones, turning the body's own switches up or down. Albuterol works because your lungs already have beta2 receptors waiting for epinephrine; morphine works because your nervous system already has mu receptors waiting for endorphins.

Two properties describe the fit. Affinity is how tightly a drug binds its receptor. Intrinsic activity is whether binding actually triggers a response. That pair generates the entire vocabulary below — and it explains selectivity, too. A drug is called selective when it prefers one receptor subtype, but selectivity is dose-dependent and never absolute: metoprolol is beta1-selective at ordinary doses, which is why it is the safer choice if a patient with asthma must take a beta blocker at all, but at higher doses it spills onto the beta2 receptors in the airway and can trigger bronchospasm anyway. Nonselective agents such as propranolol block beta2 from the first dose and are avoided in reactive airway disease.

Agonists, antagonists, and everything between

An agonist binds and activates — it mimics the body's own messenger and turns the response on. An antagonist binds and blocks — it has affinity but no intrinsic activity, so it occupies the receptor and prevents the natural messenger (or another drug) from acting. Antagonists have no effect of their own; their entire effect is the absence of someone else's. A partial agonist binds and activates only weakly, producing a submaximal response no matter how high the dose — a ceiling that can be a safety feature.

Dose–response, potency, efficacy, and the therapeutic index

Plot dose against effect and you get the dose–response curve: little happens at the bottom (below threshold), the response rises steeply through the useful middle, then flattens into a plateau where the receptors are saturated and more drug buys no more effect — only more toxicity. That plateau is why "the pain is still there, give more" eventually stops being pharmacology and starts being harm.

Two words students routinely swap. Potency is how much drug it takes to produce a given effect — a more potent drug works at a smaller dose. Efficacy is the maximum effect the drug can produce, no matter the dose. Efficacy is what you want; potency is only a milligram number. Hydromorphone is more potent than morphine (fewer milligrams needed) but not more efficacious. Meanwhile ibuprofen has a hard analgesic ceiling that no dose can escape: for severe pain, an opioid's greater efficacy is the point.

The therapeutic index (TI) compares the dose that poisons to the dose that helps — TD50/ED50, the dose toxic to half the population divided by the dose effective in half of it. A high TI means a wide, forgiving margin. A low (narrow) therapeutic index means the effective dose and the toxic dose are uncomfortably close, and these are the drugs that get serum levels drawn, get double-checked, and land patients in the ED. Learn the list: warfarin, digoxin, lithium, phenytoin, levothyroxine, theophylline, carbamazepine, vancomycin, aminoglycosides (gentamicin), and insulin. The therapeutic range is the concentration window between "not working" and "poisoning" — and for these drugs, that window is a canyon you can fall out of in either direction.

Digoxin is the narrow-index drug that teaches the lesson. Therapeutic range is roughly 0.5–2 ng/mL, and toxicity looks like anorexia, nausea, visual halos or yellow-green vision, confusion, and bradycardia or new dysrhythmias. Now add the trap: hypokalemia potentiates digoxin toxicity, and hypokalemia is exactly what furosemide causes — and the two are prescribed together constantly. Check the apical pulse for a full minute, hold for a rate under 60, and read the potassium as part of the digoxin assessment.

Key points
  • Drugs act by binding receptors and turning the body's own signals up or down; affinity is the fit, intrinsic activity is whether it fires.
  • Agonist activates, antagonist blocks (the antidote mechanism), partial agonist activates weakly with a ceiling.
  • Potency = how many milligrams; efficacy = the maximum effect possible. Efficacy is what the patient feels.
  • A narrow therapeutic index drug — warfarin, digoxin, lithium, phenytoin, insulin — needs levels, monitoring, and double-checks.

Section 4Safe medication administration

The rights of medication administration

Everything above is theory until you are standing at a bedside with a cup in your hand. The rights of medication administration are the checklist that turns knowledge into safety. The traditional five are right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, right time, and most programs and employers add several more: right documentation, right reason (indication), right response, right to refuse, and right education. Verify them at three points — when you pull the medication, when you prepare it, and again at the bedside before it goes in.

Two of these deserve emphasis because they are the ones most often skipped. Right patient means two identifiers — name and date of birth against the armband, never the room number, and never the patient's nod when you say the wrong name aloud. Right response means the job is not finished when the drug is given: you gave metoprolol, so you recheck the heart rate; you gave morphine, so you reassess pain and respiratory rate and sedation; you gave insulin, so you know when the glucose will bottom out. Documentation comes after administration, never before — charting a drug you have not yet given is how a dose gets given twice.

Routes

The route decides speed, completeness, and reversibility. The single most important safety principle in the table below is this: once it is in, you cannot take it back, and the faster the route, the more absolutely that is true. An oral overdose can sometimes be intercepted; an IV push cannot.

Orders and error prevention

A complete medication order names the patient, the date and time, the drug, the dose, the route, the frequency, and the prescriber's signature. If any element is missing, ambiguous, or simply does not make sense for this patient, the order is not valid and you do not give it — you clarify it. "The prescriber wrote it" has never been a defense; the nurse who administers is independently accountable for the drug.

High-alert medications are those that cause disproportionate harm when they go wrong: insulin, anticoagulants (heparin, warfarin), opioids, concentrated electrolytes (especially potassium chloride), chemotherapy, neuromuscular blockers, and sedatives. They earn an independent double-check — a second nurse who verifies the drug, dose, pump settings, and patient without being told what you already calculated. And a permanent rule: concentrated potassium chloride is never given IV push, ever. It must be diluted and infused.

Then there is how the numbers are written. Use a leading zero (0.5 mg, not .5 mg — a missed decimal point becomes a tenfold overdose) and never a trailing zero (5 mg, not 5.0 mg — which reads as 50). Spell out "units" rather than writing "U," which is misread as a zero. Avoid the abbreviations on your facility's do-not-use list. And know your look-alike/sound-alike (LASA) pairs: hydralazine and hydroxyzine, celecoxib and citalopram, metformin and metronidazole, lamotrigine and lamivudine, and — the pair that reaches the ICU — morphine and hydromorphone. Read the label three times — pulling it, preparing it, and returning or discarding it.

Most errors are interruption errors. The research is consistent and unglamorous: the more times a nurse is interrupted while preparing medications, the more errors occur. That is the entire reason for "do not disturb" vests, quiet zones, and no-interruption med passes. Protecting your own attention during a med pass is not rudeness — it is a clinical intervention with an evidence base.

  • The rights — patient, drug, dose, route, time, plus documentation, reason, response, refusal, education — are checked three times, and right patient means two identifiers.
  • Route determines onset and reversibility; IV is immediate and cannot be undone; never crush ER or enteric-coated tablets.
  • High-alert drugs (insulin, anticoagulants, opioids, concentrated KCl, chemo) need independent double-checks; KCl is never IV push.
  • Use a leading zero, never a trailing zero; spell out "units"; know your LASA pairs.
  • An unclear or incomplete order is clarified, not given — the administering nurse is independently accountable.

Section 5Herbal supplements & interactions

Supplements are drugs, whatever the label says

Roughly one in five adults takes an herbal product, and the majority never mention it to a clinician — because in their minds a supplement is food, not medicine. That belief is the hazard. A dietary supplement in the United States is regulated under the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA) of 1994 as a food, not a drug. It requires no pre-market proof of safety or efficacy, and the FDA can act only after harm appears. Nobody verified the potency, the purity, or the contents of the bottle in your patient's bag.

Meanwhile, the pharmacology is entirely real. Digoxin comes from foxglove, aspirin from willow bark, morphine from the poppy, atropine from belladonna, and paclitaxel from the yew tree. Plants are chemists. "Natural" has never meant safe — hemlock is natural — and a compound potent enough to help is by definition potent enough to interact.

The interactions that actually matter

Herbal interactions cluster into three predictable mechanisms, which means you can reason instead of memorize. First, CYP450 and P-glycoprotein effects: St. John's wort is a powerful inducer and can wreck a drug regimen by driving levels down. Second, bleeding risk: the products with antiplatelet activity stack on top of anticoagulants, antiplatelets, and NSAIDs — the memorable cluster is the four G's plus feverfew (garlic, ginger, ginkgo, ginseng, feverfew). Third, additive pharmacology: kava and valerian pile onto CNS depressants; ginseng pushes glucose down on top of antidiabetics.

How to ask

The interview technique changes the answer. "Are you taking any medications?" reliably gets a "no" from a patient with three supplement bottles at home, because they do not classify them as medications. Ask instead: "What do you take that you buy without a prescription — vitamins, herbs, teas, powders, protein shakes, anything from the health-food aisle, anything a family member recommends?" Then ask them to bring in the actual bottles. Document each one on the medication list. And teach without judgment: the goal is not to talk the patient out of a supplement, it is to keep them from a bleed, a rejected transplant, or a serotonin crisis they never saw coming. Two teaching points earn their place in nearly every discharge: tell every prescriber about every supplement, and stop most herbal products one to two weeks before surgery.

The purse check is a nursing intervention. The medication reconciliation that catches a supplement is worth more than the one that perfectly lists eight prescriptions and misses the St. John's wort quietly driving the transplant patient's cyclosporine to zero. Ask about the bottles nobody wrote a prescription for — that is where the interaction is hiding.

Key points
  • Supplements are regulated as food, not drugs — no pre-market proof of safety, efficacy, potency, or purity.
  • St. John's wort is the highest-stakes common supplement: a CYP3A4/P-gp inducer that drives warfarin, contraceptives, cyclosporine, and antiretrovirals to failure, plus serotonin syndrome risk.
  • The bleeding cluster — garlic, ginger, ginkgo, ginseng, feverfew — stacks with anticoagulants, antiplatelets, and NSAIDs; stop 1–2 weeks before surgery.
  • Kava and black cohosh are hepatotoxic; valerian and kava add to CNS depressants; echinacea is a problem in immunosuppression.
  • Ask specifically about non-prescription products and put every one on the medication list.

The chapter in one breath

  • Every drug has a chemical, generic, and trade name; nurses work in generic names, and stems like -olol and -pril announce the class on sight.
  • A class shares a mechanism, so it shares effects and nursing implications — learn the prototype and you own the family.
  • Pharmacokinetics (ADME) is what the body does to the drug; bioavailability, protein binding, and the first-pass effect explain why the same dose behaves differently in different patients.
  • Half-life drives the dosing interval: about 4–5 half-lives to reach steady state and 4–5 to clear; a loading dose skips the wait.
  • Pharmacodynamics is what the drug does to the body: agonists activate, antagonists block (the antidote mechanism), partial agonists have a ceiling.
  • Potency is milligrams, efficacy is the maximum effect; a narrow therapeutic index (warfarin, digoxin, lithium, phenytoin, insulin) demands levels and double-checks.
  • Safe administration is the rights, two identifiers, the right route, an independent double-check for high-alert drugs, and never IV push potassium.
  • Herbal supplements are unregulated drugs: St. John's wort induces CYP3A4 and wrecks regimens, the four G's plus feverfew bleed, kava and black cohosh injure the liver — so ask about the bottles no one prescribed.

A drug that binds a receptor and activates it, mimicking the body's own messenger.
Antagonist
A drug that binds a receptor and blocks it, producing no effect of its own; the mechanism behind antidotes such as naloxone.
Bioavailability
The fraction of an administered dose that reaches the systemic circulation intact; 100% for IV, always less for oral.
Distribution
Movement of a drug from the blood into tissues; shaped by perfusion, protein binding, and barriers such as the blood–brain barrier and placenta.
Efficacy
The maximum effect a drug can produce regardless of dose — what the drug is capable of.
Excretion
Removal of drug and metabolites from the body, chiefly by the kidneys.
First-pass effect
Metabolism of an orally absorbed drug by the liver before it reaches systemic circulation; bypassed by IV, sublingual, and transdermal routes.
Half-life (t½)
The time required for plasma drug concentration to fall by 50%; sets the dosing interval and predicts steady state and clearance.
High-alert medication
A drug that causes disproportionate harm when used in error — insulin, anticoagulants, opioids, concentrated electrolytes, chemotherapy — warranting an independent double-check.
Loading dose
A large initial dose given to reach a therapeutic concentration quickly, rather than waiting four to five half-lives.
Metabolism (biotransformation)
Chemical alteration of a drug, mostly by hepatic CYP450 enzymes; usually inactivates the drug, but activates a prodrug.
Pharmacodynamics
What the drug does to the body — receptor binding, mechanism of action, and the dose–response relationship.
Pharmacokinetics
What the body does to the drug — absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion.
Potency
The amount of drug required to produce a given effect; a more potent drug works at a lower dose. Not the same as efficacy.
Prodrug
An inactive drug that must be metabolized into its active form — codeine converted to morphine, for example.
Prototype drug
The representative, well-studied member of a class used as the template for the rest of the family.
Side effect
A predictable, dose-related effect other than the intended one; often tolerable, sometimes the reason a patient quits the drug.
Steady state
The plateau at which drug entering the body equals drug leaving it; reached after roughly four to five half-lives of regular dosing.
Therapeutic index
The ratio of the toxic dose to the effective dose; a low (narrow) index means a dangerously small margin for error.
Trough level
The lowest drug concentration in a dosing interval, drawn about 30 minutes before the next dose; the level that reveals accumulation.

Check yourself

1. A patient's home list reads Tylenol, Percocet, and NyQuil. Why does a nurse translate that list into generic names before giving anything?
All three contain acetaminophen — the patient is triple-dosing one drug. Trade names and combination products hide duplicates. Only the generic name reveals that the same drug appears three times, risking a hepatotoxic total daily dose.
2. You have never seen the drug "carvedilol" before. What are your first two questions, and what does the name already tell you?
"What class is it?" and "what is the prototype?" — the -olol stem says beta blocker, prototype metoprolol. From the class you can predict lower heart rate and blood pressure, the need to hold for bradycardia or hypotension, and the rule against abrupt discontinuation.
3. Distinguish a therapeutic classification from a pharmacologic classification, using one drug.
Therapeutic = what it treats; pharmacologic = how it works. Metoprolol is therapeutically an antihypertensive/antianginal and pharmacologically a beta1-selective adrenergic blocker. Every drug carries both labels at once.
4. Name the four pharmacokinetic processes and define bioavailability.
Absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion (ADME). Bioavailability is the fraction of a dose that reaches systemic circulation intact. IV is 100% bioavailable by definition; oral is always less, because of incomplete absorption and the first-pass effect.
5. A malnourished patient with an albumin of 2.1 g/dL is started on a usual dose of a highly protein-bound drug. What is the risk and why?
Toxicity — only the free (unbound) drug is active, and with less albumin to bind it, a larger free fraction circulates. The "normal" dose behaves like a bigger one. Expect exaggerated effects and monitor closely.
6. Explain the first-pass effect and give one clinical consequence.
Orally absorbed drug travels through the portal vein to the liver and is partly metabolized before reaching the body. Consequence: oral doses must be larger — oral morphine is roughly three times the IV dose. Routes that bypass the liver first pass (sublingual, transdermal, IV) act faster and need less drug, which is why nitroglycerin goes under the tongue.
7. A drug has a half-life of 12 hours. Roughly how long until steady state, and how long until it is cleared after the last dose? What if the patient cannot wait?
About 4–5 half-lives each way — roughly 48–60 hours to steady state, and the same to clear. If the patient needs a therapeutic level now, a loading dose fills the tank immediately and maintenance doses hold it there.
8. Contrast an agonist, an antagonist, and a partial agonist, and explain why naloxone requires ongoing monitoring after it works.
Agonist binds and activates; antagonist binds and blocks with no effect of its own; partial agonist activates weakly and plateaus. Naloxone is a mu antagonist with a shorter duration than most opioids — when it wears off, opioid still on board can re-bind and the patient can re-sedate and stop breathing. Keep monitoring; repeat dosing may be needed.
9. Hydromorphone is more potent than morphine. Does that make it a better analgesic? Explain using potency and efficacy.
No — potency only means fewer milligrams are needed; efficacy is the maximum effect achievable. Both are full mu agonists with comparable efficacy. Potency matters for dosing math and for the danger of confusing the two drugs; efficacy is what determines how much pain can be relieved.
10. Define therapeutic index, and explain why a patient on both furosemide and digoxin needs a potassium check.
Therapeutic index compares the toxic dose to the effective dose; a narrow index means little margin for error — and digoxin's is narrow (about 0.5–2 ng/mL). Furosemide causes hypokalemia, and hypokalemia potentiates digoxin toxicity. Watch for anorexia, nausea, visual halos or yellow-green vision, confusion, and bradycardia; check the apical pulse for a full minute and hold for a rate under 60.
11. An order reads "Insulin 10.0 U SubQ now." Name three problems and state what you do.
The trailing zero can be read as 100; "U" can be misread as a zero (making it 100 units); and the order lacks the insulin type. Insulin is a high-alert drug: clarify the order with the prescriber, have it rewritten as "10 units" with the specific insulin named, and obtain an independent double-check before administering.
12. A transplant patient on cyclosporine mentions she started St. John's wort for her mood. Why is this an emergency-level finding, and what else would you screen for?
St. John's wort is a potent CYP3A4 and P-glycoprotein inducer — it accelerates cyclosporine metabolism, drops the level below therapeutic, and can cause graft rejection. Notify the prescriber immediately. Also screen for warfarin (falling INR), oral contraceptives (unintended pregnancy), antiretrovirals, digoxin, and anticonvulsants — and for SSRIs or triptans, where the combination risks serotonin syndrome.
