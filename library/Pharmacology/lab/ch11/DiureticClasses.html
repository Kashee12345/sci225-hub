Diuretics: Where They Work, What They Do to Potassium

Every diuretic is defined by one spot on the nephron. That spot sets how much sodium the drug can dump — and which way the patient's potassium moves. Tap a site on the tubule, or pick a class.

One tubule, four doorsFiltrate travels the nephron in order: proximal tubule → descending limb → thick ascending limb → distal tubule → collecting duct. Each segment reclaims a different share of the filtered sodium, so a drug's potency is decided before it is ever dosed — by where on that path it lands.
The earlier and the busier the segment, the more sodium a drug can dump. The thick ascending limb reabsorbs the biggest slice, so the drug that blocks it is the strongest — and the collecting duct handles the smallest, so the drug that blocks it is the weakest.

Three of the four waste potassium. One hoards it. Loop, thiazide and osmotic all drive K⁺ down; only the potassium-sparing class drives it up. That single split decides whether you reach for a potassium supplement — or lock it away.
Loop and thiazide diuretics lower potassium by the same trick: they let extra sodium reach the collecting duct, where it is traded for potassium that then leaves in the urine. Potassium-sparing drugs work at that very trade — which is exactly why they are so often added to a loop or thiazide rather than used alone.
