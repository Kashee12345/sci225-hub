Losing Fluid, and Putting It Back

Plasma sodium is a ratio — sodium divided by the water it is dissolved in. Sweat carries away both, but sweat is more dilute than plasma, so the ratio starts moving the moment you begin. Set how much you sweat and how salty it is, then choose what you drink to replace it. Replace too little and sodium climbs. Replace the volume with plain water and it falls. Two errors, two opposite failures — watch the same body travel toward each.

The two ledgers underneath

Body mass change is the water ledger. Plasma sodium is set by both ledgers together — which is why the scale alone cannot tell the two failures apart.

This is a teaching model of a ratio, not a clinical tool and not advice for any person. It has no kidneys that adjust, no thirst that intervenes, and no hormonal feedback — it simply carries your numbers forward, which is what makes the mechanism visible and also why it overstates what a real body would do. Nothing here prescribes a fluid volume, a sodium amount, or a product for anyone. Symptomatic hyponatremia is a medical emergency handled by clinicians using a measured blood sodium, not something to manage from a chart. Body-mass change is used only as a short-term measure of fluid; it says nothing about body size, composition, or health, and no body here is better or worse than another.
