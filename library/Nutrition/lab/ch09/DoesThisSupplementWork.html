Does This Supplement Work?

Two questions get collapsed into one on every supplement label. How sure are we that it does anything? is a question about evidence. How much does it do? is a question about size. An aid can score high on one and low on the other, and the most expensive mistakes live in that gap. Map twenty-two common ergogenic aids on both axes, then translate the effect into a number you can actually feel.

The horizontal axis is strength of evidence supporting a performance benefit — not strength of evidence overall. A substance can sit far left because it has been studied hard and come back consistently null, or because almost nobody has studied it. Those are completely different situations and the axis cannot tell them apart, so each entry says which it is.

This page describes published evidence. It does not recommend anything to anyone. Effect sizes are approximate central estimates pooled across heterogeneous trials in mostly healthy, mostly trained adults — individual responses vary widely, and several aids on this map have well-documented non-responders. Doses are reported as what the trials administered, not as instructions. Whether any supplement is appropriate for a particular person depends on medical history, medications, pregnancy or lactation, kidney and liver function, age, and competitive testing status — that is a conversation with a qualified clinician or sports dietitian, not a number on a chart. Nutrient supplements that correct a diagnosed deficiency are a clinical decision made on laboratory results, not a performance choice. Nothing here labels any substance, food, or eating pattern as good or bad.
