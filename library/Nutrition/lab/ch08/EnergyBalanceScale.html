The Energy Balance Scale

Energy balance is the first law of thermodynamics applied to a body: energy taken in, minus energy spent, equals energy stored. It is a description of physics — not a verdict on a person, a body, or a meal. The interesting part is that the two pans are not independent. Move any slider and watch both sides respond.

Intake against expenditure

TEE = RMR + TEF + NEAT + EAT. Total energy expenditure is four things, not one: what it costs to stay alive at rest, what it costs to process the food itself, all the movement you never planned, and the movement you did. TEF is not a slider because you do not set it — it is a percentage of whatever you eat, so it moves the moment intake moves. That single coupling is why the pans can never be adjusted one at a time.
These are illustrative demonstrations of physiology built from round numbers. None of them is a plan, a target, or a recommendation for any particular person.
Body weight is a crude readout of all this. The scale in a bathroom cannot separate fat from lean tissue, from glycogen (stored with roughly 3 g of water per gram), from gut contents, from hydration — so day-to-day swings of 1–2 kg are mostly water, not stored energy. BMI is weaker still: it is mass divided by height squared, a nineteenth-century population ratio that says nothing about tissue type, distribution, fitness, or health, and was never designed to assess an individual. strong evidence Everything on this page is a teaching model of average physiology — not medical or dietary advice, and no substitute for an assessment by a qualified clinician.
