Food Security & Sustainability

Every other chapter in this book asks what a nutrient does inside a body. This one asks a harder question: whether the food carrying that nutrient ever reaches the body at all. Food security is the difference between knowing what a person should eat and their being able to eat it — and it is shaped by income, transportation, time, geography, harvest, war, and price far more than by knowledge or willpower. At the same time, the system that grows and moves that food is the single largest human pressure on land, fresh water, and the climate, which means the way we feed eight billion people now determines how easily we can feed them later. This chapter looks at both ends of that problem: who is missing food today, and what the food system costs to run.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Define food security and its four dimensions, and describe the food security continuum used to measure it.
  • Summarize the prevalence and documented health consequences of food insecurity across the lifespan.
  • Distinguish stunting, wasting, and underweight, and explain why the first 1,000 days matter so much.
  • Describe the major micronutrient deficiencies of global public health importance, with their RDAs and deficiency syndromes.
  • Trace a food from production to plate and identify where nutrients, food, and money are lost along the way.
  • Compare the environmental footprint of major food groups and explain why what you eat outweighs how far it travelled.
  • Explain how fortification, biofortification, and food assistance programs work, and which have strong evidence behind them.
  • Separate strong evidence from preliminary evidence when evaluating claims about diets and sustainability.

Section 1Food security and insecurity

What food security actually means

Food security exists when all people, at all times, have physical, social, and economic access to sufficient, safe, and nutritious food that meets their dietary needs and food preferences for an active and healthy life. That definition, from the 1996 World Food Summit, is worth reading slowly, because every clause is doing work. All times rules out a household that eats well three weeks a month and runs out in the fourth. Economic access makes clear that food sitting on a shelf a person cannot afford does not count. Food preferences acknowledges that a culturally unacceptable food is not a real option. And safe reminds us that contaminated food is not food.

Researchers break the concept into four dimensions, and a household can fail on any one of them independently. Availability asks whether enough food physically exists in the area. Access asks whether this household can obtain it — money, transportation, distance, and time all live here. Utilization asks whether the body can actually use the food, which brings in food safety, clean water, cooking facilities, and whether infection or disease is stealing nutrients. Stability asks whether the other three hold up over time rather than collapsing at the end of the month or the end of a growing season.

Measuring it: a continuum, not a switch

Food insecurity is not a synonym for hunger, and it is not a yes-or-no state. In the United States it is measured with a household survey module that sorts households along a continuum — 18 items for households with children, 10 for those without. High food security means no reported problems. Marginal food security means anxiety about food supply but no real change in diet or intake. Low food security means reduced quality, variety, or desirability of the diet, with little or no reduction in the amount eaten. Very low food security means eating patterns were actually disrupted and intake reduced — skipped meals, smaller meals, whole days without eating — because there was not enough money for food. The first two categories are counted as food secure; the last two are counted as food insecure.

Internationally, the parallel instrument is the Food Insecurity Experience Scale (FIES), an eight-question module used by the Food and Agriculture Organization to compare countries. Recent global estimates put roughly 2.3 billion people at moderate or severe food insecurity, and around 700–800 million people — close to one in eleven — as chronically undernourished, meaning habitual intake below the energy required for a normal active life. In the United States, roughly one in eight households reports food insecurity in a given year, with about one in twenty reaching very low food security. These figures shift year to year with prices, employment, and the generosity of assistance programs, so always check the current release rather than quoting a number from memory.

What food insecurity does to health

The evidence linking food insecurity to worse health is strong and consistent, though most of it is observational, so causal direction runs both ways: illness makes households poorer, and poverty makes households sicker. In children, food insecurity is associated with iron deficiency, more frequent illness, poorer school performance, and higher rates of anxiety and behavioral difficulty. In adults, it is associated with higher rates of type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and depression, and with markedly worse control of chronic disease — a person rationing insulin and food in the same month has a predictable outcome. Older adults face an added layer, because fixed incomes, limited mobility, and medication costs collide.

One finding surprises students: food insecurity is often associated with higher body weight in some populations, particularly among women. This is not a paradox once you look at the mechanics rather than reaching for a judgment. Energy-dense, shelf-stable foods cost less per calorie and waste less; cycles of scarcity and relative plenty change eating patterns; chronic stress alters appetite regulation and sleep; and cooking requires time, equipment, and utilities that may not be there. The physiology is ordinary. The environment is the variable. Describing it any other way — as a failure of discipline or of taste — is both unkind and factually wrong.

Two questions find most of it. The validated Hunger Vital Sign screener asks whether, in the past 12 months, "we worried whether our food would run out before we got money to buy more" and whether "the food we bought just didn't last and we didn't have money to get more." An affirmative or "sometimes true" answer to either flags likely household food insecurity. It takes fifteen seconds, and it works far better than guessing from appearance or address.

Key points
  • Food security = reliable access to enough safe, nutritious, acceptable food; its four dimensions are availability, access, utilization, and stability.
  • Food insecurity is a continuum: high, marginal, low, and very low food security — the last two count as food insecure.
  • Roughly one in eight U.S. households and about 2.3 billion people globally experience some level of food insecurity.
  • Food insecurity tracks with worse chronic disease control, poorer child development, and higher rates of depression; associations with higher body weight reflect cost and access, not character.

Section 2Global malnutrition

Malnutrition means more than "too little"

Malnutrition is any deficiency, excess, or imbalance in energy or nutrient intake. It therefore covers three overlapping problems: undernutrition (not enough energy or protein), micronutrient deficiency (not enough of a specific vitamin or mineral), and diet-related chronic disease. Many countries now carry two of these at once in the same communities and sometimes in the same household — a stunted child and an adult with type 2 diabetes under one roof. That pattern is called the double burden of malnutrition, and it exists because cheap energy became widely available faster than cheap nutrient density did.

Stunting, wasting, and the first 1,000 days

Child undernutrition is classified by comparing a child to the WHO Child Growth Standards, using how many standard deviations (a z-score) they sit from the median. Stunting is low height-for-age below −2 SD: it reflects chronic, cumulative deprivation and is the marker of long-run damage. Wasting is low weight-for-height below −2 SD: it reflects acute, recent loss and carries the highest near-term mortality risk. Underweight is low weight-for-age and mixes the two, which is why it has largely been replaced by the more specific measures. In the field, mid-upper arm circumference (MUAC) is used as a fast screening measure in children 6–59 months, and the presence of bilateral pitting edema is by itself sufficient to classify severe acute malnutrition regardless of any measurement.

Roughly 150 million children under five are stunted worldwide and about 45 million are wasted. The reason stunting gets so much attention is timing. The first 1,000 days — from conception to a child's second birthday — is the window in which the brain, the immune system, and linear growth are laid down. Deficits accumulated in that window are only partially recoverable afterward, and long-term cohort studies consistently associate them with reduced school attainment and lower adult earnings — associations that are strong and reproducible, though the observational designs cannot fully separate early nutrition from the poverty that usually accompanies it. It is one of the few places in nutrition where the phrase "critical period" is literally true rather than rhetorical.

The two classic severe syndromes are still worth knowing by name. Marasmus is severe wasting from grossly inadequate energy intake: the child is emaciated, with loss of subcutaneous fat and muscle, and no edema. Kwashiorkor presents with bilateral pitting edema, an enlarged fatty liver, and characteristic skin and hair changes. Kwashiorkor was historically attributed to protein deficiency with adequate energy, and that framing persists in many textbooks, but the mechanism is not settled — oxidative stress, aflatoxin exposure, and gut microbiome differences have all been implicated. Treat the protein-deficiency story as a useful teaching heuristic, not an established cause. Both syndromes are treated under medical supervision, and reintroducing food carries its own danger: refeeding syndrome, in which the shift back to carbohydrate metabolism drives phosphate, potassium, and magnesium into cells and can precipitate cardiac and neurologic collapse. This is a hospital-managed problem, not something to improvise.

Hidden hunger: micronutrient deficiency

A person can eat enough calories and still be badly deficient, which is why micronutrient deficiency is called hidden hunger. Four deficiencies dominate global public health. Two more deserve mention because they are the historical face of nutritional disease. Thiamin deficiency causes beriberi — "wet" beriberi with high-output heart failure and edema, "dry" beriberi with peripheral neuropathy — and, in the context of chronic alcohol use, Wernicke encephalopathy. Niacin deficiency causes pellagra, remembered as the three Ds: dermatitis in sun-exposed skin, diarrhea, and dementia. Both were epidemic in populations dependent on a single staple grain, though by different routes: beriberi followed polished (milled) rice, which loses the thiamin-rich bran, while pellagra followed maize-based diets, in which niacin is bound in a form the body cannot absorb unless the grain is treated with alkali as in traditional nixtamalization. Both largely vanished from industrialized countries once staple grains were enriched — a point we return to in Section 5.

Why therapeutic foods are fatty. Ready-to-use therapeutic food (RUTF), the peanut-paste product used to treat severe acute malnutrition at home, is deliberately built around fat. The reason is arithmetic: fat yields 9 kcal per gram while carbohydrate and protein each yield 4 kcal per gram (alcohol yields 7, and contributes nothing here). Packing energy into fat lets a small 92-gram sachet deliver about 500 kcal, needs no water to prepare — which matters where water is unsafe — and resists spoilage without refrigeration.

Key points
  • Malnutrition spans undernutrition, micronutrient deficiency, and diet-related chronic disease; many countries carry a double burden.
  • Stunting (height-for-age) marks chronic deprivation; wasting (weight-for-height) marks acute risk; MUAC and bilateral edema screen for severe acute malnutrition.
  • The first 1,000 days is a genuine critical window — deficits there are only partly recoverable.
  • Iron, vitamin A, iodine, zinc, and folate are the deficiencies of greatest global public health importance; RDAs differ by age, sex, and pregnancy.

Section 3The food system: from production to plate

The stages, and what leaks out of each

A food system is everything involved in getting food from the ground to a person and dealing with what is left over: inputs (seed, water, fertilizer, fuel, labor), production, post-harvest handling and storage, processing, distribution and transport, retail, preparation and consumption, and waste. Nutrition students often study only the last two stages, which is a mistake — the price, safety, and nutrient content of what lands on a plate were largely determined upstream.

Loss is enormous and it happens at both ends. Roughly 13 percent of food produced is lost between harvest and retail, mostly to inadequate storage, cooling, and transport — a problem of infrastructure, concentrated in lower-income settings. A further 19 percent or so is wasted at retail, food service, and household level, with households the single largest slice, concentrated in higher-income settings. Combined, close to a third of the food grown is never eaten, along with the land, water, fuel, and labor that produced it.

Processing: not one thing

"Processed" is one of the least useful words in nutrition because it collapses opposite things. Pasteurizing milk, freezing peas within hours of picking, canning tomatoes, drying beans, and adding folic acid to flour are all processing, and each of them makes food safer, cheaper, more available, or more nutritious. Freezing in particular often preserves vitamin C and folate better than a fresh product that spent days in transit and storage. Refining, by contrast, removes: milling a whole grain strips the bran and germ, taking most of the fiber, magnesium, vitamin E, and much of the B-vitamin and iron content with them.

The current research focus is on ultra-processed foods, formulations built largely from industrial ingredients and additives rather than recognizable whole foods. Here it is important to separate evidence tiers. Large observational cohorts consistently associate higher ultra-processed intake with higher rates of cardiometabolic disease — that association is strong and reproducible, but observational, and confounded by income, smoking, and overall diet. Direct experimental evidence is thin: the best-known controlled feeding trial enrolled about twenty adults for a few weeks. The classification itself is also contested, since it groups mass-produced bread with candy. Treat the mechanism as preliminary, and be skeptical of anyone who states it as settled in either direction.

The food environment

Where food is sold determines what gets eaten as reliably as any nutrition label. Low-access areas — often called food deserts — are neighborhoods where the nearest full grocery store is far enough away that a household without a car realistically shops at a convenience store. Many communities also experience the opposite density problem, a high concentration of inexpensive prepared food and few fresh options. Some scholars prefer the term food apartheid to emphasize that these patterns follow historical policy decisions about zoning, lending, and transit rather than natural geography. Layer on price — energy-dense foods are consistently cheaper per calorie than nutrient-dense ones — and the shape of a household's diet becomes largely predictable from its zip code and income.

Food safety belongs here too, since it is the utilization dimension of food security made concrete. The WHO estimates roughly 600 million foodborne illnesses and 420,000 deaths per year worldwide. Most of what prevents them is unglamorous: handwashing, separating raw and ready-to-eat foods, cooking to adequate internal temperature, and keeping food out of the 40–140 °F (4–60 °C) range where bacterial growth is fastest.

Frozen is not a lesser food. Produce destined for freezing is usually picked ripe and blanched and frozen within hours, which halts the enzymatic and oxidative losses that continue in a "fresh" item during days of shipping and storage. For vitamin C and folate, frozen vegetables frequently measure equal to or higher than their fresh counterparts at the point of eating. Frozen and canned foods are also cheaper, keep longer, and waste less — which makes them a food security tool, not a compromise.

Key points
  • A food system runs from inputs through production, storage, processing, distribution, retail, consumption, and waste.
  • About 13 percent of food is lost before retail and roughly 19 percent wasted after — close to a third of all food grown.
  • Processing includes both nutrient-preserving steps (freezing, canning, fortification) and nutrient-removing ones (refining); the word alone tells you nothing.
  • Observational evidence linking ultra-processed foods to disease is strong; the causal mechanism remains preliminary.

Section 4Sustainable eating and the footprint of food

The scale of the problem

Food production is the largest way humans touch the planet. Agriculture occupies roughly half of habitable land, accounts for about 70 percent of global freshwater withdrawals, and food systems as a whole generate somewhere near a quarter to a third of anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions. Agriculture is also the dominant driver of biodiversity loss and of deforestation. The implication is that a diet has an environmental cost the same way it has a nutritional profile, and the two do not always point in the same direction.

The most useful evidence base here is life-cycle assessment (LCA), which tallies emissions, land, and water across a product's whole chain. A landmark 2018 synthesis covering roughly 38,000 farms across 119 countries produced the ranking below. The numbers are approximate and vary widely by production system, so read them as orders of magnitude rather than precise values.

What actually moves the needle

Three findings from this literature are robust enough to rely on. First, the type of food dominates. The spread between the highest- and lowest-impact foods is on the order of fifty- to a hundredfold — beef sits near 100 kg CO2e per kg while peas sit near 1 — which is far larger than the (still substantial) spread between the best and worst producers of the same food. Second, transport is a minor term for most foods — on the order of 6 percent of food-related emissions overall, though the exact share depends on where the analysis draws its system boundary. "Food miles" is therefore a poor guide: a legume shipped across an ocean by sea generally beats local beef by a wide margin. The real exception is highly perishable produce moved by air, which is a small share of trade but carries an outsized footprint. Third, reducing waste is a free win, because wasted food carries every upstream cost with it and delivers nothing.

Comparing per kilogram of food can mislead when foods serve different purposes, so researchers often normalize per gram of protein or per 100 kcal. Doing so narrows some gaps and widens others, but it does not reverse the basic ordering: ruminant meat sits at the top, legumes and grains at the bottom. Where evidence is genuinely preliminary is the claim that particular grazing or "regenerative" systems sequester enough soil carbon to offset ruminant emissions. Some systems do sequester meaningful carbon, but soil carbon saturates, the measurements are difficult, and current evidence does not support treating it as a general offset.

Eating sustainably without moralizing

The most-cited reference scenario is the planetary health diet published by the EAT-Lancet Commission: a roughly 2,500 kcal pattern that is largely plants, with modest amounts of animal foods, designed to sit within estimated environmental limits while meeting nutrient needs. It is a modeling exercise for global averages, not a personal prescription, and its authors say so. Applied bluntly it can be unaffordable and culturally inappropriate, and it does not automatically supply enough iron, zinc, calcium, vitamin B12, or omega-3 fats for every individual without attention to food choices or supplementation.

A more honest framing is that sustainability is a matter of ratios and margins, not moral categories. Shifting some meals toward legumes, choosing poultry or fish where a ruminant would otherwise appear, using frozen and canned foods, buying imperfect produce, and simply throwing away less all produce measurable gains without declaring any food forbidden. Foods are not virtuous or sinful, and neither are the people eating them. Cost, time, culture, allergy, and appetite are legitimate constraints, and advice that ignores them is advice that will not be followed.

Skip the food miles, watch the food type. If you change one thing about how you evaluate a meal's footprint, change this: the question "where did it come from?" is far less informative than "what is it?" Sea freight adds very little per kilogram, while ruminant methane and land conversion add a great deal. Locally raised beef still outweighs imported lentils by well over an order of magnitude.

  • Food type matters far more than food miles; transport is around 6 percent of food emissions.
  • Ruminant meats sit at the high end of the footprint range and legumes, grains, and vegetables at the low end — a spread on the order of fifty- to a hundredfold.
  • Claims that regenerative grazing offsets ruminant emissions are preliminary and should not be treated as established.

Section 5Programs and solutions

Food assistance: the income layer

The dominant lever against food insecurity in high-income countries is not education. It is money. The largest U.S. program, the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP), provides an electronic benefit that households spend on groceries, and it reaches roughly 40 million people in a typical month. Because it works through purchasing power, its effects on food insecurity are relatively fast and well documented; when benefit levels rise, measured food insecurity falls, and when they lapse, it rises.

Other programs target specific windows of risk. WIC serves pregnant and postpartum people, infants, and children under five with a prescribed food package, breastfeeding support, and nutrition services — and it has among the strongest evidence bases of any nutrition program, with consistent associations with improved birth outcomes and better dietary quality. That evidence is largely observational rather than randomized — withholding a benefit for a trial is rarely ethical — so it carries some risk of selection effects, but the findings hold across many designs and decades. School meal programs feed children where they already are, and universal-eligibility approaches remove the stigma and paperwork that suppress participation. Emergency food — food banks and pantries — is the visible face of the response, but it was designed as a supplement for short crises and cannot substitute for stable income.

Fortification: the quiet giant

Fortification is the addition of nutrients to a food that did not originally contain them in that amount; enrichment specifically restores nutrients lost during processing. Both work through the food supply rather than through individual behavior, which is exactly why they succeed — no one has to remember anything, change what they like, or be able to afford anything extra. The public health record here is remarkable.

InterventionTarget deficiencyDocumented result

Two cautions keep fortification honest. First, it reaches only people who eat the fortified vehicle, so choosing the vehicle is a political and cultural decision as much as a technical one — fortifying wheat flour misses populations whose staple is rice, maize, or cassava. Second, more is not automatically better: nutrients have tolerable upper intake levels, and mandatory fortification plus voluntary fortification plus supplements can stack. Designing that ceiling correctly is the job of population-level policy, not individual dosing.

Community approaches and the limits of any one lever

Between national policy and the individual sits a large, creative middle layer: community gardens and urban farms, farmers-market incentive programs that double the value of federal benefits when spent on produce, campus and workplace pantries, gleaning and food-recovery networks that route edible surplus to people instead of landfills, mobile markets in low-access neighborhoods, and community-based management of acute malnutrition, which moved treatment of severe acute malnutrition out of hospitals and into homes using ready-to-use therapeutic food and dramatically improved survival and coverage.

What ties the effective interventions together is that they change the environment rather than lecturing the person inside it. Nutrition education has value — people deserve to understand their food — but knowledge is rarely the binding constraint. Income, price, distance, time, and safety usually are. The most useful posture for anyone working in this space is therefore to screen routinely, refer without judgment, know the local resources by name, and remember that a person skipping meals at the end of the month is responding rationally to their circumstances, not failing at nutrition.

Fortification is among the highest-leverage nutrition interventions ever deployed. Salt iodization alone is credited with protecting the cognitive development of a large share of a generation, at a cost measured in cents per person per year, without asking anyone to change a single habit. When you are tempted to think population nutrition means persuading individuals, remember that the biggest wins came from changing what was already in the bag.

Key points
  • Benefit programs like SNAP work through purchasing power; WIC and school meals target high-risk windows; food banks supplement rather than replace income.
  • Fortification and enrichment succeed because they require no behavior change — iodized salt, folic acid, vitamin D, and enriched flour are among public health's largest wins.
  • Biofortification breeds nutrients into the crop itself; evidence is strongest for orange-fleshed sweet potato and vitamin A.
  • Screen with the Hunger Vital Sign, refer without judgment, and target the environment rather than the individual's willpower.

The chapter in one breath

  • Food security means reliable access to enough safe, nutritious, acceptable food, and rests on four dimensions: availability, access, utilization, and stability.
  • Food insecurity is measured on a continuum (high, marginal, low, very low) and affects roughly one in eight U.S. households and about 2.3 billion people globally.
  • Its health consequences are real and well documented — worse chronic disease control, poorer child development, higher rates of depression — and its association with higher body weight reflects cost and access, not character.
  • Stunting marks chronic deprivation and wasting marks acute risk; the first 1,000 days is a genuine critical window, and iron, vitamin A, iodine, zinc, and folate drive most hidden hunger.
  • Energy yields set the arithmetic of therapeutic feeding: fat 9 kcal/g, carbohydrate and protein 4 kcal/g, alcohol 7 kcal/g.
  • The food system loses roughly a third of what it grows — about 13 percent before retail, 19 percent after — and "processed" describes both nutrient-preserving and nutrient-removing steps.
  • Environmentally, what you eat matters far more than how far it travelled; ruminant meats sit at the high end of the footprint range, legumes and grains at the low end.
  • Fortification, benefit programs, and community-level access outperform individual education, because they change the environment rather than the person's willpower.

Ready-to-use therapeutic food (RUTF)
An energy- and nutrient-dense paste, high in fat, used to treat severe acute malnutrition at home without added water or refrigeration.
Refeeding syndrome
Potentially fatal electrolyte and fluid shifts — notably falling phosphate, potassium, and magnesium — when nutrition is reintroduced to a severely malnourished person; managed medically.
Stunting
Height-for-age more than 2 standard deviations below the WHO growth standard median; a marker of chronic deprivation.
Wasting
Weight-for-height more than 2 standard deviations below the WHO growth standard median; a marker of acute deficit and high short-term mortality risk.

Check yourself

1. Name the four dimensions of food security and give an example of a household that fails on utilization while succeeding on availability and access.
Availability, access, utilization, and stability. A household with food in the house and money to buy more still fails on utilization if the water supply is contaminated, there is no working stove or refrigerator, or chronic diarrheal illness prevents the body from absorbing what is eaten.
2. A survey classifies a household as having low food security rather than very low. What is the practical difference?
Low food security means the quality, variety, or desirability of the diet was reduced, but the amount eaten was largely maintained; very low food security means eating patterns were actually disrupted and intake reduced — meals skipped or missed for lack of money. Both are counted as food insecure.
3. Explain why food insecurity is sometimes associated with higher body weight, without resorting to a judgment about the people involved.
Energy-dense, shelf-stable foods cost less per calorie and waste less; cycles of scarcity and relative plenty alter eating patterns; chronic stress affects appetite regulation and sleep; and cooking requires time, equipment, and utilities that may be unavailable. The physiology is ordinary — the environment is the variable.
4. Distinguish stunting from wasting. Which signals the more urgent short-term risk, and which signals the more lasting harm?
Stunting is low height-for-age (below −2 SD) and reflects chronic deprivation with lasting cognitive and economic consequences; wasting is low weight-for-height (below −2 SD) and reflects acute loss with the highest short-term mortality risk. Wasting is the emergency; stunting is the long shadow.
5. Why is the "first 1,000 days" singled out in global nutrition policy?
Because conception through a child's second birthday is the window in which brain development, immune development, and linear growth are established, and deficits accumulated then are only partially recoverable later. It is one of the few genuine critical periods in nutrition.
6. A 92-gram sachet of ready-to-use therapeutic food delivers about 500 kcal. Using the energy yields of the macronutrients, explain why the formulation is built around fat.
Fat yields 9 kcal per gram while carbohydrate and protein each yield 4 kcal per gram (alcohol yields 7). Concentrating energy in fat is the only way to fit that many calories into a small sachet, and the low water content also means no water is needed to prepare it and it resists spoilage without refrigeration.
7. State the adult RDA for iodine and for iron, noting how each differs by sex or pregnancy, and name the deficiency syndrome for each.
Iodine: 150 µg/day for adults, rising to 220 µg/day in pregnancy; deficiency causes goiter and, in pregnancy, congenital hypothyroidism with irreversible neurodevelopmental harm. Iron: 8 mg/day for men 19–50 and 18 mg/day for women 19–50, falling to 8 mg/day after menopause and rising to 27 mg/day in pregnancy; deficiency causes microcytic, hypochromic anemia. Always match the value to age, sex, and pregnancy status.
8. Roughly what fraction of food produced is never eaten, and where does the loss concentrate in low-income versus high-income settings?
Close to a third — about 13 percent lost between harvest and retail and roughly 19 percent wasted at retail, food service, and household level. Pre-retail loss (storage, cooling, transport) dominates in lower-income settings; household waste dominates in higher-income ones.
9. A classmate says "processed food is bad for you." Give two counterexamples and explain what the word actually fails to capture.
Pasteurizing milk and freezing vegetables within hours of harvest are both processing, and both make food safer or better preserve vitamin C and folate; fortifying flour with folic acid is processing that prevents neural tube defects. "Processed" lumps nutrient-preserving steps together with refining, which strips fiber, magnesium, and B vitamins — the word describes a method, not a nutritional verdict.
10. Rank these by approximate greenhouse gas footprint per kilogram, highest to lowest: lentils, poultry, beef, cheese. Then explain why "buy local" is a weak lever.
Beef (roughly 60–100 kg CO2e/kg) > cheese (~20–24) > poultry (~4–10) > lentils (~1–2). Transport is only about 6 percent of food-related emissions, so what the food is matters far more than how far it travelled — local beef still outweighs imported lentils by well over an order of magnitude. Air-freighted perishables are the main exception.
11. Which of these has strong evidence and which is preliminary: (a) salt iodization reducing iodine-deficiency disorders, (b) regenerative grazing offsetting ruminant emissions, (c) folic acid fortification reducing neural tube defects, (d) ultra-processed foods causing cardiometabolic disease?
Strong: (a) salt iodization and (c) folic acid fortification, both with large documented population-level reductions. Preliminary: (b) regenerative grazing as a general offset, since soil carbon saturates and measurement is difficult, and (d) causal claims about ultra-processed foods — the observational association is strong and reproducible, but experimental evidence is thin and the classification itself is contested.
12. Why do fortification and benefit programs generally outperform nutrition education at the population level, and what does that imply for how you talk with someone who is food insecure?
Because they change the environment rather than the person — fortification requires no one to remember, prefer, or afford anything, and benefits relieve the actual binding constraint, which is money. Practically: screen routinely (the two-item Hunger Vital Sign works), refer without judgment, know the local resources by name, and recognize that skipping meals at the end of the month is a rational response to circumstances, not a nutrition failure.
