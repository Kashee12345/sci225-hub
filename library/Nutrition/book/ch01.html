Nutrition · Chapter 1

Nutrition Basics

Every day you make dozens of decisions about what to eat, and every one of them delivers a specific chemical payload to a body that has very specific chemical needs. Nutrition is the science that connects those two things — the food on the plate and the physiology inside the person. This chapter builds the scaffolding the rest of the book hangs on: what a nutrient actually is, the six classes they sort into, how food energy is measured and where the familiar 4-4-9 numbers come from, the system of reference standards that tells us how much of a nutrient people need, and how all of that gets translated into practical guidance. None of it requires you to label a food good or bad — it requires you to understand what that food does.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Define nutrition, food, and nutrient, and explain what makes a nutrient essential.
  • Name the six classes of nutrients and sort them into macronutrients and micronutrients.
  • State the energy yield of carbohydrate, protein, fat, and alcohol in kilocalories per gram, and use those factors to work out the energy in a food.
  • Distinguish a kilocalorie from a calorie and from a kilojoule.
  • Explain what the DRIs are and define EAR, RDA, AI, and UL, including who each one applies to.
  • Explain why a nutrient recommendation changes with age, sex, pregnancy, and lactation.
  • Contrast nutrient density with energy density, and describe what "empty calories" means and why the phrase is imprecise.
  • Tell the difference between a nutrition claim backed by strong evidence and one that is still preliminary.

Section 1What nutrition is

Food, nutrients, and the difference between them

Nutrition is the science of how the body takes in, breaks down, absorbs, transports, uses, and disposes of the substances in food — and how those processes affect health across a lifetime. Food is what you actually eat: a physical, cultural, sensory, economic thing, bought at a store, cooked in a kitchen, shared at a table. Nutrients are the individual chemical compounds inside that food which the body requires to grow, to repair itself, to run its machinery, and to regulate that machinery.

Holding those two words apart matters more than it looks. Food is never a single nutrient; a chickpea delivers protein, starch, fiber, folate, iron, magnesium, and water simultaneously, and those components arrive bound up in a physical matrix that changes how fast they are digested. This is why nutrition research so often finds that a whole food behaves differently from the isolated nutrient it contains — and why a supplement that delivers one compound in a capsule frequently fails to reproduce the benefit seen with the food.

Essential nutrients: the short list your body cannot make

The body manufactures thousands of compounds for itself. A nutrient is called essential (or indispensable) when the body cannot synthesize it at all, or cannot synthesize enough of it to meet its needs, so it has to come from the diet. There are roughly forty of them, and the definition is strictly operational: remove the nutrient from the diet, a characteristic deficiency develops; put it back, the deficiency resolves. That reversal is the test.

The essential list includes nine essential amino acids (histidine, isoleucine, leucine, lysine, methionine, phenylalanine, threonine, tryptophan, and valine), two essential fatty acids (linoleic acid, an omega-6, and alpha-linolenic acid, an omega-3), thirteen vitamins, a set of minerals, and water. Notice what is not on it: there is no essential carbohydrate. The body can make the glucose it needs from other substrates, which is why very-low-carbohydrate diets are survivable — though that fact says nothing about whether they are optimal, which is a separate question with a much messier evidence base.

Why diet matters to health — and how strongly

Diet influences health through at least three distinct routes, and they carry very different weights of evidence. The first is deficiency: the classic nutrient-deficiency diseases — scurvy, beriberi, pellagra, rickets, iodine-deficiency goiter, iron-deficiency anemia — are causally settled. We know the nutrient, the syndrome, and the cure, and that knowledge is among the most solid in all of medicine. The second is dietary pattern and chronic disease: large cohort studies plus a smaller number of randomized trials link overall eating patterns rich in vegetables, fruits, legumes, whole grains, nuts, and unsaturated fats to lower rates of cardiovascular disease. This evidence is good but is about patterns, not single foods. The third route is food insecurity and access, which shapes what is realistically eatable long before preference enters the picture.

The contrast with single-nutrient supplement claims is instructive, and worth internalizing early. Observational studies once suggested that people with high beta-carotene intakes had less lung cancer. When that was tested directly in large randomized trials of beta-carotene supplements in smokers, lung cancer incidence went up, not down. Similarly, large trials of vitamin D supplementation have not reproduced the broad non-skeletal benefits that observational data implied. The pattern repeats often enough to be a rule of thumb: a nutrient's behavior inside a whole diet is not reliably predicted by its behavior inside a pill.

Nutrition is not a morality system. Foods are not clean or dirty, sinful or virtuous, and eating one does not make a person good or bad. Foods have compositions, and bodies have requirements. Throughout this book we describe what a food contains and what the evidence shows it does — that is the whole job. Framing that is shame-based has not been shown to improve health outcomes, and it makes accurate self-report of intake far less likely, which degrades the data clinicians depend on.

Section 2The six classes of nutrients

Six classes, two categories

Every nutrient in every food belongs to one of exactly six classes: carbohydrate, lipid (fat), protein, vitamin, mineral, and water. That list has not changed in a century, and memorizing it early pays off, because almost every later topic in nutrition is an elaboration of one of these six.

The classes then split along a second axis. Macronutrients are needed in gram quantities each day: carbohydrate, lipid, protein, and — by most conventions — water. Micronutrients are needed in milligram or microgram quantities: the vitamins and minerals. "Macro" and "micro" refer to the amount required, not the importance. The recommended intake of carbohydrate for adults is 130 g a day — a figure derived from the brain's average minimum glucose use, not from carbohydrate being essential — while the recommendation for iodine is 150 µg — a difference approaching a millionfold, yet running out of iodine damages the thyroid just as surely as running out of energy starves the body.

The energy-yielding three, plus the three that are not

Only three classes supply usable energy: carbohydrate, lipid, and protein. Vitamins, minerals, and water supply none. This is a point students get wrong constantly, usually about vitamins — a "B-complex for energy" supplement does not contain any energy. Several B vitamins act as coenzymes in the pathways that release energy from macronutrients, which is a genuine and important role, but the coenzyme is a tool, not fuel. Giving extra tools to a system that already has enough does not make it run faster.

A quick tour of the six. Carbohydrates — sugars, starches, and fibers — are the body's most readily used fuel — glucose is the brain's preferred substrate and, because mature red blood cells have no mitochondria, their obligatory one; fiber is a carbohydrate that human enzymes cannot digest, so it reaches the colon largely intact and feeds the gut microbiota. Lipids include triglycerides, phospholipids, and sterols; they are the densest fuel store, they build every cell membrane, they carry the fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E, and K, and they supply the two essential fatty acids. Proteins are chains of amino acids that build and repair tissue and serve as enzymes, transporters, antibodies, and some hormones; the body will burn protein for fuel, but that is a fallback, not its job. Vitamins are organic compounds — four fat-soluble (A, D, E, K) and nine water-soluble (vitamin C and the eight B vitamins) — that regulate metabolic processes. Minerals are inorganic elements, split into major minerals needed at more than 100 mg/day (calcium, phosphorus, magnesium, sodium, potassium, chloride, sulfur) and trace minerals needed in smaller amounts (iron, zinc, iodine, selenium, copper, manganese, fluoride, chromium, molybdenum). Water is a nutrient in its own right, makes up roughly 50–70% of adult body weight, and is the one whose absence kills fastest — days, not weeks or months.

Deficiency and excess both have consequences

Because micronutrients are needed in tiny amounts, it is tempting to treat them as trivial. The deficiency syndromes say otherwise, and they are worth knowing by name. Vitamin C deficiency produces scurvy; thiamin (B1) deficiency produces beriberi, and in the setting of chronic alcohol use, Wernicke–Korsakoff syndrome; niacin (B3) deficiency produces pellagra. Vitamin D deficiency causes rickets in growing children and osteomalacia in adults. Vitamin A deficiency causes night blindness progressing to xerophthalmia and is a leading cause of preventable childhood blindness worldwide. Iodine deficiency causes goiter and, during pregnancy, impaired neurological development in the child. Iron deficiency causes iron-deficiency anemia. Vitamin B12 deficiency causes megaloblastic anemia together with neurological damage that can become permanent.

The mirror image is real too. Fat-soluble vitamins are stored rather than readily excreted, so chronic high intakes of preformed vitamin A can cause liver damage and are teratogenic in pregnancy. Excess iron is toxic, and acute iron ingestion is a classic pediatric poisoning. Water itself can be harmful in extreme excess by diluting blood sodium (hyponatremia). The take-home is that a nutrient's dose–response curve has two ends, which is exactly the problem the upper-limit standard in Section 4 exists to solve.

"Macro" is about quantity, not rank. Students often hear "micronutrient" and mentally file it as minor. Iodine is required at roughly 150 µg per day for adults — about the mass of a few grains of salt — yet insufficient iodine during pregnancy is one of the most common preventable causes of impaired cognitive development in the world. Small requirement, enormous consequence.

Section 3Calories and energy

What a Calorie actually is

A calorie is a unit of energy: the heat needed to raise the temperature of one gram of water by one degree Celsius. That unit is inconveniently tiny for food, so nutrition uses the kilocalorie — 1,000 calories, written kcal, the heat needed to raise one kilogram of water by one degree Celsius. Here is the vocabulary trap: the "Calories" printed on a food label are kilocalories. A 200-Calorie granola bar contains 200 kcal, which is 200,000 calories in the strict physics sense. Some countries and most scientific journals use the SI unit instead, the kilojoule (kJ), where 1 kcal = 4.184 kJ. Nothing about the food changes; only the units do.

Food energy was originally measured with a bomb calorimeter: burn a weighed sample of food in oxygen inside a sealed chamber surrounded by water, and measure how much the water warms. That gives gross energy. The human body is not a bomb calorimeter — it does not absorb everything and does not oxidize everything completely — so the values used in nutrition are physiological ones, refined by W. O. Atwater and colleagues and known as the Atwater factors.

The 4-4-9 rule (and 7 for alcohol)

These four numbers are among the very few worth committing to memory exactly:

With those factors you can compute a food's energy from its composition. A food supplying 30 g carbohydrate, 10 g protein, and 12 g fat delivers (30 × 4) + (10 × 4) + (12 × 9) = 120 + 40 + 108 = 268 kcal. You can also run it backwards to find the share of energy from each macronutrient: in this example fat supplies 108 of 268 kcal, or about 40% of the energy, even though it is only about 23% of the 52 g of macronutrient weight. That gap — fat being a small share of the mass but a large share of the energy — is the direct consequence of the 9.

Energy in, energy out

Total energy expenditure has three main components. Basal metabolic rate (BMR) is the energy needed to keep a resting body alive — breathing, circulation, ion gradients, cell turnover — and it is by far the largest piece, typically around 60–70% of the total in a sedentary adult. Physical activity is the most variable component, ranging from a few percent to a very large share in athletes and heavy manual workers. The thermic effect of food, the energy spent digesting and processing a meal, accounts for roughly 10% of intake. Individual requirements differ substantially with body size, body composition, age, sex, growth, pregnancy, lactation, and activity, which is precisely why the standards in the next section are stated for defined groups rather than as one number for everybody.

"Empty calories" is shorthand, not chemistry. No calorie is literally empty — a calorie is a unit of energy, and energy always arrives attached to carbohydrate, fat, protein, or alcohol. The phrase is a loose way of saying that a food supplies energy with relatively few vitamins, minerals, fiber, or protein alongside it. Section 5 gives that idea a cleaner name: nutrient density.

  • A food-label Calorie is a kilocalorie (kcal); 1 kcal = 4.184 kJ.
  • Energy yields: carbohydrate 4, protein 4, fat 9, alcohol 7 kcal/g; fiber contributes roughly 2 kcal/g.
  • Vitamins, minerals, and water supply no energy, and alcohol supplies energy without being a nutrient.

Section 4Dietary standards

The DRI framework

The Dietary Reference Intakes (DRIs) are a set of reference values for nutrient intake, developed by expert committees under the U.S. National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine in partnership with Canadian authorities. They are not a single number per nutrient. They are a family of four related values — EAR, RDA, AI, and UL — each answering a different question, and each set separately for defined life-stage and sex groups: infants, children by age band, adolescent males and females, adult males and females by age band, pregnancy, and lactation.

Two structural points prevent most of the confusion. First, the DRIs describe healthy people; they are not treatment doses for disease. Second, the RDA and AI are goals for usual intake averaged over time, not targets to hit precisely at every meal or even every day. A person who eats no vitamin A on Tuesday and plenty on Wednesday is fine; the body's storage and turnover are what these values are built around.

EAR, RDA, AI, and UL — one at a time

The Estimated Average Requirement (EAR) is the intake estimated to meet the requirement of half the healthy individuals in a life-stage and sex group. Because half the group needs more than the EAR, it is a poor goal for an individual. Its real uses are two: assessing the adequacy of intakes in populations, and serving as the mathematical starting point for the RDA.

The Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA) is the intake sufficient to meet the requirement of nearly all — about 97–98% — of healthy individuals in that group. It is derived from the EAR, conventionally as the EAR plus two standard deviations of the requirement. The RDA is the appropriate individual goal, and it is deliberately generous: most people need less than the RDA, and an intake below it is not by itself evidence of deficiency.

The Adequate Intake (AI) is used when the scientific evidence is too thin to establish an EAR, and therefore too thin to calculate an RDA. It is an observed or experimentally determined approximation of the intake of apparently healthy people. An AI is used the same way an RDA is used — as an individual goal — but it carries less certainty, and that difference in confidence is the entire reason for the separate label.

The Tolerable Upper Intake Level (UL) is the highest usual daily intake likely to pose no risk of adverse effects for almost all individuals in the group. Above the UL, risk of harm rises. Two cautions: the UL is not a recommended intake, and the absence of a UL for a nutrient means the data were insufficient to set one, not that unlimited intake is safe. For several nutrients the UL applies only to specific forms — the folate UL applies to synthetic folic acid from supplements and fortified foods, not to folate occurring naturally in food.

The related Acceptable Macronutrient Distribution Ranges (AMDRs) work differently, expressing goals as a percentage of total energy for adults: carbohydrate 45–65%, fat 20–35%, and protein 10–35% of calories. There is also an Estimated Energy Requirement (EER) for calories — notably there is no RDA for energy, because setting an energy value that covers 97–98% of a group would systematically overshoot the average person's need.

Section 5Dietary guidelines and nutrient density

From nutrient numbers to something you can eat

Nobody shops for 18 mg of iron. The DRIs are stated in nutrients, but people buy, cook, and eat foods, so a translation layer is required. That layer is the food-based dietary guideline, and most countries publish one. In the United States the Dietary Guidelines for Americans are revised on a five-year cycle by the Departments of Agriculture and Health and Human Services, accompanied by a consumer graphic — historically a pyramid, more recently the MyPlate plate icon. Other countries use their own imagery, from Canada's plate to Japan's spinning top, but the underlying nutrient science is broadly shared.

Recent editions of the U.S. guidelines converge on a few durable themes. Follow a healthy dietary pattern across every life stage rather than chasing individual nutrients. Make that pattern adaptable to personal preference, culture, tradition, and budget — a guideline that ignores cost or culture will not be followed, and an unfollowed guideline improves nothing. Meet nutrient needs primarily from nutrient-dense foods. And limit added sugars, saturated fat, sodium, and alcohol, with typical quantified targets of under 10% of calories from added sugars, under 10% of calories from saturated fat, and under 2,300 mg of sodium per day for adults. The MyPlate graphic renders this as proportions: roughly half the plate vegetables and fruit, about a quarter grains with at least half of those whole grains, about a quarter protein foods, plus dairy or a fortified alternative.

Nutrient density versus energy density

Nutrient density is the amount of beneficial nutrients a food provides relative to its energy content — nutrients per calorie. Energy density is a separate measure: calories per gram of food. The two vary independently, and confusing them causes real errors. Nuts are high in energy density and also high in nutrient density. A sugar-sweetened soft drink is the opposite pairing — appreciable energy with almost no accompanying nutrients. Lettuce sits at a third corner: very low energy density, and because the calorie denominator is so small it rates well on nutrients per calorie, even though one serving supplies only small absolute amounts. Fortified breakfast cereal can be nutrient-dense for certain micronutrients while also carrying substantial added sugar. The concept is a comparison tool, not a ranking of foods into approved and forbidden.

Which brings us to empty calories, a phrase you will hear constantly. It refers to energy delivered with few accompanying nutrients — classically from added sugars, solid fats, and alcohol. It is a useful shorthand and an imprecise one, and current guidance has largely moved to naming the specific components (calories from added sugars, calories from saturated fat) rather than labeling whole foods "empty." That shift is deliberate, because in practice the label got applied to foods, and then to the people eating them. A more accurate framing: nearly every diet contains some foods eaten mainly for pleasure, celebration, or convenience, and the useful question is what the overall pattern looks like — not whether any single item earns approval.

How much confidence do the guidelines deserve?

Different pieces of the guidance rest on different foundations, and a careful reader separates them. The strongest support is for the nutrient standards themselves — the essentiality of each nutrient and the intakes that prevent deficiency, established through decades of depletion–repletion work. Also strong: folic acid fortification reducing neural tube defects, and reduced sodium intake lowering blood pressure. Good, mostly from cohort studies plus a limited set of randomized trials: dietary patterns emphasizing vegetables, fruits, legumes, whole grains, nuts, and unsaturated fats associate with lower cardiovascular risk. Weaker or contested: precise optimal percentages for individual macronutrients, and most claims that a single nutrient in supplement form prevents chronic disease.

None of this makes the guidelines arbitrary. It means they are a reasonable synthesis of evidence of mixed strength, aimed at populations, and they should be read that way — as a starting framework, not a prescription for any particular person. Individual recommendations belong with a qualified clinician or registered dietitian who knows the person's history, medications, laboratory values, resources, and preferences.

Judge the pattern, not the plate. The evidence linking diet to chronic disease is overwhelmingly about what people eat over months and years, not about any one meal. A single food cannot be evaluated in isolation, and no single food ruins or rescues a diet. This is also why guidance framed around adding foods — more vegetables, more legumes, more whole grains — tends to be both more accurate and more sustainable than guidance framed around forbidding them.

Key points
  • Food-based dietary guidelines translate nutrient standards into foods, food groups, and proportions people can act on.
  • Common quantified targets for adults: added sugars under 10% of calories, saturated fat under 10% of calories, sodium under 2,300 mg/day.
  • MyPlate proportions: about half vegetables and fruit, a quarter grains (at least half whole), a quarter protein foods, plus dairy or a fortified alternative.
  • Nutrient density is nutrients per calorie; energy density is calories per gram — they are independent measures.
  • "Empty calories" is informal shorthand for energy with few nutrients; naming added sugars, saturated fat, or alcohol directly is more precise and less prone to misuse.
  • The dietary pattern, not the individual food, is the level at which the chronic-disease evidence is strongest.

The chapter in one breath

  • Nutrition studies how the body uses food; food is what is eaten and nutrients are the chemicals within it, with essential nutrients being those the body cannot make in adequate amounts.
  • There are exactly six classes of nutrients: carbohydrate, lipid, protein, vitamin, mineral, and water.
  • Macronutrients are required in grams (carbohydrate, lipid, protein, water); micronutrients in milligrams or micrograms (vitamins, minerals) — the labels describe quantity, not importance.
  • Energy yields are carbohydrate 4, protein 4, fat 9, and alcohol 7 kcal/g; vitamins, minerals, and water yield none, and a label "Calorie" is a kilocalorie (1 kcal = 4.184 kJ).
  • The DRIs set nutrient reference values for healthy people by life stage and sex: EAR covers 50% of a group, RDA covers 97–98%, AI substitutes when evidence is thin, and UL is a safety ceiling rather than a goal.
  • Any cited value needs its population and units — iron is 8 mg/day for adult men, 18 mg/day for women 19–50, and 27 mg/day in pregnancy.
  • Dietary guidelines convert nutrient standards into food-group proportions such as MyPlate, with typical limits on added sugars, saturated fat, sodium, and alcohol.
  • Nutrient density (nutrients per calorie) is the more precise successor to "empty calories," and the dietary pattern over time — not any single food — is what the chronic-disease evidence actually addresses.

The SI unit of energy used on food labels in many countries; 1 kcal = 4.184 kJ.
Lipid
The macronutrient class that includes triglycerides, phospholipids, and sterols; yields 9 kcal/g, builds cell membranes, and carries the fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E, and K.
Macronutrient
A nutrient required in gram quantities each day: carbohydrate, lipid, protein, and (by convention) water.
Micronutrient
A nutrient required in milligram or microgram quantities each day: the vitamins and minerals.
Mineral
An inorganic element required in the diet; major minerals are needed at more than 100 mg/day, trace minerals in smaller amounts. Minerals supply no energy.
Nutrient density
The amount of beneficial nutrients a food supplies relative to its energy content — nutrients per calorie.
Nutrition
The science of how the body ingests, digests, absorbs, transports, uses, and excretes the substances in food, and how those processes affect health.
Protein
The macronutrient class made of amino acid chains; yields 4 kcal/g and supplies structure, enzymes, transporters, antibodies, and some hormones. Nine amino acids are essential.
Recommended Dietary Allowance (RDA)
The average daily intake sufficient to meet the requirement of about 97–98% of healthy individuals in a life-stage and sex group; the appropriate individual intake goal.
Tolerable Upper Intake Level (UL)
The highest average daily intake likely to pose no risk of adverse effects for almost all individuals in a group; a ceiling, never a target.
Vitamin
An organic compound required in small amounts to regulate metabolism; four are fat-soluble (A, D, E, K) and nine are water-soluble (vitamin C and eight B vitamins). Vitamins supply no energy.
Water
An essential nutrient making up roughly 50–70% of adult body weight; it serves as solvent, transport medium, temperature regulator, and chemical reactant, and supplies no energy.

Check yourself

1. Distinguish "food" from "nutrient," and explain why a whole food often behaves differently from an isolated nutrient it contains.
Food is the edible material as eaten; a nutrient is a specific chemical inside it that the body requires. A whole food delivers many nutrients at once, embedded in a physical matrix that alters how quickly they are digested and absorbed — which is one reason isolated supplements repeatedly fail to reproduce the benefits observed with whole foods.
2. What makes a nutrient "essential"? What is the experimental test?
The body cannot synthesize it in adequate amounts, so the diet must supply it. The test is depletion and repletion: remove it and a characteristic deficiency appears; restore it and the deficiency resolves. There are roughly forty essential nutrients, including 9 essential amino acids, 2 essential fatty acids, 13 vitamins, several minerals, and water.
3. Name the six classes of nutrients and state which are macronutrients and which are micronutrients.
Carbohydrate, lipid, protein, vitamin, mineral, and water. Macronutrients: carbohydrate, lipid, protein, and (by convention) water. Micronutrients: vitamins and minerals. "Macro" and "micro" describe the quantity required, not the importance — iodine is needed at about 150 µg/day for adults yet its shortage during pregnancy is a major preventable cause of impaired development.
4. Which nutrient classes yield energy, and which yield none? Address the common belief that vitamins provide energy.
Only carbohydrate, lipid, and protein yield energy. Vitamins, minerals, and water yield none. Several B vitamins act as coenzymes in the pathways that release energy from macronutrients, which is why they get marketed as "energy" nutrients — but a coenzyme is a tool, not fuel, and adding more to a system that already has enough does not generate energy.
5. State the energy yield in kcal per gram for carbohydrate, protein, fat, and alcohol.
Carbohydrate 4 kcal/g, protein 4 kcal/g, fat 9 kcal/g, alcohol 7 kcal/g. Fiber contributes roughly 2 kcal/g from colonic fermentation. Alcohol yields energy but is not a nutrient, because no body function requires it.
6. A serving of food contains 40 g carbohydrate, 15 g protein, and 10 g fat. How many kilocalories does it supply, and what percentage comes from fat?
(40 × 4) + (15 × 4) + (10 × 9) = 160 + 60 + 90 = 310 kcal; fat supplies 90/310, or about 29%. Note that fat is only about 15% of the food's weight but nearly 29% of its energy — the direct consequence of 9 kcal/g.
7. A label lists "230 Calories." Explain what that unit actually is, and convert it to kilojoules.
The capital-C Calorie on a label is a kilocalorie (kcal) — 1,000 small calories — so the food supplies 230 kcal. At 4.184 kJ per kcal, that is about 962 kJ. A kilocalorie is the heat needed to raise one kilogram of water by one degree Celsius.
8. Define the EAR and the RDA, state the percentage of the group each covers, and explain which one belongs on an individual's goal sheet.
The EAR meets the requirement of 50% of healthy individuals in a life-stage and sex group; the RDA meets the requirement of about 97–98% of them. The RDA is the individual goal. The EAR is used for assessing population adequacy and as the mathematical basis of the RDA (conventionally EAR plus two standard deviations); because half the group needs more than the EAR, it is unsuitable as a personal target.
9. When is an AI used instead of an RDA, and what does that substitution tell you about the evidence?
An AI is set when the evidence is insufficient to establish an EAR, which means an RDA cannot be calculated. It is an observed or experimentally derived approximation of the intake of apparently healthy people, used as an individual goal but with explicitly lower confidence. Fiber (38 g/day for men and 25 g/day for women aged 19–50) and total water (3.7 L/day for adult men, 2.7 L/day for adult women) are AIs, not RDAs.
10. What is the UL, and what are the two most common ways students misread it?
The Tolerable Upper Intake Level is the highest usual daily intake likely to pose no risk of adverse effects for almost all individuals in a group. First misreading: treating it as a recommended or target intake — it is a ceiling. Second: assuming that a nutrient with no UL is safe in unlimited amounts, when in fact it means the data were insufficient to set one. Some ULs also apply only to a specific form, such as the folate UL of 1,000 µg/day, which covers synthetic folic acid, not naturally occurring food folate.
11. Why must a nutrient recommendation always be reported with its population? Illustrate with iron.
Because requirements differ by age, sex, pregnancy, and lactation, a bare number is meaningless. The iron RDA is 8 mg/day for men 19 and older, 18 mg/day for women aged 19–50, 8 mg/day for women 51 and older, and 27 mg/day during pregnancy, with a UL of 45 mg/day for adults. Quoting "the iron RDA is 18 mg" without the group is wrong more often than it is right.
12. Distinguish nutrient density from energy density, and explain why "empty calories" is being replaced by more specific language.
Nutrient density is beneficial nutrients per calorie; energy density is calories per gram of food. They vary independently — nuts are high in both; a sugar-sweetened drink is fairly energy-dense but low in nutrient density; lettuce is very low in energy density yet rates well on nutrients per calorie. "Empty calories" is informal shorthand for energy arriving with few nutrients, but no calorie is literally empty, and in practice the label migrated from components to whole foods and then to the people eating them. Naming the specific component — calories from added sugars, from saturated fat, or from alcohol — is both more precise and less prone to misuse.
