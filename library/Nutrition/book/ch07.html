Minerals & Water

Everything you have studied so far — carbohydrate and protein at 4 kilocalories per gram, fat at 9, alcohol at 7 — burns. Minerals and water do not. They yield zero kilocalories per gram, and yet nothing in the body works without them: no heartbeat, no nerve signal, no bone, no thyroid hormone, no oxygen delivery. Minerals are simple inorganic elements the body cannot make and cannot destroy, so they must arrive in food and leave in urine, sweat, and stool. Water is the medium all of it happens in, and it is the nutrient a person runs out of fastest. This chapter walks through the major minerals, the trace minerals, the special case of iron, and the fluid-and-electrolyte system that keeps blood pressure and cell volume where they belong.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Distinguish major minerals from trace minerals and explain why neither yields energy.
  • State the main function, a reference intake with its population and units, and the deficiency or toxicity picture for calcium, phosphorus, magnesium, sodium, potassium, and chloride.
  • Do the same for the trace minerals iron, zinc, iodine, selenium, and fluoride.
  • Explain bioavailability and name specific dietary enhancers and inhibitors of mineral absorption.
  • Contrast heme and nonheme iron, describe how absorption is regulated, and sequence the stages from depleted stores to iron-deficiency anemia.
  • Describe the functions of body water, account for daily fluid balance, and cite the Adequate Intake for total water by sex.
  • Explain how the sodium–potassium pump, osmosis, and hormones distribute fluid between compartments.
  • Summarize what the evidence does and does not support about sodium, potassium, and blood pressure, separating strong findings from preliminary ones.

Section 1Major minerals

What a mineral is, and what it is not

A mineral is a single inorganic element — calcium, iron, zinc — that the body needs but cannot synthesize. This makes minerals fundamentally different from the vitamins in the previous chapter. A vitamin is a complex organic molecule that heat, light, oxygen, or long storage can destroy. A mineral is an element: you cannot break calcium down into something that is not calcium. Minerals survive cooking, survive the oven, and survive being burned to ash. What they can do is dissolve, which is why boiling vegetables and pouring off the water leaches potassium and magnesium down the drain.

Because minerals are not oxidized for fuel, they supply 0 kcal per gram. Their value is structural and regulatory: they build the crystal lattice of bone, carry electrical charge across membranes, sit at the active site of enzymes, and hold the osmotic pressure that decides where water goes.

Nutritionists split minerals by how much the body needs, not by how important they are. A major mineral (or macromineral) is one required in amounts greater than 100 mg per day, or present in the body in amounts above about 5 grams. A trace mineral is required in smaller amounts — often milligrams or micrograms. A trace mineral is not a minor mineral: iodine deficiency is measured in micrograms and causes goiter and impaired brain development.

The structural three: calcium, phosphorus, magnesium

Calcium is the most abundant mineral in the body, and roughly 99 percent of it is locked into bone and teeth as hydroxyapatite. The remaining one percent, circulating in blood and inside cells, does the exciting work: muscle contraction, nerve transmission, hormone secretion, and blood clotting. The RDA is 1,000 mg per day for adults 19–50, rising to 1,200 mg per day for women 51 and older and men 71 and older, and it is highest of all in adolescence — 1,300 mg per day for ages 9–18, when the skeleton is being built. Pregnancy in adults does not raise the requirement (still 1,000 mg for ages 19–50), because absorption efficiency increases instead; pregnant and lactating teenagers stay at 1,300 mg. The Tolerable Upper Intake Level is 2,500 mg per day for adults 19–50 and 2,000 mg for adults 51 and older.

Phosphorus partners with calcium in bone, but it is also in every ATP molecule, every phospholipid membrane, and the backbone of DNA and RNA. The RDA is 700 mg per day for adults and 1,250 mg per day for ages 9–18. Dietary deficiency is rare because phosphorus is abundant in protein-containing foods and in phosphate additives; the clinical problem is more often the opposite, when failing kidneys cannot clear phosphate.

Magnesium is a cofactor for more than 300 enzymes, including every reaction that uses ATP — ATP is biologically active as an Mg-ATP complex. About half of body magnesium is in bone. The RDA differs by sex and shifts at age 31: 400 mg per day for men 19–30 and 420 mg for men 31 and older; 310 mg for women 19–30 and 320 mg for women 31 and older, with roughly 40 mg more in pregnancy. Note a quirk: the UL of 350 mg per day applies only to supplemental magnesium, not to magnesium from food, because it is set by the laxative effect of magnesium salts rather than by any harm from food.

The salts of body fluid: sodium, potassium, chloride

These three are the body's principal electrolytes — minerals that dissociate into charged ions in solution and thereby conduct electricity, hold water in place, and maintain acid–base balance. Sodium and chloride dominate the fluid outside cells; potassium dominates the fluid inside them. Because these ions determine osmotic pressure, they determine blood volume, and therefore blood pressure. Section 5 takes that story further.

Their reference values are set as Adequate Intakes rather than RDAs, because the evidence was not strong enough to define an average requirement. For adults, sodium AI is 1,500 mg per day, chloride AI is 2,300 mg per day for ages 19–50 (falling to 2,000 mg at 51–70 and 1,800 mg at 71 and older), and potassium AI is 3,400 mg per day for men 19 and older and 2,600 mg per day for women 19 and older, with 2,900 mg in pregnancy. In its 2019 review, the National Academies replaced the sodium UL with a Chronic Disease Risk Reduction Intake of 2,300 mg per day — a threshold above which reducing intake is expected to reduce chronic disease risk. Average intake in the United States runs near 3,400 mg per day.

Section 2Trace minerals

Small amounts, large consequences

Trace minerals are needed in milligram or microgram quantities, and the total amount of some of them in an adult body would not cover a fingernail. Their smallness is deceptive. Most trace minerals work as the functional core of a protein: iron sits in the heme group of hemoglobin, zinc holds the shape of hundreds of transcription factors, iodine is built directly into thyroid hormone, and selenium is incorporated as the amino acid selenocysteine into glutathione peroxidase and the enzymes that activate thyroid hormone. Remove a few micrograms and an entire hormone axis stalls.

Because the working range is narrow, trace minerals are also the group where the gap between "not enough" and "too much" is smallest. Selenium is the classic example: the RDA is 55 mcg per day for adults, and the UL is 400 mcg per day — barely seven-fold. Some vitamins have a far wider margin (vitamin E, RDA 15 mg against a UL of 1,000 mg), though others are tight as well (niacin, folate), so the rule is not absolute — the point is that for most trace minerals there is very little room above the requirement.

Five worth knowing well

Iron carries oxygen in hemoglobin and myoglobin and drives electron transport. Its RDA is strongly sex- and life-stage-dependent: 8 mg per day for men 19 and older, 18 mg per day for women 19–50, 8 mg per day for women 51 and older, and 27 mg per day in pregnancy. Section 3 is devoted to it.

Zinc participates in more than 300 enzymes and in the zinc-finger motifs that let transcription factors grip DNA. The RDA is 11 mg per day for men and 8 mg per day for women, rising to 11 mg in pregnancy and 12 mg during lactation; the UL is 40 mg per day. Deficiency shows up as growth faltering in children, delayed wound healing, impaired immune response, loss of taste sensitivity, hair loss, and a characteristic rash. Chronic high-dose supplementation causes a copper deficiency, because zinc induces the intestinal protein that traps copper.

Iodine exists for one purpose: thyroid hormone. The RDA is 150 mcg per day for adults, 220 mcg per day in pregnancy, and 290 mcg per day during lactation, with a UL of 1,100 mcg per day for adults. Deficiency enlarges the thyroid into a goiter and causes hypothyroidism; deficiency during pregnancy impairs fetal brain development and has historically been the leading preventable cause of intellectual disability worldwide. Excess iodine can also disturb thyroid function, so the dose–response curve is U-shaped.

Selenium defends cells against oxidative damage through glutathione peroxidase and activates thyroid hormone through the deiodinases. RDA is 55 mcg per day for adults (60 mcg in pregnancy, 70 mcg during lactation), UL 400 mcg per day. Deficiency is regional and tied to selenium-poor soils; in parts of China it produced Keshan disease, a cardiomyopathy. Toxicity, called selenosis, produces brittle nails, hair loss, garlic-smelling breath, and gastrointestinal upset.

Fluoride is the outlier: it is not required for life, but it measurably strengthens tooth enamel against acid demineralization. The AI is 4 mg per day for men 19 and older and 3 mg per day for women, with a UL of 10 mg per day for adults. The evidence that fluoride reduces dental caries is strong and long-standing. Too much during the years when permanent teeth are forming produces dental fluorosis — white flecking to brown mottling of enamel — which is why fluoride intake in young children is watched closely.

Bioavailability: the label number is not the absorbed number

Bioavailability is the fraction of an ingested nutrient that is actually absorbed and usable. For minerals it can swing enormously depending on what else is on the plate. Phytate (phytic acid) in whole grains, legumes, nuts, and seeds binds zinc, iron, calcium, and magnesium; oxalate in spinach, rhubarb, and beet greens binds calcium so tightly that spinach is a poor calcium source despite a high calcium number on paper. Working the other way, vitamin C sharply increases nonheme iron absorption, and soaking, sprouting, fermenting, and leavening with yeast all reduce phytate and raise mineral availability — which is a large part of why traditional food preparation methods around the world involve exactly those steps.

Minerals also compete. Large supplemental doses of one divalent mineral can crowd out another at shared intestinal transporters — zinc against copper, calcium against iron, iron against zinc. This competition is a supplement-dose phenomenon; it is not something that ordinary mixed meals produce.

Section 3Iron and anemia

Two forms of dietary iron

Iron arrives in food in two chemically distinct forms, and they behave so differently that they are absorbed by separate pathways. Heme iron is iron already held inside a porphyrin ring, released from the hemoglobin and myoglobin of meat, poultry, and fish. It is taken up intact by the intestinal cell and absorbed at roughly 15 to 35 percent — efficiently, and largely indifferent to whatever else is in the meal. Nonheme iron is free ionic iron, and it is what you get from plants, eggs, dairy, and enriched or fortified grains. Its absorption ranges from about 2 to 20 percent and is exquisitely sensitive to the rest of the meal.

Most dietary iron in most diets is nonheme, even in people who eat meat. That is why the modifiers matter so much. Vitamin C reduces ferric iron to the absorbable ferrous form and holds it in solution; a citrus fruit, tomatoes, or peppers eaten with a bean dish can multiply the iron absorbed from it. A separate, still incompletely characterized enhancer in meat, poultry, and fish — historically called the MFP factor — increases nonheme absorption from the same meal. Pulling the other direction are phytates in whole grains and legumes, polyphenols and tannins in tea and coffee, calcium in large supplemental doses, and oxalates. Because the DRI committee accounted for this, the iron requirement for people eating no meat, poultry, or fish is estimated at roughly 1.8 times the standard RDA.

Absorption is the control valve

Here is the fact that organizes all of iron physiology: the body has no regulated route for excreting iron. Small amounts are lost in shed skin and gut cells, in menstrual blood, and in any bleeding, but there is no hormone that tells the kidney to dump excess iron. Iron balance is therefore controlled almost entirely at the point of absorption.

The gatekeeper is a liver hormone called hepcidin. When body iron is plentiful — or when inflammation is present — hepcidin rises, binds the export protein ferroportin on intestinal and macrophage membranes, and degrades it, so absorbed iron stays trapped in the mucosal cell and is shed when that cell sloughs off. When iron is scarce, hepcidin falls, ferroportin persists, and more iron enters the blood, where transferrin carries it to the marrow. Storage is as ferritin, mostly in liver, spleen, and marrow.

The same logic explains two clinical realities. First, iron supplements taken in large doses are poorly and increasingly inefficiently absorbed, because a big dose transiently drives hepcidin up. Second, in chronic inflammatory disease, hepcidin stays high and iron is locked away in storage even though the marrow needs it — the anemia of inflammation, which is a different problem from dietary iron deficiency and is not fixed the same way.

From depleted stores to anemia

Iron deficiency is not an on/off state. It develops in stages, and anemia is the last one, which means a person can be meaningfully iron-depleted with a completely normal hemoglobin.

Section 4Water

What water does

Water is the nutrient the body needs in the largest quantity and can survive without for the shortest time — days, against weeks without food. It makes up roughly 50 to 70 percent of body weight, and the variation is mostly a matter of tissue composition rather than anything else: lean tissue is about 73 percent water while adipose tissue holds much less, so people with more lean mass have a higher percentage. Infants are the wettest, near 70–75 percent, and the proportion declines gradually across the lifespan, which is one reason dehydration is a more serious threat at the two ends of life.

Its jobs are so basic they are easy to overlook. Water is the universal solvent in which nearly every metabolic reaction occurs, and it is a direct participant: every hydrolysis reaction in digestion consumes a water molecule. It is the transport medium — blood plasma, lymph, urine. It regulates temperature, because water has an unusually high heat capacity and heat of vaporization, so evaporating a modest volume of sweat sheds a large amount of heat. It lubricates and cushions as synovial fluid, cerebrospinal fluid, mucus, tears, and amniotic fluid. And because it carries the electrolytes, it maintains blood volume and therefore blood pressure.

Body water is divided into two compartments. Roughly two-thirds is intracellular fluid (ICF), inside cells, and one-third is extracellular fluid (ECF) — the interstitial fluid between cells plus the plasma inside vessels. Water moves freely between them by osmosis, following whichever side has the higher concentration of dissolved particles. Nothing pumps water; water always follows solute.

Where it comes in, where it goes out

Intake has three sources. Beverages of all kinds supply roughly 80 percent of total water intake, and this includes coffee and tea — the mild diuretic effect of caffeine at ordinary intakes does not outweigh the fluid the drink itself provides. Food supplies most of the remainder, since fruits and vegetables can be 85–95 percent water and even bread is about a third water. A small final contribution is metabolic water, roughly 250–350 mL per day, produced as a byproduct when carbohydrate, fat, and protein are oxidized.

Output has four routes. Urine is the only one that is finely regulated, and the kidney must produce a minimum of roughly 500 mL per day — the obligatory water loss — simply to carry out the day's urea, sodium, and other solutes. Insensible losses through skin and exhaled air run near 700–1,000 mL per day and continue whether you notice them or not. Sweat is small at rest and enormous during heat and exertion, sometimes exceeding a liter per hour. Feces account for only 100–200 mL in health, but that figure rises dramatically in diarrheal illness, which is why diarrhea is a leading cause of dehydration deaths in young children worldwide.

Two hormone systems keep the ledger balanced. Osmoreceptors in the hypothalamus sense rising blood concentration and trigger thirst plus release of antidiuretic hormone (ADH, vasopressin), which tells the kidney to reabsorb water. Falling blood volume or pressure activates the renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system, and aldosterone makes the kidney retain sodium — water follows the sodium osmotically.

How much? The honest answer

The Adequate Intake for total water — from beverages plus food combined — is 3.7 L per day for men 19 and older and 2.7 L per day for women 19 and older. About 3.0 L and 2.2 L of those respective totals come from beverages, with food supplying the balance. In pregnancy the total-water AI is 3.0 L per day, and during lactation it is 3.8 L per day.

Two cautions about those numbers. First, they are population Adequate Intakes drawn from what generally healthy people consume; they are not a target every individual must hit. Real requirements swing widely with air temperature, humidity, altitude, physical activity, fever, illness, and body size. Second, the familiar "eight glasses a day" rule is not a DRI and has no clear origin in the evidence; eight 8-ounce glasses is about 1.9 L, which is in the right neighborhood for beverages for some women and low for many men, and it ignores food entirely. For most healthy adults, thirst plus drinking with meals tracks needs well. The important exception is older adults, in whom the thirst response is genuinely blunted, so thirst becomes a less reliable signal.

Too much plain water is also a real problem. Exercise-associated hyponatremia occurs when endurance athletes drink large volumes of plain water over hours, diluting blood sodium faster than the kidney can excrete the excess. Symptoms — headache, nausea, confusion — look deceptively like dehydration, and it can be fatal. The physiology is worth understanding: hydration is a balance problem, not a maximization problem, and "drink as much as you possibly can" is not better advice than "drink to thirst."

Section 5Electrolytes and fluid balance

Ions, compartments, and the pump that keeps them apart

An electrolyte is a mineral salt that dissociates into ions in water. Sodium (Na+) and chloride (Cl−) are the dominant ions of extracellular fluid; potassium (K+), magnesium, and phosphate dominate inside the cell. Plasma sodium is normally about 135–145 mEq/L and plasma potassium about 3.5–5.0 mEq/L — and notice how narrow that potassium window is, which is exactly why potassium disturbances threaten the heart.

That separation is not passive; it is bought with energy. The sodium–potassium pump (Na+/K+-ATPase) in every cell membrane exports three sodium ions and imports two potassium ions per molecule of ATP. Running this pump consumes a substantial share of resting energy expenditure — estimates vary by tissue and by method, but it is a continuous, non-negotiable cost. The payoff is the membrane potential that makes nerve impulses and muscle contraction possible, and the sodium gradient that powers the intestinal co-transporters for glucose and amino acids — which, incidentally, is the physiological principle behind oral rehydration solution containing both salt and sugar.

Because water follows solute by osmosis, the distribution of these ions decides the distribution of fluid. Retain sodium and the extracellular compartment expands; lose sodium and it shrinks. This is the mechanical link from a mineral to a vital sign.

Sodium, potassium, and blood pressure

This is one of the most heavily studied questions in nutrition, so it is worth separating what is well established from what is still being worked out.

Strong evidence. Reducing sodium intake lowers blood pressure on average. This comes from many randomized controlled feeding trials, pooled in multiple meta-analyses, and the effect is consistent. The magnitude is modest at the individual level and larger in specific groups: people who already have hypertension, older adults, and Black adults tend to show bigger reductions — a trait called salt sensitivity. The DASH–Sodium trial demonstrated that lowering sodium and following an eating pattern rich in fruits, vegetables, and low-fat dairy each lowered blood pressure, and that the two effects added together.

Moderate evidence. Higher potassium intake also lowers blood pressure in randomized trials, but the base here is thinner than for sodium and is usually graded moderate rather than strong: the effect is clearest in adults who already have hypertension and whose sodium intake is high, and is small or inconsistent in adults with normal blood pressure. Tellingly, when the National Academies reviewed potassium in 2019 they judged the chronic-disease evidence insufficient to set a CDRR — unlike sodium, which did receive one. The direction of effect is nonetheless consistent with the idea that the two ions act on the same system from opposite directions.

Preliminary evidence. Whether lowering sodium reduces heart attacks and strokes — not just blood pressure — rests on a thinner base, because the trials required are long and expensive. Long-term follow-up of the Trials of Hypertension Prevention found fewer cardiovascular events in the sodium-reduction groups, and a large trial of potassium-enriched salt substitute in rural China reported fewer strokes and deaths. Those are encouraging, but they are a small number of trials in specific populations, and generalizing them to everyone would be overstating the case. Observational work suggesting that the sodium-to-potassium ratio predicts risk better than either ion alone is similarly plausible but not proven, since observational data cannot separate the ratio from everything else that accompanies a potassium-rich diet.

What is genuinely uncertain. Whether very low sodium intakes carry risk of their own is still argued, and the studies on both sides have measurement problems — estimating a person's habitual sodium intake accurately is notoriously hard. Honest teaching says so.

Where the sodium actually comes from

Roughly 70 percent of sodium in the United States food supply is already in packaged, processed, and restaurant foods before anyone reaches for a salt shaker — in bread, cured meats, cheese, soups, sauces, and savory snacks. Table salt added at home and at the table is a minority contributor. One teaspoon of table salt contains about 2,300 mg of sodium, since salt is roughly 40 percent sodium by weight, which is why food labels list sodium and not salt.

That distribution matters for how the topic should be framed. Sodium intake is largely a property of the food supply rather than a personal failing, and reformulation by manufacturers moves population intake far more than individual vigilance does. Salt is also not a villain: it is the vehicle for iodine in iodized salt, it makes food palatable, and food that people do not want to eat has no nutritional value at all. The useful skill is reading a Nutrition Facts label and knowing roughly where a day's total lands — not anxiety about any single meal.

Replacing some sodium chloride with potassium chloride lowers sodium intake and raises potassium intake at once, and in one large trial it reduced strokes. But potassium is cleared by the kidney, and people with reduced kidney function, or taking potassium-sparing diuretics, ACE inhibitors, or angiotensin receptor blockers, can accumulate dangerous levels. This is the clearest illustration of why nutrition education stops where individual clinical care begins: the same intervention that helps one population can harm another, and only a clinician who knows a specific person can tell which is which. Key points
  • Sodium and chloride rule the extracellular fluid; potassium rules the intracellular fluid, a separation maintained by the Na+/K+-ATPase (3 Na out, 2 K in, per ATP).
  • Water follows solute osmotically, so sodium balance sets extracellular volume and therefore blood pressure.
  • Strong evidence: lowering sodium lowers blood pressure. Moderate evidence: raising potassium lowers it too, mainly in people with hypertension. Weaker evidence: the size of the resulting reduction in cardiovascular events.
  • About 70 percent of dietary sodium comes from packaged and restaurant foods; 1 teaspoon of salt is about 2,300 mg sodium.

The chapter in one breath

  • Minerals are inorganic elements that supply 0 kcal/g (versus carbohydrate and protein at 4, fat at 9, alcohol at 7) yet are required for structure, signaling, and enzyme function.
  • Major minerals — calcium, phosphorus, magnesium, sodium, potassium, chloride — are needed above 100 mg/day; calcium RDA is 1,000 mg/d for adults 19–50, 1,200 mg/d for women 51+ and men 71+, and 1,300 mg/d for ages 9–18.
  • Trace minerals work in mcg-to-mg amounts inside specific proteins, and their deficiency-to-toxicity window is narrow — selenium RDA 55 mcg/d against a UL of 400 mcg/d.
  • Bioavailability is the real variable: phytate, oxalate, tannins, and mineral–mineral competition reduce absorption; vitamin C, MFP factor, and soaking, sprouting, or fermenting increase it.
  • Iron comes as well-absorbed heme (15–35 percent) and meal-sensitive nonheme (2–20 percent); because iron excretion is unregulated, hepcidin controls balance at absorption.
  • Iron deficiency runs depleted stores → early functional deficiency → microcytic, hypochromic anemia, so a normal hemoglobin does not rule out depletion — and not every anemia is iron.
  • Water is 50–70 percent of body weight and serves as solvent, transport, coolant, cushion, and reactant; total-water AI is 3.7 L/day for men and 2.7 L/day for women, including food.
  • Electrolytes set fluid distribution: lowering sodium lowers blood pressure (strong evidence), raising potassium does too but on a smaller trial base and mainly in people with hypertension (moderate evidence), while the effect of either on cardiovascular events rests on fewer trials still (weaker evidence).

ReferenceWords worth knowing

A quick reference for the terms in this chapter — skim it now, or circle back whenever a word trips you up.

Adequate Intake (AI)
A reference intake used when evidence is insufficient to set an RDA; based on observed intakes of apparently healthy groups. Sodium, potassium, chloride, fluoride, and water all use AIs.
Bioavailability
The proportion of an ingested nutrient that is actually absorbed and available for use — for minerals, heavily modified by the rest of the meal.
Chronic Disease Risk Reduction Intake (CDRR)
A reference value introduced in 2019 for sodium (2,300 mg/day for adults), marking an intake above which reduction is expected to lower chronic disease risk.
Dental fluorosis
White flecking to brown mottling of tooth enamel caused by excess fluoride during the years permanent teeth are forming.
Electrolyte
A mineral salt that dissociates into charged ions in solution, conducting electricity and governing fluid distribution; chiefly sodium, potassium, and chloride.
Extracellular fluid (ECF)
Body water outside cells — interstitial fluid plus blood plasma; about one-third of total body water, dominated by sodium and chloride.
Ferritin
The storage form of iron and the usual laboratory marker of iron stores; also an acute-phase reactant, so inflammation can falsely raise it.
Goiter
Enlargement of the thyroid gland, classically from iodine deficiency as the gland strains to make hormone without its raw material.
Heme iron
Iron bound within a porphyrin ring, found in meat, poultry, and fish; absorbed at roughly 15–35 percent and largely unaffected by other meal components.
Hepcidin
A liver hormone that controls iron absorption by degrading ferroportin; it rises with high iron stores and with inflammation, shutting the gate.
Hyponatremia
Blood sodium below the normal range, from sodium loss or from excess water; symptoms range from headache and nausea to confusion and seizures.
Insensible water loss
Unnoticed daily water loss through the skin and exhaled air, roughly 700–1,000 mL per day in an adult at rest.
Intracellular fluid (ICF)
Body water inside cells; about two-thirds of total body water, dominated by potassium, magnesium, and phosphate.
Iron-deficiency anemia
The final stage of iron depletion, in which hemoglobin falls and red cells are microcytic (MCV below about 80 fL) and hypochromic.
Major mineral (macromineral)
A mineral required in amounts greater than 100 mg per day: calcium, phosphorus, magnesium, sodium, potassium, chloride, sulfur.
Metabolic water
Water generated as a byproduct of oxidizing carbohydrate, fat, and protein — roughly 250–350 mL per day.
Nonheme iron
Free ionic iron from plants, eggs, dairy, and fortified grains; absorbed at roughly 2–20 percent and strongly modified by enhancers and inhibitors.
Phytate (phytic acid)
A phosphorus storage compound in whole grains, legumes, nuts, and seeds that binds zinc, iron, calcium, and magnesium and lowers their absorption.
Salt sensitivity
The tendency for blood pressure to change appreciably with sodium intake; more common with existing hypertension, older age, and among Black adults.
Sodium–potassium pump
The Na+/K+-ATPase, which moves three sodium ions out and two potassium ions into the cell per ATP, maintaining the membrane potential.
Tolerable Upper Intake Level (UL)
The highest daily intake unlikely to cause harm in almost all people; exceeding it raises risk of toxicity, as with selenium above 400 mcg/day.
Trace mineral
A mineral required in amounts below 100 mg per day, including iron, zinc, iodine, selenium, copper, manganese, fluoride, chromium, and molybdenum.

Check yourself

1. A student says minerals must supply some energy because they are called nutrients. Correct the statement and give the energy yields that do apply to the energy nutrients.
Minerals and water supply 0 kcal per gram. Only carbohydrate (4 kcal/g), protein (4 kcal/g), fat (9 kcal/g), and alcohol (7 kcal/g) yield energy. Minerals are inorganic elements used for structure, signaling, and enzyme function, not as fuel.
2. Define the cutoff that separates major minerals from trace minerals, and explain why the label "trace" does not mean "less important."
Major minerals are needed in amounts greater than 100 mg per day; trace minerals in smaller amounts. The division is about quantity, not importance — iodine is needed in micrograms, but deficiency causes goiter, hypothyroidism, and impaired fetal brain development.
3. State the calcium RDA for a 30-year-old adult, a 60-year-old woman, and a 15-year-old, and explain why the teenager's is highest.
1,000 mg/day at age 30; 1,200 mg/day for a woman of 60; 1,300 mg/day at age 15. Adolescence is when the skeleton is being built and peak bone mass accrued, so the requirement is highest then. Adult pregnancy does not raise the requirement, because absorption efficiency rises instead.
4. Why can someone have a chronically low calcium intake and still show a normal serum calcium?
Serum calcium is tightly defended by parathyroid hormone, calcitriol, and calcitonin, which draw on the skeleton to hold blood levels steady. The cost is paid in bone mineral over years, so a normal chemistry panel cannot confirm adequate intake — intake history and bone density can.
5. The UL for magnesium is 350 mg/day, yet the RDA for an adult man over 31 is 420 mg/day. Explain why this is not a contradiction.
The magnesium UL applies only to magnesium from supplements and medications, not from food. It is set by the osmotic laxative effect of concentrated magnesium salts, a problem that food-borne magnesium does not produce.
6. Give the RDA for iodine in a nonpregnant adult and in pregnancy, and name the consequences of deficiency at each stage.
150 mcg/day for adults, rising to 220 mcg/day in pregnancy and 290 mcg/day in lactation. Deficiency causes goiter and hypothyroidism in the adult; in pregnancy it impairs fetal brain development and has been the leading preventable cause of intellectual disability worldwide. The adult UL is 1,100 mcg/day, and excess iodine can also disturb thyroid function.
7. Selenium's RDA is 55 mcg/day and its UL is 400 mcg/day. Use that pair of numbers to explain a general principle about trace minerals.
The window between requirement and toxicity is narrow — only about seven-fold — so "more" is not a safety margin. Toxicity (selenosis) presents as brittle nails, hair loss, garlic-smelling breath, and gastrointestinal upset, while deficiency in selenium-poor regions produced Keshan cardiomyopathy.
8. Compare heme and nonheme iron by source, typical absorption, and sensitivity to the rest of the meal.
Heme iron comes from meat, poultry, and fish, absorbs at about 15–35 percent, and is largely unaffected by other foods; nonheme iron comes from plants, eggs, dairy, and fortified grains, absorbs at about 2–20 percent, and is highly sensitive to the meal. Enhancers include vitamin C and the MFP factor; inhibitors include phytates, tannins in tea and coffee, oxalates, and high-dose calcium supplements.
9. Explain why iron balance is regulated at absorption rather than at excretion, and name the hormone responsible.
The body has no regulated route for excreting iron — losses are passive, through shed cells and bleeding — so the only control point is how much enters. The liver hormone hepcidin sets that gate by degrading ferroportin; hepcidin rises with high iron stores and with inflammation, which is why chronic inflammation can lock iron away in storage.
10. Sequence the three stages of iron deficiency with the laboratory change that marks each, and explain why a normal hemoglobin does not rule out a problem.
Stage 1, depleted stores — serum ferritin falls; stage 2, early functional deficiency — transferrin saturation falls and total iron-binding capacity rises; stage 3, iron-deficiency anemia — hemoglobin and hematocrit fall with a microcytic, hypochromic picture. Anemia is the last stage, so stores can be substantially depleted while hemoglobin is still normal. Note too that macrocytic anemia points instead toward vitamin B12 or folate.
11. State the Adequate Intake for total water by sex, say what "total" includes, and explain what is wrong with treating "eight glasses a day" as the requirement.
3.7 L/day for men 19 and older and 2.7 L/day for women 19 and older, counting beverages plus the water in food (3.0 L and 2.2 L respectively from beverages). "Eight glasses" is roughly 1.9 L, is not a DRI, has no clear evidentiary origin, and ignores food entirely. Needs vary widely with heat, activity, illness, and body size, and AIs describe populations rather than individual targets.
12. Describe what the sodium–potassium pump does, and use it to explain how sodium intake connects to blood pressure. Then state which part of the sodium evidence is strong and which is weaker.
The Na+/K+-ATPase moves three sodium ions out of the cell and two potassium ions in per ATP, keeping sodium extracellular and potassium intracellular; because water follows solute by osmosis, sodium balance sets extracellular fluid volume and therefore blood pressure. Strong evidence: randomized trials consistently show that lowering sodium lowers blood pressure, with larger effects in salt-sensitive groups. Moderate evidence: raising potassium lowers blood pressure as well, but on a smaller trial base and most clearly in people who already have hypertension. Weaker evidence: how much any of this reduces heart attacks and strokes rests on a small number of long trials in specific populations, and the risk of very low sodium intakes remains disputed.
