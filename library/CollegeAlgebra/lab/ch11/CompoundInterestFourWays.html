Compound Interest, Four Ways

One deposit. Same rate. Same number of years. The only difference is how often the bank stops and adds the interest in — once a year, once a month, once a day, or without ever stopping. Move the sliders, then walk through where each dollar figure actually comes from.

Two errors that ruin the answer. r goes in as a decimal — 5% is 0.05, not 5. And n counts periods per year, not in total; monthly is always n = 12 whether t is 1 year or 40. The formula needs n > 0 and 1 + r/n > 0, which is automatic for any real deposit.

Where that number comes from
Cut the year in half and the dollar grows twice. The two skinny strips are the ordinary interest, r. The little green corner is interest earned by interest — r2/4 — and it is the entire reason a shorter compounding period pays more.
