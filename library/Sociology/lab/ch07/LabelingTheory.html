The Label That Sticks

Almost everyone breaks a rule at some point, and almost no one builds a life out of it. Edwin Lemert called that ordinary rule-breaking primary deviance. What turns it into secondary deviance — deviance performed from inside a deviant identity — is not the act. It is what other people do next. Who worked this out

What labeling theory does not explain. The model starts after the act, so it has little to say about why the primary deviance happened in the first place. Critics add that it can slide toward treating real harm as merely a matter of definition, and that some labels deter rather than amplify. The defensible claim is narrower, and well supported: reactions are part of the causal story, and official responses meant to reduce deviance sometimes manufacture it.
