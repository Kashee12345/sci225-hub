Isolation and the Human

We assume that a baby raised among people will simply become a person — that language, attachment, and a sense of self arrive on their own. The only evidence about what happens otherwise comes from children who were hidden away, and from a few studies nobody would approve today. “There is no sense of ‘I’ without its correlative sense of you, or he, or they.”

Charles Horton Cooley, Human Nature and the Social Order (1902). His looking-glass self and George Herbert Mead’s account of the self emerging through role-taking both require other people to already be there. Isolation is what it looks like when that requirement goes unmet.
Read these honestly. None of them are experiments on humans — they are crimes and disasters, described afterward. Every case is confounded: starvation, beating, and possible disability from birth are tangled together with the isolation itself, and no child can be re-run as a control. The bars above are a qualitative summary of what observers reported, not measured scores. The defensible conclusion is not “nurture wins” — it is that the capacities we call natural do not appear without other people, and that the same deprivation costs more the longer it runs.
