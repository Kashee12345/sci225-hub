Keeping the levels straight
An institution is a pattern, not a place.

A social institution is an enduring cluster of norms, statuses, roles, and organizations that a society develops around a persistent need. Keep three levels apart: the family is an institution, the household down the street is a group, and a school district is an organization within the institution of education. Institutions outlast every person who passes through them — which is exactly what makes them social facts in Émile Durkheim’s sense: external to the individual, and exerting constraint on them.
Three cautions before you use this. First, Robert K. Merton’s two axes are separate: recognized vs unrecognized, and helps vs harms. A dysfunction can be perfectly visible or entirely unnoticed — so "latent" is not a polite word for "bad."

Second, always ask functional for whom? Merton insisted that a consequence beneficial to one group may be damaging to another, which is the precise point where functionalism and conflict theory meet rather than merely disagree.

Third, resist the functionalist fallacy: that an institution exists does not prove it is necessary, efficient, or good. Merton’s own repair was functional alternatives — the need may be perfectly real while the current arrangement is only one of several ways of meeting it.
