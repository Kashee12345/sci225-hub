Crowds, Mobs, and Mass Behavior

Collective behavior is relatively spontaneous, loosely structured action by people who are responding to the same thing at the same time — and it is not one thing. Sociologists sort it with two questions: are these people in the same place, and what, if anything, do they share? Tap each form and watch the gathering rearrange itself.

Read this crowd through a paradigm
The crowd does not have a mind. Gustave Le Bon’s contagion theory (1895) claimed crowds hypnotize their members into a single irrational “collective mind.” Sociologists reject it: crowds are mixed, most members never act, and Le Bon wrote as a frightened critic of the masses. Convergence theory answers him from the other side — the crowd does not create the disposition, people who already share one converge. Emergent norm theory is the account most sociologists use today.

Crowd vs. mass. Herbert Blumer’s line: a crowd is people in one another’s presence; a mass is a large number of people who are physically dispersed and anonymous to one another but attending to the same object. A public is the arguing kind of mass — dispersed people concerned with the same issue who do not agree about it.
