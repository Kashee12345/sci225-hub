The Five Steps

A one‑sample test is not a formula to memorise; it is a procedure, and it is the same procedure every time. State the hypotheses. Fix the error rate you will tolerate and check that the model is allowed. Standardise the result. Find the tail area. Decide, and say what it means in the sentence of the problem. Change the parameter, change the units, change the direction of the claim — the five steps do not move. Below you can pick a study, set the world it comes from, actually draw a sample, and walk the same five steps over it while every number recomputes. Then draw a different sample and watch the arithmetic change while the procedure does not.

Where the wording goes wrong

The order is not decorative. Steps 1 and 2 must be finished before the data is looked at, because both of them are choices that the data could otherwise be allowed to make for you. If you pick the direction of Ha after seeing which way the sample fell, you get to test whichever tail happens to look good — and the true false‑alarm rate of that procedure is roughly double the α printed next to it. If you pick α after seeing the p‑value, α has stopped being a risk you accepted and become a description of a number you already have. Steps 3 and 4 are pure arithmetic with no judgement in them at all. Step 5 is where judgement returns, and where the sentence has to be about the population, in its own units, with the direction and the level named.
“Fail to reject” is not “accept.” A large p‑value means the data would be unremarkable if H0 held. It does not mean H0 holds, because the same data would usually also be unremarkable under a great many nearby alternatives. Set n to its smallest value on any study here, push the truth well away from the null value, and watch test after test fail to reject something that is plainly false: the procedure did not find evidence, which is a statement about the instrument, not a verdict on the world. A test can accumulate evidence against H0 and never for it.
The p‑value is not the probability that H0 is true. Every number in step 4 is computed inside the world H0 describes; the calculation never asks how plausible that world was to begin with, and nothing in the five steps supplies that information. p is P(a result at least this extreme  |  H0 true), not P(H0 true  |  this result). In the same way, α is not the chance you are wrong on this study — it is the share of studies that would reject in the long run if H0 were true, which is exactly what the grid of repeated tests measures when the truth slider is parked on the null value.
One last habit worth building: after step 5, ask the question the test never asked. A rejection says a difference is more than noise; it does not say the difference is large enough to act on. Rejecting H0 for a bottling line running 0.3 mL off target is honest statistics and terrible advice. Report the estimate and an interval alongside the decision, and let the size of the effect argue for itself.
