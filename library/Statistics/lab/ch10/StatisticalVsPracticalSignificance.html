Statistical vs Practical Significance

A blood-pressure drug truly lowers systolic pressure by 0.4 mmHg — an amount no patient would ever feel. Run it as a 40-patient pilot and nothing shows. Run it across half a million health records and the same trivial effect comes back with a p-value smaller than one in a hundred billion. Slide the sample size and watch it happen.

What the p-value does as the study grows

The curve is the p-value a trial would get if it landed exactly on the true effect; the solid dot is the trial you actually ran, knocked off the curve by sampling noise. Any non-zero effect, however small, eventually drags this curve to the floor.

How big could the real effect be?

—
Two different questions. “Is the effect distinguishable from zero?” is answered by the p-value and is answered better by a bigger n. “Is the effect big enough to bother with?” is answered by the size of the effect and its interval — and no sample size on earth can turn a small answer into a large one.
