Watch Bias Appear

In this city we are allowed to know the answer: 68% of bus riders are satisfied. Now run a survey and see what it reports. Choosing people at random is only half the job — the other half is who among them chooses to answer. Turn that dial and watch the estimate walk away from a truth that never moved.

How many people answered tells you about the survey’s noise. How they were chosen — and who among them chose to reply — tells you about its aim. Only one of those two problems is cured by collecting more data.
A margin of error cannot see bias. The ± is built from the respondents alone, as though the method were pointed at the truth. Run the voluntary-response poll: thousands of replies, a margin of error under a single point, and an answer roughly 50 points wrong — with nothing anywhere in the arithmetic to warn you.
