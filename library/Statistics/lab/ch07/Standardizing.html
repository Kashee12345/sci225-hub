Standardizing

There are infinitely many normal curves — one for every pair of μ and σ — and nobody wants a table for each. So we stop measuring in beats, millimeters, or grams and start measuring in standard deviations from the mean. Subtract μ, divide by σ, and every normal curve in the world collapses onto one.

The recipe, and its reverse. Standardizing is z = (x − μ) ÷ σ — subtract the center, then divide by the spread. Going back the other way is x = μ + zσ. Standardizing any set of values, normal or not, always yields a mean of exactly 0 and an SD of exactly 1. But when the values were normal to begin with, something stronger happens: the result is not merely centered and rescaled, it is N(0, 1) itself — the standard normal. That is the curve every table, every calculator key, and every software routine is built around, and it is why one of them is enough.
Three things standardizing does not do. It does not make data normal: subtracting and dividing by constants is a linear rescaling, so skew, outliers, and extra peaks all survive untouched. The percentages on this page come from the normal model — the z-score itself is only arithmetic and assumes nothing, but reading a percentile off it requires that the values really do follow this curve. And a z-score is not a grade: negative is not "bad" and positive is not "good," since which direction counts as trouble depends entirely on the variable.
