The Empirical Rule in Action

Once a measurement is roughly normal, two numbers answer almost any question about it. The 68–95–99.7 rule chops the bell into eight slices whose sizes never change — so you can convert a mean and a standard deviation into real percentages, and real head counts, without a table or a calculator. The eight slices, memorised once: 0.15%  |  2.35%  |  13.5%  |  34%  ||  34%  |  13.5%  |  2.35%  |  0.15%, running from the far left tail to the far right. They add to 100%, and every empirical-rule answer is just a sum of some of them. Notice the halves: 34 + 34 = 68, then add 13.5 twice to get 95, then 2.35 twice to get 99.7.
Three limits worth stating out loud. First, the rule is a rounding — the exact areas are 68.27%, 95.45% and 99.73%, and the "2.35%" slice is really 2.14%; the panel above shows both so you can see the gap. Second, it only holds when the distribution is approximately normal and roughly symmetric; on skewed data it can produce answers that are not merely imprecise but impossible. Third, it describes what fraction of a group falls in a range — it never tells you where any one individual will land.
