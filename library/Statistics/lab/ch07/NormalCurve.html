One smooth, symmetric, bell-shaped curve describes an enormous amount of real data — and it is pinned down by just two numbers. The mean says where the bell sits; the standard deviation says how wide it spreads. Drag them and watch the curve slide and stretch.

How much area sits inside ± kσ?
The empirical rule (68–95–99.7): in any normal distribution about 68% of values fall within 1 SD of the mean, about 95% within 2 SDs, and about 99.7% within 3. Move μ and σ wherever you like — the endpoints change, the percentages do not.
Two things the curve is not. The height of the curve is a density, not a probability — a single exact value on a continuous scale has probability 0, so probability lives in area, never in height. And "normal" here is only the name of a shape: it does not mean typical, healthy, or correct, and a histogram that merely looks bell-ish is not automatically normal.
