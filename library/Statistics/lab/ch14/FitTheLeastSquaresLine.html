Fit the Least-Squares Line

The points below are fixed. Your job is the line. For any line you draw, each point misses it by a vertical gap — a residual. Square every gap, add them up, and you get the sum of squared residuals (SSR). The least-squares line is the single line that makes that total as small as it can possibly be. Tilt the slope and shift the intercept below and watch SSR fall — every square, the running total, and the fit landscape recompute the instant the line moves.

Why squared, and why vertical? The gaps are measured straight up and down, because the line predicts y from x and a residual is a prediction error in y. Each gap is squared before adding, so a miss of 6 counts 4× as much as a miss of 3 — the line is pulled hardest toward the points it is furthest from, and squaring keeps positive and negative misses from cancelling. On the plot, a residual literally becomes a square whose area is that penalty; least-squares shrinks the total shaded area to the smallest it can be.
The minimum is unique — and it sits on the mean point. There is exactly one least-squares line, given by b = Σ(x−x̄)(y−ȳ) ÷ Σ(x−x̄)² and a = ȳ − b·x̄. At that line, and only there, the residuals add up to zero and the line passes through the mean point (x̄, ȳ). Snapping does the algebra for you — but sliding there by hand shows you it really is the bottom of the bowl.
