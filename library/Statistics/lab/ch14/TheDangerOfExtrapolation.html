The Danger of Extrapolation

A pediatrician measures children ages 2 to 9 and fits a straight line to height — and within that window the line is superb. But a regression line only knows the data it was built from. Slide the prediction age below and watch what happens when the line is pushed past the last data point: inside the range it tracks reality almost perfectly, yet extended to adulthood it insists a grown-up should be over eleven feet tall. Interpolation vs extrapolation. Predicting inside the range of the x-values used to build the line is interpolation — the data vouch for it. Predicting outside that range is extrapolation, and it rests on a promise the data never made: that the same straight-line pattern keeps going. Here the real relationship bends — children stop growing — so the further out you push, the more the line's rigid slope betrays you.
A high R² does not license extrapolation. The line fits the childhood data beautifully, and no amount of that good fit tells you what happens at age 30 — because there is no age-30 data underneath it. Trust a regression line for prediction only across the span of x it was actually shown.
