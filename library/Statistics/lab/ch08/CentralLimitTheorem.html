Averaging is a shape-destroying operation. A single observation carries the population's whole personality — its floor, its long tail, its lumps. Add several together and the extremes have to conspire to survive: one huge value gets diluted by the ordinary ones beside it. What comes out the other side keeps only two facts about where it came from — the center μ and the spread σ/√n — and forgets the rest. The population's shape is not inherited by x̄. It is averaged away.
Read the theorem carefully — four things it does not say. (1) Your data do not become normal. The CLT is about the sampling distribution of x̄ — the pattern the mean would trace out over repeated studies. A sample of 5,000 from a skewed population still has a skewed histogram, and always will. Panel 1 above never moves. (2) “n ≥ 30” is a rule of thumb, not a theorem. The theorem describes the limit as n → ∞ and promises nothing at any particular n. How fast the bell arrives depends on the population: n = 30 is generous for the flat one and badly insufficient for the extreme one. (3) It needs conditions. Observations must be independent, and the population must have a finite variance. A population with tails heavy enough that σ does not exist — the Cauchy is the standard example — never converges to a bell at any n. (4) We divided by the true σ here. In real work σ is unknown and you divide by the sample's own s instead. That adds a second source of randomness on top of everything shown above, and it is the entire reason the t‑distribution exists.
