Innate Immunity

Long before your body knows the name of an invader, it is already fighting back. Innate immunity is the defense you were born with — a set of barriers, cells, and chemical alarms that respond within seconds to minutes, the same way every time, to almost anything that doesn't belong. It doesn't need to have met the microbe before, and it doesn't remember it afterward. From the skin that keeps microbes out, to the neutrophils that swarm a wound, to the fever that makes your patient miserable at 3 a.m., innate immunity is the fast, blunt, always-on first responder of the immune system. This chapter walks through its layers in the order the body meets an invader — and shows you why so much of nursing is really the art of protecting, and reading, these defenses.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Contrast innate and adaptive immunity by speed, specificity, and memory.
  • Describe the first-line defenses — skin, mucous membranes, and normal flora — and how each keeps microbes out.
  • Identify the major phagocytic cells and walk through the steps of phagocytosis.
  • Explain the inflammatory response and relate the four cardinal signs to what is happening in the tissue.
  • Describe how pyrogens reset the hypothalamic thermostat to produce a fever, and weigh its benefits and dangers.
  • Summarize the outcomes of the complement cascade — opsonization, inflammation, and cytolysis.
  • Explain how interferons protect cells from viral infection.
  • Connect each of these defenses to concrete nursing assessments at the bedside.

Section 1First line: barriers

Two immune systems, one goal

Your body defends itself with two overlapping systems. Innate immunity is present from birth, responds within seconds to minutes, and treats all invaders with the same non-specific weapons — it has no memory, so the tenth exposure gets the same response as the first. Adaptive immunity (the subject of the next chapter) is slower to start, exquisitely specific to one particular microbe, and it remembers, which is why a second exposure is met faster and harder. Innate immunity buys the days that adaptive immunity needs to gear up. In practice they work as one team, but learning them separately makes each far easier to understand.

Innate immunity is often taught as three lines of defense. The first line is the set of physical, chemical, and biological barriers that stop microbes from ever entering — the focus of this section. The second line is the internal response that kicks in once something breaches a barrier: phagocytes, inflammation, fever, and antimicrobial proteins (Sections 2–5). Adaptive immunity is the third line.

Skin and mucous membranes

The skin is the single largest organ of the body and the most important physical barrier there is. Its outer layer, the epidermis, is built of tightly packed cells stuffed with keratin and stacked into dead, shingled sheets that are constantly shed — taking any attached microbes with them. Most bacteria simply cannot penetrate intact skin. This is exactly why every break in the skin matters clinically: an IV site, a surgical incision, a pressure injury, or a simple scratch is a door in the wall, and the nurse's job of keeping that door clean and dressed is frontline infection control.

Where the body opens to the outside — the respiratory, digestive, urinary, and reproductive tracts — the barrier is not dry skin but a mucous membrane. These surfaces are kept moist and coated with mucus, a sticky gel that traps microbes before they can reach the cells beneath. In the airway, cells carrying tiny hair-like cilia beat in waves to sweep the trapped debris up and out of the lungs — the so-called mucociliary escalator. Tears, saliva, and the flushing action of urine physically wash microbes away. Understanding this tells you why an intubated patient, whose tube bypasses the mucociliary escalator, is at such high risk for pneumonia, and why we encourage coughing, deep breathing, and adequate hydration.

Chemical and microbial reinforcements

Barriers are not just walls; they are hostile chemical environments. The skin secretes sebum (oily, acidic) and sweat, keeping the surface at a low, unfriendly pH. Lysozyme, an enzyme found in tears, saliva, and mucus, chews apart bacterial cell walls. Stomach acid is brutally low in pH, and the vagina is kept acidic by resident lactobacilli. On top of all this sits the normal flora — the harmless microbes that already occupy the skin and mucosal surfaces. By taking up space and consuming nutrients, they crowd out would-be pathogens, a protection called competitive exclusion. Wipe out that flora — with broad-spectrum antibiotics, for instance — and opportunists like Candida or Clostridioides difficile move into the vacancy.

Aseptic technique, clean dressings, and removing devices as soon as they are no longer needed are how nurses substitute for the skin the patient no longer has intact.

Section 2Second line: cells

The white blood cell responders

Once a microbe slips past the barriers, it meets the cellular second line — the white blood cells, or leukocytes. These are made in the bone marrow and patrol the blood and tissues. Two of them do most of the eating, and they are the cells you must know. Neutrophils are the most numerous white cell and the first to arrive at an infection — fast, aggressive, short-lived front-line soldiers. Macrophages (from circulating monocytes that mature after leaving the blood) are the larger, longer-lived cleanup crew; they patrol the tissues, engulf microbes and debris, and help kick off other immune responses. A microbe-eating cell of either kind is called a phagocyte, and the act of eating is phagocytosis (Greek for "cell eating").

These cells don't reach a wound by accident. Chemical signals released at the site of infection — from damaged cells, from the microbes themselves, and from complement (Section 5) — draw phagocytes toward the trouble, a directed migration called chemotaxis. To get out of the bloodstream, circulating white cells first stick to the vessel wall (margination) and then squeeze between the cells of the vessel lining into the tissue, a maneuver called diapedesis (or extravasation).

How a phagocyte recognizes an enemy

How does a phagocyte tell "self" from "invader"? It carries pattern-recognition receptors (PRRs) — sensors that latch onto molecular signatures shared by whole classes of microbes but absent from human cells, called pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs). A classic PAMP is the lipopolysaccharide (LPS) in the outer membrane of Gram-negative bacteria. Because these patterns are broad, one receptor recognizes many microbes — the hallmark of innate, non-specific immunity. Recognition is made even easier when a microbe is coated with tags called opsonins (antibodies or the complement protein C3b); this coating, opsonization, is like painting a target "eat me" and dramatically speeds engulfment.

Phagocytosis, step by step

The killing sequence is worth memorizing because it recurs throughout immunology. (1) Chemotaxis — the phagocyte migrates toward the microbe. (2) Adherence — it binds the microbe, helped by opsonins. (3) Ingestion — the cell membrane wraps around the microbe and pinches off an internal bubble called a phagosome. (4) Fusion — the phagosome merges with a lysosome (full of digestive enzymes) to form a phagolysosome. (5) Killing and digestion — enzymes, reactive oxygen (the "respiratory burst"), and low pH destroy the microbe. (6) Exocytosis — the indigestible remains are dumped back out. The spent neutrophils, dead microbes, and tissue fluid left behind are what we recognize as pus.

Read the differential before you read the fever. A rising neutrophil count (neutrophilia), often with immature "bands," points toward a bacterial infection — the marrow is shipping out first responders. A very low neutrophil count (neutropenia), common in chemotherapy patients, strips away the front-line phagocytes: such a patient can be gravely infected yet show little pus, redness, or swelling, because there are too few neutrophils left to produce those local signs. A new fever may be the only clue — which is why fever in a neutropenic patient is a medical emergency, and why neutropenic precautions exist.

  • Phagocytes reach infection by chemotaxis, margination, and diapedesis.
  • They recognize microbes by PRRs binding PAMPs, and engulf opsonized microbes fastest.

Section 3Inflammation

The four cardinal signs

Inflammation is the tissue's coordinated reaction to injury or infection, and it produces four classic signs that have been recognized since antiquity: redness, heat, swelling, and pain (in Latin, rubor, calor, tumor, dolor). A fifth is often added: loss of function (functio laesa). Far from being the enemy, these signs are the visible proof that the second line has arrived and is working. When you assess a wound, an IV site, or a joint, you are reading the four cardinal signs directly — they are the everyday language of infection assessment.

What is actually happening in the tissue

Each sign has a mechanism. When cells are injured or microbes are detected, resident mast cells and damaged tissue release chemical mediators — above all histamine, along with prostaglandins, bradykinin, and cytokines. These do two things. First, they cause vasodilation, widening the blood vessels so more blood floods the area — that extra warm blood is the redness and the heat. Second, they increase vascular permeability, making the vessel walls leaky so fluid, proteins, and cells pour out into the tissue — that accumulation of fluid (edema) is the swelling. The pressure of that swelling on nerve endings, along with mediators like prostaglandins and bradykinin that sensitize those nerves, produces the pain. The whole point is to deliver phagocytes and clotting proteins to the site and to wall the infection off with a mesh of fibrin so it cannot spread.

Acute, chronic, and when inflammation turns dangerous

Acute inflammation is short-lived and resolves as the threat is cleared. When a threat can't be cleared, inflammation becomes chronic: macrophages persist and may wall off the invader in a ball of immune cells called a granuloma (classically in tuberculosis). Localized inflammation is protective — but when the same mediators spill into the bloodstream system-wide, the result can be catastrophic. In sepsis, a body-wide inflammatory response to infection causes widespread vasodilation and leaky vessels, dropping the blood pressure and starving organs of oxygen. The very tools that heal a local wound, unleashed everywhere at once, can kill.

The signs you are trained to fear are the immune system working. Warmth, redness, and swelling at a wound mean inflammation is active — useful locally, but a red flag when it spreads. Learn to escalate the pattern: a hot, swollen, red, painful limb with fever and a climbing heart rate may be moving from local inflammation toward systemic infection. Early recognition of sepsis is one of the highest-value skills a nurse has.

  • Mast cells and injured tissue release histamine and other mediators that cause vasodilation and increased vascular permeability.
  • Inflammation delivers phagocytes and walls off infection with fibrin.
  • Localized inflammation heals; systemic inflammation (sepsis) is life-threatening.

Section 4Fever

Resetting the thermostat

Fever (pyrexia) is an abnormally high core body temperature, and it is not the infection getting the upper hand — it is a deliberate, regulated defense. Your temperature is controlled by a "thermostat" in the hypothalamus, which normally holds the body near 37°C (98.6°F). Fever happens when substances called pyrogens raise the hypothalamic set-point. There are two kinds. Exogenous pyrogens come from outside the body — most famously the LPS of Gram-negative bacteria (also called endotoxin). Endogenous pyrogens are made by the patient's own immune cells: the cytokines interleukin-1 (IL-1), IL-6, and tumor necrosis factor (TNF), released by macrophages when they meet a microbe. These pyrogens drive the production of prostaglandin E₂ in the hypothalamus, which is what actually pushes the set-point up.

Here is the key idea: once the set-point is raised, the body isn't "overheating" — it now believes 39°C is the correct temperature and works to reach it. That is why the onset of a fever brings shivering and a feeling of cold: muscles contract to generate heat and blood vessels constrict to conserve it, all to climb toward the new, higher target.

The three phases of a fever

A fever runs a predictable course, and recognizing the phase changes what you do. In the onset (chill phase), the set-point jumps; the patient feels cold, shivers, and has cool, pale skin as the body drives its temperature up. In the plateau (stadium), the body has reached the new set-point — the patient feels warm, flushed, and dry, and the temperature holds steady at its elevated level. In defervescence (the flush or crisis), pyrogens fall and the set-point drops back to normal; now the body is "too hot," so it sweats and flushes to shed the excess heat. Reading these phases is practical: a shivering patient is climbing a fever, and piling on blankets to stop the shivering only drives the temperature higher.

Friend or foe?

A moderate fever helps: higher temperatures slow the growth of many pathogens, speed up the enzymes of the immune response, and enhance the activity of phagocytes and other defenders. That is why routinely erasing every low fever with antipyretics is not always wise — the fever is doing work. But fever also has costs and limits. It raises metabolic demand, the heart rate, and fluid loss; in young children a rapid rise can trigger a febrile seizure; and a very high temperature — roughly above 40–41°C (104–106°F) — begins to denature the body's own proteins and becomes a medical emergency. Antipyretics such as acetaminophen and NSAIDs work by blocking prostaglandin production, lowering the set-point back down.

In some patients, the absence of fever is the alarm. Very old, very young, and immunocompromised patients — and those on steroids — may mount little or no fever even with serious infection. Never let a normal temperature falsely reassure you if the rest of the picture (confusion, low blood pressure, rising heart rate, low urine output) says sepsis. And treat a fever above 40°C, or any fever with altered mental status, as urgent.

Key points
  • Fever is a regulated rise in the hypothalamic set-point, not loss of control.
  • Exogenous pyrogens (e.g., LPS) and endogenous pyrogens (cytokines IL-1, IL-6, TNF) act via prostaglandin E₂.
  • Phases: onset (chills, climbing) → plateau (steady, high) → defervescence (sweating, falling).
  • Moderate fever aids defense; very high fever (>40°C) is dangerous, and some patients can't mount one at all.

Section 5Complement & interferons

Complement: a cascade of ready-made proteins

Complement is a set of about 30 blood proteins, made mostly by the liver, that circulate in an inactive form and wait. When triggered, they activate one another in sequence — a cascade, where each activated protein cuts and activates the next, amplifying the signal at every step. Three pathways can set it off: the classical pathway (triggered by antibodies bound to a microbe), the alternative pathway (triggered directly by microbial surfaces), and the lectin pathway (triggered by carbohydrates on microbes). All three converge on the central protein, C3, which is split into C3a and C3b — the hinge of the whole system.

From there, complement produces three life-saving outcomes. Opsonization: C3b coats the microbe as an opsonin, tagging it so phagocytes grab it far more easily (linking this section straight back to phagocytosis). Inflammation: the fragments C3a and C5a are anaphylatoxins and chemotactic factors — they trigger mast cells to release histamine and they call phagocytes to the scene. Cytolysis: the terminal proteins assemble into a ring called the membrane attack complex (MAC), which punches a hole in the microbe's membrane so its contents leak out and it bursts. Together, these are sometimes remembered as the three jobs of complement: tag, recruit, and destroy.

Interferons: the antiviral alarm

Barriers, phagocytes, and complement are formidable against bacteria — but a virus hides inside a host cell, where those defenses can't reach. For viruses, innate immunity has a different tool: interferons (IFNs), a family of cytokines named because they "interfere" with viral replication. When a cell is infected by a virus, it releases type I interferons (IFN-α and IFN-β). These don't save the dying cell that made them; instead they act as a warning to neighboring cells. A neighbor that receives the interferon signal switches on an antiviral state — it produces enzymes that degrade viral genetic material and shut down protein synthesis if a virus enters — so the virus finds no factory to hijack. A separate interferon, IFN-γ, mainly activates macrophages and bridges to adaptive immunity.

This matters clinically in two directions. The malaise, muscle aches, and fever of a viral illness like influenza are largely the effects of your own interferons working, not the virus directly. And because interferons are natural antivirals and immune modulators, purified versions are used as medications — for certain viral hepatitis infections, some cancers, and multiple sclerosis.

Complement deficiency has a signature. Patients who can't build a functioning MAC (terminal complement deficiency) are strikingly prone to Neisseria infections — recurrent meningococcal disease in particular. It's a neat illustration of how one missing innate protein leaves a specific hole in the defenses, and why complement isn't just textbook trivia.

Key points
  • Complement is a cascade of ~30 proteins; three pathways (classical, alternative, lectin) converge on C3.
  • Its three outcomes are opsonization (C3b), inflammation (C3a/C5a), and cytolysis (the MAC).
  • Interferons are antiviral cytokines: a virus-infected cell releases type I IFN to put neighboring cells into an antiviral state.
  • Interferons cause much of the "sick" feeling of viral illness and are used as drugs.

The chapter in one breath

  • Innate immunity is the fast, non-specific, memory-free defense you are born with; it buys time for adaptive immunity.
  • The first line keeps microbes out: skin and mucous membranes, chemical defenses (lysozyme, acid, sebum), and normal flora.
  • When barriers fail, phagocytes — neutrophils first, then macrophages — find microbes by chemotaxis and destroy them by phagocytosis.
  • Inflammation (redness, heat, swelling, pain) comes from histamine-driven vasodilation and leaky vessels; systemic, it becomes sepsis.
  • Fever is a raised hypothalamic set-point driven by pyrogens (LPS; cytokines IL-1/IL-6/TNF) via prostaglandin E₂ — helpful in moderation, dangerous when very high.
  • Complement tags (opsonization), recruits (inflammation), and destroys (MAC); interferons warn neighboring cells to resist viruses.
  • Nearly every innate defense maps onto a bedside assessment — skin integrity, the WBC differential, wound signs, temperature phase, and sepsis recognition.

Check yourself

1. In three words — speed, specificity, and memory — how does innate immunity differ from adaptive immunity?
Innate is fast, non-specific, and has no memory; adaptive is slower, highly specific, and remembers. Innate immunity responds the same way to almost any invader within minutes, buying the days that adaptive immunity needs to gear up a targeted response.
2. Name the three types of first-line barrier and give one example of each.
Physical (skin, mucous membranes), chemical (lysozyme, stomach acid, sebum), and biological (normal flora). All three keep microbes from ever entering; any break in the skin defeats the physical barrier.
3. What is the mucociliary escalator, and why is an intubated patient at higher risk of pneumonia?
The mucociliary escalator is the coordinated beating of airway cilia that sweeps mucus-trapped microbes up and out of the lungs. An endotracheal tube bypasses this defense, so trapped microbes are not cleared — raising the risk of ventilator-associated pneumonia.
4. Which phagocyte arrives first at an infection and is the most abundant white blood cell? What does a rising count of it suggest?
The neutrophil. A rising neutrophil count (neutrophilia), often with immature "bands," suggests a bacterial infection, because the marrow is releasing first responders.
5. Put the steps of phagocytosis in order and name the two internal vesicles formed.
Chemotaxis → adherence → ingestion → fusion → digestion → exocytosis; the microbe is enclosed in a phagosome, which fuses with a lysosome to form a phagolysosome. Enzymes and reactive oxygen inside the phagolysosome kill the microbe.
6. Define opsonization and name two opsonins. Why does it matter to a phagocyte?
Opsonization is coating a microbe with tags — antibody or complement C3b — that make it much easier to engulf. The tags act like an "eat me" label, so phagocytes adhere to and ingest the microbe far faster.
7. List the four cardinal signs of inflammation and match each to its mechanism.
Redness and heat = vasodilation (more warm blood); swelling = fluid leaking from permeable vessels (edema); pain = pressure on nerves plus sensitizing mediators. Histamine from mast cells is the central driver of the vascular changes.
8. A patient's local wound inflammation progresses to fever, low blood pressure, and a fast heart rate. What dangerous process should you suspect, and why is it so deadly?
Sepsis — a body-wide inflammatory response to infection. The same vasodilation and vascular leak that heal a local wound, unleashed system-wide, drop the blood pressure and starve organs of oxygen. Early recognition is critical.
9. Distinguish exogenous from endogenous pyrogens, and name the hypothalamic mediator through which they act.
Exogenous pyrogens come from outside the body (classically LPS/endotoxin of Gram-negative bacteria); endogenous pyrogens are the patient's own cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF). Both raise the hypothalamic set-point via prostaglandin E₂.
10. A patient is shivering and feels cold at the start of a fever. What is happening to the set-point, and why is loading on blankets counterproductive?
The set-point has jumped up, so the body shivers and constricts vessels to climb toward the new, higher target (the onset/chill phase). The temperature is already rising; adding blankets to stop the shivering only helps it climb higher.
11. State the three outcomes of complement activation and the key player in each.
Opsonization (C3b coats the microbe), inflammation (C3a and C5a are anaphylatoxins/chemotactic), and cytolysis (the membrane attack complex, C5b–C9, lyses the microbe). All three pathways converge on C3. "Tag, recruit, destroy."
12. How do interferons protect the body against a virus, and why do they make you feel sick?
A virus-infected cell releases type I interferon that warns neighboring cells to enter an antiviral state — degrading viral genetic material and halting protein synthesis so the virus finds no factory to hijack. Interferons themselves cause much of the fever, aches, and malaise of a viral illness.
