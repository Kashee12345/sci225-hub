Microbiology · Chapter 15

Infectious Diseases by Body System

Microbes do not respect the neat chapter boundaries of a textbook — they land somewhere on a real patient and cause trouble that a nurse has to recognize, report, and help treat. This chapter reorganizes what you already know about pathogens around the place they strike: the skin, the lungs, the gut, the urinary and reproductive tracts, and the bloodstream and brain. For each region we will name the usual offenders, describe what the infection looks like at the bedside, and connect it to the assessments and precautions that keep one patient's infection from becoming the whole unit's problem. Think of it as a field guide — organized the way you actually meet infections on the floor.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Recognize the common skin and wound infections — impetigo, cellulitis, and MRSA — and the assessment findings that flag them.
  • Distinguish the major respiratory infections (pneumonia, tuberculosis, influenza, strep throat) and the isolation precautions each requires.
  • Explain how gastrointestinal pathogens cause illness through infection versus toxin, and why C. difficile and norovirus demand soap-and-water hand hygiene.
  • Describe the pathogens and presentations of urinary tract infections and common sexually transmitted infections.
  • Recognize the warning signs of bacteremia, sepsis, and meningitis and the urgency each carries.
  • Match each body-system infection to its typical causative organism and route of transmission.
  • Connect bedside nursing actions — hand hygiene, device care, early recognition — to the prevention of healthcare-associated infection.

Section 1Skin & wound infections

The skin is a wall, until it isn't

Intact skin is one of the body's best defenses: a dry, slightly acidic, constantly shedding barrier that most microbes simply cannot cross. Skin and soft-tissue infections almost always begin where that wall is breached — a scratch, an insect bite, a surgical incision, a pressure injury, an IV site, or the cracked skin of eczema. Once organisms get past the surface, the two players you will meet again and again are Staphylococcus aureus and Streptococcus pyogenes (group A strep), both of which live harmlessly on skin and in noses until an opening lets them in.

What the infection looks like depends mostly on how deep it goes. Impetigo is a superficial infection of the outermost skin, common in children, producing fragile blisters that rupture into a golden, honey-colored crust. Cellulitis is deeper, involving the dermis and the fat beneath it: a spreading area of warmth, redness, swelling, and tenderness with poorly defined edges, often on a leg. Erysipelas is a brighter, sharply raised, well-demarcated cellulitis of the upper dermis, classically caused by group A strep. The deepest and most dangerous, necrotizing fasciitis, races along the tissue planes and produces pain out of proportion to what the skin shows — a surgical emergency, not an antibiotic-only problem.

MRSA and the infected wound

MRSA — methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus — is ordinary S. aureus that has acquired resistance to the entire penicillin family, so it shrugs off the drugs that would once have cured it. It is a leading cause of skin abscesses (patients often report a "spider bite" they never actually saw a spider make) and a notorious cause of surgical-site and device-related infections. MRSA is spread by contact — hands, shared equipment, contaminated surfaces — which is why colonized or infected patients are placed on contact precautions (gown and gloves) and why hand hygiene between patients is non-negotiable.

A wound infection announces itself with the classic signs of local inflammation — increasing redness, warmth, swelling, pain, and purulent (pus) drainage — sometimes with a spreading red border, foul odor, or fever. The single most useful nursing skill here is the ability to tell colonization from infection: nearly every open wound grows bacteria on a swab, but only a wound with the signs of tissue invasion needs antibiotics. A clean-looking chronic wound with a positive culture and no inflammation is usually colonized, and treating it does more harm than good.

Key points
  • Skin infections start where the barrier is broken; S. aureus and group A strep are the usual causes.
  • Depth drives the picture: impetigo (superficial) → cellulitis (dermis) → necrotizing fasciitis (surgical emergency).
  • MRSA resists the penicillin family, spreads by contact, and warrants contact precautions.
  • A wound with spreading redness, warmth, pain, and pus is infected; a positive swab without those signs is usually just colonized.

Section 2Respiratory infections

Upper tract, lower tract

The respiratory system is split into an upper tract (nose, sinuses, throat, larynx) and a lower tract (trachea, bronchi, and the alveoli of the lungs), and the division matters because lower-tract infections are generally more serious. Upper-tract infections — the common cold, most sore throats, sinusitis — are usually viral and self-limited. The important exception is strep throat (streptococcal pharyngitis), caused by group A strep, which produces a sudden painful sore throat, fever, and swollen tonsils without the cough and runny nose of a cold. It matters because untreated it can lead to rheumatic fever, so it is one of the few sore throats worth culturing and treating with antibiotics.

The lungs are supposed to be nearly sterile, defended by the mucus blanket, the sweeping cilia, and the cough reflex. When those defenses fail — because a patient is intubated, sedated, weak, or aspirating — organisms reach the alveoli and cause pneumonia. Nurses classify pneumonia by where it was acquired: community-acquired pneumonia (CAP), most often from Streptococcus pneumoniae; hospital-acquired and ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP), which involve tougher, more resistant organisms; and aspiration pneumonia, from inhaled mouth or stomach contents in patients with impaired swallowing or a depressed gag reflex.

Tuberculosis and influenza

Tuberculosis (TB), caused by the slow-growing acid-fast bacillus Mycobacterium tuberculosis, is the respiratory infection whose precautions you must never get wrong. TB spreads by tiny airborne droplet nuclei that hang in the air, so a suspected case goes into a negative-pressure airborne isolation room, and staff wear a fitted N95 respirator — not a surgical mask. Classic active TB brings a chronic productive cough (sometimes with blood), night sweats, fever, and weight loss. Treatment is long — months of multiple drugs — and the nurse's central role is supporting adherence, because stopping early is exactly how drug-resistant TB is bred.

Influenza is a viral lower-and-upper respiratory infection that arrives abruptly with fever, body aches, headache, and a dry cough. It spreads by droplets, so influenza patients are placed on droplet precautions (a surgical mask within a few feet), and because it is viral, antibiotics do nothing — antiviral drugs help only if started early. The most important nursing contribution against influenza happens before flu season: vaccination of patients and staff alike.

Droplet is not airborne — and the mask you grab proves you know it. Flu and bacterial pneumonia travel on large droplets that fall within a few feet: a surgical mask is enough.   • Pneumonia is classified by where it was acquired — community, hospital/ventilator, or aspiration — which predicts the likely organism.
  • TB is airborne: negative-pressure room and N95; its long therapy makes adherence a nursing priority.
  • Influenza is viral and droplet-spread; vaccination and droplet precautions matter more than antibiotics.

Section 3Gastrointestinal infections

Two ways the gut gets sick

Gastrointestinal (GI) pathogens almost all share one route of entry — the fecal-oral route, meaning microbes (or their toxins) from stool reach a new mouth by way of contaminated food, water, or hands. But they make you sick in two very different ways. In a true infection, the organism is swallowed, colonizes the gut, and invades or irritates the lining; symptoms take a day or more to appear because the microbe has to multiply first. In an intoxication, the microbe has already grown in the food and produced a toxin, and it is the pre-formed toxin you swallow — so symptoms hit fast, often within hours (the vomiting of Staphylococcus aureus food poisoning is the textbook example).

Foodborne illness is the umbrella term for all of this, and the usual suspects — Salmonella, Campylobacter, Shiga-toxin-producing E. coli, Norovirus — produce overlapping pictures of nausea, vomiting, crampy abdominal pain, and diarrhea that may be watery or bloody. For most healthy adults the illness is self-limited and the treatment is rehydration, not antibiotics. The nurse's clinical eye is on the vulnerable: infants, older adults, and immunocompromised patients, who can dehydrate dangerously, and on the warning signs of bloody diarrhea, which can signal the E. coli strain that damages the kidneys.

The two that spread on the unit

Clostridioides difficile (C. diff) is the infection you help cause if you are not careful with antibiotics. Broad-spectrum antibiotics flatten the normal gut flora, and this spore-forming organism blooms into severe, foul-smelling watery diarrhea and colitis. Two facts drive nursing practice: C. diff forms spores that alcohol hand rub does not kill, so you must wash with soap and water after caring for these patients; and the room needs a bleach-based (sporicidal) cleaner. Patients go on contact precautions, and new-onset diarrhea in anyone on antibiotics should raise the question immediately.

Norovirus is the explosive, highly contagious cause of "stomach flu" that shuts down cruise ships, long-term-care units, and hospital wards. It takes only a handful of viral particles to infect, it spreads by the fecal-oral route and by aerosolized vomit, and — like C. diff spores — it resists alcohol gel, so soap-and-water hand hygiene and bleach cleaning are again the rule. Sudden onset of vomiting and watery diarrhea sweeping through a group of people points straight at norovirus.

Key points
  • GI pathogens spread by the fecal-oral route and cause illness by infection (slow onset) or pre-formed toxin (fast onset).
  • Most foodborne illness is self-limited; the mainstay is rehydration, and the risk is dehydration in the very young, old, and immunocompromised.
  • C. difficile follows antibiotic use, forms spores, and demands soap-and-water hand hygiene plus contact precautions.
  • Norovirus is intensely contagious, resists alcohol gel, and calls for the same soap-and-water and bleach-cleaning response.

Section 4Urogenital infections & STIs

Urinary tract infections

The urinary tract infection (UTI) is one of the most common infections nurses manage, and it is usually an inside job: the culprit is most often the patient's own Escherichia coli, harmless in the colon but troublesome once it climbs the short distance up the urethra into the bladder. Anatomy explains why women get far more UTIs than men — a shorter urethra sits closer to the anus. A lower UTI, or cystitis (bladder infection), brings dysuria (burning with urination), urinary frequency and urgency, and cloudy or foul-smelling urine. If the infection ascends to the kidneys — pyelonephritis — the patient turns systemically ill: fever, chills, and flank (back) pain over the kidney.

The version that belongs to nursing above all others is the catheter-associated UTI (CAUTI). An indwelling urinary catheter is a superhighway that lets bacteria bypass every normal defense and travel straight to the bladder, and the risk climbs with every day the catheter stays in. The single most effective prevention is simple and squarely a nursing responsibility: do not place a catheter unless it is truly indicated, and remove it the moment it is no longer needed. A subtle but crucial point — a positive urine culture in a patient with no symptoms (asymptomatic bacteriuria) is usually not treated, because treating it breeds resistance without helping the patient.

Sexually transmitted infections

Sexually transmitted infections (STIs) are spread through sexual contact and range from bacterial infections that antibiotics cure to viral infections that we manage but cannot eradicate. Two features make STIs a distinct nursing challenge: many are asymptomatic, so people transmit them unknowingly, and all of them carry stigma, which means your ability to take a nonjudgmental history and protect confidentiality is part of the clinical care. Chlamydia (from Chlamydia trachomatis) is the most commonly reported bacterial STI and is frequently silent, especially in women, where untreated infection can scar the fallopian tubes and cause infertility. Gonorrhea (from Neisseria gonorrhoeae) often travels with it. Syphilis, caused by the spirochete Treponema pallidum, progresses in stages if untreated, beginning with a painless sore.

The viral STIs are lifelong companions rather than curable episodes. Genital herpes (herpes simplex virus) causes recurring outbreaks of painful blisters. Human papillomavirus (HPV) causes genital warts and, in its high-risk types, cervical and other cancers — which is exactly why the HPV vaccine is a cancer-prevention tool. HIV attacks the immune system itself. Across all of them the nursing themes are consistent: encourage screening (because many are silent), support partner treatment to stop the ping-pong of reinfection, and teach prevention without shame.

  • Many STIs are silent; nursing care centers on nonjudgmental screening, partner treatment, and prevention (including the HPV vaccine).

Section 5Bloodstream & CNS infections

From bacteremia to sepsis

The bloodstream is supposed to be sterile, so finding microbes there is always significant. Bacteremia simply means bacteria are present in the blood — sometimes transiently and harmlessly (brushing your teeth can do it), but sometimes as the launching point for disaster. The danger is sepsis: not the infection itself but the body's dysregulated, overwhelming response to it, in which widespread inflammation causes blood vessels to leak and dilate, blood pressure to fall, and organs to lose their perfusion. Left unchecked it progresses to septic shock — dangerously low blood pressure that does not correct with fluids — and to multi-organ failure. Common sources include pneumonia, UTIs, abdominal infections, and, critically for hospitalized patients, infected central lines (a CLABSI, or central-line-associated bloodstream infection).

Recognizing sepsis early is one of the highest-value skills a nurse has, because survival is measured in hours. The bedside picture is a patient who looks and acts wrong on top of an infection: fever or an abnormally low temperature, a fast heart rate, fast breathing, and — often the first and most sensitive sign — new confusion or altered mental status. Falling blood pressure, decreasing urine output, and mottled skin signal that shock is setting in. When sepsis is recognized, care moves fast along a bundle: draw blood cultures, give broad-spectrum antibiotics quickly, and infuse fluids to restore perfusion.

Meningitis

Meningitis is inflammation of the meninges, the membranes wrapping the brain and spinal cord, and bacterial meningitis is one of the true medical emergencies in this book — it can kill a previously healthy young person within a day. The classic triad is fever, severe headache, and a stiff neck (nuchal rigidity), often with photophobia (light hurts), vomiting, and a rapidly declining level of consciousness. A spreading purple, non-blanching rash points to the especially feared meningococcus, Neisseria meningitidis. Because that organism spreads by respiratory droplets and can move through close-contact groups like dorms and barracks, a suspected case goes on droplet precautions, and close contacts may need preventive antibiotics.

Diagnosis rests on a lumbar puncture to sample the cerebrospinal fluid, and the nursing imperative is speed: antibiotics should not wait. Viral (aseptic) meningitis, by contrast, is far more common and usually much milder, resolving on its own. The nurse's job is to keep bacterial meningitis at the front of the mind whenever fever meets a stiff neck and a changed mental status — because the cost of moving slowly is measured in lives and lifelong disability.

Key points
  • Bacteremia is bacteria in the blood; sepsis is the body's overwhelming, harmful response to infection.
  • Septic shock is sepsis with fluid-resistant low blood pressure; early recognition and the sepsis bundle save lives.

The chapter in one breath

  • Skin infections begin at a break in the barrier; depth predicts severity, from impetigo to necrotizing fasciitis, and MRSA drives contact precautions.
  • Respiratory infections range from viral colds to pneumonia; the precaution level is everything, and TB is airborne (N95, negative pressure) while flu is droplet.
  • GI pathogens spread fecal-orally by infection or pre-formed toxin; C. difficile and norovirus resist alcohol gel and demand soap-and-water hygiene.
  • UTIs usually come from the patient's own E. coli; CAUTI is prevented by removing catheters early, and asymptomatic bacteriuria is left untreated.
  • STIs are often silent; nursing centers on nonjudgmental screening, partner treatment, and vaccination (HPV).
  • Sepsis is the body's dangerous response to infection — new confusion, fast vitals, and low blood pressure demand rapid cultures, antibiotics, and fluids.
  • Bacterial meningitis — fever, stiff neck, headache, confusion — is a true emergency requiring immediate treatment and droplet precautions.

Check yourself

1. A child has fragile blisters on the face that rupture into a golden, honey-colored crust. Name the infection and its two usual causes.
Impetigo, caused by Staphylococcus aureus or group A Streptococcus pyogenes. It is a superficial epidermal infection; the honey-colored crust is its classic sign.
2. Why are patients with MRSA placed on contact precautions, and what does "MRSA" actually mean?
MRSA is methicillin-resistant S. aureus — ordinary staph that resists the penicillin family — and it spreads by contact. Gown and gloves plus hand hygiene stop transmission via hands, equipment, and surfaces.
3. A wound swab grows bacteria, but the wound has no redness, warmth, swelling, or drainage. Should it be treated with antibiotics? Why or why not?
No — this is colonization, not infection. Almost every open wound grows bacteria; without signs of tissue invasion, antibiotics cause harm (side effects, resistance) without benefit.
4. A patient with suspected tuberculosis is admitted. What isolation and respiratory protection are required, and why does TB differ from influenza here?
A negative-pressure airborne isolation room and a fitted N95 respirator. TB spreads by tiny airborne nuclei that float, so a surgical mask is inadequate; influenza spreads by larger droplets that fall within a few feet, where a surgical mask suffices.
5. What distinguishes strep throat from a typical viral cold, and why does the distinction matter?
Strep throat brings a sudden sore throat, fever, and swollen tonsils without cough or runny nose, and it is bacterial. It matters because untreated group A strep can lead to rheumatic fever, so it is worth culturing and treating with antibiotics.
6. Contrast a foodborne infection with a foodborne intoxication in terms of onset time.
Infection: the swallowed microbe must multiply first, so symptoms take a day or more. Intoxication: a pre-formed toxin is swallowed, so symptoms hit within hours. Rapid vomiting after a meal (e.g., S. aureus toxin) suggests intoxication.
7. After caring for a patient with C. difficile, a nurse uses alcohol hand rub. What is the problem, and what should be done instead?
Alcohol gel does not kill C. diff spores — the nurse must wash with soap and water. The friction of soap and running water physically removes the spores; the room also needs a bleach-based cleaner.
8. Why does new-onset diarrhea in a patient on broad-spectrum antibiotics raise concern for C. difficile?
The antibiotics wipe out the normal gut flora, removing competition and letting spore-forming C. difficile overgrow. The result is severe watery diarrhea and colitis; it warrants contact precautions and soap-and-water hygiene.
9. What organism causes most UTIs, and why do UTIs occur more often in women?
Most UTIs are caused by Escherichia coli from the patient's own colon. Women have a shorter urethra located closer to the anus, giving bacteria a shorter path into the bladder.
10. State the single most effective way a nurse can prevent CAUTIs.
Avoid placing indwelling urinary catheters unless truly indicated, and remove them as soon as they are no longer needed. Risk rises with every catheter day, so every catheter-free day is a day the patient cannot develop a CAUTI.
11. Distinguish bacteremia, sepsis, and septic shock.
Bacteremia = bacteria in the blood; sepsis = the body's dysregulated, life-threatening response to infection; septic shock = sepsis with low blood pressure that does not correct with fluids. The progression is toward organ dysfunction and demands rapid cultures, antibiotics, and fluids.
12. A patient with a UTI becomes newly confused, with a fast heart rate and rising respiratory rate. What must you suspect, and why act quickly?
Sepsis — new confusion in an infected patient is sepsis until proven otherwise. Altered mental status is often the earliest sepsis sign, and survival depends on early recognition and prompt cultures, antibiotics, and fluids.
