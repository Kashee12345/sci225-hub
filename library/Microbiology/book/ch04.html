Microbiology · Chapter 4

Bacterial Diversity & Classification

There are more kinds of bacteria than there are kinds of everything else alive, and only a small fraction ever make a person sick — but that fraction is the fraction you will meet at the bedside. The trick to keeping the clinically important organisms straight is not memorizing a list; it is learning the handful of features microbiologists use to sort bacteria into groups. Shape, staining, oxygen needs, and a few special quirks together place almost any pathogen into a recognizable box, and the box tells you how it spreads, what illness it causes, and which drugs will work. This chapter walks through those sorting features one at a time and hangs the most important pathogens on them, so that a culture result or a Gram-stain report starts to read like a sentence you already understand.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Name the three basic bacterial shapes and the common arrangements, and read the clues hidden in a genus name.
  • Explain what the Gram stain reveals and why gram-positive and gram-negative cell walls respond differently.
  • Identify the major gram-positive pathogens — Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, and Clostridium/Clostridioides — and the diseases they cause.
  • Identify the major gram-negative pathogens — E. coli, Salmonella, and Pseudomonas — and explain why endotoxin makes them dangerous.
  • Describe the special bacteria — mycobacteria, spirochetes, and mycoplasmas — that break the usual staining and cell-wall rules.
  • Classify bacteria by their oxygen requirements and explain why this changes how specimens are collected and where infections form.
  • List the other axes — temperature, pH, salt, intracellular lifestyle — used to describe and identify bacteria.
  • Connect each classification feature to a concrete nursing action at the bedside.

Section 1Shapes & arrangements

Three basic shapes

Under the microscope, the overwhelming majority of bacteria take one of three shapes, and the shape is the very first thing a lab reports because it narrows the field so quickly. A coccus (plural cocci) is round or spherical, like a tiny ball. A bacillus (plural bacilli) is a rod, shaped like a stick or a grain of rice. A spirillum (plural spirilla) is spiral or curved, coiled like a corkscrew or bent like a comma. Between these three there are in-between forms worth knowing: a coccobacillus is a short, plump rod that can be mistaken for a coccus, and a vibrio is a short comma-shaped curve — the shape that gives Vibrio cholerae its name.

Shape is not just description. It is built into the name of nearly every organism you will chart, so the moment you learn to hear it, the vocabulary of microbiology starts teaching itself. Staphylococcus and Streptococcus are spheres; Bacillus and Lactobacillus are rods; Spirochetes are coils. Reading the shape out of a genus name is a habit worth building on day one.

Arrangements: how the cells hang together

When a bacterium divides, the two daughter cells may drift apart or may stay stuck together, and the pattern they form is a second identifying clue — especially for cocci. The prefixes are logical once you learn them. Diplo- means the cells sit in pairs (diplococci, as in Neisseria). Strepto- means they form chains, like a string of beads (streptococci). Staphylo- means they clump in irregular grape-like clusters (staphylococci). Cocci can also form flat squares of four (tetrads) or cubical packets of eight (sarcinae). Rods more often line up end-to-end in short chains or side-by-side like a fence (a palisade), the arrangement typical of Corynebacterium.

A Gram stain result is a head start, not the finish line. When the lab calls to say a blood culture shows "gram-positive cocci in clusters," the team is already thinking Staphylococcus and choosing empiric coverage — hours before the organism is formally named. Shape plus arrangement plus stain color is the fastest actionable information microbiology produces, which is why the preliminary Gram report matters so much to timing antibiotics.

Section 2Gram-positive pathogens

What "gram-positive" means

The Gram stain, devised by Hans Christian Gram in 1884, sorts almost all bacteria into two great camps based on how their cell wall holds a purple dye. Gram-positive bacteria have a thick outer layer of peptidoglycan that traps the crystal-violet stain and turns the cells deep purple. Because that wall is exposed on the surface, gram-positive organisms are the classic targets of the penicillin family, which sabotages peptidoglycan as it is built. The three genera below account for a huge share of the gram-positive disease a nurse will ever see.

Staphylococcus — the cluster

Staphylococcus are gram-positive cocci in grape-like clusters. The species that matters most, Staphylococcus aureus, is a spectacularly versatile pathogen: it lives harmlessly in the nose of roughly a third of healthy people, yet given a break in the skin it causes boils, abscesses, cellulitis, wound infections, bloodstream infections, and endocarditis, and its toxins cause a form of food poisoning and toxic shock syndrome. MRSA — methicillin-resistant S. aureus — is the resistant form that drives contact precautions on so many units. A related group, the coagulase-negative staphylococci such as S. epidermidis, are low-virulence skin residents that nonetheless cause trouble by colonizing catheters, prosthetic joints, and heart valves.

Streptococcus — the chain

Streptococcus are gram-positive cocci in chains, and they are sorted clinically by the way they break down blood on an agar plate and by surface antigens. Group A strep (S. pyogenes) causes strep throat, impetigo, cellulitis, scarlet fever, the flesh-destroying necrotizing fasciitis, and the delayed immune complications of rheumatic fever and post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis. Group B strep (S. agalactiae) colonizes the vagina in some pregnant patients and is a leading cause of newborn sepsis and meningitis — the reason for routine screening late in pregnancy. Streptococcus pneumoniae (a diplococcus) is a top cause of community-acquired pneumonia, otitis media, and meningitis, and is the target of the pneumococcal vaccines.

Clostridium — the spore-former

Clostridium and its relative Clostridioides are gram-positive rods with two dangerous properties: they are obligate anaerobes (Section 5), and they form endospores — dormant, armored survival capsules that shrug off heat, drying, and alcohol. The roll call is memorable: Clostridioides difficile (antibiotic-associated colitis), Clostridium tetani (tetanus), Clostridium botulinum (botulism), and Clostridium perfringens (gas gangrene and food poisoning). Because their spores resist alcohol, an alcohol hand rub does not remove C. difficile — hands must be washed with soap and water, and surfaces cleaned with a sporicidal (bleach) agent.

Section 3Gram-negative pathogens

What "gram-negative" means — and why endotoxin matters

Gram-negative bacteria have only a thin layer of peptidoglycan, so they lose the purple crystal violet during decolorizing and take up the pink counterstain instead. What makes them clinically distinct, though, is a second membrane wrapped outside the peptidoglycan — the outer membrane — whose outer face is studded with lipopolysaccharide (LPS). The lipid A portion of LPS is a potent endotoxin: when gram-negative bacteria die and break apart in the bloodstream, released endotoxin triggers a runaway inflammatory cascade — fever, falling blood pressure, and, at the extreme, septic shock. This is why a gram-negative bloodstream infection is a medical emergency, and why the sepsis alerts nurses respond to so often trace back to these organisms.

Escherichia coli — the everyday enteric

Escherichia coli is a gram-negative rod and a normal resident of the colon, where it is harmless. Out of place, it is the single most common cause of urinary tract infections, and it is a frequent cause of gram-negative sepsis and neonatal meningitis. Certain strains are outright intestinal pathogens: enterohemorrhagic E. coli (such as O157:H7) produces Shiga toxin and can cause bloody diarrhea and hemolytic uremic syndrome, a cause of acute kidney failure in children. Because E. coli is a facultative anaerobe, it thrives both in the oxygen-poor gut and in the bladder.

Salmonella — the food-borne invader

Salmonella are gram-negative rods spread by contaminated food and water and by contact with reptiles and poultry. Most infections are a self-limited gastroenteritis — the classic food poisoning from undercooked eggs or chicken — but Salmonella Typhi causes typhoid fever, a systemic illness with sustained high fever. Salmonella is a reminder that hydration and hand hygiene, not routine antibiotics, are the mainstay for uncomplicated infectious diarrhea in an otherwise healthy adult.

Pseudomonas aeruginosa — the opportunist of wet places

Pseudomonas aeruginosa is a gram-negative rod, an obligate aerobe, and one of the great opportunists of the hospital. It lives in moist reservoirs — sink drains, respiratory equipment, standing water, even flower vases — and it attacks patients whose defenses are down: it causes ventilator-associated pneumonia, infections of burns and chronic wounds, catheter-associated UTIs, and the chronic lung colonization seen in cystic fibrosis. It is famous for producing a blue-green pigment and a grape-like or "corn tortilla" odor, and for being intrinsically resistant to many antibiotics, which makes it hard to treat and a frequent driver of multidrug-resistant outbreaks.

A gram-negative-rod result on a blood culture should raise your vigilance for hypotension, tachycardia, and rising lactate — and prompt fast source control and timely antibiotics.

Section 4Special bacteria

Mycobacteria — the waxy, acid-fast rods

A few important bacteria refuse to fit the Gram-stain scheme, and the most important of these are the mycobacteria. Their cell wall is loaded with a waxy lipid called mycolic acid, which repels ordinary dyes; to see them you need a special acid-fast stain that forces dye into the wall and then resists decolorizing with acid-alcohol. Mycobacterium tuberculosis causes tuberculosis, and its features drive real nursing practice: the waxy wall makes it slow-growing (cultures take weeks) and hardy in the environment, it spreads through the air on tiny droplet nuclei, and it therefore requires airborne isolation — a negative-pressure room and a fitted N95 respirator, not a simple surgical mask. Its cousin Mycobacterium leprae causes leprosy (Hansen disease).

Spirochetes — the corkscrews

Spirochetes are long, thin, tightly coiled bacteria that move with a distinctive corkscrew motion driven by internal axial filaments (endoflagella). They are so slender that many cannot be seen on a routine Gram stain or grown on ordinary media, so they are usually diagnosed by blood tests rather than culture. The three to know are Treponema pallidum (syphilis), Borrelia burgdorferi (Lyme disease, spread by tick bite), and Leptospira (leptospirosis, from water contaminated with animal urine). Their stealthy, hard-to-detect nature is exactly why serologic testing and a careful history matter so much for these infections.

Mycoplasma — the wall-less minimalists

Mycoplasma are the smallest free-living bacteria and are unique in having no cell wall at all. Without a rigid wall they have no fixed shape (they are pleomorphic) and, crucially, no peptidoglycan for a penicillin to attack — so beta-lactam antibiotics are useless against them. Mycoplasma pneumoniae causes "walking pneumonia," a mild, drawn-out atypical pneumonia common in younger people, and is treated with a macrolide or tetracycline instead of a penicillin. The wall-less lesson is a clean illustration of selective toxicity from the other direction: no wall means no target.

No wall, no penicillin. Whenever an organism lacks a peptidoglycan wall — Mycoplasma is the textbook case — the entire penicillin/cephalosporin family has nothing to bind. It is a memorable check on the reflex to reach for a beta-lactam: match the drug's target to the bug's structure, and a wall-less organism needs a drug that works some other way.

Section 5Oxygen & other groupings

Sorting bacteria by their relationship to oxygen

Oxygen is life-giving to your cells, but to many bacteria it is either essential, irrelevant, or lethal — and how an organism relates to oxygen is a core classification axis with direct bedside consequences. An obligate aerobe must have oxygen to grow (Pseudomonas, Mycobacterium tuberculosis). An obligate anaerobe is poisoned and killed by oxygen and grows only where it is absent (Clostridium, Bacteroides). A facultative anaerobe is the flexible middle: it prefers oxygen but can switch to fermentation without it (E. coli, Staphylococcus, most of the organisms we culture easily). A microaerophile needs oxygen but only at low levels (Campylobacter, Helicobacter pylori), and an aerotolerant anaerobe does not use oxygen at all but survives being exposed to it.

Why oxygen grouping changes nursing care

This is not an academic distinction. Anaerobes flourish in exactly the places oxygen cannot reach: the depths of a puncture wound, the center of an abscess, dead and crushed tissue, the gut, and the pockets of the mouth — which is why deep, dirty, or bite wounds and gas gangrene are anaerobic problems. It also shapes specimen handling: because oxygen kills obligate anaerobes on contact, a specimen collected to look for them must be protected from air and transported to the lab quickly in special anaerobic conditions, or the organism dies before it can be grown and the culture falsely comes back negative.

The other axes of classification

Oxygen is only one of several dials microbiologists turn. Bacteria are also grouped by temperature preference (most human pathogens are mesophiles that thrive near body temperature, 37°C), by pH and salt tolerance (a few extremophiles love acid or high salt), and by whether they can survive only inside host cells. Two clinically important groups are obligate intracellular bacteria — Chlamydia and Rickettsia — which cannot reproduce outside a host cell at all, so they too cannot be grown on standard plates and are diagnosed by other means. Put together, these axes — shape, Gram reaction, oxygen need, growth requirements, and lifestyle — are the coordinates that let a lab pin down almost any organism.

An anaerobic wound culture only works if air never gets in. When the concern is a deep abscess or necrotic wound, the specimen has to reach the lab fast and protected from oxygen — obligate anaerobes die on exposure to air, and a mishandled sample yields a falsely negative culture while the infection keeps advancing. Prompt, correct collection is a nursing contribution to getting the diagnosis right.

  • Anaerobes cause deep-wound, abscess, and dead-tissue infections, and their specimens must be protected from air during transport.
  • Other axes include temperature, pH, salt, and an intracellular lifestyle (e.g., Chlamydia, Rickettsia).
  • Combining all the features — shape, stain, oxygen, growth needs — is how the lab identifies an organism.

The chapter in one breath

  • Bacteria are sorted first by shape (coccus, bacillus, spirillum) and arrangement (pairs, chains, clusters), clues usually built into the genus name.
  • The Gram stain splits bacteria into gram-positive (thick peptidoglycan, purple) and gram-negative (thin wall + outer membrane, pink).
  • Key gram-positive pathogens: Staphylococcus (clusters), Streptococcus (chains), and spore-forming anaerobic Clostridium/Clostridioides.
  • Key gram-negative pathogens: E. coli, Salmonella, and Pseudomonas — their endotoxin (LPS) can drive septic shock.
  • Special bacteria break the rules: mycobacteria are acid-fast, spirochetes are hard-to-stain coils, and mycoplasmas have no cell wall (so penicillins fail).
  • Oxygen requirements (aerobe, anaerobe, facultative, microaerophile) shape where infections form and how specimens must be collected.
  • Additional axes — temperature, pH, salt, and an intracellular lifestyle — complete the coordinate system labs use to identify any organism.

Check yourself

1. A blood culture is reported as "gram-positive cocci in clusters." What genus should the team think of first, and how does the report point there?
Staphylococcus. Cocci (round) in grape-like clusters is the staphylococcal pattern; "gram-positive" plus shape plus arrangement lets the team start empiric coverage before final identification.
2. What does a coccus, a bacillus, and a spirillum each look like, and give one example organism for each.
Coccus = sphere (e.g., Staphylococcus); bacillus = rod (e.g., E. coli); spirillum = spiral/corkscrew (e.g., Treponema pallidum). Vibrios (curved commas) and coccobacilli (short plump rods) are in-between forms.
3. Structurally, what is the difference between a gram-positive and a gram-negative cell wall, and why does each stain the color it does?
Gram-positive cells have thick peptidoglycan that traps crystal violet (purple); gram-negative cells have thin peptidoglycan plus an outer membrane, lose the crystal violet, and take up the pink counterstain. The thick wall is why penicillins target gram-positives so well.
4. Name the three Clostridium/Clostridioides diseases from the chapter and explain why alcohol gel does not stop C. difficile.
Any of: C. difficile colitis, tetanus, botulism, gas gangrene. Alcohol gel fails because these bacteria form endospores that resist alcohol. Hands must be washed with soap and water and surfaces cleaned with a bleach (sporicidal) agent.
5. Group A strep and Group B strep both matter clinically but in different patients. Contrast them.
Group A (S. pyogenes) causes strep throat, cellulitis, necrotizing fasciitis, and rheumatic fever; Group B (S. agalactiae) colonizes the vagina and causes newborn sepsis and meningitis. Group B is why pregnant patients are screened late in pregnancy.
6. Why is a gram-negative rod in the bloodstream considered an emergency? Name the responsible molecule.
The gram-negative outer membrane carries LPS/endotoxin (lipid A); when the bacteria die and break apart, endotoxin can trigger fever, hypotension, and septic shock. This warrants vigilance for sepsis and prompt antibiotics and source control.
7. A previously healthy adult has a UTI. Which gram-negative rod is the most likely cause, and where does it normally live?
Escherichia coli, which normally lives harmlessly in the colon. It becomes a pathogen when it reaches the bladder; it is the single most common cause of UTIs.
8. Why is Pseudomonas aeruginosa such a persistent hospital problem? Give two reasons tied to its biology.
It lives in moist reservoirs (sinks, respiratory equipment, water) and is intrinsically resistant to many antibiotics. As an opportunist, it strikes vulnerable patients — ventilator pneumonia, burns, chronic wounds, and the cystic-fibrosis lung.
9. Why does Mycobacterium tuberculosis not show up on a routine Gram stain, and what isolation does a patient with TB require?
Its waxy mycolic-acid wall repels ordinary dyes, so it needs an acid-fast stain. TB spreads by air and requires airborne isolation — a negative-pressure room and a fitted N95 respirator. The waxy wall also makes it slow-growing and hardy.
10. Mycoplasma pneumoniae causes walking pneumonia. Why will a penicillin not work against it?
Mycoplasma has no cell wall and therefore no peptidoglycan for a penicillin to attack. Treatment uses a macrolide or tetracycline instead — no wall, no beta-lactam target.
11. Define obligate aerobe, obligate anaerobe, and facultative anaerobe, giving one example of each.
Obligate aerobe needs oxygen (Pseudomonas); obligate anaerobe is killed by oxygen (Clostridium); facultative anaerobe uses oxygen if present but can grow without it (E. coli). Microaerophiles need low oxygen (H. pylori).
12. A deep abscess is being cultured for anaerobes. Why must the specimen be protected from air and delivered quickly?
Obligate anaerobes are killed by oxygen, so air exposure or delay can kill them before they can be grown, producing a falsely negative culture. Prompt, oxygen-protected collection is a nursing contribution to an accurate diagnosis.
