Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cells

Almost every microbe that concerns a nurse is a single cell — and that cell is built one of two ways. It is either a prokaryote, a small cell with its DNA loose in the cytoplasm, or a eukaryote, a larger cell that locks its DNA inside a nucleus, exactly like your own cells do. Bacteria are prokaryotes; fungi and protozoa are eukaryotes; your patient is made of eukaryotic cells. That single architectural split is not just trivia — it is the reason an antibiotic can flood a patient's bloodstream and destroy the bacteria while leaving the patient unharmed. This chapter takes a bacterial cell apart piece by piece, from the wall that gives it shape to the spore that lets it survive a decade on a bedrail, and shows you why each structure matters at the bedside.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Contrast prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells by size, nucleus, organelles, and ribosomes.
  • Explain how the prokaryote–eukaryote divide creates selective toxicity for antimicrobial drugs.
  • Describe the bacterial cell envelope — plasma membrane, cytoplasm, and nucleoid — and what each part does.
  • Explain the structure of peptidoglycan and contrast Gram-positive and Gram-negative cell walls.
  • Connect the cell wall and endotoxin (LPS) to how antibiotics work and to Gram-negative sepsis.
  • Describe the roles of the capsule, flagella, pili, and fimbriae in disease and gene transfer.
  • Explain how endospores form, why they resist heat and disinfectants, and what that means for infection control.

Section 1Two kinds of cell

The single deepest divide in biology

Of all the ways you could sort living cells, one line runs deeper than any other: does the cell keep its DNA inside a membrane-bound nucleus, or not? Cells that do not are prokaryotes (from Greek roots meaning "before the nut," the "nut" being the nucleus). Cells that do are eukaryotes ("true nut"). This is not a small stylistic difference — it comes with a whole package of other features, and knowing which side of the line a microbe sits on tells you how it is built, how fast it grows, and which drugs can touch it.

Prokaryotes are the bacteria and the archaea. They are small — typically 0.2 to 2 µm across — and simple, with their single circular chromosome floating loose in the cytoplasm. Eukaryotes — the fungi, protozoa, plants, animals, and every cell in your patient's body — are usually ten to a hundred times larger across and far more elaborate inside. When you say a patient has a "bacterial" infection versus a "fungal" one, you are really naming which kind of cell you are fighting.

What prokaryotes lack — and what they have

The headline is what prokaryotes are missing. They have no nucleus, and no other membrane-bound organelles either — no mitochondria, no endoplasmic reticulum, no Golgi apparatus. All of that chemistry happens either in the open cytoplasm or against the inner face of the plasma membrane. But "simple" does not mean "defenseless." Prokaryotes make up for the lack of internal compartments with tough external gear: most wear a rigid cell wall, many add a slimy capsule, and many carry flagella to swim and pili to stick — the structures the rest of this chapter is about.

One difference is easy to overlook but medically enormous: the ribosome. Both cell types build proteins on ribosomes, but the machines are not the same size. Prokaryotes use a 70S ribosome; eukaryotes use a larger 80S ribosome. That size gap is a gift to medicine, because a drug can be designed to jam the 70S machine while ignoring the 80S one.

Why the difference is a drug target

Here is the payoff. Because bacterial cells have structures your cells simply do not have — a peptidoglycan wall, a 70S ribosome, unique enzymes — a drug can attack those targets and destroy the bacterium while leaving the patient's eukaryotic cells alone. This principle is called selective toxicity, and it is the entire reason antibacterial therapy is possible. Penicillin blocks cell-wall building, which human cells never do. Many antibiotics latch onto the 70S ribosome, which human cells do not have. The wider the gap between microbe and host, the safer the drug.

Section 2The bacterial cell envelope

The plasma membrane: the real boundary

Working from the outside in, the wall gives a bacterium its shape, but the plasma membrane is the layer that actually keeps the cell alive. It is a phospholipid bilayer — two sheets of fatty molecules with their water-loving heads facing out and their water-fearing tails tucked inside — peppered with proteins. It is selectively permeable, meaning it decides what crosses: nutrients in, wastes out, ions carefully balanced. Because prokaryotes have no mitochondria, the membrane also does a job your cells hand off to organelles: it runs the electron transport chain and generates the cell's energy right there in the membrane.

That makes the membrane a tempting drug target, but a risky one — bacterial and human membranes are chemically similar, so drugs that punch holes in membranes tend to be toxic. The polymyxins (colistin) do exactly this and are held in reserve as last-resort agents against highly resistant Gram-negative bacteria precisely because they can also injure the patient's kidneys.

Cytoplasm, ribosomes, and inclusions

Fill the membrane and you have the cytoplasm — a crowded, water-based gel where nearly all of the cell's chemistry happens. Suspended in it are millions of 70S ribosomes, the protein factories introduced in Section 1 and the target of whole families of antibiotics (aminoglycosides, tetracyclines, macrolides). Many bacteria also stockpile spare nutrients in inclusions — little granules of stored carbon, phosphate, or sulfur they can draw on when times are lean.

The nucleoid and plasmids

A prokaryote's DNA is not gone — it is just not gift-wrapped in a nucleus. The single, circular chromosome coils into a dense region called the nucleoid, which has no surrounding membrane, so the DNA sits in direct contact with the cytoplasm. This is one reason bacteria can grow so fast: transcription and translation happen in the same space, at the same time.

Many bacteria carry extra, much smaller loops of DNA called plasmids, separate from the chromosome. Plasmids are not usually needed for basic survival, but they often carry the genes that matter most to nurses: antibiotic-resistance genes, toxin genes, and the machinery to pass those genes to other bacteria. A plasmid can hop from one bacterium to another, which is how resistance spreads through a ward faster than any single organism could reproduce.

Section 3The cell wall

Peptidoglycan: the mesh that holds shape

Outside the membrane, most bacteria wear a rigid cell wall made of peptidoglycan (also called murein) — a material found nowhere in the human body. Picture a chain-link fence wrapped completely around the cell: long sugar chains running in parallel, cross-linked to each other by short chains of amino acids. That cross-linking turns a floppy bag into a rigid mesh strong enough to resist the cell's internal osmotic pressure, which would otherwise make it swell and burst. The wall is what gives a bacterium its shape — rod, sphere, or spiral — and its very existence, absent from human cells, is why it is such a clean drug target.

This is exactly where the beta-lactam antibiotics work. Penicillins, cephalosporins, and carbapenems block the enzymes that stitch the peptidoglycan cross-links. The bacterium keeps trying to grow, the wall can no longer hold, and the cell ruptures. Because human cells have no wall to protect, these drugs are among the safest antibiotics we have.

Gram-positive vs Gram-negative walls

Not all walls are built alike, and the difference is the single most useful early clue in a microbiology lab. The Gram stain sorts bacteria into two great camps by how their walls hold a purple dye:

Gram-positive bacteria have a thick peptidoglycan layer on the outside. It traps the crystal-violet dye and the cell stays purple. Examples: Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, Enterococcus, Clostridioides.

Gram-negative bacteria have only a thin peptidoglycan layer, but they add a second, outer membrane on top of it. That outer membrane lets the purple dye wash out during decolorizing, so the cell picks up the pink counterstain and appears red/pink. Examples: Escherichia coli, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas, Neisseria.

Why the Gram stain still runs the show

The Gram result is not just a color — it predicts behavior. That extra outer membrane makes Gram-negative bacteria harder to kill, because it blocks many drugs from ever reaching the wall. And the outer membrane carries a molecule that matters enormously to nurses: lipopolysaccharide (LPS), better known as endotoxin. When Gram-negative bacteria die and break apart, LPS is released into the blood and triggers a violent immune response — fever, plummeting blood pressure, and, at the extreme, septic shock. This is why a Gram-negative bloodstream infection can spiral so fast.

Section 4External structures

The capsule: a slippery shield

Some bacteria wrap themselves in a thick, sticky outer layer called a capsule (a looser version is called a slime layer). It is not needed to live, but where it matters, it matters a great deal. A capsule is a major virulence factor: it is slippery enough that the patient's immune cells struggle to grab and swallow the bacterium, a trick called resisting phagocytosis. This is why the most dangerous strains of Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, and Neisseria meningitidis are the encapsulated ones. Reassuringly, the capsule is also what several key vaccines are aimed at — teach the immune system to recognize the capsule, and it can clear the organism before it causes meningitis or pneumonia.

Flagella: swimming and chemotaxis

Flagella are long, whip-like tails that rotate like a propeller to push a bacterium through liquid. They are not random wanderers — bacteria steer toward nutrients and away from toxins in a directed movement called chemotaxis. Motility can be a virulence trait: it helps organisms like E. coli and Pseudomonas climb a urinary catheter or reach and colonize new tissue. The number and position of flagella (one end, both ends, or all over) is also a useful clue to a bacterium's identity.

Pili and fimbriae: sticking and swapping genes

Two shorter appendages round out the outside of the cell, and the naming trips students up, so hold them apart:

Fimbriae are numerous, short, hair-like fibers that a bacterium uses to attach to surfaces and to host cells. Attachment is the essential first step of nearly every infection — a microbe that cannot stick gets flushed away. Fimbriae are exactly how E. coli grips the lining of the bladder to start a urinary tract infection, and how bacteria anchor into the slimy communities called biofilms on catheters and implants.

Pili (specifically the sex pilus, or conjugation pilus) are usually longer and far fewer — often just one or two. Their job is different: a pilus builds a bridge to another bacterium and pulls it close so the two can swap DNA in a process called conjugation. This is one of the main ways antibiotic-resistance plasmids move from cell to cell.

Section 5Endospores

What an endospore is

A handful of Gram-positive bacteria — most importantly the genera Bacillus and Clostridioides/Clostridium — can do something no other microbes can: when conditions turn hostile, they seal a copy of their DNA inside an armored survival capsule called an endospore. The spore is not a way to reproduce — one cell makes one spore — it is a way to wait out disaster. Inside its tough coat, the spore is metabolically dormant: no growth, no chemistry, no vulnerability. It can persist for years, even decades, in soil, on surfaces, or on hospital equipment.

How a spore forms and wakes

The normal, actively growing bacterium is called the vegetative cell. When it senses starvation or other stress, it begins sporulation: it copies its chromosome, walls off a small portion of cytoplasm around that DNA, and wraps it in layer after protective layer — a dense core stripped of most of its water, a thick cortex, and a tough protein coat. The rest of the original cell then disintegrates, releasing the finished endospore.

The spore stays dormant until conditions improve. When nutrients return, it germinates — the coat cracks, water rushes back in, metabolism restarts, and it grows back into an ordinary, dividing vegetative cell. Crucially, the vegetative cell is the form that causes active disease and that ordinary antibiotics and disinfectants can kill; the spore is the form that survives to start the cycle again.

Why spores defeat routine disinfection

The endospore's low water content and layered armor make it extraordinarily resistant — to heat, drying, radiation, and most chemical disinfectants, including the alcohol-based hand rubs that work so well against almost everything else. This is not an academic point; it changes bedside practice. Two organisms make it real:

Clostridioides difficile spreads by spores that survive on bedrails, commodes, and hands. Alcohol gel does not kill them — so for a patient with C. diff, the rule is soap-and-water handwashing (which physically washes spores away) plus a sporicidal cleaner such as diluted bleach for surfaces. Clostridium tetani and Clostridium botulinum spores lurk in soil and improperly canned food. And because spores can shrug off boiling, true sterilization of instruments requires an autoclave — pressurized steam at 121°C — not just hot water.

The chapter in one breath

  • Cells are prokaryotic (no nucleus — bacteria) or eukaryotic (nucleus + organelles — fungi, protozoa, human).
  • Prokaryote-only structures — peptidoglycan wall, 70S ribosome — give antibiotics their selective toxicity.
  • The cell envelope is the plasma membrane (transport + energy), cytoplasm with 70S ribosomes, and the DNA-holding nucleoid; plasmids carry and spread resistance.
  • The wall is peptidoglycan, the target of beta-lactams; Gram-positive = thick wall/purple, Gram-negative = thin wall + outer membrane/pink.
  • The Gram-negative outer membrane carries LPS (endotoxin), a driver of septic shock.
  • External structures — capsule (resists phagocytosis), flagella (motility), fimbriae (attachment), pili (gene transfer) — are key virulence and resistance-spreading tools.
  • Endospores let Bacillus and Clostridioides survive heat and disinfectants — the reason C. diff needs soap-and-water and instruments need autoclaving.

Check yourself

1. A patient's chart says the infection is caused by a eukaryotic microbe. What two large groups does that point to, and why does it change your drug choice?
Fungi and protozoa are the eukaryotic microbes. Because they are eukaryotes (like human cells), antibacterial antibiotics won't touch them — a fungal infection needs an antifungal, not an antibiotic.
2. Explain selective toxicity and name two prokaryote-only structures that make it possible.
Selective toxicity is harming the microbe while sparing the patient by attacking a structure the microbe has but human cells don't. Two examples: the peptidoglycan cell wall and the 70S ribosome — both absent from human cells.
3. Where do prokaryotes carry out energy production, given that they have no mitochondria?
On the plasma membrane. The electron transport chain runs in the membrane itself, doing the job that mitochondria perform in eukaryotic cells.
4. What is a plasmid, and why is it a major infection-control concern?
A small loop of DNA separate from the chromosome, often carrying resistance or toxin genes. Plasmids can transfer between bacteria (even different species), spreading antibiotic resistance without any reproduction — so one resistant patient can seed a whole ward.
5. Peptidoglycan is described as an ideal antibiotic target. Which drug class exploits it, and why is that class so safe?
The beta-lactams (penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems) block cell-wall building. Human cells have no cell wall, so the drug has no equivalent target in the patient — making beta-lactams among the safest antibiotics.
6. On a Gram stain, one organism is purple and another is pink. Describe the wall structure behind each color.
Purple = Gram-positive, with a thick surface peptidoglycan layer that traps crystal violet. Pink = Gram-negative, with a thin peptidoglycan layer plus an outer membrane that lets the dye wash out, so it takes the pink counterstain.
7. A patient with a Gram-negative bloodstream infection suddenly spikes a fever and becomes hypotensive. What molecule likely explains this, and where does it come from?
Endotoxin (LPS) from the Gram-negative outer membrane. When the bacteria die and break apart, LPS is released into the blood and drives fever, dropping blood pressure, and potentially septic shock.
8. Distinguish fimbriae from pili by number, length, and function.
Fimbriae are many and short and are used for attachment to surfaces and host cells; pili (the sex pilus) are few and long and are used to transfer DNA by conjugation. Fimbriae start infections and biofilms; pili spread resistance genes.
9. Why is the capsule considered a virulence factor, and how do we turn it against the microbe?
The capsule's slippery coat helps the bacterium resist phagocytosis, so encapsulated strains cause worse disease (e.g., pneumococcus, meningococcus). Several vaccines target the capsule so the immune system can recognize and clear the organism.
10. Explain the difference between a vegetative cell and an endospore. Which one causes active disease?
The vegetative cell is the active, growing, dividing form — and the one that causes active disease and is killed by drugs and disinfectants. The endospore is a dormant, armored survival form that waits out harsh conditions. A spore germinates back into a vegetative cell when conditions improve.
11. Why must you use soap and water — not alcohol hand rub — when caring for a patient with C. difficile?
C. diff spreads by endospores, and alcohol does not kill spores. Soap-and-water handwashing physically washes the spores off the hands, and surfaces need a sporicidal agent such as diluted bleach.
12. Why does true sterilization of instruments require an autoclave rather than boiling water?
Endospores can survive boiling. An autoclave uses pressurized steam at 121°C, hot enough to destroy even resistant spores — the standard for sterilizing surgical instruments.
