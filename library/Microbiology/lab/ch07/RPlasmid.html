Anatomy of an R-Plasmid

A plasmid is a small circle of DNA that copies itself apart from the chromosome. When it carries resistance genes, we call it an R-plasmid — and a single ring can hold the machinery to copy itself, spread to new cells, and defeat several antibiotics at once. Tap each gene to see what it does.

One ring, many jobsAn R-plasmid is not just a bag of resistance genes. It also carries an origin of replication so it can copy itself, and transfer genes so it can move to a new cell. The resistance genes ride along as cargo — often stacked together, which is why one plasmid can make a microbe resistant to many drugs at once.
R-plasmids are the main reason antibiotic resistance spreads so fast: they replicate, they hop between cells by conjugation, and they keep collecting new resistance genes.
