Markers of the Transition to Adulthood

When does someone become an adult? Not, it turns out, on a birthday or at any single milestone. Psychologist Jeffrey Jensen Arnett found that young people judge adulthood by gradual, internal criteria far more than by classic life events. The transition, redefined Emerging adulthood (roughly ages 18–25, reaching toward 29) is Arnett's name for the distinct stretch between adolescence and settled adulthood. Its hallmark: adulthood is felt to arrive through three internal criteria — not through hitting demographic milestones like marriage or a first job.
This stage overlaps Erikson's Intimacy vs. Isolation and prolongs the identity “moratorium” (Marcia) — active exploration without firm commitment — well into the twenties.
The Big Three (Arnett's top criteria): 1) accept responsibility for yourself · 2) make independent decisions · 3) become financially independent. All are gradual, individual, and character-based — you become an adult by degrees.
Arnett's five features of emerging adulthood: identity exploration · instability · self-focus · feeling in-between · a sense of possibilities.
