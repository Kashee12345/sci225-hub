The Cognitive Continuum

Forgetting a name is not the same as forgetting the way home. In late adulthood, thinking naturally slows — but a measurable, disease-driven decline is something else entirely. Drag the slider to move from normal aging, through mild cognitive impairment, to dementia, and watch the one thing that truly separates them: whether a person can still live independently.

The dividing line is function. Normal aging is not a disease — thinking slows, but daily life is untouched. MCI is a decline that testing can measure, yet the person still lives independently. The moment cognitive loss robs someone of independent daily living — managing money, medications, or getting around safely — it becomes dementia. MCI is also the window to search for reversible causes: depression, thyroid disease, B12 deficiency, or medication effects.
