The Pillars of Resilience

When a stressor can’t be avoided, resilience is what lets people bend without breaking — and decades of research show it is built, not inborn. Psychologist Ann Masten calls it “ordinary magic,” and George Bonanno finds that resilience — not lasting breakdown — is the most common response to adversity. That capacity rests on a handful of well-studied pillars. Tap each column to see what holds well-being up.

What keeps a person standingTwo people can meet the same hardship and fare very differently. The difference is rarely a single heroic trait — it is a set of protective factors that most people can strengthen: who supports you, how you read the threat, whether life feels meaningful, whether you believe your actions matter, how skillfully you cope, and how well you care for your body. Tap a pillar to explore the evidence behind it.
Resilience is built, not born. Ann Masten calls it ordinary magic — it grows out of everyday relationships, skills, and habits. Every pillar shown here can be strengthened deliberately, which is why resilience can be learned and taught.
Not the same as “toughing it out.” Resilience is not bottling up feelings or going it alone. Reaching out for support, allowing yourself to grieve or rest, and asking for professional help are themselves resilient acts — strengths, not failures.
