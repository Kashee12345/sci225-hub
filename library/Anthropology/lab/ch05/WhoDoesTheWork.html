Anthropology · Chapter 5

Who Does the Work?

No society has ever handed out its work at random. Every one of them sorts jobs by age and by sex — those two are as close to universal as anything anthropologists have found. What each society then puts in each box turns out to vary so wildly between neighbours that it cannot be read off the body. And a third axis, specialisation, only opens up once there is enough surplus food to feed someone who does not grow any. Explore all three.

The lifespan

Three rules that hold everywhere

  • 1Every society divides work by age and by sex. No society yet described divides it by neither. These are the two universal axes.
  • 2What goes in each box is not universal. Outside a short list of tasks, the assignment differs so much between neighbouring peoples that biology cannot be the explanation.
  • 3Specialisation beyond age and sex has a price: someone can only do one thing all day if other people's food feeds them. Full-time specialists appear with the surplus and the centre that collects it.
Age and sex everywhere · specialisation only where there is surplus. Emile Durkheim built his whole first book, The Division of Labour in Society (1893), on the second half of that sentence: as work divides, what holds a society together stops being likeness and starts being need for one another.
