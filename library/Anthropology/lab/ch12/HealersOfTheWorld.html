Healers of the World

Every society has people it turns to when a body goes wrong — and almost none of them look like a doctor. Some are chosen by spirits through an illness they cannot refuse; others spend years memorising a corpus, or sit an examination. Some read illness as an agent — an ancestor, a rival, a broken observance. Others read it as an imbalance in humours, elements or fluids. Before you startAnthropology treats every one of these as a medical system — a body of theory, a way of diagnosing, a trained practitioner and a public that believes in them. That includes the last one on the list. Arthur Kleinman’s point was that biomedicine is not the neutral yardstick the others are measured against; it is one ethnomedicine among many, with its own cosmology, its own rituals and its own long initiation.
Nobody uses only one. People move between systems, often in the same week — a clinic for the antibiotics, a healer for the reason it happened to them. Anthropologists call this medical pluralism, and the order in which a family tries the options is their hierarchy of resort. Cheapest and closest is usually first; the expensive specialist is usually last.
