Who Holds the Levers?

Doing most of the work does not make you powerful. If it did, the women who grow most of the world’s hoe-farmed food would run those societies, and they do not. What tracks power is narrower and stranger: who controls goods once they leave the household, whose kin are standing nearby, and who is allowed to speak in public. Friedl’s rule. Ernestine Friedl’s comparison found that the best single predictor of women’s standing is not how much of the work they do — it is whether they control the distribution of valued goods beyond their own household. Trobriand women hand out wealth at mortuary exchanges; Yoruba and Asante women run markets; a Maasai wife gives out her house’s milk. Watch the giving, not the labour.
These are analytic types, not a ladder. No people climbs from one to the next, none is earlier or simpler as a society, and the variation inside a type is often larger than the gap between types — the Haudenosaunee and the societies of the New Guinea Highlands are both horticultural and sit at opposite ends of this chart. Note also that no confirmed matriarchy — women holding public power the way men hold it in patriarchies — has been documented anywhere. Matrilineal descent is a different thing, and it is common.
