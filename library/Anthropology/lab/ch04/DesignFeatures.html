Design Features: What Sets Human Language Apart

In 1960 the linguist Charles Hockett asked a question that sounds simple and is not: what would a communication system have to have before we agreed to call it a language? He answered with a list of design features — properties you can go and test for, in any species. Three of them do most of the work: arbitrariness, displacement and productivity. Below, six real communication systems are put against five features. Tap any cell in the grid. The result is not the one most people expect.

Hockett’s questionPeople have been announcing for centuries that some single thing separates human speech from animal signalling. Hockett’s contribution was to stop asserting and start listing — to break “language” into properties that could be checked one at a time, against real animals, by experiment. Once you do that, the tidy line between us and everything else stops being a line and becomes a pattern of overlaps. Pick a feature and a system to start filling the grid in.
No single feature is the answer. Honeybees have displacement. White-crowned sparrows have dialects learned from a tutor. Bonobos handle hundreds of arbitrary symbols. Every individual feature on this grid turns up somewhere outside our species. What turns up nowhere else is the combination: unrestricted displacement, genuine productivity and duality of patterning, all running on a signal set that is learned from a community and can therefore have a history. Ask which feature is uniquely human and the honest answer is: none of them — and all of them at once.
