No society leaves the body as it found it. Skin is cut, tapped, stained, stretched, coiled and hung with beads — and in every case the result is legible to somebody. Three lensesAnthropologists separate the emic view — the meaning as people inside the practice state it — from the etic view, the analyst’s comparative reading. The pair of terms comes from the linguist Kenneth Pike (1954) and entered anthropology through Marvin Harris; the commitment behind them is older, in Malinowski’s 1922 insistence that fieldwork exists to grasp the native’s point of view. Switch lenses on any practice.
Skin is never neutralMarking the body is a human universal, but what is marked, who may do the marking, and who is meant to read it vary enormously. Choose a practice on the left to begin the comparison.
No ladder of “primitive” to “modern.” Franz Boas’s argument holds here: these are not rungs on an evolutionary staircase. A laser-removable tattoo and a cut scar are the same human act — using skin to say something — worked with different tools, for different audiences, under different economies.
