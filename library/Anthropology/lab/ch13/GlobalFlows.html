Appadurai’s five –scapes

Who named what: Arjun Appadurai (1990; Modernity at Large, 1996) named five global flows — ethnoscapes (people), technoscapes (machinery and productive capacity), financescapes (capital), mediascapes (images and the means to circulate them) and ideoscapes (political ideas: rights, sovereignty, democracy, representation). His argument is that these five are disjunctive — they move at different speeds, along different routes, and frequently in opposite directions. The suffix -scape is deliberate: like a landscape, each one looks different depending on where you are standing.
Does flow make everyone the same? Appadurai’s answer is no — what arrives is indigenized, remade in local terms. Roland Robertson called this glocalization; Ulf Hannerz described a creolizing world of mixed forms; Daniel Miller’s fieldwork in Trinidad found Coca-Cola absorbed into an existing local category of “black sweet drink” — a global product doing thoroughly local work. Homogenization and heterogenization happen at the same time, which is why the “everywhere is becoming America” story keeps failing its own evidence.
Flow is not freedom. Anna Tsing (Friction, 2005) showed that global connection is always awkward and unequal — it produces friction, not frictionless movement. A shipping container clears a border in hours; the person who loaded it may wait years for a visa. And Eric Wolf (Europe and the People Without History, 1982) added the historical correction: there were never sealed, separate cultures waiting for globalization to connect them. Long-distance flows are old. What is new is their speed and reach.
