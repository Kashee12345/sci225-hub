One discipline, four doorwaysNone of these is a fifth field. Development and business anthropology are cultural anthropology pointed at a project or a firm; forensic anthropology is biological anthropology pointed at a medicolegal case; medical anthropology draws on both. What travels into every one of them is the same equipment — long observation, the emic view of the people involved, and the refusal to judge a practice by the observer’s standards rather than its own.
Two ways to earn a living from this. Some applied anthropologists are hired inside an institution to make its work land better; others study the institution itself. Both are honest work, and the second keeps the first honest.
Whose side are you on? An applied anthropologist has a client and a community, and their interests do not always match. The AAA Code of Ethics resolves it in one direction: the primary obligation is to do no harm to the people studied — ahead of the funder, the agency, and the anthropologist’s own career.
