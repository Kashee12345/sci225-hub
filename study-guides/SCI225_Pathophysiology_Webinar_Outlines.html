SCI225 Pathophysiology — Webinar Outlines for Faculty

Prepared by Dr. Garg

A chapter-by-chapter teaching framework for synchronous webinar sessions. Each unit is scoped for a 90-minute live session but scales to 60 min (trim polls/cases) or 120 min (add the deep-dive extensions noted).

Course: SCI225 Pathophysiology Format: 6 live webinars, 90 min each Audience: Pre-health / nursing / allied Companion: SCI225 Midterm Review Guide

How to use this guide

Every unit outline follows the same spine so students can predict the flow and faculty can swap in/out without rebuilding the lesson plan:

  1. Learning Objectives — 4–6 measurable outcomes mapped to Bloom's levels. Copy directly into LMS.
  2. Opening hook / clinical case — 5 min. Grounds the pathophysiology in a real patient before touching a slide.
  3. Time-blocked outline — the main content flow with suggested minutes per segment.
  4. Poll / discussion prompts — 2–4 per session. Keep students active; catches misconceptions early.
  5. Common student misconceptions — call these out explicitly; they are the top wrong answers on assessments.
  6. Closing + assessment bridge — 5 min. Tie back to the opening case and preview next week.
ENGAGEMENT PRINCIPLES
Use the "rule of three": no more than 10–12 minutes of lecture before a poll, discussion, or case turn. Students in pathophysiology retain structure → function → failure mode patterns far better than memorized lists — always frame content that way. Reserve the last 10 minutes for Q&A; never end exactly on time or students stop asking by minute 85.

Unit 1  •  Cellular Biology, Genetics, Adaptation & Injury  Ch 1, 2, 4

Pre-Webinar Prep (student-facing)

  • Read Ch 1 (cell structure), Ch 2 (cell injury/adaptation), Ch 4 (genetics) — 90 min reading
  • Watch short pre-recorded video: 4 modes of cellular adaptation (5 min)
  • Bring one example of a disease in their own family or clinical experience that involves cellular adaptation
By session end, students will be able to:
  1. Differentiate the four modes of cellular adaptation (hypertrophy, hyperplasia, atrophy, metaplasia) with an example of each.
  2. Distinguish reversible cell injury from irreversible injury and identify the point of no return (mitochondrial permeability).
  3. Contrast necrosis and apoptosis across 5 key features (trigger, membrane, inflammation, ATP requirement, clinical context).
  4. Apply Mendelian vs multifactorial inheritance to predict offspring risk in a pedigree.
  5. Identify one autosomal dominant, one autosomal recessive, and one X-linked disorder and explain the inheritance logic.
Case: A 58-year-old long-time smoker presents with a chronic cough. Biopsy of the bronchial lining shows squamous epithelium replacing the normal ciliated columnar epithelium. Ask: What is this change called? Is it reversible? Protective or pathologic? (Answer: metaplasia — reversible, adaptive, but loses mucociliary clearance and may progress to dysplasia/cancer.) Return to this case at the close.

TimeSegmentKey teaching points
0:00–0:05Welcome + hook caseSet norms, introduce the smoker case, collect instant predictions
0:05–0:20Cell structure refresherOrganelles as "failure points" — mitochondria = ATP, ER = protein folding, lysosomes = autophagy. Frame each organelle as structure → function → what happens when it fails.
0:20–0:35Cellular adaptation (the big 4)Hypertrophy (cell size ↑) vs hyperplasia (cell number ↑) — compare cardiac (HTN) vs endometrial (estrogen). Atrophy: disuse, denervation, ischemia. Metaplasia: Barrett's, smoker's bronchus.

Poll 1: Which of the following is an example of physiologic hyperplasia? (breast tissue in pregnancy, endometrial hyperplasia, prostatic hyperplasia, compensatory liver regeneration)
Poll 2: A mother and father are both unaffected carriers of CF. Probability that their child is affected? (25% / 50% / 75% / 100%)
Discussion: "Why doesn't muscle grow by making new muscle cells?" — gets students to articulate post-mitotic vs labile vs stable tissue categories.
Think-pair-share: Each student names one adaptive change in their own body from the last year (pregnancy breast changes, muscle growth, callus formation, tonsillar hyperplasia with infection). Short share-out.
  • "Hypertrophy and hyperplasia are the same." No — size vs number. Cardiac muscle can only hypertrophy (post-mitotic).
  • "Apoptosis is just cell death." It is programmed and regulated, requires ATP, does NOT cause inflammation, and is physiologic (embryogenesis, menstrual shedding).
  • "Metaplasia is cancer." Not yet — it's adaptive and reversible. Dysplasia is the step toward malignancy.
  • "All genetic disease is heritable." Many mutations are somatic (cancer) and not passed on.
  • Males are "carriers" of X-linked recessive. No — hemizygous males express it fully. Only heterozygous females are carriers.

Assessment Bridge & Homework

  • Practice Qs 1–8 from the Midterm Review Guide Unit 1 section.
  • Short case write-up (200 words): a patient with cardiac hypertrophy from untreated hypertension — what adapts first, what fails eventually, and why?
  • Preview: next week's hook is a toddler with recurrent ear infections — we are building toward primary immunodeficiency.

Unit 2  •  Immunity, Inflammation, Infection  Ch 6, 7, 8, 9

Pre-Webinar Prep

  • Read Ch 6 (innate/adaptive immunity), 7 (inflammation), 8 (infection), 9 (immunopathology) — ~2 hours
  • Watch 3-min animation: "Cardinal signs of inflammation in real time"
  • Fill out a 5-question pre-check (posted in LMS) — results used to tailor emphasis
  1. Differentiate innate vs adaptive immunity across speed, specificity, and memory.
  2. Sequence the events of acute inflammation and explain each cardinal sign (rubor, tumor, calor, dolor, functio laesa) mechanistically.
  3. Classify hypersensitivity reactions I–IV with one prototype disease for each.
  4. Contrast active vs passive immunity; predict which applies in common clinical scenarios (vaccine, IVIG, maternal antibody, natural infection).
  5. Define autoimmunity and identify three autoimmune diseases with their primary mechanism.
  6. Describe how HIV produces AIDS at the cellular level.
Case: A 24-year-old college student arrives at urgent care 30 minutes after eating a peanut-butter cookie with swollen lips, hives, wheezing, and BP 78/42. The epinephrine autoinjector her roommate used is about to run out of effect. Ask: What is happening at the cellular level? Which immunoglobulin? Which hypersensitivity type? Why does the wheeze and the hypotension happen from the same chemical? (Type I, IgE, mast-cell histamine — bronchoconstriction AND systemic vasodilation.)

0:55–1:10Hypersensitivity I–IVType I (IgE/anaphylaxis), II (cytotoxic/blood transfusion reaction), III (immune complex/lupus, serum sickness), IV (delayed T-cell/TB skin test, contact dermatitis). One prototype per type.

Poll 1: Which cell is the first responder at the site of bacterial invasion? (macrophage, neutrophil, lymphocyte, eosinophil)
Poll 2: A nurse with a positive PPD (TB skin test) demonstrates which hypersensitivity type?
Discussion: "Is fever helpful or harmful?" — gets students weighing host defense vs organ stress.
Case branch: Switch the anaphylaxis patient to a post-transfusion hemolysis patient. What type of hypersensitivity now? Why different?
  • "Inflammation = infection." Inflammation is the response; infection is one trigger among many (trauma, autoimmune, ischemia, neoplasia).
  • "Passive immunity lasts long." No — it wanes in weeks to months because YOU aren't making the antibody.
  • "Autoimmune disease means weak immune system." It's misdirected, not weak.
  • "HIV kills you." HIV destroys CD4 cells; opportunistic infections kill AIDS patients.
  • "All allergies are IgE." Only Type I hypersensitivity is IgE-mediated.

Assessment Bridge & Homework

  • Practice Qs 9–18 from the Midterm Review Guide.
  • Match-the-disease worksheet: 10 diseases → correct hypersensitivity type.
  • Preview: Unit 3 builds directly on this — chronic inflammation and failed apoptosis are the root of cancer.

Unit 3  •  Oncology  Ch 11, 12, 13

Pre-Webinar Prep

  • Read Ch 11 (neoplasia basics), 12 (specific cancers), 13 (childhood cancers) — ~2 hours
  • Short quiz: TNM staging basics (5 questions, auto-grade)
  • Each student brings one cancer that runs in their family or that they have cared for — used in final discussion.
  1. Differentiate benign from malignant neoplasms across 7 features (differentiation, growth rate, invasion, metastasis, recurrence, mortality, nomenclature).
  2. Explain carcinogenesis as a multi-step process (initiation → promotion → progression) and name one chemical, physical, and viral carcinogen for each step.
  3. Describe TNM staging vs histologic grading and distinguish what each actually measures.
  4. Identify paraneoplastic syndromes and why cancer can cause symptoms unrelated to tumor location.
  5. Recognize the presentation and key risk factors of the "big five" cancers: lung, breast, colon, prostate, leukemia.
Case: A 67-year-old man with a 45-pack-year smoking history presents with new-onset seizures. CT head shows a solitary brain mass. Biopsy of the brain lesion reveals squamous cell carcinoma. Ask: Did this cancer start in the brain? Why is the histology "squamous"? What does this tell us about prognosis and treatment? (Metastatic lung cancer — primary tumor outside the CNS. Treats the lung, not the brain, as primary.)

90-Minute Session Outline

TimeSegmentKey teaching points
0:00–0:05Welcome + metastasis hookQuick poll: "Is this brain cancer?" Students usually say yes. Revisit at close.
0:05–0:20Benign vs malignant7-feature comparison table. Nomenclature rules: -oma (benign) vs -carcinoma/-sarcoma (malignant). Exceptions: lymphoma, melanoma, hepatoma — all malignant despite "-oma."
0:20–0:30CarcinogenesisInitiation (DNA mutation, irreversible) → promotion (proliferation, reversible) → progression (genetic instability, metastasis). Proto-oncogenes vs tumor suppressors (2-hit hypothesis, Knudson/Rb).
0:30–0:35POLL #1"Which is always true of a malignant tumor?" Distractors: encapsulated, slow-growing, well-differentiated, can metastasize.
0:35–0:50Staging vs gradingTNM: Tumor size, Nodes, Metastasis. Staging = how far. Grading = how ugly (differentiation). Staging usually predicts prognosis better. Walk through one real patient in TNM.
0:50–1:05Routes of spread + paraneoplasticDirect extension, lymphatic (most carcinomas), hematogenous (sarcomas, liver, lung, bone, brain), seeding. Paraneoplastic: SIADH (small-cell lung), ACTH (lung), hypercalcemia (squamous), Cushing.
1:05–1:10POLL #2"Patient with lung cancer has low Na and high urine osmolality." Most likely paraneoplastic syndrome?
1:10–1:25Big five cancersLung (smoking, most common cause of cancer death), breast (BRCA, estrogen), colon (adenoma→carcinoma, screening saves lives), prostate (PSA, older men), leukemia (ALL kids, AML adults, CML Philadelphia chr, CLL older).
1:25–1:30Close + case recall + previewReturn to metastatic lung. Preview Unit 4 — neurologic structure before we see MS, stroke, Alzheimer's next.
Poll 1: Which of the following describes a benign tumor? (poorly differentiated, invasive, encapsulated, frequently metastasizes)
Poll 2: A T2N1M0 breast cancer means…
Discussion: "Why do cancers preferentially metastasize to certain organs (Paget's 'seed and soil')?"
Case turn: Swap the lung cancer → colon cancer with liver mets. Route? Stage? How do the symptoms differ from the primary site?
  • "Benign means harmless." A benign brain tumor in the posterior fossa can kill you. Location + compression matter.
  • "Staging = grading." Two entirely different things. Staging = spread; grading = differentiation.
  • "All -omas are benign." Lymphoma, melanoma, hepatoma are malignant.
  • "Metastatic means treat where it spread." No — primary dictates treatment (breast cancer in the bone is still treated as breast cancer).
  • "Cancer grows fast." Many cancers (prostate, some breast) grow slowly. Growth rate is not a reliable malignancy marker.

Assessment Bridge & Homework

  • Practice Qs 19–28 in the Midterm Review Guide.
  • Students submit one screening recommendation (USPSTF) for one of the big five, with rationale.
  • Preview: Unit 4 is pure structure — learn it cold so Unit 5 diseases make sense.

Unit 4  •  Structure & Function of the Nervous System  Ch 14

Pre-Webinar Prep

  • Read Ch 14; map out the CNS vs PNS divisions before class
  • Label-the-brain exercise pre-posted: cerebrum, cerebellum, brainstem, diencephalon
  • Short anatomy video review (students often arrive rusty on A&P)
  1. Name and locate the four lobes of the cerebrum and one cardinal function of each.
  2. Trace the pathway of an action potential across a synapse, identifying the 5 key steps.
  3. Differentiate sympathetic ("fight or flight") and parasympathetic ("rest and digest") effects across 6 organ systems.
  4. Describe the three meningeal layers and relate them to the three types of intracranial hemorrhage.
  5. Explain how the blood-brain barrier both protects and complicates drug delivery.
Case: A 22-year-old is hit in the temple by a line drive at a softball game. She walks off the field, feels fine for an hour, then collapses with a fixed, dilated pupil on the right. Ask: What is bleeding? Which artery, which space, which pattern on CT? Why the lucid interval? (Epidural hematoma — middle meningeal artery, arterial bleed under the skull but above dura, "lens-shaped" on CT.)

Poll 1: Which of the following is a parasympathetic effect? (pupil dilation, bronchodilation, ↑ GI motility, ↑ HR)
Poll 2: A patient needs a drug to act in the CNS. Which property predicts ability to cross the BBB? (large, water-soluble / small, lipid-soluble / protein-bound / ionized)
Discussion: "Why is the CSF sample more protective than blood for diagnosing meningitis?"
Think-pair-share: Sympathetic activation before a presentation — students describe their own heart, sweating, pupils, stomach sensations. Maps physiology to lived experience.
  • "Sympathetic is bad, parasympathetic is good." Both are necessary and balanced. Loss of parasympathetic = tachycardia.
  • "The BBB blocks everything." Lipid-soluble drugs (alcohol, most anesthetics) cross freely. That's why they work.
  • "Epidural and subdural are the same." Arterial vs venous, lens vs crescent, young vs elderly. Very different clinical pictures.
  • "Neurons regenerate like skin cells." CNS neurons do not regenerate. That's why stroke damage is permanent.
  • "CSF is made by the brain." Specifically by the choroid plexus in the ventricles, not by neurons.

  • Practice Qs 29–36 in the Midterm Review Guide.
  • Anatomy checkpoint quiz due before Unit 5 — 10 label-the-structure questions.
  • Preview: Unit 5 is where structure meets pathology. Come prepared to think in lesions.

Unit 5  •  Disorders of the Nervous System  Ch 16, 17, 18

Pre-Webinar Prep

  • Read Ch 16 (CNS disorders), 17 (PNS disorders), 18 (pediatric neuro) — densest reading in the course
  • Watch short stroke-scale demonstration video (NIHSS basics)
  • Pre-fill a case comparison worksheet: ischemic stroke vs hemorrhagic stroke on 5 features
  1. Differentiate ischemic vs hemorrhagic stroke in presentation, timing, imaging, and treatment window (tPA eligibility).
  2. Compare Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, Huntington's, and ALS by onset, affected neurons, cardinal signs, and cognitive vs motor profile.
  3. Describe the pathophysiology of multiple sclerosis and explain why symptoms come and go.
  4. Classify seizures (focal vs generalized) and identify status epilepticus as an emergency.
  5. Recognize bacterial vs viral meningitis on CSF analysis (protein, glucose, cell count, appearance).
  6. Describe one pediatric neuro condition (cerebral palsy, neural tube defect, or hydrocephalus) with its cause and clinical picture.
Case: A 68-year-old with atrial fibrillation on no anticoagulation wakes up at 7 AM with right arm weakness and slurred speech. Her husband calls 911 immediately; she arrives at the ED at 7:40 AM. Last known well: 11 PM the night before. Ask: Is she a candidate for tPA? Why or why not? What imaging do we need first? (No — time window is measured from last known well, not symptom recognition. Must exclude hemorrhage with non-contrast CT first.)

Poll 1: A patient's tremor is present at rest but improves with movement. Which disease? (Parkinson's, ALS, MS, Huntington's)
Poll 2: CSF shows ↓ glucose, ↑ protein, ↑ neutrophils. Diagnosis?
Discussion: "Why does MS come and go, but ALS relentlessly progress?" — gets at demyelination (potentially repairable) vs neuronal death (not).
Case branch: Switch the stroke patient to a hemorrhagic bleed — same symptoms, CT shows blood. How does treatment flip 180°?
  • "All strokes get tPA." Only ischemic within the time window, with no contraindications, after hemorrhage is excluded.
  • "Parkinson's is a memory disease." It's a movement disorder; dementia can occur late but isn't the cardinal feature.
  • "MS is the same as ALS." MS = demyelination, cognitive OK, relapsing. ALS = motor neuron death, cognition preserved, relentless.
  • "All seizures are grand mal." Absence, focal, and myoclonic seizures are very different in presentation and treatment.
  • "Meningitis always has fever and stiff neck." The elderly and immunocompromised often lack classic signs.

Unit 6  •  Endocrine System  Ch 19, 20

Pre-Webinar Prep

  • Read Ch 19 (endocrine regulation), 20 (endocrine disorders) — ~90 min reading
  • Watch 5-min video on hypothalamic-pituitary-target axis (negative feedback)
  • Complete a "normal values" pre-quiz: TSH, glucose, HbA1c, cortisol, Na, K (acts as anchor for the session)
  1. Explain the hypothalamic-pituitary-target gland axis using negative feedback.
  2. Differentiate type 1 and type 2 diabetes across pathogenesis, typical patient, acute complication, and management.
  3. Recognize DKA vs HHS by clinical and laboratory presentation.
  4. Identify Cushing syndrome, Addison disease, Graves, Hashimoto, SIADH, and DI by their hallmark features.
  5. Interpret paired TSH/T4 patterns to distinguish primary vs secondary thyroid disease.
  6. Explain why stopping chronic corticosteroids abruptly can be fatal.
Case: A 16-year-old presents with 3 weeks of polyuria, polydipsia, 12-pound weight loss despite "eating all the time." Today, she's breathing fast and deep, her breath smells like fruit, and she's confused. Glucose 580, pH 7.15, HCO₃ 10, ketones positive. Ask: Which type of diabetes? Which acute complication? Why Kussmaul breathing? What's the fluid and insulin plan? (New-onset T1DM with DKA. Kussmaul = compensation for metabolic acidosis.)

Poll 1: A patient is hypertensive with hypokalemia and metabolic alkalosis. Most likely hormonal cause? (↑ aldosterone, ↑ cortisol, ↓ insulin, ↓ ADH)
Poll 2: Patient has weight gain, cold intolerance, bradycardia, and elevated TSH. Most likely diagnosis?
Discussion: "Why do we taper steroids instead of stopping cold?" — negative feedback suppressed adrenal axis needs weeks to recover.
Case integration: T2DM patient with new weight loss and polyuria — is this still T2, or a "burned out" T2 now insulin-deficient, or a new diagnosis?
  • "Low TSH = hypothyroid." FALSE in primary hyperthyroidism: low TSH + high T4 = hyper (negative feedback suppressed TSH).
  • "Type 2 diabetics don't get DKA." Rare but possible — most present with HHS instead.
  • "Cushing and Addison are the same spectrum." Opposite: Cushing = too much cortisol; Addison = too little.
  • "SIADH and DI are both water problems." Yes but opposite directions. SIADH retains water (low Na); DI loses water (high Na).
  • "Stop steroids when symptoms resolve." Chronic steroids must be tapered — abrupt stop risks adrenal crisis (hypotension, shock, death).

Universal Webinar Delivery Checklist

Before the session (faculty)

  • LMS open, attendance ready, recording permissions confirmed
  • Polling tool (Mentimeter/Poll Everywhere/Zoom poll) pre-loaded with both polls for this unit
  • Case hook slide ready as the first slide shared — not the agenda
  • Review student pre-quiz responses (if used) to tailor emphasis areas
  • Confirm companion review guide section URL in chat ready to drop

During the session

  • Use the "rule of three" — interaction at least every 10–12 minutes
  • Call on 2–3 students by name per session; encourages presence without cold-calling anxiety
  • Answer questions in chat live when possible; park the rest for the final 10 minutes
  • Watch for the specific misconceptions flagged in each unit — they WILL appear
  • End 2–3 minutes early — questions bloom in the "unofficial" time

After the session

  • Post recording + poll results + cleaned chat transcript to LMS within 24 h
  • Send summary email linking the relevant section of the review guide and the 2 polls
  • Flag students with <60% on polls for office-hours outreach
  • Update faculty notes on what landed / what fell flat for next offering

Adapting to 60 min or 120 min
