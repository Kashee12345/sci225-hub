1.4 Cell Injury, Necrosis & Apoptosis

Hypoxic injury cascade
↓ O₂ → ↓ ATP → Na⁺/K⁺ pump fails → Na⁺ + H₂O into cell → cell swelling (reversible) → Ca²⁺ influx → enzyme activation (phospholipases, proteases) → membrane damage → necrosis (irreversible)
Necrosis subtypes
CoagulativeSolid organ ischemia (MI, kidney)
LiquefactiveBrain infarct, abscess
CaseousTB granuloma — "cheese-like"
FatPancreatitis, trauma to breast
FibrinoidVessel walls (vasculitis, malignant HTN)
GangrenousLimb ischemia (dry/wet)
NecrosisApoptosis
UnregulatedRegulated, programmed
Cell swells/burstsCell shrinks into bodies
Inflammation yesInflammation no
PathologicPhysiologic or pathologic
Apoptosis pathways: Intrinsic — mitochondrial; cytochrome c release → caspase cascade (DNA damage, withdrawal of growth factors). Extrinsic — death receptors (Fas/TNF) → caspase cascade.
Free radicals (ROS: O₂⁻, H₂O₂, OH•) damage membranes via lipid peroxidation, denature proteins, break DNA. Defenses: SOD, catalase, glutathione, vitamins C/E. Overwhelm → ischemia-reperfusion injury, aging, cancer.

1.5 Practice Questions

Q1. A smoker's biopsy shows replacement of ciliated columnar epithelium with stratified squamous cells. This is:
A. Atrophy   B. Hyperplasia   C. Metaplasia   D. Dysplasia
Show answer & rationale
C. Metaplasia. One mature cell type replaced by another to tolerate chronic stress. Reversible if stimulus removed; can progress to dysplasia/cancer. A = size ↓. B = number ↑. D = disordered (pre-malignant) but cells don't change type.
Q2. In hypoxic injury, cell swelling occurs because:
A. ATP ↑ forces water in   B. Na⁺/K⁺ pump fails → Na⁺ and water accumulate   C. Membrane permeability ↓   D. Ca²⁺ efflux
Show answer
B. No ATP → pump stops → intracellular Na⁺ rises → water follows osmotically → swelling. This is still reversible until membrane damage.
Q3 (SATA). Which are consistent with apoptosis (not necrosis)? Select all.
A. Cell shrinkage   B. Surrounding inflammation   C. Caspase activation   D. Membrane rupture   E. Programmed, ATP-dependent
Show answer
A, C, E. Apoptosis is orderly, non-inflammatory, caspase-mediated. B and D describe necrosis.
Q4. A child has Trisomy 21. The most likely cause is:
A. Autosomal recessive inheritance   B. Nondisjunction during meiosis   C. X-linked mutation   D. Mitochondrial inheritance
Show answer
B. Failure of chromosome pairs to separate during meiosis → gamete with extra chromosome → Trisomy 21.

2.6 Infection & Stress Response

Infection sequence
Encounter → entry → colonization → invasion → multiplication → tissue damage (toxins, host inflammation)
Fever: pyrogens (LPS, IL-1, TNF, IL-6) → hypothalamus raises thermoregulatory set point → shivering/vasoconstriction until new set point reached. Benefits: slows bacterial growth, enhances immune function.
Stress response (Selye GAS): Alarm (sympathetic + epi) → Resistance (HPA axis: CRH → ACTH → cortisol; ↑ glucose, ↓ inflammation, ↓ immune) → Exhaustion (if chronic → ↑ infection, HTN, DM, depression).

2.7 Practice Questions

Q5. A bee sting causes hypotension, hives, and wheezing within minutes. This is mediated by:
A. IgG complement fixation   B. IgE on mast cells releasing histamine   C. T-cell cytotoxicity   D. Immune complex deposition
Show answer
B. Type I hypersensitivity/anaphylaxis — IgE cross-linking triggers mast cell degranulation → histamine → vasodilation, permeability, bronchoconstriction.
Q6 (SATA). Early acute inflammation involves:
A. Vasodilation   B. ↑ capillary permeability   C. Neutrophil migration   D. Memory B-cell proliferation   E. Histamine release
Show answer
A, B, C, E. D is adaptive immunity — not the early driver of acute inflammation.
Q7. A patient receives a TB skin test and develops induration at 48 hours. This is:
A. Type I   B. Type II   C. Type III   D. Type IV
Show answer
D. Type IV delayed T-cell hypersensitivity. No antibodies involved; sensitized T cells react 24–72 hours after exposure.
Q8 (cloze). In anaphylaxis, the allergen binds ____ on mast cells; the mediator released is ____.
Show answer
IgE ; histamine.

3.4 Practice Questions

Q9. A tumor biopsy shows overexpression of growth factor receptors and continuous proliferative signaling. Which mechanism is active?
A. Tumor suppressor gain of function   B. Oncogene activation   C. Restored differentiation   D. Enhanced apoptosis
Show answer
B. Oncogenes are "gain-of-function" — activated drive continuous growth signals. Tumor suppressors go the other way (loss).
Q10. A small tumor cannot grow past 2 mm without:
A. Apoptosis   B. Angiogenesis   C. Differentiation   D. Cell cycle arrest
Show answer
B. Angiogenesis — new vessels (VEGF) supply O₂ and nutrients; diffusion alone supports only very small tumors.
Q11. TNM staging assesses:
A. Cell differentiation grade   B. Tumor size, node involvement, metastasis   C. Mutation burden   D. Apoptotic index
Show answer
B. Staging = how far it has spread; grading = how abnormal cells look.

4.2 Action Potentials & Neurotransmission

Action potential
Resting (−70 mV) → stimulus opens Na⁺ channels → depolarization → threshold reached → Na⁺ rushes in (spike) → K⁺ out (repolarization) → hyperpolarization → Na⁺/K⁺ pump restores
Saltatory conduction: AP jumps node → node along myelinated axon → faster, energy-efficient. Demyelination (MS, Guillain-Barré) slows conduction → weakness, numbness.

GlutamateMain excitatory; excitotoxicity in stroke

Reflex arc (monosynaptic)

4.3 CSF, BBB & Cerebral Circulation

CSF produced by choroid plexus of ventricles → flows through lateral → 3rd → 4th → subarachnoid space → absorbed by arachnoid villi. Cushions brain, delivers nutrients, removes waste.
Blood-Brain Barrier: tight endothelial junctions + astrocyte foot processes. Selectively permeable — excludes most pathogens/drugs; allows O₂, CO₂, glucose, lipid-soluble molecules.
Cerebral autoregulation maintains flow across MAP 60–160 mmHg. Below → ischemia; above → edema/hemorrhage. Brain uses ~20% of cardiac output despite being 2% of body weight — very vulnerable to hypoxia.

Unit 5  •  Disorders of the Nervous System  Ch 16, 17, 18

5.1 Increased ICP, Stroke

Monro-Kellie: skull is rigid with 3 fixed contents (brain 80%, blood 10%, CSF 10%). If one ↑, another must ↓ or ICP rises. Normal ICP < 15 mmHg.
Increased ICP cascade
↑ ICP → ↓ cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP = MAP − ICP) → ischemia → edema → further ↑ ICP → herniation → death
Cushing's triad (late, ominous sign of ↑ ICP): HTN (widened pulse pressure) + bradycardia + irregular respirations. Reflects brainstem compression.
Ischemic stroke (~85%)Hemorrhagic stroke (~15%)
CauseThrombus or embolus blocks arteryVessel rupture (HTN, aneurysm, AVM)
Mechanism↓ blood flow → ↓ ATP → Na⁺/K⁺ fail → glutamate excitotoxicity → neuronal deathBlood compresses tissue + ↑ ICP
OnsetSudden focal deficitSudden worst-headache, ↓ LOC
Tx windowtPA ≤ 4.5 hr if no bleedControl BP, surgery; tPA contraindicated
Why focal deficits in stroke: specific artery territory loses O₂ → neurons in that region fail → symptoms map to function (MCA → contralateral face/arm weakness, aphasia if left hemisphere).

5.6 Practice Questions

B. Cushing's. Cortisol excess → central obesity, moon face, hyperglycemia, HTN, immunosuppression.

A. HHS — severe dehydration without ketosis   B. DKA — absolute insulin deficiency → fat breakdown → ketoacids   C. Hypoglycemia   D. SIADH

B. DKA. No insulin → cells can't use glucose → lipolysis → ketones → metabolic acidosis → Kussmaul compensatory hyperventilation.

Final Study Tips

  • Explain out loud. If you can say why each finding happens, you're ready.
  • Follow the cascade. Most questions ask you to trace stimulus → mechanism → result → compensation.
  • Watch for triads. Cushing's triad (ICP), 3 P's (DM), classic anaphylaxis triad (hypotension + airway + skin).
  • Differentiate look-alikes. DKA vs HHS; Cushing vs Addison; Hashimoto vs Graves; SIADH vs DI; ischemic vs hemorrhagic stroke.
  • Up vs down. Low hormone → up-regulation ↑ sensitivity; high hormone → down-regulation ↓ sensitivity.
