Unit 1  •  Hormonal Regulation  LO1

1.1 Negative Feedback — The Universal Rule

Hormones are kept within tight ranges by negative feedback. The pattern repeats across every axis: hypothalamus releases a "releasing hormone" → anterior pituitary releases a "stimulating hormone" → target gland makes the final hormone → the final hormone shuts off hypothalamus and pituitary. Lose the final hormone, and the upstream signals climb. Have too much, and they fall.
Hypothalamus (releasing hormone) → Anterior pituitary (stimulating hormone) → Target gland (final hormone) → Target tissue effects → Final hormone inhibits hypothalamus & pituitary
Why negative feedback matters for the lab patterns. If a final hormone is high because of disease in the target gland itself, the upstream signals (releasing/stimulating hormones) will be low because feedback is intact. If a final hormone is high because the pituitary is making too much stimulating hormone, the stimulating hormone is also high. This is how you tell primary (gland problem) from secondary (pituitary problem) disease.

The posterior pituitary releases ADH and oxytocin — but doesn't make them. They're produced by the hypothalamus and stored in the posterior pituitary for release.

1.3 Thyroid Hormone Synthesis — High Yield

Thyroid follicular cell takes up iodide → oxidizes iodide to iodine → iodine attaches to tyrosine residues on thyroglobulin (organification) → coupling to form T3 and T4 → stored in colloid → released into blood when TSH stimulates
Iodide uptake is the first step in thyroid hormone synthesis — without iodine, the gland cannot make hormone. Long-term iodine deficiency causes hypothyroidism and a compensatory goiter (the gland grows under chronic TSH stimulation).
Why goiter forms. If T4 is low (any reason — iodine deficiency, autoimmune destruction, etc.), the pituitary senses it and pumps out more TSH. TSH is a growth signal for the thyroid → the gland enlarges trying to make more hormone. The same mechanism produces goiter in both hypothyroidism (compensating) and hyperthyroidism (Graves auto-antibody activates the TSH receptor and drives growth).

The posterior pituitary stores. The anterior pituitary makes. When pituitary stalk damage shuts down hormone release, anterior pituitary hormones (TSH, ACTH, FSH/LH, GH, prolactin) all drop because they require hypothalamic releasing hormones. Posterior pituitary function depends on the still-functional hypothalamic neurons.

Practice Questions — Hormonal Regulation

Q1. Which step occurs first in normal thyroid hormone synthesis?
A. Coupling of iodinated tyrosines into T3 and T4
B. Iodide uptake by thyroid follicular cells
C. Storage of T3/T4 in the colloid
D. Release of T4 into the blood
Show answer
B. Iodide must be taken up first; without iodine, no hormone can be made. Then oxidation, organification onto thyroglobulin, coupling, storage, release.
Q2. Which structure releases the hormone that initiates the thyroid hormone regulatory pathway?
A. Anterior pituitary
B. Hypothalamus (TRH)
C. Thyroid gland
D. Adrenal cortex
Show answer
B. Hypothalamus releases TRH → pituitary releases TSH → thyroid releases T3/T4. The cascade always starts with the hypothalamus.
Q3. A 52-year-old has a traumatic brain injury that damages the pituitary stalk. Over the next few days, TSH, ACTH, and LH all become very low. Which mechanism best explains this?
A. Direct destruction of the thyroid, adrenal, and gonads
B. Loss of hypothalamic releasing hormones reaching the anterior pituitary, causing decreased pituitary hormone release
C. Negative feedback from elevated peripheral hormones
D. Posterior pituitary failure
Show answer
B. Stalk damage interrupts the portal blood flow that delivers TRH, CRH, GnRH and other releasing factors from hypothalamus to anterior pituitary. The pituitary becomes "deaf" to upstream signals → broad anterior pituitary hormone deficiency (panhypopituitarism).
Q4. Dropdown. In normal feedback, when serum cortisol is high, [select: CRH and ACTH increase / CRH and ACTH decrease / cortisol release increases further], which represents [select: positive feedback / negative feedback / sympathetic compensation].
Show answer
CRH and ACTH decrease · negative feedback. Final hormones turn off the upstream signals to maintain a set point.

Unit 3  •  Pituitary Disorders  LO3

3.1 Anterior Pituitary Disorders

DisorderMechanismClinical picture
AcromegalyPituitary adenoma secreting excess GH in adults (after epiphyses close)Slowly progressive enlargement of hands, feet, jaw, facial bones; coarse features; large tongue; HTN; insulin resistance; diabetes; sleep apnea
GigantismExcess GH in children (before epiphyses close)Excessive linear growth, very tall stature
ProlactinomaPituitary adenoma producing prolactinGalactorrhea, amenorrhea, infertility, decreased libido; in men: erectile dysfunction
HypopituitarismSurgery, trauma, tumor, Sheehan syndrome (postpartum pituitary necrosis), radiationMultiple hormone deficiencies: low TSH, ACTH, LH/FSH, GH; cold intolerance, fatigue, low BP, infertility, hypoglycemia
Pituitary mass effectMacroadenoma compresses adjacent structuresCompression of optic chiasm → bitemporal hemianopsia (loss of peripheral visual fields); headaches
Acromegaly classic vignette: "Slowly progressive enlargement of the hands and facial bones; pituitary tumor releasing growth hormone." GH stimulates the liver to release IGF-1, which drives the growth of soft tissue, organs, and remaining cartilage. Diagnosis: elevated IGF-1, oral glucose suppression test (GH should suppress; in acromegaly it does not). Treatment: transsphenoidal resection.
Bitemporal hemianopsia = the visual field defect caused by a pituitary macroadenoma pressing upward on the optic chiasm. The crossing fibers from the nasal halves of each retina (which carry temporal vision) are compressed → loss of peripheral vision in both eyes.
Hypopituitarism's broad effects. When the anterior pituitary fails, every downstream gland it controls quiets down. Patients lose stress response (low cortisol from low ACTH), get cold and slow (low T4 from low TSH), lose fertility (low LH/FSH), lose growth signaling (low GH), and may not lactate (low prolactin). The thyroid and adrenal effects are usually most clinically threatening.

3.2 Posterior Pituitary — SIADH vs Diabetes Insipidus

SIADH (too much ADH)Diabetes Insipidus (too little ADH)
MechanismExcess ADH → kidneys reabsorb too much waterInadequate ADH → kidneys can't concentrate urine
CausesSmall-cell lung cancer (paraneoplastic), CNS disease, drugs (SSRIs, carbamazepine), pulmonary diseaseCentral: pituitary surgery, trauma, tumor. Nephrogenic: lithium, hypercalcemia, kidney disease.
Serum sodiumLOW (dilutional hyponatremia)HIGH (free water loss)
Serum osmolalityLOWHIGH
UrineConcentrated (high osmolality), low volumeDilute (low osmolality), large volume (polyuria)
SymptomsConfusion, weakness, seizures (from hyponatremia and brain swelling)Severe thirst, polyuria, dehydration if no access to water
TreatmentFluid restriction, treat cause; vaptans for severe casesCentral: desmopressin (synthetic ADH). Nephrogenic: treat cause.
"S = Soaked, D = Dry" — and remember they have opposite labs:
SIADH = Soaked, low Na, concentrated urine.
DI = Dry, high Na, dilute urine.
Don't reverse the urine pattern. SIADH retains water → urine has nowhere to dump it → urine is concentrated. DI loses water → urine is dilute. Beginners often pick the opposite because "high Na means concentrated" feels intuitive but is wrong here — what matters is what the kidney is doing with water, not the serum number.

Practice Questions — Pituitary Disorders

Q10. A patient has slowly developed enlarged hands and facial bones over years. Imaging shows a pituitary tumor releasing growth hormone. Which diagnosis?
A. Gigantism
B. Acromegaly
C. Cushing disease
D. Prolactinoma
Show answer
B. Excess GH in adulthood (post-epiphyseal closure) → bone widening rather than lengthening = acromegaly. Gigantism would be the same hormone but in a child.
Q11. A patient with lung cancer develops confusion. Labs: Na 118, low serum osmolality, concentrated urine. Most likely diagnosis?
A. Diabetes insipidus
B. SIADH (paraneoplastic from small-cell lung cancer)
C. Hypothyroidism
D. Adrenal insufficiency
Show answer
B. Small-cell lung cancer is the textbook paraneoplastic SIADH cause. Excess ADH retains water → dilutional hyponatremia → confusion. Treat with fluid restriction.
Q12. After pituitary surgery, a patient develops thirst, polyuria, hypernatremia, and dilute urine. Best diagnosis?
A. SIADH
B. Central diabetes insipidus
C. Adrenal insufficiency
D. Hypothyroidism
Show answer
B. Pituitary surgery → loss of ADH release → kidneys can't reabsorb water → polyuria, dilute urine, hypernatremia. Treat with desmopressin.
Q13. A patient previously had pituitary tumor surgery. Labs now show low T4 and low TSH. Which best explains the picture?
A. Primary hypothyroidism (Hashimoto's)
B. Secondary (central) hypothyroidism from pituitary failure
C. Subclinical hyperthyroidism
D. Iodine deficiency
Show answer
B. Both T4 and TSH are low → the gland is making less hormone because the pituitary is no longer signaling it. In primary hypothyroidism, T4 is low but TSH is HIGH (intact feedback).
Q14. A patient has a pituitary macroadenoma growing upward toward the optic chiasm. The expected visual deficit is:
A. Monocular blindness
B. Bitemporal hemianopsia (loss of peripheral fields in both eyes)
C. Central scotoma
D. Homonymous hemianopsia
Show answer
B. The chiasm crossing fibers carry temporal visual fields; compressing them loses peripheral vision bilaterally — classic pituitary macroadenoma sign.

Unit 4  •  Thyroid Disorders  LO3

4.1 Hyperthyroidism — Graves Disease

Graves disease is the most common cause of hyperthyroidism. It's a type II hypersensitivity: IgG autoantibodies bind the TSH receptor on thyroid cells and activate it (mimicking TSH). The gland makes too much T3/T4 regardless of feedback. Result: high T4, suppressed TSH (because feedback is intact and pituitary senses too much T4).

Classic Graves features:

  • Weight loss despite increased appetite
  • Heat intolerance, sweating
  • Tremor, anxiety, irritability
  • Tachycardia, palpitations, atrial fibrillation
  • Diffuse goiter (auto-antibodies stimulate growth of the whole gland)
  • Exophthalmos / proptosis (bulging eyes — orbital infiltration unique to Graves)
  • Pretibial myxedema (some patients)
  • Lab: ↑ T3/T4, ↓ TSH, positive anti-TSH receptor antibodies
Thyroid storm = life-threatening hyperthyroidism. Fever, tachycardia (often AF), agitation, delirium, possible cardiac collapse. Triggered by surgery, infection, or stopping antithyroid drugs. Treatment: beta blockers, methimazole/PTU, iodide, supportive care.

Physiologic effects of excess thyroid hormone

  • ↑ basal metabolic rate, ↑ heat production, heat intolerance
  • ↑ heart rate, ↑ contractility, palpitations, AF risk
  • ↑ GI motility (diarrhea, weight loss)
  • ↑ CNS excitability (anxiety, tremor, insomnia)
  • ↑ bone turnover (osteoporosis with chronic hyperthyroidism)

4.2 Hypothyroidism — Hashimoto Thyroiditis

Hashimoto thyroiditis is the most common cause of hypothyroidism in iodine-replete countries. It's a type IV hypersensitivity: T cells gradually destroy thyroid follicles. Antibodies (anti-TPO, anti-thyroglobulin) are markers but not the main destructive force. Result: low T4, high TSH (pituitary trying to drive a failing gland).

Classic hypothyroidism features:

  • Weight gain, fatigue, cold intolerance
  • Bradycardia, hypotension
  • Constipation
  • Dry skin, brittle hair/nails, hair loss
  • Slowed mentation, depression, lethargy
  • Goiter (compensatory growth from chronic TSH stimulation)
  • Menstrual irregularity, infertility
  • Lab: ↑ TSH, ↓ T4, positive anti-TPO antibodies
Myxedema coma = severe untreated hypothyroidism with profound bradycardia, hypotension, hypothermia, and altered mental status. Mortality is high without prompt IV levothyroxine.
Why TSH is the best screening test for thyroid disease. Even small changes in T4 produce large changes in TSH (the hypothalamic-pituitary axis is very sensitive to feedback). TSH high → primary hypothyroidism. TSH low + high T4 → primary hyperthyroidism. TSH low + low T4 → secondary (pituitary) hypothyroidism.
The TSH-T4 reading rule:
TSH ↓ + T4 ↑ → primary HYPER (Graves)
TSH ↑ + T4 ↓ → primary HYPO (Hashimoto)
TSH ↓ + T4 ↓ → secondary HYPO (pituitary failure)
TSH ↑ + T4 ↑ → very rare TSH-secreting pituitary tumor

Unit 5  •  Adrenal Disorders  LO3

5.1 Cushing Syndrome (Excess Cortisol)

Excess cortisol exposure — from any source. Cortisol's normal jobs (stress response, gluconeogenesis, immune suppression, blood pressure support) become pathologic when amplified.
Causes: exogenous corticosteroid therapy (most common), pituitary adenoma making ACTH (Cushing disease, specifically), adrenal adenoma, ectopic ACTH (small-cell lung cancer).

Classic Cushing features:

  • Central (truncal) obesity, thin extremities
  • Moon face, buffalo hump, supraclavicular fat pads
  • Purple striae on abdomen, breasts, thighs
  • Skin thinning, easy bruising, poor wound healing
  • Hyperglycemia, secondary diabetes
  • Hypertension
  • Osteoporosis, proximal muscle weakness
  • Mood changes, depression, psychosis
  • Infection susceptibility
  • Lab: elevated cortisol that does not suppress on dexamethasone test
"Cortisol stays high despite normal feedback signals." When the source of excess cortisol is autonomous (adrenal tumor or exogenous steroids), cortisol does not respond to the normal negative feedback — ACTH cannot turn it off. This is the lab finding that distinguishes Cushing from a normal stress response.

5.2 Addison Disease (Primary Adrenal Insufficiency)

Loss of adrenal cortex function — most often autoimmune destruction in developed countries; TB worldwide. Loss of all three cortical zones means low cortisol AND low aldosterone AND low androgens. Pituitary responds to low cortisol with high ACTH.

Classic Addison features:

  • Fatigue, weight loss, weakness
  • Hypotension, especially orthostatic — from aldosterone loss + cortisol loss
  • Hyperpigmentation of skin, gums, palmar creases — high ACTH stimulates melanocytes (POMC precursor includes MSH)
  • Hyperkalemia, hyponatremia from aldosterone deficiency
  • Hypoglycemia (cortisol normally raises glucose)
  • Salt craving
  • Anorexia, nausea, vomiting
  • Lab: low cortisol, high ACTH, hyperkalemia, hyponatremia
Addisonian crisis = acute adrenal insufficiency. Triggers include surgery, infection, trauma, or sudden steroid withdrawal in a chronically treated patient. Profound hypotension, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, hypoglycemia. Give IV hydrocortisone immediately.
"You can stop chronic steroids whenever you want." No — chronic exogenous steroids suppress the HPA axis. The adrenal cortex atrophies. Abrupt withdrawal can trigger Addisonian crisis. Always taper.
Cushing vs Addison — opposite directions:
Cushing = too much cortisol → high BP, high glucose, weight gain, moon face, striae, low K, immunosuppression.
Addison = too little cortisol + aldosterone → low BP, low glucose, weight loss, hyperpigmentation, high K, low Na.

Practice Questions — Adrenal

Appendix  •  Top Missteps & Master Mnemonics

Top 15 Week 6 Missteps

#The trapWhat's actually true
1"Low TSH = hypothyroid."Low TSH + HIGH T4 = primary HYPERthyroid (negative feedback suppressing TSH).
2"SIADH = dilute urine."SIADH = concentrated urine (kidney holding water). DI = dilute urine.
3"Posterior pituitary makes ADH and oxytocin."It stores them; the hypothalamus makes them.
4"Acromegaly causes excessive linear growth."Excess GH AFTER epiphyseal closure → bone widening (hands, feet, jaw). Gigantism = before closure.
5"Pituitary stalk damage drops only one hormone."It typically drops MOST anterior pituitary hormones (TSH, ACTH, LH/FSH, GH).
6"Graves is destruction of the thyroid."Graves is activation by TSH receptor antibodies. Hashimoto is destruction.
7"Cushing causes weight loss."Cushing = central weight gain, moon face, buffalo hump.
8"Addison causes hypokalemia."Addison = hyperkalemia, hyponatremia (aldosterone loss).
9"Steroids can be stopped abruptly when you feel better."Chronic steroids must be tapered to avoid Addisonian crisis.
10"Goiter only happens in hyperthyroidism."Goiter happens in both hyper- and hypothyroidism — chronic TSH stimulation in either.
11"Bitemporal hemianopsia is a stroke."Classic for pituitary macroadenoma compressing the optic chiasm.
12"Insulin comes from the adrenal."Insulin = pancreatic beta cells in islets of Langerhans.
13"DI and SIADH are both water gain."DI = water loss (high Na). SIADH = water gain (low Na). Opposite directions.
14"Parathyroid is one big gland."Four small glands on the posterior surface of the thyroid.
15"Hyperpigmentation in adrenal disease means Cushing."Hyperpigmentation = Addison (high ACTH stimulates melanocytes via MSH).

Week 6 Study Strategy

  • Drill negative feedback first. If you can read TSH/T4 or ACTH/cortisol pairs and call primary vs secondary, half the unit is yours.
  • Pair every disorder with its lab fingerprint. Graves = ↑T4 ↓TSH. Hashimoto = ↓T4 ↑TSH. Cushing = ↑ cortisol no suppression. Addison = ↓ cortisol ↑ ACTH.
  • Lock down SIADH vs DI as opposites. Soaked vs Dry. They will be on the test side by side.
  • Memorize the FLAT PEG anterior hormones and the posterior pair (ADH, oxytocin).
  • Cushing vs Addison side by side. Cortisol direction dictates everything else (BP, glucose, K, weight, pigmentation).
  • "Acromegaly = adults; gigantism = children." Same hormone, different growth plate status.
  • Anatomy patterns are easy points. Butterfly = thyroid. Top of kidneys = adrenal. Behind thyroid = parathyroid. Above pituitary = hypothalamus. Anterior chest = thymus.
