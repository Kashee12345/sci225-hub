Unit 1  •  Biology of Cancer  LO1

Cancer is the outcome of accumulated mutations in a cell that disable the normal controls on growth, death, and tissue boundaries. The result: a clone of cells that proliferates, refuses to die, evades the immune system, builds its own blood supply, invades adjacent tissue, and ultimately spreads.

1.1 Three Gene Categories That Matter in Cancer

Gene typeNormal jobCancer changeHow many "hits" needed?Examples
Proto-oncogene → oncogenePromote cell growth (gas pedal)Activated / overexpressed → uncontrolled growthJust one allele needs to be activated (gain of function — dominant)HER2 (breast), RAS (many), MYC (lymphoma), BCR-ABL (CML)
Tumor suppressor geneBrake on cell growth (brake pedal)Inactivated / lost → no brakesBoth alleles must be lost (Knudson's two-hit hypothesis — recessive at the cell level)TP53 (~half of all cancers), RB1 (retinoblastoma), BRCA1/2, APC (colon)
DNA repair geneFix mutations as they happenLoss of repair → mutations accumulate elsewhereBoth alleles (similar logic to suppressors)BRCA1/2, MLH1/MSH2 (Lynch syndrome)
Why oncogenes need only one hit but tumor suppressors need two.
A proto-oncogene normally has carefully regulated activity. Activate one copy abnormally and it overproduces growth signals — the other normal copy can't shut it off. Gain of function = dominant.
A tumor suppressor still works if one copy is intact (the other does the job). You need to lose both copies before the brake is gone — Knudson's two-hit. Loss of function = recessive at the cellular level.

How oncogenes get activated

  • Point mutation — single nucleotide change makes the gene constitutively active (RAS)
  • Gene amplification — extra copies of the gene (HER2 amplified in some breast cancers)
  • Chromosomal translocation — gene is moved next to an active promoter (BCR-ABL Philadelphia chromosome in CML)
  • Insertion / viral integration — virus inserts near the gene (HTLV-1, HPV)
HER2 overexpression in breast cancer is a textbook oncogene-activation example. The HER2 gene is amplified, the receptor is overexpressed on the cell surface, and the tumor responds to anti-HER2 therapy (trastuzumab). When you see "HER2 amplified" or "HER2 positive," think oncogene activation by amplification.

1.2 Stages of Cancer Development

Normal → hyperplasia → dysplasia → carcinoma in situ → invasive carcinoma → metastatic carcinoma
StageWhat's happening
HyperplasiaIncreased number of normal-looking cells in response to a stimulus. Reversible if stimulus removed.
DysplasiaDisorganized, abnormal-looking cells; loss of normal architecture; nuclear pleomorphism (size/shape variation), increased nuclear-to-cytoplasm ratio. Still confined to the tissue. Often reversible if cause removed.
Carcinoma in situSeverely dysplastic cells fill the full thickness of the epithelium but have NOT yet broken through the basement membrane. "Pre-invasive cancer."
Invasive carcinomaTumor cells breach the basement membrane and invade surrounding stroma. Capable of metastasis from this point.
Metastatic diseaseTumor cells reach distant sites via lymphatic or hematogenous spread.
What the basement membrane crossing means. Once tumor cells break through the basement membrane, they have access to lymphatics and blood vessels. Crossing the basement membrane is the defining event that converts pre-invasive dysplasia/CIS into "real" cancer — and it requires acquiring the ability to make matrix-degrading enzymes (matrix metalloproteinases).
"Dysplasia is cancer." Not yet — dysplasia is pre-cancerous, often reversible. Carcinoma in situ is the immediate precursor. Invasive carcinoma is the first stage that meets the definition of cancer (basement membrane crossed).

Practice Questions — Biology of Cancer

Show answer
A, B, D. All gain-of-function changes in proto-oncogenes. C (TP53 loss) and E (BRCA1 loss) are tumor-suppressor inactivations — the opposite mechanism, requiring both alleles to be lost.

A. Hyperplasia
B. Dysplasia / carcinoma in situ
C. Invasive adenocarcinoma
D. Metastatic disease

Show answer
A, B, C. D (loss of both alleles) is the mechanism for tumor suppressor inactivation, not oncogene activation. E describes a DNA repair gene defect.

Unit 2  •  Tumor Behavior — Spread, Angiogenesis, Cachexia  LO1

2.1 Invasion and Metastasis

Tumor at primary site → degrades basement membrane (matrix metalloproteinases) → invades local stroma → enters lymphatic or blood vessel → survives in circulation → arrests in distant capillary bed → extravasates into new tissue → grows new colony at distant site
Metastasis = the ability to seed new tumors in distant tissues. This is the property that makes cancer lethal. Without it, even large primary tumors can often be removed surgically. Cells must acquire all of the following: ability to detach from neighbors, degrade matrix, intravasate into vessels, survive shear forces and immune attack, and proliferate in foreign tissue.
RouteTypical forCommon destinations
LymphaticMost carcinomasRegional lymph nodes first, then central nodes
Hematogenous (blood)Sarcomas; many late-stage carcinomasLiver, lung, bone, brain — the "big four" metastatic sites
Direct extensionMost tumorsAdjacent organs and structures
SeedingTumors of body cavities (ovarian, gastric)Peritoneum, pleura
"Which characteristic of tumor cells directly allows spread to distant organs?" The answer is the ability to invade through the basement membrane and enter blood or lymphatic vessels. Without invasion, there is no metastasis.

2.2 Angiogenesis

A tumor larger than ~1–2 mm cannot survive on diffusion alone — it must build new blood vessels (angiogenesis) to deliver oxygen and nutrients and remove waste. Tumors secrete vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) and other angiogenic factors that recruit endothelial cells from existing capillaries to form new vessels.
Why anti-angiogenic drugs work in cancer. Drugs like bevacizumab (anti-VEGF) starve tumors by blocking the new-vessel signal. Tumors cannot grow past the diffusion limit without their own vasculature, and without growth, metastasis is also limited.

2.3 Cancer Cachexia and Paraneoplastic Syndromes

Cancer cachexia = the wasting syndrome of advanced cancer. Severe weight loss, muscle loss, fatigue, and weakness despite adequate caloric intake. Driven by tumor- and host-derived inflammatory cytokines — TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6 — which:
  • Suppress appetite (anorexia)
  • Increase resting metabolic rate
  • Promote skeletal muscle protein breakdown (proteolysis)
  • Promote lipid breakdown (lipolysis)
  • Cause insulin resistance
The tumor itself also competes for nutrients, but the cytokine-driven hypermetabolism is the key reason eating more does not fix the wasting.
Why patients lose weight despite eating. The body is in a catabolic state — breaking down muscle and fat faster than food can replace. Caloric supplementation alone cannot overcome the inflammatory drive. Best management requires treating the underlying cancer plus supportive nutrition.

Paraneoplastic syndromes — tumor effects unrelated to direct invasion

SyndromeCancerMechanism

"Bystander mutations" — a melanoma sample with many mutations that don't change tumor behavior is showing passenger mutations. Cancers accumulate hundreds of incidental mutations alongside the few "driver" mutations that actually fuel growth. Recognizing this distinction matters for understanding tumor genomics.

Practice Questions — Tumor Behavior

Q6. Which characteristic of tumor cells most directly allows spread to distant organs?
A. Increased size of tumor cells
B. Ability to invade through the basement membrane and enter blood or lymphatic vessels
C. Slower-than-normal cell division
D. Increased oxygen consumption
Show answer
B. Crossing the basement membrane and entering vessels is the gateway to metastasis. Without invasion, tumors stay local.
Q7. A lung tumor shows extensive new blood vessel formation on biopsy. Which process most directly accounts for this?
A. Apoptosis
B. Angiogenesis driven by tumor-released VEGF
C. Necrosis of stromal cells
D. Inflammatory edema
Show answer
B. VEGF and other tumor-secreted angiogenic factors recruit endothelial cells to build new vessels — without them, tumors cannot grow past 1–2 mm.
Q8. Select all that apply. A patient with metastatic breast cancer has weight loss, muscle loss, and high inflammatory markers. Which mechanisms explain cachexia in this patient?
A. TNF-α, IL-1, and IL-6 drive proteolysis and lipolysis
B. Hypermetabolism increases caloric expenditure
C. Cytokines suppress appetite
D. The tumor itself uses energy and nutrients
E. The patient is simply not eating enough food
Show answer
A, B, C, D. E is incorrect — patients with cachexia often eat normally; cytokine-driven catabolism is the dominant mechanism. Just adding calories is not sufficient.
Q9. A breast biopsy shows marked HER2 overexpression. Which gene mechanism is responsible?
A. Loss of both copies of a tumor suppressor
B. Gain of function in a proto-oncogene through gene amplification
C. Mutation of a DNA repair gene
D. Translocation of HER2 onto chromosome 22
Show answer
B. HER2 amplification produces extra copies of the gene → overexpression of the receptor → unchecked growth signaling. Targeted therapy (trastuzumab) blocks this receptor.

Unit 3  •  Naming, Grading & Staging  LO1

3.1 Naming Conventions

Tissue of originBenignMalignant
Epithelium-oma (e.g., adenoma, papilloma)-carcinoma (adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma)
Connective tissue (bone, muscle, fat, cartilage)-oma (lipoma, chondroma, leiomyoma)-sarcoma (osteosarcoma, liposarcoma, rhabdomyosarcoma)
Hematopoietic (blood-forming)—Leukemia (in blood/marrow); lymphoma (in lymph nodes/tissue)
Embryonic / "blast" cells—-blastoma (neuroblastoma, retinoblastoma, hepatoblastoma)
"All -omas are benign." Watch out for these malignancies that end in -oma despite being malignant: lymphoma, melanoma, hepatoma (hepatocellular carcinoma), seminoma. Memorize this short list.

3.2 Grading vs Staging — Don't Confuse Them

GradingStaging
What it measuresHow differentiated (how much the cells resemble normal tissue)How far the cancer has spread
SourceMicroscopic appearance (pathologist looking at biopsy)Clinical exam, imaging, surgical findings
ScaleGrade I (well-differentiated, looks like normal) → Grade IV (poorly differentiated, anaplastic)TNM: Tumor size · Nodes · Metastasis. Combined into stages 0–IV.
What it predictsHow aggressive the tumor cells are intrinsicallyPrognosis and treatment plan (usually predicts outcome better than grade)
"Stage = Spread, Grade = Grossness."
Stage answers "where is it?" Grade answers "how ugly is it under the microscope?"
For most cancers, stage predicts prognosis better than grade. A small, low-stage but high-grade tumor often has a better outcome than a large, widely metastatic but low-grade tumor.

Unit 4  •  Cancer Epidemiology — Risk Factors & Prevention  LO2

4.1 Modifiable Risk Factors

ExposureCancers most associatedMechanism
Tobacco / smokingLung, larynx, oral, esophagus, bladder, kidney, pancreas, cervixCarcinogens in smoke (nitrosamines, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons) cause DNA damage; chronic inflammation; metaplasia → dysplasia → cancer
Chronic alcohol useOral cavity, pharynx, larynx, esophagus, liver, breast, colorectalAlcohol metabolism produces acetaldehyde — a highly toxic, carcinogenic metabolite that damages DNA
Obesity / metabolic syndromeEndometrial, postmenopausal breast, colorectal, kidney, esophageal adenocarcinoma, pancreatic, liverChronic inflammation, insulin resistance, hyperinsulinemia, ↑ estrogen from adipose tissue
UV radiation (sun, tanning beds)Basal cell, squamous cell, melanomaUV-induced pyrimidine dimers in skin DNA → mutations in TP53 and other genes; cumulative damage with age
Ionizing radiationLeukemia, thyroid, breastDirect DNA strand breaks

Hepatitis B/CHepatocellular carcinomaChronic inflammation → cirrhosis → cancer
H. pyloriGastric, MALT lymphomaChronic gastritis → atrophy → metaplasia → dysplasia → cancer
Diethylstilbestrol (DES)Clear cell adenocarcinoma of the vagina/cervix in daughters of mothers who took DESTransplacental hormonal disruption of müllerian development
Why smoking causes lung cancer. Each cigarette delivers thousands of carcinogens that damage bronchial DNA. The bronchial epithelium first undergoes metaplasia (ciliated columnar → squamous), then dysplasia, then carcinoma in situ, and finally invasive cancer. The progression takes years. Stopping smoking reduces risk over time but never returns it to the never-smoker baseline.
Why obesity increases cancer risk. Adipose tissue is metabolically active — it produces inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α), aromatizes androgens to estrogen (raising postmenopausal breast and endometrial cancer risk), and drives insulin resistance with hyperinsulinemia (a growth signal for tumor cells). Metabolic syndrome links these together: central obesity, hypertension, dyslipidemia, hyperglycemia, hyperinsulinemia.
Alcohol and acetaldehyde. Ethanol → alcohol dehydrogenase → acetaldehyde → aldehyde dehydrogenase → acetate. Acetaldehyde is the carcinogenic intermediate. Patients with reduced ALDH activity (common in some Asian populations) accumulate more acetaldehyde and have higher cancer risk per drink.

B. UVB radiation produces pyrimidine dimers in DNA, which if not repaired produce mutations including in TP53. Cumulative damage drives basal cell, squamous cell, and melanoma development.

D. Lactate

B. Alcohol → acetaldehyde → acetate. Acetaldehyde is the carcinogenic intermediate that damages DNA in the liver, oral cavity, esophagus, and beyond.

D. Ovarian serous carcinoma

B. Daughters of mothers who took DES (an estrogen used to prevent miscarriage from the 1940s–70s) have an elevated risk of clear cell adenocarcinoma of the vagina/cervix — a classic transplacental carcinogen example.

B. Metabolic syndrome and obesity drive multiple cancers via inflammation, insulin/IGF signaling, and aromatase-mediated estrogen excess.

A. Trisomy 21
B. BRCA1 or BRCA2 mutation
C. Klinefelter syndrome
D. Lynch syndrome

B. Two first-degree relatives with breast cancer should prompt BRCA testing referral. BRCA1/2 mutations are autosomal dominant tumor-suppressor losses associated with breast and ovarian cancer (and others).

Unit 5  •  Pediatric Cancers  LO3

Childhood cancers are biologically different from adult cancers. They more often arise from embryonal tissue ("-blastomas"), are more often associated with germline genetic predisposition, and tend to involve the blood, brain, and abdominal organs more than the epithelial sites that drive adult cancers.
Why leukemia is the most common childhood cancer. Children's bone marrow is highly proliferative — building red cells, white cells, and platelets at high rates throughout growth. Highly proliferating tissues are more vulnerable to mutations during DNA replication. Add limited exposure to environmental carcinogens that drive adult epithelial cancers, and the result is a disease pattern dominated by hematopoietic malignancies.

5.1 Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL)

ALL is the most common childhood cancer. Peak age 2–5. Driven by accumulation of immature lymphoblasts in the marrow that crowd out normal hematopoiesis.

Classic presentation:

  • Anemia → fatigue, pallor
  • Thrombocytopenia → bruising, petechiae, mucosal bleeding
  • Neutropenia → frequent or severe infections, fever
  • Lymphadenopathy and hepatosplenomegaly
  • Bone pain (especially limp or refusal to walk)
  • Lab: high WBC with many blasts, low Hgb, low platelets
"4-year-old with fatigue, fever, frequent infections, pallor, and petechiae + anemia, thrombocytopenia, and increased immature WBCs" = ALL, classic stem.

5.2 CNS Tumors — Second Most Common Childhood Cancer

In contrast to adults (where most brain tumors are supratentorial), most childhood brain tumors arise infratentorially — in the cerebellum or brainstem. The most common pediatric CNS tumor is medulloblastoma of the cerebellum.

Classic presentation of pediatric posterior fossa tumor:

  • Morning headaches, vomiting (especially on waking)
  • Cerebellar signs — unsteady gait, ataxia, dysmetria, intention tremor
  • Obstructive hydrocephalus from 4th ventricle compression — papilledema, increased head circumference in infants
  • Cranial nerve palsies if brainstem involvement

5.3 Embryonal Tumors — The "-blastomas"

TumorOrigin / locationClassic ageHallmark presentation
Wilms tumor (nephroblastoma)Kidney2–5 yearsPainless unilateral abdominal mass, sometimes with hematuria; the tumor stays confined to the kidney early
NeuroblastomaAdrenal medulla / sympathetic chain< 5 years (often < 2)Abdominal mass with calcifications, irritability, hypertension, may cross the midline; high urinary catecholamine metabolites (HVA, VMA)
RetinoblastomaRetina (RB1 gene)< 5 yearsLeukocoria ("white pupil reflex"), strabismus; can be hereditary (germline RB1 mutation, often bilateral) or sporadic
HepatoblastomaLiver< 3 yearsAbdominal mass, ↑ AFP
The abdominal mass distinction:
Wilms = kidney, unilateral, does NOT cross midline.
Neuroblastoma = adrenal/sympathetic, often crosses midline, calcifications on imaging, urinary HVA/VMA elevated.
This pair shows up on every pediatric oncology test.
"Leukocoria = retinoblastoma." White pupil reflex on a flash photograph in a young child is retinoblastoma until proven otherwise. Examine the other eye too — bilateral disease suggests a germline RB1 mutation and additional cancer risk later in life.
Common features of childhood cancer compared to adult cancer:
  • More often arise from embryonal tissues
  • More often associated with germline predisposition
  • Generally more chemo-sensitive and curable
  • Less linked to environmental carcinogen exposure
  • Lymphoma, leukemia, and CNS tumors dominate the case list

6.1 Primary Bone Tumors

TumorAgeLocationImaging clue

Classic osteosarcoma vignette: "Adolescent with persistent knee or upper-arm pain and swelling; imaging reveals destructive lesion of distal femur or proximal humerus with periosteal reaction." Survivors of hereditary retinoblastoma are at much higher risk of osteosarcoma later — both involve RB1.

6.2 Lymphomas

Hodgkin lymphomaNon-Hodgkin lymphoma
Age peakBimodal: 15–35 and >55All ages, increases with age
SpreadContiguous, predictable from one node group to nextNon-contiguous, often extranodal

"Adolescent with painless neck swelling, fever, and night sweats" = think Hodgkin lymphoma. Confirm with lymph node biopsy looking for Reed-Sternberg cells. Treatment is highly effective.
B symptoms: drenching night sweats, fevers, unexplained weight loss > 10% in 6 months. Their presence stages the disease as "B" (vs "A" without them) and signals more aggressive disease.

Soft tissue sarcomas in young patients

  • Rhabdomyosarcoma — malignant tumor of skeletal muscle origin; most common soft tissue sarcoma in children. Head/neck, GU tract, extremities.
  • Liposarcoma, leiomyosarcoma, fibrosarcoma — older patients more typically.
  • Sarcomas tend to spread hematogenously (often to lung) rather than lymphatically.

Appendix  •  Top Missteps & Master Mnemonics

Top 15 Week 3 Missteps

#The trapWhat's actually true
1"Oncogene activation needs both alleles."Only ONE allele needs to be activated (gain of function = dominant). Tumor suppressors need both lost.
2"All -omas are benign."Lymphoma, melanoma, hepatoma, seminoma are malignant despite the "-oma" suffix.
3"Dysplasia is cancer."Pre-cancerous, often reversible. Carcinoma in situ is the immediate precursor; invasive crosses the basement membrane.
4"Stage and grade are the same."Grade = differentiation (microscope). Stage = spread (TNM).
5"Cachexia is just not eating enough."Cytokine-driven hypermetabolism — proteolysis, lipolysis, anorexia, insulin resistance. Eating more does not fix it.
6"Wilms and neuroblastoma are the same."Wilms = kidney, doesn't cross midline. Neuroblastoma = adrenal/sympathetic, often crosses midline, calcifications, ↑ HVA/VMA.
7"Leukocoria is harmless."Leukocoria = retinoblastoma until proven otherwise. Urgent referral.
8"Childhood cancers are caused by smoking."Children's cancers are largely not driven by environmental carcinogens; they often arise from embryonal tissue with germline factors.
9"Hodgkin and non-Hodgkin lymphoma are clinically identical."Hodgkin spreads contiguously, has Reed-Sternberg cells, and has bimodal age distribution.
10"Osteosarcoma occurs in the diaphysis."Osteosarcoma = metaphysis (distal femur, proximal tibia, proximal humerus). Ewing = diaphysis.
11"VEGF blockers cure cancer."Anti-VEGF therapy slows angiogenesis but is one tool among many; resistance develops.
12"BRCA1/2 mutations only matter for breast cancer."Also raise risk for ovarian, prostate, pancreatic, and others.
13"Acetaldehyde is a harmless byproduct."It's the carcinogenic intermediate of alcohol metabolism that damages DNA.
14"DES affects the mother only."Daughters of DES-exposed mothers have ↑ risk of clear cell vaginal/cervical cancer.
15"Obesity is a cardiovascular issue only."Obesity drives endometrial, postmenopausal breast, colorectal, kidney, esophageal, pancreatic, and liver cancers.

Week 3 Study Strategy

  • Drill the gas-vs-brake gene framework first. Half the LO1 questions decompose to oncogene vs tumor suppressor logic.
  • Memorize the development cascade. Hyperplasia → dysplasia → CIS → invasive → metastatic. Basement membrane crossing is the milestone.
  • Understand cachexia is cytokine-driven, not anorexia. SATA traps will include "the patient is just not eating." That's wrong.
  • Pair every risk factor with its mechanism. Smoking → carcinogens + metaplasia. Alcohol → acetaldehyde. Obesity → inflammation + insulin + estrogen.
  • Match each pediatric tumor to its single best diagnostic clue. Crossing the midline (neuroblastoma), confined to kidney (Wilms), white pupil (retinoblastoma), posterior fossa ataxia (medulloblastoma).
  • Know which "-omas" are malignant. Lymphoma, melanoma, hepatoma, seminoma. Easy points if you have it.
  • For adolescent cancers, lock down osteosarcoma vs Ewing and Hodgkin lymphoma's classic vignette.
