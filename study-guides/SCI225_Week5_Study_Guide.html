Unit 1  •  Stroke & Cerebrovascular Disease  LO1

1.1 Ischemic vs Hemorrhagic Stroke

Ischemic (~85%)Hemorrhagic (~15%)
MechanismVessel blocked → no blood downstreamVessel ruptures → blood floods brain tissue
SubtypesThrombotic (atherosclerotic), embolic (often AFib), lacunar (small penetrating vessels)Intracerebral (HTN), subarachnoid (aneurysm)
Risk factorsHTN, AFib, atherosclerosis, diabetes, smoking, hyperlipidemia, obesity, prior TIAHTN (intracerebral), berry aneurysm/AVM (subarachnoid), anticoagulant use

FAST for stroke recognition:
Face drooping  ·  Arm weakness  ·  Speech difficulty  ·  Time to call 911
"Time is brain" — every minute, ~2 million neurons die during ischemic stroke.

1.2 The Ischemic Cascade — Why Neurons Die

Vessel occlusion → ↓ O₂ + glucose → ↓ ATP → Na/K pump fails → cell swelling + depolarization → excessive glutamate release → Ca²⁺ influx → activation of destructive enzymes → free-radical injury → neuron death
The primary reason neurons die during a stroke is energy failure — without ATP, cells cannot maintain ion gradients. The neuron depolarizes, dumps glutamate, and gets flooded with calcium. This is "excitotoxicity." The penumbra (the area around the core infarct) is salvageable if perfusion is restored quickly — that's the window tPA targets.
Stroke risk factors students must know cold:
  • Atrial fibrillation — clots form in left atrium, travel to brain (embolic stroke)
  • Hypertension — accelerates atherosclerosis and is the #1 modifiable risk factor
  • Diabetes — accelerates vascular disease
  • Smoking — endothelial dysfunction, hypercoagulability
  • Hyperlipidemia / atherosclerosis — plaque rupture and embolism
  • Prior TIA — major warning sign; ~10–15% risk of stroke in following 90 days
  • Carotid stenosis, sickle cell disease, oral contraceptives + smoking
TIA = "warning stroke." Same mechanism as ischemic stroke but symptoms resolve completely (typically < 1 hour, by definition < 24 h). Treat aggressively — antiplatelet, statin, BP control, AFib management — to prevent the next event.

Practice Questions — Stroke

Q1. A 70-year-old with atrial fibrillation suddenly develops right arm weakness and slurred speech. Which mechanism is most likely?
A. Subarachnoid hemorrhage from berry aneurysm
B. Embolic ischemic stroke from a left atrial clot
C. Hypertensive intracerebral hemorrhage
D. Brain tumor
Show answer
B. AFib + sudden focal deficit = embolic stroke until proven otherwise. Atrial stasis allows clot formation; clot embolizes to a cerebral artery. Anticoagulation (warfarin or DOAC) prevents this in AFib.
Q2. What is the primary reason neurons begin to die during a stroke?
A. Bacterial infection of the brain
B. Loss of ATP causes ion-pump failure, excitotoxic glutamate release, and calcium-mediated cell injury
C. The brain becomes too acidic from increased breathing
D. Antibodies attack neurons
Show answer
B. Ischemia → energy failure → ion gradient collapse → excitotoxicity → Ca²⁺ influx → enzyme activation → cell death. Restoring perfusion quickly is the only way to save the penumbra.
Q3. Select all that apply. Which findings are associated with increased stroke risk?
A. Atrial fibrillation
B. Hypertension
C. Diabetes mellitus
D. Daily moderate exercise
E. Carotid artery stenosis
F. Prior transient ischemic attack
Show answer
A, B, C, E, F. D is protective. The other five are major modifiable or addressable risk factors.
Q4. A patient awakens with a unilateral facial droop, arm weakness, and slurred speech. Last seen normal 7 hours ago. Which intervention is most appropriate first?
A. Administer tPA immediately
B. Obtain a non-contrast CT head to exclude hemorrhage and assess eligibility for thrombolysis or thrombectomy
C. Begin therapeutic anticoagulation
D. Discharge with antiplatelet therapy
Show answer
B. Always image first to exclude hemorrhage. tPA window is 3–4.5 h from last known well — this patient is past the window for tPA but may still be a thrombectomy candidate up to 24 h with eligible imaging.

Unit 2  •  Other CNS Disorders  LO1

2.1 Multiple Sclerosis

MS is a chronic autoimmune demyelinating disease of the CNS. T cells attack oligodendrocytes, stripping myelin from axons in the brain, spinal cord, and optic nerves. Lesions accumulate in different locations over time → "dissemination in time and space."

Classic MS features:

  • Optic neuritis (painful monocular vision loss) — often the first symptom
  • Sensory symptoms: numbness, tingling, Lhermitte's sign (electric shock with neck flexion)
  • Motor: weakness, spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski (UMN signs)
  • Cerebellar: ataxia, intention tremor, scanning speech
  • Bladder dysfunction, fatigue, cognitive changes
  • Heat intolerance (Uhthoff phenomenon — symptoms worsen with elevated body temperature)
  • Relapsing-remitting most common pattern; primary or secondary progressive in some
"Young woman with episodes of vision loss and limb weakness, with a normal neuro exam between episodes" = MS until proven otherwise. F:M ratio is roughly 3:1; onset typically 20–40.

2.2 Parkinson's Disease

Loss of dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra (part of the basal ganglia). Dopamine normally facilitates voluntary movement; without it, the basal ganglia over-inhibit motor cortex output → bradykinesia and rigidity.
TRAP — Parkinson's cardinal features:
Tremor (resting, "pill-rolling," 4–6 Hz)
Rigidity (cogwheel)
Akinesia / bradykinesia (slow movement initiation)
Postural instability (falls, shuffling gait, stooped posture)
Plus: mask-like face, micrographia, hypophonia
Why levodopa works. Dopamine itself can't cross the BBB. Levodopa (L-DOPA) does, and is converted to dopamine in the brain. It's combined with carbidopa to prevent peripheral conversion (which would cause nausea and reduce CNS delivery).

2.3 Seizures

A seizure is a sudden, abnormal, excessive and synchronous electrical discharge of cortical neurons. The clinical manifestation depends on which area of cortex is firing.
TypeFeatures
Generalized tonic-clonic ("grand mal")Sudden loss of consciousness → tonic (stiffening) → clonic (rhythmic jerking) → postictal confusion. May have tongue biting, incontinence.
Absence ("petit mal")Brief staring spells (5–10 sec), no postictal phase. Children. EEG: 3-Hz spike-and-wave.
Focal aware ("simple partial")Localized motor or sensory phenomenon, awareness preserved.
Focal impaired awareness ("complex partial")Often temporal lobe; automatisms (lip-smacking), staring, postictal confusion.
Status epilepticusSeizure > 5 min OR repeated without recovery between. Medical emergency.
Common seizure triggers: fever (febrile seizures in children), hypoglycemia, hyponatremia, alcohol withdrawal, illicit drugs (cocaine, methamphetamine), sleep deprivation, head injury, stroke, brain tumor, infection, missed antiepileptic doses.

2.4 Increased ICP — How It Injures Neurons

Rising ICP injures neurons by direct compression and by reducing cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP):
CPP = MAP − ICP
As ICP climbs toward MAP, CPP falls toward zero — and neurons are starved of blood flow even though large vessels are still patent. Sustained low CPP causes ischemic neuronal death; severe ICP elevation causes herniation and brainstem death.
Recall the Cushing triad from Week 4: HTN + bradycardia + irregular respirations = late ICP elevation. Restlessness, confusion, behavior change come earlier.

Q6. A patient with Parkinson's has resting tremor and progressive difficulty initiating movement. Which structural and biochemical change is responsible?
A. Loss of acetylcholine in the cortex
B. Loss of dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra
C. Loss of GABA in the basal ganglia
D. Loss of serotonin in the brainstem
Show answer
B. Substantia nigra dopamine loss is the defining lesion of Parkinson's. Levodopa-carbidopa replaces the missing transmitter.
Q7. A patient develops sudden loss of awareness with rhythmic jerking of all four extremities, then becomes confused for 20 minutes. Which type of seizure?
A. Absence seizure
B. Focal aware seizure
C. Generalized tonic-clonic seizure
D. Myoclonic seizure
Show answer
C. Loss of consciousness + rhythmic jerking + postictal confusion = tonic-clonic. Absence has no postictal phase. Focal aware preserves awareness.
Q8. Which mechanism most directly causes neuron injury when intracranial pressure rises?
A. Increased oxygen delivery to neurons
B. Reduced cerebral perfusion pressure → ischemia
C. Excess CSF nutrition of neurons
D. Increased lymphatic drainage
Show answer
B. CPP = MAP − ICP. As ICP climbs, perfusion falls. Neurons that don't receive blood die. Direct compression also contributes, especially with herniation.

Unit 3  •  Spinal Cord & Peripheral Nerve Disorders  LO1

3.1 Spinal Cord Injury — Phases & Sequelae

Acute trauma → primary mechanical damage → secondary injury (edema, hemorrhage, ischemia, glutamate excitotoxicity, free radicals) → spinal shock (areflexia) → reflex return → spasticity (chronic UMN-type)
SCI involves a primary injury (mechanical) and a more damaging secondary injury cascade: vasogenic edema, hemorrhage, ischemia, calcium-mediated cell death, and inflammation. This secondary phase enlarges the lesion over hours to days — which is why minimizing inflammation and maintaining perfusion matters in early management.
Why SCI recovery is slow and incomplete. CNS axons do not regenerate effectively. Adult neurons have limited proliferative capacity, scar tissue (gliosis) blocks regrowth, and inhibitory molecules in the CNS environment actively prevent axon extension. PNS injuries can recover; CNS ones largely cannot.

3.2 Spinal Shock vs Neurogenic Shock vs Autonomic Dysreflexia

Spinal shockNeurogenic shockAutonomic dysreflexia
TimingImmediately after SCI; lasts days to weeksImmediately after high SCI (T6 or above)Weeks to years after SCI at T6 or above
MechanismLoss of all reflex activity below the lesion (transient)Loss of sympathetic tone below lesion → unopposed parasympatheticPainful or noxious stimulus below the lesion (full bladder, fecal impaction) triggers massive sympathetic surge below; reflex parasympathetic response above
FindingsFlaccid paralysis, areflexia, no autonomic compromise requiredHypotension, bradycardia, warm/dry skin below lesionSevere HTN, pounding headache, bradycardia, flushing/sweating above lesion, pale/cool below
TreatmentSupportive; reflexes return on their ownFluids + vasopressors; atropine for bradycardiaFind and remove the stimulus immediately (catheterize, disimpact); raise head; antihypertensive
Neurogenic shock is the only "warm shock" of the bunch. Hypovolemic, cardiogenic, and obstructive shock all show cold skin from compensatory vasoconstriction. Neurogenic shock has lost sympathetic vasoconstriction — vessels dilate, skin is warm and dry, BP and HR are both LOW (vs other shocks where HR is high).
"All shock has tachycardia." No — neurogenic shock has bradycardia (loss of sympathetic input to the heart). This is a frequent test trap.

3.3 Disk Herniation & Radiculopathy

The intervertebral disk has a tough outer ring (annulus fibrosus) and a gel-like center (nucleus pulposus). Lifting injuries or aging can cause the nucleus to herniate posteriorly through the annulus, compressing a spinal nerve root → radiculopathy — pain, numbness, weakness in that nerve's distribution.

The classic lumbar disk herniation: L4-L5 or L5-S1 → nerve root compression → "sciatica" — back pain radiating down the back of the leg, often into the foot, with dermatomal sensory loss and possibly weakness.

Cauda equina syndrome is a surgical emergency: large central lumbar herniation compresses the cauda equina. Look for: saddle anesthesia, urinary retention/incontinence, bilateral leg weakness, loss of anal sphincter tone. Decompress within 24–48 h.

Practice Questions — Spinal Cord & PNS

Q9. Order. Place the events of secondary spinal cord injury in correct sequence:
— Free radical generation and inflammation worsen tissue damage
— Mechanical trauma to the spinal cord
— Calcium influx activates destructive enzymes
— Local ischemia and hemorrhage develop
— Cells fail to maintain ion gradients and depolarize
Show answer
Correct order: Mechanical trauma → ischemia and hemorrhage → cells lose ion gradients and depolarize → Ca²⁺ influx activates enzymes → free radical generation and inflammation. Same energy-failure cascade as stroke, played out in cord tissue.
Q10. A patient with a C5 spinal cord injury has BP 78/45, HR 52, warm dry skin, and paralysis below the lesion. Which best explains this?
A. Hypovolemic shock from blood loss
B. Cardiogenic shock
C. Neurogenic shock from disrupted sympathetic outflow
D. Septic shock
Show answer
C. High SCI knocks out sympathetic tone → vasodilation → hypotension, warm skin, and bradycardia (no sympathetic input to the heart). Treat with fluids and vasopressors; atropine for bradycardia.
Q11. A patient asks why recovery from spinal cord injury is often slow and incomplete. Best response:
A. "Spinal nerves regenerate quickly within days."
B. "Adult CNS neurons have limited regenerative capacity, and scar tissue plus inhibitory factors prevent axon regrowth."
C. "Recovery depends on diet alone."
D. "Recovery is complete within 2 weeks for most patients."
Show answer
B. CNS axons cannot regrow effectively because of intrinsic neuron limitations, glial scar formation, and inhibitory molecules in the CNS environment. PNS nerves can regrow; CNS largely cannot.
Q12. A patient lifts a heavy box and develops back pain that radiates down the back of one leg with numbness in a dermatomal pattern. Which mechanism best explains the leg symptoms?
A. Stroke in the spinal cord
B. Compression of a lumbar nerve root by herniated disk material
C. Cerebellar lesion
D. Peripheral neuropathy from diabetes
Show answer
B. Lumbar disk herniation → nerve root compression → radiculopathy in that root's distribution. This is sciatica — a textbook presentation.

Unit 4  •  Meningitis  LO1

Meningitis = inflammation of the meninges (especially arachnoid and pia) and the CSF in the subarachnoid space. Most often infectious (bacterial, viral, fungal). Inflammation drives the classic clinical syndrome: fever, headache, neck stiffness (nuchal rigidity).

Classic findings

  • Fever — almost universal
  • Severe headache — diffuse, worsening
  • Nuchal rigidity — patient cannot touch chin to chest
  • Photophobia
  • Altered mental status / decreased alertness
  • Kernig sign — pain with knee extension when hip is flexed
  • Brudzinski sign — hip/knee flex involuntarily when neck is flexed
  • Petechial / purpuric rash in meningococcal disease

CSF analysis — bacterial vs viral vs fungal

BacterialViralFungal/TB
AppearanceCloudy/turbidClearClear or slightly cloudy
WBC predominanceNeutrophils ↑↑Lymphocytes ↑Lymphocytes ↑
GlucoseLow (bacteria consume it)NormalLow
Protein↑↑Normal or mildly ↑↑
Opening pressure↑Normal↑
Bacterial meningitis is a medical emergency. Antibiotics within 1 hour. Don't wait for LP results to start treatment if clinical suspicion is high — give empirical IV antibiotics + dexamethasone, then image and LP.
Common pathogens by age:
  • Newborn (< 1 mo): Group B Strep, E. coli, Listeria
  • Infants/children: Streptococcus pneumoniae, Neisseria meningitidis, Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib — much less common now thanks to vaccine)
  • Young adults / college: N. meningitidis (dorm outbreaks)
  • Older adults: S. pneumoniae, Listeria

Unit 5  •  Neuromuscular Junction (NMJ)  LO2

5.1 NMJ Anatomy & Function

Action potential reaches motor neuron terminal → voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open → Ca²⁺ entry triggers vesicle fusion → acetylcholine (ACh) released into synaptic cleft → ACh binds nicotinic ACh receptors on muscle → Na⁺ influx → muscle depolarization → contraction
Acetylcholinesterase in the synaptic cleft rapidly degrades ACh, ending the signal. This allows precise control of muscle contraction. Drugs that block acetylcholinesterase (like pyridostigmine) leave more ACh in the cleft and improve transmission — useful in myasthenia gravis.
Step at NMJWhat goes wrong → disease
Ca²⁺ entry into nerve terminalLambert-Eaton syndrome (autoimmune; antibodies vs voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channel)
ACh releaseBotulism (botulinum toxin blocks ACh release)
Postsynaptic ACh receptorMyasthenia gravis (autoantibodies block AChR)
AcetylcholinesteraseOrganophosphate poisoning (inhibits enzyme → ACh excess → cholinergic crisis)

5.2 Myasthenia Gravis (MG)

Autoimmune disease — IgG antibodies bind and block acetylcholine receptors on the postsynaptic muscle membrane. The released ACh has nowhere to bind (or fewer functional receptors), so each muscle action potential is weaker. With repeated activity, ACh stores deplete and weakness progresses; rest restores transmission. This is type II hypersensitivity (receptor blockade).

Hallmark presentation:

  • Fatigable muscle weakness — worsens with use, improves with rest
  • Ptosis (drooping eyelids) and diplopia (double vision) — often the first symptoms
  • Weak chewing, swallowing, speech (bulbar muscles)
  • Generalized weakness — proximal > distal; respiratory muscles in severe disease (myasthenic crisis)
"Patient brushes hair or holds objects overhead and gets weaker; rest helps" — that exam vignette is myasthenia gravis. Confirm with anti-AChR antibodies, edrophonium (Tensilon) test, EMG (decremental response).
MG association with the thymus. About 75% of MG patients have thymic abnormalities (hyperplasia or thymoma). Thymectomy can improve symptoms. Treatments: pyridostigmine (acetylcholinesterase inhibitor), corticosteroids, immunosuppressants, IVIG/plasmapheresis for crisis.
Why pyridostigmine helps. By blocking acetylcholinesterase, pyridostigmine slows ACh breakdown — more ACh stays in the cleft, raising the chance of binding the (limited) functional receptors. It treats symptoms; it doesn't fix the autoimmunity.

5.3 Botulism

Botulinum toxin (from Clostridium botulinum) is one of the most potent toxins known. It enters motor nerve terminals and cleaves the SNARE proteins required for ACh vesicle fusion → blocks ACh release. No ACh in the cleft → no muscle activation → flaccid paralysis.

Classic presentation:

  • Sources: improperly canned/preserved foods (adult botulism), honey in infants < 1 year (infant botulism), wound contamination
  • Descending symmetric flaccid paralysis (opposite of GBS, which ascends)
  • Cranial nerves first: blurred vision, diplopia, ptosis, dysphagia, dysarthria
  • Progresses to respiratory muscle weakness — respiratory failure is the killer
  • No sensory loss; mental status preserved
"Botulism causes spasms." Wrong — botulism causes flaccid paralysis (no ACh = no muscle activation). Tetanus, by contrast, causes spasms (it blocks inhibitory neurotransmitters in the spinal cord).
"4 D's of botulism" (cranial nerves): Diplopia, Dysphagia, Dysarthria, Dysphonia. Plus Descending paralysis.

Unit 6  •  Pediatric Neurologic Disorders  LO3

6.1 Hydrocephalus in Children

Excess CSF accumulation in the ventricles. In infants (skull bones not yet fused), this enlarges the head; in older children with closed sutures, it raises ICP.
Communicating = absorption fails (post-meningitis, post-bleed, congenital).
Non-communicating (obstructive) = flow blocked, often at the cerebral aqueduct (aqueductal stenosis is the most common congenital cause).

Classic findings in infant hydrocephalus:

  • Rapid head growth — head circumference crosses percentiles
  • Bulging fontanelle with widened sutures
  • "Sunset eyes" — downward eye deviation from pressure on the upper midbrain
  • Poor feeding, vomiting, irritability or lethargy
  • High-pitched cry, scalp vein engorgement
Untreated hydrocephalus causes permanent brain injury by compressing brain tissue against the skull and reducing perfusion. Even if CSF pressure is later relieved, the damaged white matter and cortex don't recover. Early shunt placement (VP shunt) prevents progression.
Late ICP signs in young children — vomiting, irritability, bradycardia, irregular breathing, decreased alertness — should prompt urgent imaging, not just a "wait and see." This is the pediatric Cushing-triad analog.

6.2 Neural Tube Defects

The neural tube closes between weeks 3 and 4 of embryonic development. Failure of closure produces a spectrum of defects depending on level and severity.
DefectDescriptionSeverity
Spina bifida occultaVertebral arch fails to fuse; spinal cord and meninges intact; tuft of hair or dimple over the areaOften asymptomatic
MeningoceleMeninges protrude through the vertebral defect; spinal cord intactMild to moderate
MyelomeningoceleMeninges and spinal cord protrude → motor/sensory deficits, bowel/bladder dysfunction, frequently associated with hydrocephalus and Chiari II malformationSevere
AnencephalyFailure of cranial neural tube closure; absent brain developmentIncompatible with life
Folic acid prevention. Adequate folate before conception and through the first trimester reduces neural tube defects by 50–70%. Recommendation: 400 mcg daily for women of childbearing age; 4 mg daily for women with a prior affected pregnancy. The critical window is the first month — often before a woman knows she's pregnant — which is why supplementation is recommended for all women who could become pregnant.

6.3 Cerebral Palsy

Non-progressive motor disorder caused by injury to the developing brain. Causes include prematurity, hypoxic-ischemic injury at birth, intrauterine infection, kernicterus, postnatal stroke or trauma, and congenital brain malformation.

Cardinal features:

  • Motor impairment present from infancy/early childhood
  • Most common type: spastic (UMN signs — increased tone, hyperreflexia, scissoring gait)
  • Other types: dyskinetic (chorea, athetosis), ataxic (cerebellar)
  • Often associated with intellectual disability, seizures, vision/hearing problems, learning disorders — but cognitive function may also be entirely normal
  • Non-progressive — the brain injury itself doesn't worsen, though musculoskeletal problems may evolve with growth
"Cerebral palsy is progressive." No — the brain injury is fixed (occurred prenatally, perinatally, or in early childhood). The clinical picture may evolve as the child grows, but the lesion itself is static. Contrast with neurodegenerative diseases.

6.4 Pediatric Meningitis

Children with meningitis present similarly to adults: fever, headache, vomiting, neck stiffness, photophobia. CSF analysis is the same (neutrophil-predominant low-glucose CSF in bacterial). Petechial rash strongly suggests meningococcal disease — call infection control. Vaccines (pneumococcal, Hib, meningococcal) have dramatically reduced incidence.
In infants, the classic neck stiffness is often absent. Look for: poor feeding, lethargy, irritability, bulging fontanelle, fever or hypothermia, seizures. A high index of suspicion is needed.

Appendix  •  Top Missteps & Master Mnemonics

Top 15 Week 5 Missteps

#The trapWhat's actually true
1"All strokes get tPA."Only ischemic, within 3–4.5 h of last known well, hemorrhage excluded, no contraindications.
2"Atrial fibrillation causes hemorrhagic stroke."AFib → embolic ischemic stroke (clot from atrium).
3"Neurogenic shock is hot and tachycardic."It's hot but bradycardic — sympathetic outflow lost.
4"Spinal shock is permanent."Temporary phase. Spasticity follows as reflex arcs reorganize.
5"Disk herniation is a brain problem."It's PNS — root compression below the cord level.
6"Bacterial meningitis CSF has lymphocytes."Bacterial = neutrophils. Lymphocytes = viral or fungal/TB.
7"Myasthenia gravis is muscle weakness from low calcium."It's autoantibodies blocking AChR at the NMJ. Type II hypersensitivity.
8"Botulism causes spasms."Botulism = flaccid, descending paralysis. Tetanus causes spasms.
9"GBS and botulism are the same."GBS ascends; botulism descends. GBS is autoimmune demyelination; botulism blocks ACh release at NMJ.
10"Cerebral palsy is progressive."Non-progressive brain injury from before/around birth.
11"Folic acid late in pregnancy prevents neural tube defects."Closure is at weeks 3–4. Folic acid must be on board before conception.
12"Hydrocephalus = too much CSF production."Usually impaired flow or absorption, not overproduction.
13"Pyridostigmine cures myasthenia."It treats symptoms by inhibiting AChE. Doesn't fix the autoimmunity.
14"Sciatica is a CNS disorder."It's a radiculopathy from PNS root compression.
15"Infant meningitis presents with neck stiffness."Classic neck stiffness is often absent in infants. Look for irritability, bulging fontanelle, poor feeding, fever or hypothermia.

Week 5 Study Strategy

  • Lock down stroke first. Ischemic vs hemorrhagic mechanism, FAST, tPA window, AFib → embolism — all guaranteed test points.
  • Memorize the SCI shocks. Spinal shock (areflexia phase), neurogenic shock (warm + brady), autonomic dysreflexia (massive HTN above lesion). Each has a different timeline and management.
  • Learn the meningitis CSF profile cold. Bacterial vs viral vs fungal — neutrophils vs lymphocytes, glucose, protein.
  • Drill the NMJ direction-of-weakness pattern. MG fatigable, botulism descending, GBS ascending. These three trip students up regularly.
  • For pediatric topics, connect timing to mechanism. Neural tube defects = weeks 3–4. CP = peri/postnatal injury. Hydrocephalus = any age, depending on cause.
  • "Static vs progressive" — cerebral palsy is the textbook static lesion. Don't miss it.
  • Recognize folic-acid-before-conception teaching. Frequent SATA distractor.
