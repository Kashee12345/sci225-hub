Unit 1  •  Cells of the Nervous System & Conduction  LO1

1.1 Neurons and Neuroglia (Support Cells)

A neuron has three parts: dendrites (receive), cell body/soma (process), axon (transmit). The axon is wrapped in myelin, which insulates it and lets the action potential jump from one node of Ranvier to the next — this is saltatory conduction. Myelinated axons conduct ~50× faster than unmyelinated ones.
The 6 neuroglial (support) cell types — "MOSES + AM":
Microglia · Oligodendrocytes · Schwann · Ependymal · Satellite · Astrocytes · plus Many neurons supported by all of them.
4 are CNS (astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, microglia, ependymal). 2 are PNS (Schwann, satellite).
CellLocationFunctionIf damaged…
AstrocytesCNSForm the blood-brain barrier; potassium buffering; metabolic supportToxins cross BBB → neuroinflammation, edema
OligodendrocytesCNSMake myelin in CNS — one cell wraps multiple axonsSlowed/lost saltatory conduction in CNS (multiple sclerosis)
MicrogliaCNSResident immune / phagocytic cells; clear debrisImpaired CNS immune response and clearance
Ependymal cellsCNS — line ventricles & central canalMake and circulate CSF (choroid plexus)↓ CSF production or obstruction → hydrocephalus
Schwann cellsPNSMake myelin in PNS — one cell wraps one axon segmentPNS demyelination (Guillain-Barré, Charcot-Marie-Tooth)
Satellite cellsPNS — in gangliaSurround and support PNS neuron cell bodiesGanglion dysfunction
The CNS/PNS myelin split: "Oligo in One (CNS) — many axons. Schwann in Segments (PNS) — one axon."
This single distinction underlies the difference between MS (CNS, oligo) and Guillain-Barré (PNS, Schwann).

1.2 Conduction & Demyelinating Disorders

Stimulus → Na⁺ influx at node → action potential → impulse "jumps" from node to node along myelin → rapid signal arrival at synapse
Why demyelination slows conduction. Without myelin between nodes, the axon must depolarize the entire length of membrane like an unmyelinated fiber. The signal arrives late, weakly, or not at all. Voluntary movement and sensation become delayed, fatigue with repetition (because Na⁺ channel reserves run out), and may stop entirely as disease progresses.
DisorderWhereCell affectedClassic presentation
Multiple sclerosis (MS)CNSOligodendrocytes — autoimmune T-cell attack on CNS myelinYoung adult, female, "dissemination in time and space" — optic neuritis, weakness, sensory loss, relapsing
Guillain-Barré syndrome (GBS)PNSSchwann cells — autoimmune demyelination, often post-viralAscending symmetric flaccid weakness 1–3 weeks after a viral or GI infection (Campylobacter); CSF shows ↑ protein with normal cells
Charcot-Marie-ToothPNSHereditary demyelinating neuropathyChildhood-onset distal weakness, "stork leg" deformity, foot drop
Guillain-Barré is the most common cause of acute flaccid paralysis in developed countries. The classic story: a young adult had a GI bug 1–3 weeks ago; now they have ascending symmetric weakness, areflexia, and tingling. Watch the diaphragm — respiratory failure is the killer. Treat with IVIG or plasmapheresis.
"MS and Guillain-Barré are the same demyelinating disease." No — completely different. MS = CNS, oligodendrocytes, relapsing-remitting, lifelong. GBS = PNS, Schwann cells, single ascending paralysis episode, often resolves. The exam loves making you pick between these two.

Practice Questions — Cells & Conduction

Q1. A patient with a CNS demyelinating disorder develops slowed nerve conduction. Which cell is most likely dysfunctional?
A. Astrocyte
B. Microglia
C. Oligodendrocyte
D. Schwann cell
Show answer
C. CNS myelin is made by oligodendrocytes. Schwann cells make PNS myelin. Astrocytes form the BBB; microglia are immune cells.
Q2. A patient has demyelination and develops slowed voluntary movement. The pathophysiology is best explained by:
A. Loss of saltatory conduction along the axon
B. Failure of neurotransmitter release at the synapse
C. Reduced cerebral blood flow
D. Loss of dendritic branching
Show answer
A. Demyelination disrupts the node-to-node "jumping" of action potentials. The signal still propagates but slowly and weakly, producing delayed and weak movements.
Q3. A patient develops ascending symmetric weakness 2 weeks after a viral GI illness. Which mechanism?
A. CNS oligodendrocyte loss (multiple sclerosis)
B. Peripheral nerve demyelination from autoimmune attack on Schwann cells (Guillain-Barré)
C. Dopamine deficiency in basal ganglia
D. Anterior horn cell death (poliomyelitis)
Show answer
B. Post-infectious ascending flaccid paralysis = Guillain-Barré. Schwann cells in PNS are the target. Treat with IVIG or plasmapheresis; monitor diaphragm.

CSF — production, circulation, function

CSF is produced by the choroid plexus (specialized ependymal cells) inside the ventricles. It circulates through the lateral → 3rd → 4th ventricles → into the subarachnoid space → reabsorbed by arachnoid villi back into venous sinuses.
Functions: cushions brain, removes waste, delivers nutrients, regulates pressure.

The lucid interval is the classic epidural hematoma story. Patient is hit on the temple, briefly loses consciousness, "wakes up" feeling fine, then deteriorates rapidly as the arterial bleed expands. This is a neurosurgical emergency — drain the hematoma before brainstem herniation occurs.

2.3 Hydrocephalus

Hydrocephalus = excess CSF in the ventricles. Classified by where the problem is:
  • Communicating (non-obstructive) — CSF flows through ventricles freely but is not reabsorbed at arachnoid villi (post-meningitis, post-subarachnoid bleed, congenital)
  • Non-communicating (obstructive) — flow is blocked somewhere in the ventricular system. Classic site: cerebral aqueduct (aqueduct of Sylvius) between 3rd and 4th ventricles → CSF backs up into the lateral and 3rd ventricles → they enlarge.
  • Normal pressure hydrocephalus (NPH) — adult chronic form: Wet, Wobbly, Wacky — incontinence, gait apraxia, dementia.
Why aqueduct obstruction enlarges ventricles upstream. CSF made in the lateral ventricles must funnel through a narrow channel — the cerebral aqueduct — to reach the 4th ventricle. Block that channel, and CSF accumulates behind it. The lateral and 3rd ventricles balloon, pressing on surrounding brain tissue.
Hydrocephalus classic pictures:
- Infant with rapidly enlarging head, sunset eyes, bulging fontanelle → congenital obstructive
- Older adult with the "3 W's" (Wet, Wobbly, Wacky) → NPH
- Adult after meningitis or subarachnoid bleed → communicating

A. Epidural hematoma
B. Subdural hematoma — bridging vein rupture
C. Subarachnoid hemorrhage
D. Intracerebral hemorrhage
Show answer
B. Slow onset, crescent shape, bridging-vein source, elderly with brain atrophy that stretches those veins. Anticoagulation worsens the bleeding.
Q8. A patient develops ventricular enlargement after obstruction of the cerebral aqueduct. Which mechanism accounts for this change?
A. CSF accumulates because normal fluid drainage is blocked
B. Blood accumulates because arterial inflow is blocked
C. Lymph accumulates because venous outflow is blocked
D. Ions accumulate because neural signaling is blocked
Show answer
A. Aqueduct of Sylvius blockage prevents CSF flowing from 3rd to 4th ventricle → lateral and 3rd ventricles balloon → non-communicating (obstructive) hydrocephalus.
Q9. A 30-year-old presents with a "thunderclap headache" — the worst of her life — followed by neck stiffness. Which lesion is most likely?
A. Epidural hematoma
B. Subdural hematoma
C. Subarachnoid hemorrhage from ruptured berry aneurysm
D. Intracerebral hemorrhage
Show answer
C. "Worst headache of my life" + nuchal rigidity = subarachnoid hemorrhage until proven otherwise. CT first; if negative and suspicion remains, lumbar puncture for xanthochromia.

Unit 3  •  Sensory Function & Pain  LO2

3.1 Sensory Pathway — From Receptor to Perception

Skin receptor → peripheral nerve → dorsal horn of spinal cord → ascending tract → thalamus → primary sensory cortex (parietal lobe) → conscious perception
A signal must travel a four-neuron chain to be perceived. Cut any link — peripheral nerve, dorsal column, thalamus, cortex — and perception fails. A patient who is touched but does not feel it has a break somewhere on this pathway.

The two main ascending sensory tracts

TractCarriesDecussates (crosses)Lesion → loss
Dorsal column / medial lemniscusFine touch, vibration, proprioception, 2-point discriminationIn the medulla (high)Same side below the lesion if cord lesion; opposite side if brainstem lesion
Spinothalamic tractPain, temperature, crude touchWithin 1–2 levels of entering the cord (low)Opposite side below the lesion
"Pain crosses early, vibration crosses late." Spinothalamic = pain/temp = decussates near entry. Dorsal columns = vibration/proprioception = decussates in the medulla. This explains why hemicord lesions (Brown-Séquard) cause pain loss opposite the lesion and proprioception loss same side.

3.2 Pain Types

TypeCauseQualityExamples
Nociceptive — somaticTissue injury detected by nociceptors in skin, muscle, bone, jointSharp, throbbing, well-localizedCut, fracture, sprain, surgical incision
Nociceptive — visceralStretching/distension of organsDull, achy, poorly localized; may refer (e.g., diaphragm pain to shoulder)Appendicitis, MI (referred to arm/jaw), bowel obstruction
NeuropathicDamage to peripheral or central nerves directlyBurning, shooting, electric, tingling, numbDiabetic neuropathy, post-herpetic neuralgia, sciatica, trigeminal neuralgia, phantom limb
ReferredVisceral pain perceived at a distant somatic site (shared spinal level)Same as visceralMI → left arm/jaw; gallbladder → right shoulder; appendicitis → periumbilical
"Burning, shooting pain without a new injury" is the classic neuropathic pain stem. Don't pick nociceptive when you see those words. Neuropathic pain comes from the nerve itself being damaged or sensitized — diabetes, herpes, nerve compression, amputation.
Why neuropathic pain doesn't respond well to opioids. Opioids dampen nociceptive signaling at the dorsal horn and brain. Neuropathic pain is generated by damaged neurons firing abnormally, often above the level the opioid acts. That's why we use anticonvulsants (gabapentin, pregabalin) and antidepressants (TCAs, SNRIs) — they calm hyperexcitable nerves.

Unit 4  •  Sleep, Arousal & Consciousness  LO2

Arousal (being awake) is driven by the reticular activating system (RAS) — a network of neurons in the brainstem (especially the upper pons and midbrain reticular formation) that projects up to the thalamus and cortex. Without RAS input, the cortex cannot be aroused even if it is anatomically intact.
Why brainstem injuries cause coma. A patient with a normal cortex but a destroyed RAS cannot be awakened — the cortex needs continual "wake-up" signals from the brainstem. This is why even small brainstem strokes can produce profound consciousness deficits, while large cortical strokes may leave the patient awake but with focal deficits.

Sleep architecture

StageFeaturesRegulator
NREM 1Light sleep, easy to wakeHypothalamus dampens RAS
NREM 2Sleep spindles, K complexes—
NREM 3 (slow-wave)Deep sleep; growth hormone release; "physical restoration"—
REMRapid eye movements, dreaming, atonia of voluntary muscles, "memory consolidation"Brainstem (pons)
Sleep-wake cycle disruption. The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus is the body's circadian pacemaker. The brainstem coordinates the sleep stages themselves. Brainstem injury disrupts both REM and arousal — patients lose normal sleep architecture and may fluctuate between hypersomnia and unrousable states.
"Difficult to arouse after brainstem injury" = damaged RAS. The level of consciousness is the responsibility of the brainstem, not the cortex.

B. Cerebellum
C. Hippocampus
D. Basal ganglia
Show answer
A. RAS sits in the brainstem and drives cortical arousal. Damage there → impaired wakefulness even when the cortex is intact.

Unit 5  •  Motor System & Movement Disorders  LO3

5.1 The Two-Neuron Motor Pathway: UMN vs LMN

Motor cortex (UMN) → corticospinal tract → anterior horn cell (LMN starts) → peripheral nerve → neuromuscular junction → muscle
The motor system uses two neurons in series:
  • Upper motor neuron (UMN) — cell body in the cortex; axon descends in the corticospinal tract through internal capsule and brainstem; ends in the spinal cord.
  • Lower motor neuron (LMN) — cell body in the anterior (ventral) horn of the spinal cord; axon exits through ventral roots and peripheral nerves to reach muscle.
The signs of a lesion depend entirely on which neuron is broken.
SignUMN lesionLMN lesion
WeaknessYes (often hemiparesis)Yes (focal, in nerve distribution)
ToneIncreased (spasticity)Decreased (flaccid)
ReflexesHyperreflexic, clonusHyporeflexic or absent
BabinskiPositive (toes up)Negative
AtrophyMild, late (disuse)Marked, early
FasciculationsNoYes
ExamplesStroke, MS, spinal cord injury (chronic)Polio, peripheral nerve injury, GBS, anterior horn disease
UMN = "EVERYTHING UP except strength." Tone up, reflexes up, toes up (Babinski).
LMN = "EVERYTHING DOWN." Tone down (flaccid), reflexes down, toes down. Plus visible atrophy and fasciculations.

5.2 Major Movement and Motor Disorders

Parkinson's diseaseLoss of dopamine-producing neurons in substantia nigra → loss of basal ganglia inhibition → rigidityTRAP: Tremor (resting), Rigidity (cogwheel), Akinesia/bradykinesia, Postural instability; mask-like face, shuffling gait
Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS / Lou Gehrig's)Degeneration of both UMN and LMNMixed signs: spasticity AND atrophy/fasciculations; cognition spared; respiratory failure is the killer

Spinal shock to spasticity transition. Right after spinal cord injury, even reflexes below the lesion vanish — flaccid paralysis. Over days to weeks, the spinal cord reflex arcs reorganize and start firing without UMN inhibition → the limbs become spastic, hyperreflexic, sometimes with painful spasms. Same lesion, two phases: acute = LMN-like; chronic = UMN-like.
"Parkinson's is a memory disease." No — Parkinson's is a movement disorder. Cognition can decline late, but the cardinal features are motor (TRAP). Alzheimer's is the classic memory disease.
ALS is the only movement disorder with mixed UMN AND LMN signs. A patient with both spasticity and fasciculations, with normal cognition, points at ALS. This is a high-yield distinguishing feature.

Unit 6  •  Cognition: Dementia, Delirium & Consciousness  LO3

Dementia = chronic, progressive, irreversible decline in cognition. Memory, language, executive function, and judgment slowly erode over months to years.
Delirium = acute, fluctuating, often reversible disturbance in attention and consciousness, usually caused by medical illness, drugs, or hospitalization. Hours to days.
FeatureDementiaDelirium
OnsetInsidious (months to years)Acute (hours to days)
CourseSlowly progressiveFluctuating; classically worse at night ("sundowning")
AttentionPreserved early; declines lateImpaired from the start — defining feature
ConsciousnessClearAltered (hyperalert or drowsy)
ReversibilityUsually no (with rare exceptions)Usually yes when underlying cause is treated
Common causesAlzheimer's, vascular, Lewy body, frontotemporalInfection (UTI in elderly), medications (anticholinergics, opioids, benzos), metabolic derangement, alcohol withdrawal, post-op, ICU stay
The single best discriminator: onset. If the change happened over a weekend, it's delirium. If it happened over months, it's dementia. A demented patient who suddenly worsens may have delirium superimposed on dementia — usually triggered by a UTI, medication, or pneumonia.
Why elderly patients delirium-out so easily. Aging brains have less cognitive reserve. Add a medication, an infection, a hospital admission, or sleep deprivation, and the system tips into delirium. Treating the underlying cause and minimizing tethering, sedatives, and sensory deprivation usually reverses it.

Levels of consciousness — the spectrum

  • Alert — fully aware, responsive
  • Confused — disoriented, may follow simple commands
  • Lethargic — drowsy but rouses to voice
  • Obtunded — rouses to vigorous stimulation, slow response
  • Stuporous — rouses only to painful stimuli, returns to unresponsive state
  • Comatose — no purposeful response to any stimulus
"A demented patient is just confused." Don't blame all confusion on baseline dementia. A new behavior change in a demented patient is delirium until proven otherwise — search for infection, medication change, hypoxia, electrolyte abnormality, or pain.

Unit 7  •  Cerebral Hemodynamics & Intracranial Pressure  LO3

7.1 The Monro-Kellie Doctrine

The skull is a fixed compartment containing three things: brain tissue (~80%), blood (~10%), CSF (~10%). If one expands, the others must shrink to keep total volume — and pressure — constant. When compensation is exhausted, ICP rises sharply.
Mass added (tumor, blood, edema) → CSF first displaced → blood volume reduced → compensation exhausted → ICP rises rapidly → herniation

Normal ICP: 5–15 mm Hg. Sustained > 20 mm Hg is pathologic.

7.2 Cerebral Autoregulation

Cerebral blood vessels constrict or dilate to keep cerebral blood flow constant despite changes in MAP. Two key chemical influences:
  • ↑ CO₂ (or ↓ pH) → cerebral vasodilation → ↑ blood flow → ↑ ICP
  • ↓ CO₂ → cerebral vasoconstriction → ↓ blood flow → ↓ ICP (this is why we briefly hyperventilate patients with herniation)
  • ↓ O₂ → cerebral vasodilation
Why a CO₂ rise is dangerous in head injury. Hypoventilation → ↑ PaCO₂ → cerebral vasodilation → more blood in the skull → ↑ ICP → less cerebral perfusion. Patients with raised ICP are kept normoventilated (PaCO₂ around 35–40 mm Hg) and oxygenated to avoid this trap.

7.3 Cushing Triad — The Late Warning

When ICP rises so high that the brainstem is being compressed, the body responds with the Cushing reflex — a sympathetic surge to push blood through the brain:
  • Hypertension (↑ systolic BP, widening pulse pressure)
  • Bradycardia
  • Irregular respirations (Cheyne-Stokes or apneic)
This is a late, ominous sign — herniation is imminent.

7.4 Early vs Late Signs of Rising ICP

EarlyLate
Headache, restlessness, irritabilityDecreasing level of consciousness
Subtle confusion, change in behaviorCushing triad
Pupils still reactiveFixed, dilated pupil (often unilateral first — "blown pupil")
Vomiting (often projectile)Posturing (decorticate or decerebrate)
Visual changes, papilledema on examComa, herniation, apnea
"A head-injured patient who becomes restless and confused" is showing an EARLY sign of rising ICP — not just psychological distress. Behavior change before posturing is when the team has time to act.
"Hypotension and tachycardia mean rising ICP." Wrong direction. Cushing triad is HTN + bradycardia + irregular respirations. Hypotension + tachycardia points to hypovolemia, sepsis, or shock.

D. Mild headache
Show answer
B. Cushing triad — HTN, bradycardia, irregular respirations — is the late warning of severe ICP elevation and impending herniation. Restlessness/confusion is an earlier sign that should still trigger urgent assessment.
Q25. Select all that apply. A patient develops rising PaCO₂. Which cerebral hemodynamic responses may occur?
A. Cerebral vasodilation
B. Increased cerebral blood flow
C. Increased intracranial pressure
D. Cerebral vasoconstriction
E. Decreased intracranial pressure
Show answer
A, B, C. Hypercapnia dilates cerebral vessels → more blood in the skull → higher ICP. D and E describe the response to hypocapnia (low CO₂), which is the opposite.

Appendix  •  Top Missteps & Master Mnemonics

Top 15 Week 4 Missteps

#The trapWhat's actually true
1"Schwann cells make CNS myelin."Schwann = PNS. Oligodendrocytes = CNS.
2"MS and Guillain-Barré are the same disease."MS = CNS, oligodendrocytes, relapsing. GBS = PNS, Schwann cells, ascending, single episode.
3"Epidural hematoma is venous and slow."Epidural = arterial (middle meningeal), fast, lucid interval. Subdural = venous (bridging veins), slow.
4"Subarachnoid hemorrhage causes a mild headache."It's the "worst headache of my life" — thunderclap. With nuchal rigidity.
5"All hydrocephalus is from too much CSF production."Most is from impaired flow or absorption, not overproduction.
6"Burning, shooting pain is nociceptive."That's neuropathic. Nociceptive is sharp/throbbing/well-localized.
7"Brainstem injury causes only motor problems."It also wipes out arousal (RAS) and sleep architecture — coma and disrupted sleep-wake cycles.
8"Parkinson's is a memory disease."Movement disorder. TRAP: tremor, rigidity, akinesia, postural instability.
9"ALS spares lower motor neurons."ALS hits both UMN and LMN — that's its signature.
10"Spinal shock is permanent flaccid paralysis."It's a temporary phase. Spasticity emerges as reflex arcs reorganize.
11"Delirium is permanent."Delirium is acute and usually reversible when the cause is treated.
12"All confusion in the elderly is dementia."Acute change = delirium until proven otherwise. Look for infection, drugs, electrolytes.
13"Cushing triad is HTN + tachycardia."It's HTN + bradycardia + irregular respirations — late ICP elevation.
14"Low CO₂ raises ICP."Low CO₂ → vasoconstriction → lowers ICP. High CO₂ raises it.
15"Pain crosses the cord at the brainstem."Spinothalamic pain crosses at entry (low). Dorsal column proprioception crosses at the medulla (high).

Week 4 Study Strategy

  • Master the support cells first. Half of the LO1 questions are decided by knowing which glial cell does what.
  • Anchor every disorder to its location. CNS or PNS? Cortex, brainstem, basal ganglia, cerebellum, cord, root, peripheral nerve, neuromuscular junction, or muscle? Location dictates symptoms.
  • Drill UMN vs LMN sign tables. Many motor questions reduce to which side of the dotted line you're on.
  • Memorize the three hematomas as a set. Vessel, space, timing, and CT shape — never one without the others.
  • Know the Cushing triad cold. It's the highest-yield late-ICP teaching point and easy points if you have it.
  • Onset over hours = delirium. Onset over months = dementia. Don't overthink it.
  • Spend extra time on the sensory-tract crossings. Brown-Séquard hemicord lesion is a perennial favorite.
