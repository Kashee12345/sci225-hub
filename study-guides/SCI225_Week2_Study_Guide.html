Unit 1  •  Adaptive Immunity  LO2

Adaptive immunity is slow, specific, and has memory. Unlike innate immunity (fast, nonspecific), adaptive takes days to mount on first exposure but responds within hours on re-exposure — that is the entire reason vaccination works.

Adaptive immunity has two arms that work together:
  • Humoral (B-cell) immunity — B cells differentiate into plasma cells that make antibodies. Antibodies target extracellular threats: bacteria in the bloodstream, toxins, viruses before they enter cells.
  • Cell-mediated (T-cell) immunity — T cells directly kill infected cells (CD8 cytotoxic) or coordinate the response (CD4 helper). Needed for intracellular pathogens: viruses already inside cells, intracellular bacteria (TB), tumor cells.

Why IgM comes first. B cells initially express IgM without needing help. With help from CD4 T cells, they can "class-switch" to IgG, IgA, or IgE depending on the signal — a more specialized, longer-lasting response. The first wave (IgM) buys time while the class-switched wave (IgG) builds.

1.3 Active vs Passive Immunity & Vaccines

ACTIVE IMMUNITY — you make it
Your own B/T cells mount a response. Slow onset (days), long-lasting (often lifelong), memory formed.
Natural: surviving infection.
Artificial: vaccination.
PASSIVE IMMUNITY — given to you
Pre-formed antibodies transferred in. Immediate (hours), short-lived (weeks to months), no memory.
Natural: maternal IgG across placenta, IgA in breast milk.
Artificial: IVIG, rabies immunoglobulin, antivenom, equine antiserum.
"Given" = passive. Always. If the patient received pre-formed antibody (RhoGAM, rabies Ig, monoclonal Ab, maternal IgG, breast milk IgA), they have passive immunity with no memory. If the patient made the antibody (infection, vaccine), it is active with memory.

Live vaccines + immunocompromised = danger. An infant with profound lymphopenia and absent Igs (SCID suspect) who got a live vaccine is at risk of vaccine-derived disseminated infection. This is an exam favorite.

Practice Questions — Adaptive Immunity

Q1. Which statement best distinguishes humoral from cell-mediated immunity?
A. Humoral targets intracellular pathogens; cell-mediated targets extracellular
B. Humoral involves antibodies from B-cell-derived plasma cells; cell-mediated involves T cells directly killing infected cells
C. Humoral is innate; cell-mediated is adaptive
D. Humoral forms no memory; cell-mediated does
Show answer
B. Humoral = antibody = B cells (for extracellular threats). Cell-mediated = T cells (for intracellular and tumor cells). Both are part of adaptive immunity, both form memory.
Q2. A pediatric patient has recurrent sinus and gastrointestinal infections but normal IgG and IgM. Which immunoglobulin is most likely deficient?
A. IgE
B. IgA
C. IgD
D. IgM
Show answer
B. IgA lives in mucosal secretions and protects the respiratory and GI tracts. Selective IgA deficiency is the most common primary immunodeficiency and presents this way.
Q3. A newborn is born full-term to a fully vaccinated mother and is exclusively breastfed. The infant is protected from many pathogens during the first six months by:
A. Natural active immunity from in utero infection
B. Natural passive immunity from maternal IgG across the placenta and IgA in breast milk
C. Artificial active immunity from vaccination at birth
D. Innate immunity alone
Show answer
B. The infant "received" the antibodies → passive. The transfer was physiologic (placenta, breast milk) → natural. No memory is formed, which is why routine vaccination begins around 2 months.
Q4. Select all that apply. Which vaccines should be avoided in a patient with severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID)?
A. MMR (measles, mumps, rubella)
B. Hepatitis B
C. Varicella
D. Inactivated influenza
E. Oral polio (OPV)
Show answer
A, C, E. All three are live attenuated — contraindicated in SCID because even a "weakened" live virus can cause disseminated infection when T cells are absent. Hep B (subunit) and inactivated influenza are safe.
Q5. A patient exposed to rabies receives both the rabies vaccine and rabies immunoglobulin. The immunoglobulin provides:
A. Artificial active immunity
B. Artificial passive immunity — immediate but short-lived
C. Natural passive immunity
D. Long-term cellular memory
Show answer
B. Pre-formed antibody given = artificial passive = immediate protection, no memory. The vaccine provides the artificial active component for memory. Post-exposure rabies is a classic case for using both together.

Unit 2  •  Hypersensitivity Types I–IV  LO1

Hypersensitivity = an immune response that harms the host. The four Gell & Coombs types are grouped by mechanism, not by symptom. Learn the mechanism cold — the exam vignettes vary but the mechanism is fixed.

"ACID" — the four hypersensitivity types, alphabetized by what drives them:
Allergic  ·  Cytotoxic  ·  Immune complex  ·  Delayed
Or: I = IgE · II = IgG/IgM on a cell surface · III = immune complexes · IV = T cells (no antibody).
TypeDriverTimingClassic examples
I — Immediate / anaphylacticIgE on mast cellsMinutesAnaphylaxis (food, bee sting, latex), allergic rhinitis, atopic asthma, eczema
II — Cytotoxic / antibody-mediatedIgG or IgM binds cell-surface antigenMinutes to hoursABO transfusion reaction, hemolytic disease of newborn, Graves, myasthenia gravis, rheumatic fever, hyperacute transplant rejection
III — Immune complexAntigen-antibody complexes deposit in tissues → complement activation1–3 weeksSLE/lupus nephritis, serum sickness, farmer's lung, post-strep glomerulonephritis, Arthus reaction
IV — Delayed / cell-mediatedSensitized T cells (no antibody)24–72 hoursTB skin test (PPD), contact dermatitis (poison ivy, nickel, latex), chronic transplant rejection, graft-versus-host, type 1 DM

2.1 Type I — Immediate / IgE-Mediated

First exposure (sensitization): allergen → B cells make IgE → IgE binds Fc receptors on mast cells and basophils. No symptoms yet.
Second exposure: allergen cross-links two adjacent IgE → mast cell degranulates → histamine + leukotrienes + tryptase released → bronchoconstriction, vasodilation, ↑ capillary permeability.
Allergen (2nd exposure) → cross-links IgE on mast cell → degranulation → histamine release → smooth muscle contraction + vasodilation + capillary leak → wheezing, hives, hypotension
Anaphylaxis is systemic type I — throat/bronchus constriction (airway), hypotension from vasodilation (shock), urticaria/angioedema. Treat with IM epinephrine first (α = vasoconstriction, β1 = cardiac output, β2 = bronchodilation), then antihistamines + steroids as adjuncts.
Timing is the fastest clue. If symptoms appear within minutes and the patient has a prior exposure, think Type I. Bee stings, peanuts, shellfish, pollen, latex, cats, and medications are the classic triggers.

2.2 Type II — Cytotoxic / Antibody-Mediated

IgG or IgM binds an antigen that is attached to a cell surface. This triggers: complement activation (cell lysis), phagocytosis, NK-cell killing (antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity), or altered cell function (blocking or stimulating receptors).

The four flavors of type II — same mechanism, different tissue targets:

  • Direct cell destruction — ABO transfusion reaction (anti-A or anti-B antibodies lyse the wrong-type RBCs within minutes)
  • Hemolytic disease of the newborn — Rh-negative mom who was previously sensitized makes IgG anti-Rh → crosses placenta → attacks Rh-positive fetal RBCs → fetal anemia and jaundice. RhoGAM prevents sensitization.
  • Receptor stimulation — Graves disease: IgG binds TSH receptor on thyroid and activates it → hyperthyroidism
  • Receptor blockade — myasthenia gravis: IgG blocks ACh receptors at the neuromuscular junction → fatigable muscle weakness
  • Molecular mimicry — rheumatic fever: antibodies made against strep M protein cross-react with heart, joints, and basal ganglia → carditis, arthritis, chorea
Hyperacute transplant rejection is type II: recipient had pre-formed antibodies (from prior transplant, transfusion, or pregnancy) against donor HLA → graft is attacked within minutes of reperfusion → becomes pale and nonfunctioning. A positive pre-transplant crossmatch should prevent this.

2.3 Type III — Immune Complex

Antigen and antibody form soluble immune complexes in the blood. Complexes deposit in small vessels and filtration organs — especially kidneys (glomeruli), joints, and skin. Complement is activated locally → neutrophil recruitment → tissue damage.

High-yield type III diseases:

  • SLE (systemic lupus erythematosus) — anti-dsDNA and anti-Sm antibodies form complexes that deposit in glomeruli (lupus nephritis), joints, and skin. Malar butterfly rash, proteinuria, hematuria, low complement.
  • Serum sickness — exposure to a foreign protein (classically equine antiserum) → immune complexes form 7–14 days later → fever, rash, joint pain, proteinuria. Self-resolving.
  • Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis — strep antigens form complexes that deposit in glomeruli 1–3 weeks after infection → hematuria, edema, HTN.
  • Farmer's lung / hypersensitivity pneumonitis — inhaled moldy hay antigens → complexes in lung → dyspnea, cough hours after exposure.
  • Arthus reaction — localized type III at a vaccine injection site: erythema and swelling hours after boosters from preformed antibody + local antigen.
"Granular" deposits on immunofluorescence = immune complexes = type III. If the stem says "linear deposits," think Goodpasture (which is type II, anti-GBM).

2.4 Type IV — Delayed / T-Cell-Mediated

No antibody involved. Sensitized CD4 T cells release cytokines 24–72 hours after re-exposure to an antigen, recruiting macrophages that cause tissue damage. This is the slowest hypersensitivity by design — not because it's mild, but because it requires T cells to migrate and activate.

Classic type IV examples:

  • PPD (tuberculin skin test) — positive reaction is firm induration at 48–72 h in a TB-sensitized patient
  • Contact dermatitis — poison ivy (urushiol), nickel jewelry, latex, cosmetics, topical medications. Itchy vesicles 48–72 h after re-exposure.
  • Chronic transplant rejection — T cells target donor HLA over months to years; gradual graft dysfunction.
  • Graft-versus-host disease — donor T cells attack the recipient (opposite of rejection).
  • Type 1 diabetes — T cells destroy pancreatic β-cells.
  • Multiple sclerosis, contact-type drug reactions.
Poison ivy is NOT Type I. Students guess "allergic = IgE = Type I" reflexively. Poison ivy is delayed (hours to days), itch-predominant, T-cell mediated → Type IV. Same for nickel contact dermatitis and PPD tests.

2.5 Transplant Rejection — The Three Timelines

B. Rapid onset wheezing + hives in a sensitized patient = type I. Mast-cell degranulation releases histamine and leukotrienes that contract bronchial smooth muscle and raise capillary permeability.

B. Anti-A or anti-B antibodies in the recipient bind to donor RBCs → complement lyses them → intravascular hemolysis (fever, flank pain, hemoglobinuria = free Hb filtered into urine). Type II is always antibody directed at a cell surface antigen.

B. Graves is a type II hypersensitivity of receptor stimulation. The IgG mimics TSH and drives the thyroid to overproduce hormone. Contrast with Hashimoto (type IV, T-cell destruction of thyroid → hypothyroid).

Unit 3  •  Autoimmunity  LO1

Autoimmunity = immune system attacks the body's own tissues because it failed to distinguish self from non-self. Mechanisms overlap heavily with hypersensitivity types II, III, and IV — in fact, most named autoimmune diseases ARE one of those types targeting a specific tissue.

How tolerance breaks down:
  • Molecular mimicry — a pathogen antigen resembles a self-antigen; antibodies cross-react (rheumatic fever after strep).
  • Loss of regulatory T cells — fewer T-regs means self-reactive lymphocytes escape suppression.
  • Release of sequestered antigens — trauma exposes antigens normally hidden from the immune system (sympathetic ophthalmia after eye injury).
  • Genetic predisposition — certain HLA types raise risk (HLA-B27 in ankylosing spondylitis, HLA-DR3/DR4 in T1DM).

Why women get most autoimmune disease. SLE, RA, MS, Graves, Hashimoto, and myasthenia all skew female — estrogen is immunostimulatory, X chromosome carries many immune genes, and women mount stronger immune responses overall. This helps pregnancy (mom's immunity tolerates the fetus) but raises autoimmune risk.
Graves vs Hashimoto. Same gland, opposite directions.
Graves = IgG stimulates TSH receptor → hyperthyroid. Type II.
Hashimoto = T cells destroy the gland → hypothyroid. Type IV.
"Molecular mimicry" keywords on the exam: "rheumatic fever after strep," "antibodies cross-react with heart." These point to type II autoimmunity triggered by infection.

Unit 4  •  Immunodeficiencies  LO1, LO3

PRIMARY (congenital)
You are born with it. Usually genetic. Presents in infancy or childhood with recurrent infections.
SECONDARY (acquired)
Caused by something else. Most common cause worldwide = HIV/AIDS. Others: malnutrition, chemotherapy, steroids, aging, splenectomy, diabetes, CKD.

The pattern of infection tells you which arm is broken:
  • Recurrent pyogenic bacteria (Strep, Staph, H. flu) → B-cell / antibody problem
  • Opportunistic viruses, fungi, Pneumocystis → T-cell problem
  • Recurrent Neisseria → complement problem
  • Bacterial infections at skin and mucous membranes → phagocyte problem

4.2 Secondary Immunodeficiency — HIV / AIDS

HIV is a retrovirus that binds gp120 → CD4 receptor on helper T cells → enters and integrates into the host genome. Over years, CD4 count drops. When CD4 < 200/mm³ or an AIDS-defining opportunistic infection/malignancy occurs, the patient has AIDS.

AIDS-defining conditions (partial list): Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia, esophageal candidiasis, Kaposi sarcoma, CMV retinitis, cryptococcal meningitis, toxoplasmosis, TB.

CD4 rangeRisk
> 500/mm³Normal immune function
200–500Early symptoms: thrush, shingles, TB reactivation
50–200AIDS-defining: PCP, candidiasis (esophageal), Kaposi
< 50End-stage: CMV, MAC, CNS toxoplasmosis, lymphoma
Why HIV is so destructive. CD4 helpers coordinate BOTH arms of adaptive immunity. Lose them and the humoral arm weakens (B cells don't class-switch well), and the cellular arm collapses (CD8 T cells need CD4 help). Innate immunity and mucosal barriers remain — but once those fail, opportunistic infections take over.
"HIV kills the patient." No — HIV destroys CD4 cells. Opportunistic infections (PCP, cryptococcus, CMV) are what kill AIDS patients.

Unit 5  •  Infection Process  LO3

5.1 The Four Stages of Clinical Infection

Incubation → Prodromal → Illness (acute) → Convalescence
StageWhat's happeningKey features
1. IncubationPathogen multiplies but no symptoms yetPatient may be contagious; length varies (common cold 1–3 days, HIV years)
2. ProdromalVague, early symptoms as immune response beginsFatigue, low-grade fever, malaise; patient is highly contagious
3. Illness (acute)Full clinical diseaseCharacteristic symptoms; peak pathology; immune response in full swing
4. ConvalescenceRecovery as pathogen is clearedSymptoms resolve; memory forming; possible post-infectious complications (GN, rheumatic fever)

5.2 How Pathogens Cause Injury

  • Direct tissue invasion — bacteria or viruses physically enter and damage cells (Shigella, influenza).
  • Exotoxins — proteins secreted by (usually Gram-positive) bacteria. Specific, potent, often heat-labile. Tetanus toxin, botulinum toxin, diphtheria toxin, cholera toxin.
  • Endotoxins — lipopolysaccharide (LPS) in the Gram-negative outer membrane, released when bacteria lyse. Drives septic shock via TNF-α, IL-1.
  • Immune evasion — capsules (pneumococcus), antigenic variation (HIV, influenza), intracellular hiding (TB, listeria), IgA protease (gonorrhea).
  • Adherence — pili, fimbriae let bacteria stick to mucosa.
  • Trigger pathologic immune response — superantigens (toxic shock), molecular mimicry (rheumatic fever).
Exo vs endo toxin — the simple split:
Exotoxin = excreted by bacteria, mostly Gram-positive, protein, heat-labile, often vaccine-preventable (tetanus, diphtheria toxoid vaccines).
Endotoxin = inside the cell wall, Gram-negative, LPS, heat-stable, no specific vaccine, drives septic shock.
"The patient is febrile but not contagious." Usually wrong during infection stages. Prodromal is when patients are often most contagious because they don't know they're sick. Practice public-health reasoning: "when do we isolate?"

Correct order: Incubation → Prodromal → Illness → Convalescence. Memorize this and the fact that patients are often contagious during incubation and especially during prodromal stages.

A, B, C, D, E. F is protective, not harmful — pathogens don't usually boost T-reg function to cause disease. The rest are classic virulence mechanisms.
Q26. A student is learning about vaccines. Which statement indicates a need for further teaching?
A. "Live attenuated vaccines give the strongest immunity but are avoided in immunocompromised patients."
B. "Toxoid vaccines contain inactivated toxins that create antitoxin antibodies."

Show answer
D. mRNA vaccines deliver mRNA (not DNA) that is translated transiently in the cytoplasm and degraded — it does NOT integrate into DNA. The other statements are correct.
Q27. A patient in septic shock from a Gram-negative bacteremia has hypotension and a rising lactate. Which toxin is most responsible for the shock?
A. Tetanus exotoxin
B. Lipopolysaccharide (LPS) endotoxin
C. Diphtheria exotoxin
D. Botulinum exotoxin
Show answer
B. Gram-negative septic shock = endotoxin (LPS) → massive TNF-α/IL-1 release → vasodilation, ↑ capillary permeability, hypotension. The other three are exotoxins from Gram-positive organisms.

Unit 6  •  Stress & Immunity  LO4

Chronic psychological stress → activation of the HPA axis → sustained elevation of cortisol. Cortisol has many actions, but the relevant ones here are:
  • Suppression of lymphocyte proliferation and cytokine release
  • Redistribution of immune cells away from circulating blood
  • Decreased antibody production
  • Impaired wound healing (↓ collagen synthesis, ↓ fibroblast activity)
The result: more infections, slower wound healing, reactivation of latent viruses (HSV, VZV), and poorer response to vaccines.
Chronic stress → HPA axis → ↑ cortisol → ↓ lymphocyte function + ↓ antibodies + ↓ wound healing → ↑ infection, slow healing

Conditions and states that weaken host defense (acquired / situational)

  • Chronic psychological stress — cortisol-mediated immunosuppression
  • Severe malnutrition — protein-energy deficit → impaired T and B cell function
  • Sleep deprivation — reduces NK cell activity, T-cell responses
  • Diabetes mellitus — hyperglycemia impairs neutrophil chemotaxis and phagocytosis
  • Advanced age — immunosenescence
  • Chronic alcohol use — impairs multiple immune pathways; worsens malnutrition
  • Chemotherapy / immunosuppressants / corticosteroids — direct suppression
  • Chronic illness (CKD, cirrhosis, heart failure) — multiple pathways
  • Splenectomy — loss of defense against encapsulated organisms (pneumococcus, H. flu, meningococcus)
Why wound healing slows under stress. Cortisol suppresses fibroblasts, reduces collagen synthesis, and dampens the inflammatory response needed for tissue repair. A chronically stressed surgical patient heals more slowly and is more vulnerable to wound infection than a calm one.
Stress + infection + slow healing — when a vignette lists all three, the answer is almost always "chronic stress → cortisol → immunosuppression."

Practice Questions — Stress & Immunity

B. Chronic HPA axis activation → ↑ cortisol → immunosuppression. Acute stress is mildly immune-boosting; it's the chronic pattern that impairs defense.

Appendix  •  Top Missteps & Master Mnemonics

Top 15 Week 2 Missteps

#The trapWhat's actually true
1"Poison ivy is type I allergy."It's type IV — delayed T-cell. 48–72 h onset, no IgE involved.
2"Graves and Hashimoto are both type II."Graves is type II (IgG stimulates TSH-R). Hashimoto is type IV (T-cell destruction). Opposite gland outcomes.
3"Given antibodies = active immunity."Given = passive, always. No memory.
4"SLE has high complement."SLE has low C3/C4 — immune complexes consume complement during flares.
5"IgE is most abundant."IgG is. IgE is the least abundant and is the allergy / parasite antibody.
6"Hyperacute rejection is T-cell mediated."It's antibody-mediated type II (preformed antibodies vs donor HLA or ABO). T-cell = acute/chronic rejection.
7"HIV directly kills the patient."HIV depletes CD4. Opportunistic infections kill AIDS patients.
8"Live vaccines are safe for everyone."Contraindicated in SCID, AIDS, pregnancy, and chemotherapy.
9"CD4 < 500 = AIDS."AIDS is CD4 < 200 or an AIDS-defining illness.
10"Rheumatic fever is direct strep invasion of the heart."It's molecular mimicry — antibodies to strep M protein cross-react with heart.
11"ABO transfusion reaction is anaphylaxis."It's type II cytotoxic (antibodies vs RBC surface antigens). Anaphylaxis requires IgE.
12"Selective IgA deficiency is rare."It is the most common primary immunodeficiency.
13"Acute stress weakens immunity."Acute stress is mildly immune-boosting. Only chronic stress → cortisol → immunosuppression.
14"Exotoxins are Gram-negative."Exotoxins = mostly Gram-positive. Endotoxin (LPS) = Gram-negative.
15"Myasthenia gravis is receptor stimulation."MG is receptor blockade. Graves is the stimulation one.

Week 2 Study Strategy

  • Own the hypersensitivity types cold. If you can name the mechanism and one classic example for all four, you have the majority of Week 2's exam points.
  • Learn by timing. Minutes → Type I or hyperacute II. Hours → exotoxin, sepsis. 24–72 h → Type IV. Weeks → Type III, post-infectious autoimmunity.
  • Pair autoimmune disease to its hypersensitivity type. Every autoimmune disease in this week is one of the four types — know which.
  • Match infection pattern → defective arm. If a vignette describes recurrent Neisseria, think complement. Recurrent viruses and fungi, think T-cells.
  • Don't mix Graves and Hashimoto. Same organ, opposite direction, opposite hypersensitivity type.
  • Respect the CD4 numbers. 500 = normal floor. 200 = AIDS threshold. 50 = end-stage. These get asked directly.
  • Live vaccine + immunocompromise = no. If you see SCID, HIV advanced, chemotherapy, pregnancy, or organ transplant + a live vaccine option — it's the wrong answer.
