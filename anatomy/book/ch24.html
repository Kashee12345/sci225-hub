Metabolism & Nutrition

Every second you are alive, thousands of chemical reactions are running inside your cells — some building, some breaking down, all of them handling energy. That entire web of reactions is metabolism, and this chapter is where the food on your plate finally meets the machinery of your cells. You will follow a molecule of glucose as it is dismantled to power an ATP, watch your body flip between storing fuel after a meal and spending it between meals, and learn what the six classes of nutrients actually do once they are inside you. By the end, the deceptively simple question — why do we eat? — will have a precise, satisfying answer.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Define metabolism and distinguish anabolism from catabolism by their direction and their handling of energy.
  • Explain how ATP stores and delivers energy, and how catabolism is coupled to anabolism.
  • Trace glucose through the four steps of cellular respiration and account for the ATP produced at each stage.
  • Explain why oxygen is required for aerobic respiration and what the cell does when it runs short.
  • Contrast the absorptive and postabsorptive metabolic states and the roles of insulin and glucagon.
  • Name the six classes of nutrients, state which yield energy, and give each one's caloric value.
  • Separate the fat-soluble from the water-soluble vitamins and predict which are stored and which must be eaten regularly.
  • Describe energy balance and the three parts of total energy expenditure.

Section 1Anabolism & catabolism

One word for every reaction

Metabolism is the sum of every chemical reaction in the body — and there are staggering numbers of them running at once. To make that ocean learnable, we sort every reaction into one of two directions. Anabolism is the building direction: small molecules are joined into larger ones, usually by dehydration synthesis (removing a water molecule to form a bond). Amino acids become proteins, glucose units become glycogen, fatty acids and glycerol become fat. Building costs energy, so anabolic reactions are endergonic — they must be paid for. Catabolism is the breaking-down direction: large molecules are split into smaller ones, usually by hydrolysis (adding water to break a bond). Catabolism releases the energy that was locked in those chemical bonds, so these reactions are exergonic — they pay out. The two directions are not separate businesses; the energy released by catabolism is precisely what funds anabolism.

ATP: the cell's rechargeable battery

The body does not spend food energy directly. Instead it converts that energy into a single, universal currency — ATP (adenosine triphosphate). ATP carries its energy in the bonds between its three phosphate groups. Snap off the last phosphate and you get ADP plus a free phosphate plus a burst of usable energy; the cell uses that burst to power muscle contraction, nerve signaling, and anabolic building. Catabolism then recharges the battery by bolting the phosphate back on, turning ADP back into ATP. This constant charge-and-discharge is called energy coupling: an exergonic reaction (breaking a bond) is linked to an endergonic one (doing work) so the energy is handed off rather than lost as heat. You cycle your entire body weight in ATP roughly every day — not because you store that much, but because each molecule is recharged thousands of times.

Following the electrons

Many metabolic reactions are oxidation-reduction (redox) reactions, in which electrons are transferred from one molecule to another. A molecule that loses electrons is oxidized; one that gains them is reduced. This matters because electrons carry energy, and the cell moves them around using shuttle molecules called electron carriers — chiefly NAD⁺ and FAD. When these carriers pick up electrons (becoming NADH and FADH₂), they are essentially loading up on energy to deliver later. Hold onto that idea; in the next section those loaded carriers become the key to making most of your ATP.

Anabolic builds, catabolic breaks. The gym word gives it away: "anabolic" steroids are used to build muscle. Any time you see "ana-", think up and together; any time you see "cata-", think down and apart. And remember the payoff line — catabolism releases the energy that anabolism spends.

Key points
  • Metabolism = all body reactions; anabolism builds (endergonic, uses ATP), catabolism breaks down (exergonic, makes ATP).
  • ATP is the cell's energy currency — it discharges to ADP + phosphate to do work, and catabolism recharges it.
  • Energy coupling links energy-releasing reactions to energy-requiring ones so the energy is used, not wasted.

Section 2Cellular respiration

Burning glucose, carefully

Cellular respiration is the controlled catabolism of glucose to make ATP. It is a kind of slow, safe burning — instead of releasing all of glucose's energy at once as a destructive flash, the cell strips it away in small steps, capturing the energy in ATP as it goes. The whole process happens in four stages across two locations: the first stage in the cytoplasm, the last three inside the mitochondrion. When enough oxygen is present, a single glucose molecule can yield up to roughly 30 to 32 ATP. Follow it stage by stage.

The four stages

1. Glycolysis happens in the cytoplasm and needs no oxygen. One six-carbon glucose is split into two three-carbon pyruvate molecules. The cell spends two ATP to start the process and harvests four, for a net gain of 2 ATP, plus 2 NADH. 2. Pyruvate oxidation (the transition reaction) moves each pyruvate into the mitochondrial matrix, snips off a carbon as CO₂, and attaches the remainder to a carrier to form acetyl CoA, making more NADH. 3. The citric acid cycle (Krebs cycle), also in the matrix, feeds acetyl CoA around a loop that releases the rest of the carbons as CO₂, yields a little more ATP directly, and — most importantly — loads up many molecules of NADH and FADH₂. 4. Oxidative phosphorylation on the inner mitochondrial membrane is where the real payoff comes. The loaded carriers drop their electrons into the electron transport chain, which uses that energy to pump H⁺ ions and build a gradient; the ions then rush back through an enzyme that spins like a turbine, snapping phosphates onto ADP. This step alone makes the large majority of the ATP.

Why oxygen, and what happens without it

Here is the punchline of the whole process: oxygen is the final electron acceptor. At the very end of the electron transport chain, the spent electrons and some H⁺ ions are handed to O₂, forming H₂O (the water you make from breathing). If no oxygen is waiting there to accept them, the chain backs up like a clogged drain, the carriers stay loaded, and stages 2 through 4 grind to a halt. When that happens, the cell falls back on anaerobic fermentation: it keeps glycolysis limping along by converting pyruvate to lactic acid, which regenerates the NAD⁺ glycolysis needs. This buys a fast trickle of 2 ATP with no oxygen — useful for a sprinting muscle — but it is wildly inefficient and the lactic acid builds up, which is part of why hard exercise burns.

No oxygen, no chain. This is why suffocation, drowning, and a blocked coronary artery are so quickly fatal — without O₂ to accept electrons, oxidative phosphorylation stops and ATP output collapses to the tiny anaerobic trickle. The poison cyanide kills the same way, by jamming the electron transport chain even when oxygen is plentiful.

Section 3Metabolic states: fed vs fasting

Two modes: store or spend

Your fuel supply is not constant — it arrives in bursts at mealtimes and then dwindles for hours afterward. To cope, the body runs in two distinct metabolic states. The absorptive (fed) state is the few hours after a meal, when nutrients are pouring in from the gut and blood glucose rises. The task now is to store the surplus: extra glucose is packed into glycogen (glycogenesis) and fat (lipogenesis), and amino acids build protein. The postabsorptive (fasting) state takes over once absorption is done — overnight, between meals, or during a fast. Now the task flips to releasing stored fuel to keep blood glucose steady: glycogen is broken back to glucose (glycogenolysis), fat is broken down (lipolysis), and the liver even manufactures brand-new glucose from non-carbohydrate sources like amino acids (gluconeogenesis).

Two hormones run the switch

The state you are in is set mainly by two opposing pancreatic hormones. Insulin is the storage hormone; it rises after a meal, drives glucose into cells, and turns on all the building pathways. Glucagon is the mobilizing hormone; it rises during fasting and turns on the releasing pathways. Think of them as a seesaw: insulin high means store, glucagon high means spend. One organ is the hub for all of it — the liver, which stores glycogen after meals and pumps out glucose between them, keeping blood sugar within its narrow homeostatic range around the clock.

Protecting the brain

Why go to all this trouble? Because the brain runs almost exclusively on glucose and cannot store its own fuel, so blood glucose must never crash. During a long fast the body practices glucose sparing: most tissues switch to burning fatty acids, leaving the scarce glucose for the brain. In prolonged starvation the liver converts fat into ketone bodies, an alternative fuel the brain can partly use — a clever adaptation, though a heavy buildup of these acidic ketones can dangerously lower blood pH.

Insulin stores, glucagon spends. This single pair explains diabetes: without working insulin, cells cannot take up glucose after a meal, so blood sugar climbs while the cells starve — the body behaves as if it were fasting even while overflowing with fuel, which is why untreated diabetics can slip into dangerous ketosis.

Section 4The six nutrient classes

Three that fuel you, three that don't

Everything worth eating falls into six classes of nutrients. Three of them supply usable energy and are eaten in large amounts — the macronutrients: carbohydrates, lipids (fats), and proteins. The other three yield no energy at all but are absolutely required in smaller amounts — vitamins, minerals, and water. "No energy" does not mean "optional": you would die without water or without trace minerals long before you starved. Each class has a job. Carbohydrates are the body's preferred quick fuel. Lipids are the densest energy store and build every cell membrane. Proteins are mainly structural and functional — enzymes, muscle, antibodies — and are burned for energy only as a last resort.

How much energy is in food?

The energy in food is measured in kilocalories (written "Calories" with a capital C on food labels). Only the three macronutrients carry calories, and they are not equal. Carbohydrate and protein each release about 4 kcal per gram; fat packs more than twice that at about 9 kcal per gram, which is exactly why fatty foods are so calorie-dense and why fat is the body's chosen long-term energy store. Alcohol, though not a nutrient, also carries energy at about 7 kcal per gram.

Essential nutrients: the ones you must eat

An essential nutrient is one your body cannot make (or cannot make in sufficient amounts), so it must come from the diet. These include roughly nine essential amino acids, a couple of essential fatty acids, and all the vitamins and minerals. This is why protein quality matters: a complete protein (from meat, eggs, dairy, soy) supplies all the essential amino acids, while most single plant foods are incomplete — but combining complementary plants, such as beans with rice, covers the full set.

Section 5Vitamins

Tiny amounts, huge consequences

Vitamins are organic molecules the body needs in very small amounts to run its metabolism, mostly by acting as coenzymes — helper molecules that enzymes need in order to work. You need mere milligrams or micrograms a day, yet a shortfall of even one can cause a specific, sometimes deadly, deficiency disease. What makes a vitamin behave the way it does is a single physical property: how it dissolves. That splits all vitamins into two camps — fat-soluble and water-soluble — and that one fact predicts how each is absorbed, whether it is stored, and whether it can build up to toxic levels.

Fat-soluble: stored, and able to overdose

The fat-soluble vitamins are A, D, E, and K — remember them as the letters that "ADEK" up. Because they dissolve in fat, they are absorbed alongside dietary fat and then stored in the liver and fatty tissue. That storage is a double-edged sword: you can go a while without them because you have a reserve, but because the body cannot easily flush the excess, megadoses of A or D can accumulate to toxic levels. Their jobs are varied — A for vision, D for calcium balance and bone, E as an antioxidant, K for blood clotting.

Water-soluble: not stored, needed steadily

The water-soluble vitamins are the B-complex and vitamin C. Because they dissolve in water, the body does not stockpile them — any surplus is simply filtered out by the kidneys and lost in urine (which is why very high doses mostly make expensive urine). The flip side is that you need a steady daily supply, and deficiencies appear relatively quickly. Most B vitamins serve as coenzymes in energy metabolism — the very pathways from Sections 1 and 2. The classic deficiency diseases are worth knowing: scurvy from lack of vitamin C, rickets from vitamin D, beriberi from B₁, pellagra from B₃, and pernicious anemia from B₁₂.

Section 6Energy balance

A ledger, not a mystery

Body weight, at its foundation, follows a simple accounting rule called energy balance: the calories you take in as food versus the calories you spend out. When intake equals expenditure, weight holds steady (neutral balance). When intake exceeds expenditure, the surplus is stored, mostly as fat, and weight rises (positive balance). When expenditure exceeds intake, the body draws on its stores and weight falls (negative balance). The rule is straightforward; the reason weight is hard to control is that both sides of the ledger are governed by biology, not willpower alone.

Where the calories go

"Energy out" is not just exercise. Total energy expenditure has three parts. By far the largest is the basal metabolic rate (BMR) — the energy your body burns just staying alive at rest, keeping the heart beating, the kidneys filtering, and body temperature up. BMR accounts for roughly 60 to 75% of what you burn in a day, which surprises most people. A second slice, about 10%, is the thermic effect of food — the energy spent digesting and absorbing a meal. The remaining, most variable part is physical activity, from fidgeting to running a marathon. Because BMR dominates, factors that change it — muscle mass, age, thyroid hormone, body size — strongly influence how easily a person gains or loses weight.

The body defends its weight

Energy balance is not left to chance; it is regulated by the same homeostatic logic as everything else, with the hypothalamus as the control center reading hormonal signals about the body's fuel stores. Leptin, released by fat tissue, signals that stores are full and dampens appetite; ghrelin, from the empty stomach, signals hunger and stimulates eating. As a useful rule of thumb, a pound of body fat stores roughly 3,500 kcal — so a sustained deficit of about 500 kcal a day trims close to a pound a week, at least until the body adjusts its BMR downward to defend against the loss.

BalanceIntake vs outputResult
NeutralIn = outWeight stable
PositiveIn > outSurplus stored as fat → weight gain
NegativeIn < outStores drawn down → weight loss
BMR is the biggest spender. Most of your daily calorie burn happens while you do nothing at all — keeping you warm and running your organs. That is why crash dieting backfires: cut intake too hard and the body lowers its BMR to protect its stores, making the last pounds stubborn and rapid regain likely.

Key points
  • Energy balance compares calories in (food) with calories out; positive balance stores fat, negative balance draws it down.
  • Total expenditure = BMR (~60–75%, the largest part) + thermic effect of food (~10%) + physical activity.

The chapter in one breath

  • Metabolism is every reaction in the body; anabolism builds and uses energy, catabolism breaks down and releases it, with ATP as the shared currency.
  • Cellular respiration catabolizes glucose in four stages — glycolysis, pyruvate oxidation, the citric acid cycle, and oxidative phosphorylation — yielding up to ~32 ATP.
  • Oxygen is the final electron acceptor; without it cells fall back on anaerobic fermentation for a mere 2 ATP plus lactic acid.
  • The body alternates between an absorptive (store, insulin) and a postabsorptive (release, glucagon) state, with the liver keeping blood glucose steady for the brain.
  • Diet supplies six nutrient classes: energy-yielding carbohydrates, lipids, and proteins (4, 9, and 4 kcal/g) plus non-energy vitamins, minerals, and water.
  • Vitamins split by solubility: fat-soluble A, D, E, K are stored and can be toxic; water-soluble B and C are excreted and needed daily.
  • Energy balance weighs calories in against calories out — BMR, the thermic effect of food, and activity — and the body actively defends its weight.

Check yourself

1. A cell links glycogen breakdown to muscle contraction so the energy is used rather than lost as heat. What is this arrangement called, and which molecule carries the energy between the two events?
Energy coupling, carried by ATP. The exergonic reaction (breaking down glycogen) recharges ADP into ATP, and ATP then discharges to power the endergonic work of contraction — the energy is handed off, not wasted.
2. Classify each as anabolic or catabolic: (a) amino acids joined into a protein; (b) glycogen split into glucose. Which one releases energy?
(a) Anabolic; (b) catabolic, and (b) releases energy. Anabolism builds and costs energy (endergonic); catabolism breaks down and releases energy (exergonic). "Anabolic" builds, like anabolic steroids build muscle.
3. Put the four stages of cellular respiration in order and name where each occurs.
Glycolysis (cytoplasm) → pyruvate oxidation (mitochondrial matrix) → citric acid cycle (matrix) → oxidative phosphorylation (inner mitochondrial membrane). The first stage is in the cytoplasm and needs no oxygen; the last three are inside the mitochondrion and require it.
4. Which stage of respiration produces most of the ATP, and what molecule serves as the final electron acceptor there?
Oxidative phosphorylation; oxygen (O₂) is the final electron acceptor. Electrons from NADH and FADH₂ pass down the chain and are handed to O₂, forming water; this step makes roughly 26–28 of the ~32 ATP.
5. A sprinter's muscles run short of oxygen. What pathway keeps a trickle of ATP coming, how many ATP per glucose does it yield, and what byproduct accumulates?
Anaerobic fermentation; net 2 ATP per glucose; lactic acid. Converting pyruvate to lactic acid regenerates the NAD⁺ that glycolysis needs, keeping it running — fast but inefficient, and the lactic acid contributes to muscle burning.
6. Which metabolic state dominates two hours after a large lunch, which hormone drives it, and what is the body doing with the incoming fuel?
The absorptive (fed) state, driven by insulin, storing surplus fuel. Insulin pushes glucose into cells and turns on glycogenesis and lipogenesis, banking the surplus as glycogen and fat.
7. Overnight, blood glucose is defended without any food coming in. Name the hormone in charge and two processes it turns on to keep blood sugar up.
Glucagon; glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis. Glucagon triggers the liver to break stored glycogen into glucose and to manufacture new glucose from amino acids, keeping the glucose-dependent brain supplied.
8. List the six nutrient classes and state which three yield energy.
Carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, vitamins, minerals, water; the first three (carbohydrates, lipids, proteins) yield energy. Vitamins, minerals, and water supply no calories but are still essential to life.
9. A snack has 20 g carbohydrate, 10 g fat, and 5 g protein. Roughly how many kilocalories is that?
About 190 kcal. Carbohydrate and protein are 4 kcal/g and fat is 9 kcal/g: (20 × 4) + (10 × 9) + (5 × 4) = 80 + 90 + 20 = 190 kcal.
10. Why can fat-soluble vitamins reach toxic levels while water-soluble vitamins rarely do? Name the members of each group.
Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) are stored in liver and fat, so excess accumulates; water-soluble vitamins (B-complex and C) are excreted in urine, so excess washes out. The stored group needs less frequent intake but must be dosed cautiously; the excreted group needs steady daily replenishment.
11. Match each deficiency to its missing vitamin: scurvy, rickets, beriberi.
Scurvy → vitamin C; rickets → vitamin D; beriberi → vitamin B₁ (thiamine). Each vitamin has a signature deficiency disease, which is why a varied diet matters — a shortfall of even one micronutrient produces a specific illness.
12. Name the three components of total energy expenditure and state which is usually the largest.
Basal metabolic rate (BMR), the thermic effect of food, and physical activity; BMR is usually the largest (~60–75%). Most of your calories are burned at rest keeping organs running, which is why BMR — not exercise — dominates the daily total.
