The Axial Skeleton

Down the center of your body runs a bony core that holds you upright, guards your most delicate organs, and gives every muscle something to pull against. This is the axial skeleton — the skull, the spine, and the rib cage — eighty bones arranged along the body's long axis. In this chapter we will take that core apart piece by piece: we will name the bones of the skull, stack the twenty-six vertebrae of the backbone, dissect a single vertebra to learn the plan they all share, build the cage that shields the heart and lungs, and trace the graceful S-curve that lets the spine carry your weight without buckling. By the end, an X-ray of the chest or a diagram of the skull will read like a map you already know.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Divide the skeleton into its axial and appendicular parts and list what belongs to each.
  • Name the eight cranial and fourteen facial bones and identify the skull's major sutures.
  • List the five regions of the vertebral column with the number of bones in each.
  • Label the parts of a typical vertebra and explain what the body, arch, and processes each do.
  • Classify the ribs as true, false, or floating and name the three parts of the sternum.
  • Distinguish primary from secondary spinal curves and recognize kyphosis, lordosis, and scoliosis.
  • Connect each axial structure to the organ or function it protects or supports.

Section 1Axial vs appendicular

Two skeletons in one

The adult body has 206 bones, and anatomists sort every one of them into two families. The axial skeleton forms the central axis of the body — the vertical line running from the top of the skull, down the spine, out through the ribs. It has 80 bones, and its job is protection and support: it houses the brain, the spinal cord, the heart, and the lungs, and it gives the trunk its rigid core. The appendicular skeleton is everything hung off that axis — the appendages. It has 126 bones: the arms and legs plus the two girdles (shoulder and hip) that anchor them. Its job is movement. A quick way to keep them straight: if a bone sits on the midline or cradles an organ, it is axial; if it lets you reach, walk, or grip, it is appendicular.

What the axial 80 are made of

The eighty axial bones fall into just three groups, and the whole of this chapter is a tour of them. The skull and its associated bones account for twenty-nine, the vertebral column for twenty-six, and the thoracic cage for twenty-five. Notice how neatly these track the organs they defend: the skull around the brain, the vertebrae around the spinal cord, the rib cage around the heart and lungs. The axial skeleton is, in a real sense, the body's suit of armor.

RegionBones includedCount
Skull8 cranial + 14 facial bones22
Associated skull bones6 auditory ossicles + 1 hyoid bone7
Vertebral column24 vertebrae + sacrum + coccyx26
Thoracic cage24 ribs (12 pairs) + 1 sternum25
Axial total—80
80 + 126 = 206. If you ever blank on how the skeleton divides, anchor to the total. The axial 80 protect and support along the midline; the appendicular 126 move. Their sum is the classic adult bone count — a number worth committing to memory.

Section 2The skull

Eight bones cradle the brain

The skull is 22 bones, and the first split is between the part that surrounds the brain and the part that builds the face. The cranium (the braincase) is made of eight cranial bones. Four are single midline bones: the frontal (forehead), the occipital (back and base, pierced by the large foramen magnum where the spinal cord exits), the sphenoid (a bat-shaped keystone bone spanning the floor of the cranium), and the ethmoid (a delicate bone between the eyes forming part of the nasal cavity). The other two come as pairs: the two parietal bones roofing the top and sides, and the two temporal bones housing the ears low on each side. Together they form a rigid vault whose only purpose is to keep the brain safe.

Fourteen bones build the face

The 14 facial bones give the face its shape and frame the openings for sight, smell, and eating. Most are paired: the two maxillae (the upper jaw, fused at the midline), the two zygomatic bones (the cheekbones), the two nasal bones (the bridge of the nose), the two lacrimal bones (tiny bones at the inner eye that channel tears), the two palatine bones (the back of the hard palate), and the two inferior nasal conchae (curled shelves inside the nose). Two are single: the vomer (the lower part of the nasal septum) and the mandible (the lower jaw). The mandible is special — it is the only freely movable bone in the skull, meeting the temporal bone at the temporomandibular joint so you can chew and speak.

GroupSingle bonesPaired bones
Cranial (8)Frontal, occipital, sphenoid, ethmoidParietal (2), temporal (2)
Facial (14)Mandible, vomerMaxilla (2), zygomatic (2), nasal (2), lacrimal (2), palatine (2), inferior nasal concha (2)

Sutures, sinuses, and the infant skull

The cranial bones do not meet in smooth joints; they interlock along jagged, immovable seams called sutures. Four are worth naming: the coronal suture between the frontal and parietal bones, the sagittal suture running front-to-back between the two parietals, the lambdoid suture between the parietals and the occipital, and the squamous suture along the top of each temporal bone. In a newborn these seams have not yet closed; the gaps between them are the soft fontanelles ("soft spots") that let the skull flex during birth and expand as the brain grows. Several cranial and facial bones are also hollowed out by air-filled paranasal sinuses, which lighten the skull and add resonance to the voice.

The foramen magnum is the skull's front door. This large hole in the occipital bone is where the brainstem becomes the spinal cord. Its position at the very base of the skull — rather than at the rear — is a signature of upright, two-legged posture, balancing the head directly on top of the spine.

Section 3The vertebral column

Twenty-six bones, five regions

The vertebral column — the backbone — is a stack of 26 bones that runs from the base of the skull to the pelvis. It does three big jobs at once: it supports the head and trunk, it protects the spinal cord threading down its center, and it stays flexible enough to let you bend and twist. The individual bones are vertebrae, and they are grouped into five regions from top to bottom. A classic mnemonic ties the counts to mealtimes — breakfast at 7, lunch at 12, dinner at 5:

RegionBonesLocation & note
Cervical7 (C1–C7)The neck; C1 is the atlas, C2 the axis
Thoracic12 (T1–T12)The chest; each bears a pair of ribs
Lumbar5 (L1–L5)The lower back; largest, most weight-bearing
Sacral1 (5 fused)The sacrum; joins the hip bones
Coccygeal1 (4 fused)The coccyx, or tailbone

Counting the individual pieces, a child actually starts with 33 vertebrae; five of them fuse into the single sacrum and four into the coccyx, so the adult total settles at 26. Notice the pattern: the vertebrae grow steadily larger as you move down the column, because each lower bone must carry the weight of everything above it. The lumbar vertebrae, holding up the entire upper body, are the biggest and blockiest of all.

Discs, the cushions between

Between adjacent vertebrae (from C2 downward) sits an intervertebral disc — a pad with a tough fibrous ring, the anulus fibrosus, wrapped around a soft gel core, the nucleus pulposus. There are 23 of these discs, and they do double duty: they act as shock absorbers that cushion the jolt of every step, and they form slightly movable joints that let the spine flex. When the soft core pushes through a weakened ring — a herniated (“slipped”) disc — it can press on a nearby spinal nerve and send pain shooting down a limb.

Seven necks for almost every mammal. Nearly every mammal has exactly seven cervical vertebrae — a mouse, a human, and a giraffe all share the same count. The giraffe's neck is long not because it has more bones, but because each of its seven is enormously elongated.

  • The column is 26 bones in five regions: 7 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, plus the fused sacrum and coccyx (mnemonic 7–12–5).

  • Intervertebral discs cushion the spine and allow movement; a herniated disc can compress a spinal nerve.

Section 4A typical vertebra

The body: the weight-bearer

Vertebrae look different from region to region, but they all share one blueprint. The front of every vertebra is the body (or centrum) — a thick, roughly cylindrical block of bone that faces anteriorly and does the heavy lifting. Stacked one on top of another with the discs between them, the bodies form the load-bearing pillar that carries the weight of the head and trunk. This is why the bodies get progressively larger toward the lumbar spine: the lower you go, the more weight there is to hold up.

The arch: the shield

Behind the body is the vertebral arch, a bony ring that projects backward. It is built from two pedicles (the short walls that rise from the body) and two laminae (the roof plates that meet in the midline). The body in front and the arch behind enclose the vertebral foramen — the hole through the center of the bone. Stack all the vertebrae and their foramina line up into the continuous vertebral canal, the bony tunnel that shelters the spinal cord along its whole length. The arch, in other words, is the spine's version of the skull's cranium: pure protection.

The processes: the handles

Projecting from the arch are seven bony processes that serve as levers and joints. The single spinous process points straight back — these are the bumps you feel down the middle of your back. The two transverse processes stick out to the sides. Spinous and transverse processes are attachment points where muscles and ligaments grab hold. The remaining four are the paired superior and inferior articular processes, whose smooth facets form movable joints with the vertebrae directly above and below, linking the whole column together.

PartNumberJob
Body (centrum)1Bears weight; faces anteriorly
Pedicles + laminae2 + 2Form the vertebral arch
Vertebral foramen1Opening that transmits the spinal cord
Spinous process1Posterior; muscle/ligament attachment
Transverse processes2Lateral; muscle/ligament attachment
Articular processes4Form joints with adjacent vertebrae
Two foramina, two very different jobs. Don't confuse the vertebral foramen (the big central hole that the spinal cord runs through) with the intervertebral foramina (the small side openings between stacked vertebrae where individual spinal nerves branch out to the body). One protects the cord; the others are the exit doors for its nerves.

Section 5The thoracic cage

The sternum: the breastplate

The front of the chest is anchored by the sternum, or breastbone, a flat dagger-shaped bone with three parts. The broad manubrium sits on top and joins the collarbones. The long body forms the middle. The small xiphoid process is the pointed cartilage-tipped tail at the bottom. Where the manubrium meets the body there is a slight ridge, the sternal angle (angle of Louis), a landmark you can feel through the skin — it lines up with the second rib, giving clinicians a reliable starting point for counting ribs and placing a stethoscope.

Twelve pairs of ribs: true, false, and floating

Curving back from the sternum to the thoracic spine are 12 pairs of ribs. Every rib attaches to a thoracic vertebra behind, but they differ in how — or whether — they reach the sternum in front, and that is how we classify them. The true ribs (pairs 1–7) attach directly to the sternum, each by its own strip of costal cartilage. The false ribs (pairs 8–12) do not reach the sternum directly. Of these, pairs 8–10 join the costal cartilage of the rib just above them, while the last two — pairs 11 and 12 — have no anterior attachment at all and are called floating ribs, ending freely in the muscle of the back wall.

TypeRib pairsAnterior attachment
True ribs1–7Directly to the sternum by their own costal cartilage
False ribs8–10Indirectly — to the costal cartilage of the rib above
Floating ribs11–12None — no sternal attachment

A cage that breathes

The thoracic cage is not a solid box — it is a flexible basket that moves with every breath. When you inhale, muscles lift and swing the ribs upward and outward, enlarging the chest so the lungs can expand; when you exhale, the ribs drop back down. All the while the cage shields the heart, lungs, and great vessels beneath it, and it gives the muscles of breathing and of the upper body a framework to pull on. It is protection and machinery in a single structure.

Find the xiphoid before you compress. During CPR, the heel of the hand goes on the sternal body, not the xiphoid process. The small xiphoid can snap under pressure and injure the liver beneath it, so locating it first — and staying above it — is a safety step every rescuer learns.

Section 6Spinal curvatures

Four curves, two born and two learned

Seen from the side, a healthy spine is not straight — it makes a gentle S-shape of four curves that balance your weight over your hips and spring like a shock absorber with every step. Two of them are primary curves, present at birth and shaped like the curled-up posture of a fetus: the thoracic curve (chest) and the sacral curve (pelvis), both concave toward the front. The other two are secondary curves that develop after birth as a baby learns to hold up its head and then to walk: the cervical curve appears when an infant begins lifting its head, and the lumbar curve appears when the toddler stands and walks. Together the four curves let the column carry a heavy head and trunk without toppling.

CurveTypeAppears
CervicalSecondaryWhen an infant lifts its head
ThoracicPrimaryPresent at birth
LumbarSecondaryWhen a toddler stands and walks
SacralPrimaryPresent at birth

When curves go wrong

The curves are healthy in moderation, but when one becomes exaggerated or turns the wrong way it gets a clinical name. Kyphosis is an excessive thoracic curve — a rounded, hunched upper back, sometimes called “hunchback,” common with osteoporosis in older adults. Lordosis is an excessive lumbar curve — an exaggerated inward sway of the lower back, or “swayback.” Scoliosis is different from both: an abnormal sideways (lateral) curve of the spine, so that from behind the column bends into a C or S. Scoliosis is the one screened for in schoolchildren, because catching it early — while the skeleton is still growing — gives the best chance to correct it.

Kyphosis and lordosis run front-to-back; scoliosis runs side-to-side. A simple way to keep them apart: kyphosis and lordosis are exaggerations of the normal curves you already have (seen from the side), while scoliosis is an abnormal curve in a plane where the spine should look straight (seen from behind).

Key points
  • The spine has four curves: primary (thoracic, sacral, present at birth) and secondary (cervical, lumbar, learned with head-lifting and walking).
  • Kyphosis = excessive thoracic curve; lordosis = excessive lumbar curve — both front-to-back exaggerations.
  • Scoliosis is an abnormal lateral curve, screened for during childhood growth.

The chapter in one breath

  • The skeleton divides into the axial (80 bones — skull, spine, thoracic cage; protection and support) and the appendicular (126 bones — limbs and girdles; movement).
  • The skull is 22 bones: 8 cranial around the brain and 14 facial, with the mandible the only movable one and bones joined by immovable sutures.
  • The vertebral column is 26 bones in five regions — 7 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, plus fused sacrum and coccyx — cushioned by intervertebral discs.
  • Every vertebra has a weight-bearing body, a protective arch enclosing the vertebral foramen, and processes for muscles and joints.
  • The thoracic cage is the three-part sternum plus 12 rib pairs — true, false, and floating — guarding the heart and lungs and moving with each breath.
  • The spine's four curves (primary thoracic/sacral, secondary cervical/lumbar) form a shock-absorbing S; exaggerations are kyphosis, lordosis, and the lateral scoliosis.
  • Throughout, the axial skeleton's shape follows one theme: armor for the body's most vital organs.

ReferenceWords worth knowing

A quick reference for the terms in this chapter — skim it now, or circle back whenever a word trips you up.

Appendicular skeleton
The 126 bones of the limbs and the two girdles (shoulder and hip) that attach them; specialized for movement.
Atlas (C1)
The first cervical vertebra, which supports the skull and allows the head to nod “yes.”
Axial skeleton
The 80 bones along the body's central axis — skull, vertebral column, and thoracic cage — that protect and support.
Axis (C2)
The second cervical vertebra, whose upward peg lets the head rotate to say “no.”
Coccyx
The small tailbone at the base of the spine, formed by four fused vertebrae.
Costal cartilage
The strip of cartilage connecting a rib to the sternum, directly or by way of the rib above.
Cranial bones
The eight skull bones (frontal, occipital, sphenoid, ethmoid, and paired parietals and temporals) that enclose the brain.
Facial bones
The fourteen skull bones that shape the face and frame the eyes, nose, and mouth.
Floating ribs
Rib pairs 11 and 12, which attach only to the vertebrae behind and have no anterior connection.
Fontanelle
A soft membranous gap between the cranial bones of an infant, allowing the skull to flex and the brain to grow.
Foramen magnum
The large opening in the occipital bone through which the spinal cord joins the brain.
Intervertebral disc
A fibrocartilage pad between adjacent vertebral bodies that absorbs shock and permits movement.
Kyphosis
An excessive posterior (front-to-back) curvature of the thoracic spine, producing a hunched upper back.
Lordosis
An excessive anterior curvature of the lumbar spine, producing an exaggerated swayback.
Mandible
The lower jaw — the only freely movable bone of the skull.
Manubrium
The broad upper part of the sternum that articulates with the clavicles and the first ribs.
Sacrum
The triangular bone of five fused vertebrae that joins the vertebral column to the hip bones.
Scoliosis
An abnormal lateral (side-to-side) curvature of the spine.
Sternal angle
The palpable ridge where the manubrium meets the sternal body, aligned with the second rib.
Suture
An immovable interlocking joint between two cranial bones.
Vertebral foramen
The central opening of a vertebra, formed by the body and arch, through which the spinal cord passes.
Xiphoid process
The small pointed inferior tip of the sternum, an important landmark for CPR.

Check yourself

1. Sort these into axial or appendicular: the sternum, the femur (thigh bone), a rib, the shoulder blade, and a vertebra.
Axial: sternum, rib, vertebra. Appendicular: femur, shoulder blade. Axial bones lie on the midline and protect organs; the limbs and their girdles (like the scapula) are appendicular and built for movement.
2. The axial skeleton has 80 bones and the appendicular 126. What do these add up to, and what does each group specialize in?
206 bones total; axial = protection and support, appendicular = movement. The axial 80 form the central axis around the brain, cord, heart, and lungs; the appendicular 126 are the limbs and girdles that let you move.
3. How many cranial bones and how many facial bones make up the skull, and which four cranial bones are unpaired?
8 cranial + 14 facial = 22 bones; the single cranial bones are the frontal, occipital, sphenoid, and ethmoid. The parietals and temporals are the two paired cranial bones.
4. Which is the only freely movable bone of the skull, and what kind of joint holds the rest of the cranial bones together?
The mandible (lower jaw); the other cranial bones meet at immovable sutures. In infants the sutures are still open as soft spots called fontanelles.
5. Give the number of bones in each region of the vertebral column, top to bottom.
Cervical 7, thoracic 12, lumbar 5, sacrum 1 (5 fused), coccyx 1 (4 fused) — 26 total. The mealtime mnemonic 7–12–5 covers the three movable regions.
6. Why are the lumbar vertebrae the largest bones in the column?
Because they bear the most weight. Each vertebra carries everything above it, so the bodies grow progressively larger toward the bottom; the lumbar region supports the entire head and trunk.
7. Name the part of a vertebra that bears weight and the part that protects the spinal cord.
The body (centrum) bears weight; the vertebral arch protects the cord. Together the body and arch enclose the vertebral foramen, and the stacked foramina form the vertebral canal around the spinal cord.
8. What is the difference between the vertebral foramen and the intervertebral foramina?
The vertebral foramen is the central hole the spinal cord passes through; the intervertebral foramina are the side openings between vertebrae where spinal nerves exit. One holds the cord; the others are the exit doors for its nerves.
9. Name the three parts of the sternum from top to bottom.
Manubrium, body, xiphoid process. The sternal angle, where the manubrium meets the body, is a palpable landmark lined up with the second rib.
10. Classify rib pairs 1–7, 8–10, and 11–12 by how they attach in front.
1–7 are true ribs (direct to the sternum); 8–10 are false ribs (to the cartilage of the rib above); 11–12 are floating ribs (no anterior attachment). All 12 pairs attach to the thoracic vertebrae behind.
11. Which spinal curves are primary (present at birth) and which are secondary (developing later), and what triggers each secondary curve?
Primary: thoracic and sacral. Secondary: cervical (lifting the head) and lumbar (standing and walking). The four curves together form the spine's shock-absorbing S-shape.
12. Define kyphosis, lordosis, and scoliosis, and say which one is a sideways curve.
Kyphosis = excessive thoracic (hunchback) curve; lordosis = excessive lumbar (swayback) curve; scoliosis = an abnormal lateral, side-to-side curve. Kyphosis and lordosis exaggerate front-to-back curves; scoliosis is the lateral one screened for in growing children.
