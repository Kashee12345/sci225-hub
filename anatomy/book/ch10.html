Muscle Tissue

Muscle is the tissue that turns a decision into a movement. Every heartbeat, every blink, every stride, every push of food through your gut is a muscle cell doing the one thing muscle does supremely well — shortening on command. In this chapter we take a single skeletal muscle apart, layer by layer, until we reach the microscopic engine that actually pulls: the sarcomere. Then we watch that engine run — how a nerve signal crosses into the muscle, how calcium throws the switch, how filaments slide past one another, and how the body dials the force up from a delicate flicker to an all-out heave. By the end you will understand contraction not as magic but as a beautifully ordered chain of events you can follow from thought to motion.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Compare the three types of muscle tissue — skeletal, cardiac, and smooth — by structure, control, and location.
  • Trace the connective-tissue wrappings of a skeletal muscle from epimysium down to a single fiber, and name the parts inside that fiber.
  • Diagram a sarcomere and identify its bands, zones, and lines, and explain why skeletal muscle looks striped.
  • Explain the sliding filament theory and the steps of the cross-bridge cycle, including the roles of ATP and calcium.
  • Describe the neuromuscular junction and follow a signal from motor neuron to muscle action potential.
  • Define a motor unit and explain how recruitment and summation grade muscle tension from a twitch to tetanus.
  • Distinguish the three skeletal muscle fiber types and match each to the kind of work it does best.

Section 1How a muscle is built

Three kinds of muscle tissue

Before we open up one muscle, it helps to know that the body builds muscle in three different flavors, each tuned to a different job. Skeletal muscle attaches to bones and moves the body; it is under voluntary control, and under the microscope its cells are long, striped (striated), and packed with many nuclei. Cardiac muscle is found only in the heart wall; it is striated too, but it is involuntary, its cells are branched with usually a single nucleus, and they are joined end-to-end by intercalated discs that let the whole heart contract as one. Smooth muscle lines the walls of hollow organs — blood vessels, gut, bladder, airways — where it squeezes slowly and tirelessly; it is involuntary, has no stripes, and its cells are tapered with one central nucleus. This chapter concentrates on skeletal muscle, because it shows every principle most clearly, but keep the other two in mind — the contraction machinery is largely shared.

FeatureSkeletalCardiacSmooth
LocationAttached to bonesHeart wall onlyWalls of hollow organs & vessels
StriationsStriatedStriatedNot striated

Bundles inside bundles

A whole skeletal muscle — your biceps, say — is not one solid block but a nested set of bundles, each wrapped in its own sheet of connective tissue. Working from the outside in: the entire muscle is sheathed in the epimysium; inside that, fibers are gathered into bundles called fascicles, each wrapped by perimysium; and inside each fascicle, every individual muscle cell (a muscle fiber) is wrapped in a delicate endomysium. These three sheaths are continuous with one another and, at the ends of the muscle, they merge and extend beyond it as a tendon (a cord) or an aponeurosis (a flat sheet) that anchors the muscle to bone. This arrangement is not just tidy packaging: the connective tissue carries the blood vessels and nerves in to each fiber, and it transmits the pull of every cell to the tendon so the whole muscle moves as one.

“Epi, peri, endo” = outside, around, within. The same Greek prefixes label the wrappings of a nerve (epineurium, perineurium, endoneurium). Learn the pattern once and it works for both — epi covers the whole organ, peri surrounds each bundle, endo hugs each individual cell.

Inside a single fiber

Zoom into one muscle fiber and you find a cell built for pulling. Its plasma membrane is called the sarcolemma, and its cytoplasm the sarcoplasm (the prefix sarco- means “flesh”). The sarcolemma dives deep into the cell as tunnels called transverse (T) tubules, which carry the electrical signal into the fiber’s core. Wrapped around the working machinery is a specialized smooth endoplasmic reticulum, the sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR), which stores and releases the calcium that triggers contraction. Filling most of the cell are hundreds of long threads called myofibrils, and each myofibril is a chain of the contractile units — the sarcomeres — made of myofilaments of the proteins actin and myosin. Because a muscle fiber forms during development by many cells fusing together, it holds many nuclei pushed to the edge just under the sarcolemma, and a reserve of satellite cells sits nearby to repair damage.

Section 2The sarcomere

Stripes with a purpose

The stripes that give skeletal and cardiac muscle their name are not decoration — they are the visible signature of astonishingly ordered machinery. Inside every myofibril, the contractile proteins are lined up in identical repeating segments, and each segment is a sarcomere, the functional unit of contraction. One sarcomere runs from one Z-disc to the next Z-disc. When millions of these little units all shorten at once, in register, the whole muscle shortens — and it is their alternating dense and light regions, stacked side by side across the fiber, that our eyes read as striations.

The bands, zones, and lines

A sarcomere is built from two kinds of filament. The thick filaments are made of myosin, each with a hinged head that sticks out; the thin filaments are made of actin. Where the two overlap, the sarcomere looks dark; where only one is present, it looks lighter. Learning the map of bands is worth the effort, because in the next section you will watch these regions change size as the muscle contracts.

RegionWhat it containsAppearance
A bandThe full length of the thick filaments (with overlap at its edges)Dark; does not change length in contraction
I bandThin filaments only, spanning two adjacent sarcomeresLight; narrows during contraction
H zoneThick filaments only, center of the A band (no overlap)Lighter center; narrows during contraction
Z-disc (Z-line)Anchors the thin filaments; the border of the sarcomereDark line; Z-discs are pulled together
M-lineAnchors the thick filaments at the sarcomere’s centerFine central line

Thick, thin, and the switch proteins

The thin filament is more than plain actin. Wrapped along it lies a ropelike protein, tropomyosin, that in a resting muscle physically covers the spots where myosin would attach — the myosin-binding sites. Holding tropomyosin in place is a three-part protein complex, troponin, one piece of which (troponin C) is a calcium receptor. This is the molecular switch: when calcium binds troponin, the whole complex shifts, tropomyosin slides aside, and the binding sites open. Two more proteins keep the sarcomere honest: giant, springy titin runs from Z-disc to M-line, keeping the thick filament centered and giving muscle its passive recoil, and nebulin runs alongside the thin filament, setting its length. Structure, once again, is built exactly for the job.

A is for dark, I is for light. The bands are named for how they bend polarized light — the A band is Anisotropic (dark) and the I band is Isotropic (light). A quick mnemonic: the letters with a straight vertical stroke, A and dark, go together; the thin, light I band is the pale one that shrinks when you flex.

Section 3The sliding filament theory

Sliding, not shrinking

Here is the single most important idea in this chapter, and it surprises almost everyone the first time: the filaments do not get shorter. When a muscle contracts, the thick and thin filaments slide past one another, increasing their overlap and dragging the Z-discs closer together. Each filament keeps its exact length; the sarcomere is what shortens. That is why, in the reference figure, the A band (the length of the thick filament) never changes, while the I band and H zone shrink and the Z-discs march inward. Multiply that tiny slide across millions of sarcomeres in series and the whole muscle noticeably shortens and pulls on its tendon.

The cross-bridge cycle

The sliding is powered by the myosin heads, which act as tiny oars. Each head reaches out, grabs actin to form a cross-bridge, and swivels — a power stroke — that ratchets the thin filament a few nanometers toward the center. Then it lets go, re-cocks, and grabs again, farther along. The cycle repeats many times per second, and it runs on ATP:

StepWhat happensRole of ATP / Ca²⁺
1. Cross-bridge formsThe energized myosin head binds an exposed site on actinRequires Ca²⁺ to have opened the site
2. Power strokeThe head pivots, sliding the thin filament inward; ADP & P₃ are releasedUses energy stored from a previous ATP
3. DetachmentA fresh ATP binds the myosin head, which releases from actinATP binding breaks the cross-bridge
4. Re-cockingATP is split to ADP + P₃, re-energizing and re-setting the headATP hydrolysis re-cocks the head

Notice the twin jobs of ATP: it is needed to release the head (step 3) and to re-cock it (step 4). This has a memorable consequence — in death, when ATP production stops, myosin heads bind actin and can no longer let go, and the body stiffens into rigor mortis.

Calcium throws the switch: excitation–contraction coupling

Cross-bridges can only form if the binding sites are uncovered, and that is calcium’s job. The link between an electrical signal and a mechanical pull is called excitation–contraction coupling. A muscle action potential races along the sarcolemma and dives down the T-tubules into the fiber. That voltage change tells the sarcoplasmic reticulum to dump its stored Ca²⁺ into the sarcoplasm. Calcium binds troponin, troponin tugs tropomyosin off the actin, the binding sites open, and cross-bridging — and sliding — begins. Relaxation is just the reverse: when the signal stops, a pump called SERCA hauls Ca²⁺ back into the SR, tropomyosin re-covers the sites, cross-bridges can no longer form, and the muscle goes slack. No calcium, no contraction.

Rigor mortis is a chemistry lesson. Stiffening begins a few hours after death because cross-bridges are locked without the ATP needed to release them, and Ca²⁺ leaks freely once the SR’s pumps fall silent. It fades a day or two later only as the muscle proteins themselves break down — a vivid reminder that both binding and release cost energy.

Key points
  • In the sliding filament theory, filaments keep their length; the sarcomere shortens as thick and thin filaments overlap more.
  • The cross-bridge cycle — bind, power stroke, detach, re-cock — runs on ATP, which is needed to release and to re-energize the myosin head.
  • Excitation–contraction coupling: an action potential → T-tubules → SR releases Ca²⁺ → troponin/tropomyosin uncover binding sites; SERCA reclaims calcium to relax.

Section 4The neuromuscular junction

From nerve to muscle

A skeletal muscle fiber will not contract on its own — it waits for the command of a nerve. That command is delivered at a specialized synapse called the neuromuscular junction (NMJ). A motor neuron reaches out from the spinal cord, and its axon terminal comes to rest just above a folded patch of sarcolemma called the motor end plate. The two never touch; a tiny gap, the synaptic cleft, separates them. To bridge that gap, the neuron uses a chemical messenger — the neurotransmitter acetylcholine (ACh).

The handoff, step by step

The transfer of the signal from nerve to muscle is one of the most reliable events in physiology — a single nerve impulse essentially always triggers a muscle impulse. Follow the chain:

StepEvent
1A nerve action potential arrives at the axon terminal.
2Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open; calcium rushes into the terminal.
3Calcium triggers vesicles to release acetylcholine into the synaptic cleft.
4ACh crosses the cleft and binds nicotinic receptors on the motor end plate.
5Receptor channels open; Na⁺ floods in, creating an end-plate potential.
6The depolarization sets off a muscle action potential that spreads along the sarcolemma and down the T-tubules — and contraction follows.

Notice how this dovetails with the last section: step 6 is exactly the muscle action potential that starts excitation–contraction coupling. The NMJ is the doorway; the sliding filament is the room it opens onto.

Switching the signal off — and where it breaks

A signal that never ended would leave the muscle locked in contraction, so the cleft contains an enzyme, acetylcholinesterase (AChE), that chops ACh apart within milliseconds. This tidy on/off system is also where many drugs and diseases act, which makes the NMJ one of the highest-yield spots in clinical medicine. In myasthenia gravis, the immune system destroys ACh receptors, so muscles fatigue and weaken. Curare (an arrow poison) blocks the receptors, causing paralysis. Organophosphate pesticides and nerve agents inhibit AChE, so ACh piles up and muscles seize. Botulinum toxin (Botox) blocks ACh release, relaxing muscle — useful for spasms and wrinkles.

Follow the transmitter to predict the disease. Anything that removes ACh signaling (myasthenia gravis, curare, botulinum) causes weakness or paralysis; anything that lets ACh accumulate (AChE inhibitors, nerve agents) causes uncontrolled contraction and spasm. Knowing which side of the switch a toxin hits tells you the symptoms.

Section 5Motor units & muscle tension

The motor unit

A single motor neuron rarely commands just one muscle fiber. Instead it branches and connects to a group of fibers, and the neuron together with all the fibers it controls is a motor unit. When that neuron fires, every fiber in its unit contracts at once — they are an all-or-nothing team. The size of the unit sets how finely a muscle can be controlled. Muscles that need delicacy, like those moving the eye or the fingers, have small motor units (a neuron to only a handful of fibers). Muscles built for power, like the quadriceps or the calf, have large motor units (a neuron to hundreds of fibers). Small units mean fine control; large units mean strong, coarse force.

Twitch, summation, tetanus

One brief stimulus produces a single, quick contraction-and-relaxation called a twitch, which has three phases: a latent period (the hidden delay while calcium is released), a contraction phase, and a relaxation phase. A twitch is a flick, not a useful pull. To get smooth, strong movement, the nervous system fires the motor neuron again before the fiber fully relaxes, so the second contraction stacks on top of the first. This stacking is wave (temporal) summation. Fire faster still and the individual twitches blur into a partly smoothed unfused (incomplete) tetanus, and faster yet into a steady, maximal, perfectly smooth fused (complete) tetanus. Nearly all normal movements use tetanus, not single twitches.

ResponseStimulus patternResult
TwitchOne stimulusA single brief contraction and full relaxation
Wave summationA second stimulus before relaxation endsThe second contraction adds to the first — more tension
Unfused tetanusRapid, repeated stimuliStrong, sustained pull with slight wobbles
Fused tetanusVery high-frequency stimuliMaximal, perfectly smooth, sustained contraction

Two dials for force: recruitment and rate

The body grades how hard a muscle pulls using two controls at once. The first is recruitment — switching on more motor units. Under the size principle, small, weak, fatigue-resistant units are recruited first, and larger, more powerful ones are added only as more force is needed, so movements start smooth and controllable. The second dial is rate coding — firing each active unit faster to climb the summation-to-tetanus ladder above. Together, recruitment and rate let you lift a feather or a barbell with the same muscle. Even at rest, a few units are always active in rotation, keeping muscles firm and ready — this steady low-level tension is muscle tone.

Small first, for a reason. The size principle isn’t arbitrary — recruiting the smallest, most fatigue-resistant motor units first means everyday, low-force tasks are handled by the units that can keep going all day, while the big, quickly-tiring power units are held in reserve for when you truly need them.

Section 6The three fiber types

Speed versus stamina

Not every skeletal muscle fiber is the same. They come in three types that trade speed against endurance, because no single design can be both maximally fast and maximally tireless. Slow oxidative (Type I) fibers contract slowly and generate modest force, but they resist fatigue for hours — the endurance specialists. Fast glycolytic (Type IIx) fibers contract quickly and powerfully but tire within minutes — the sprint-and-lift specialists. In between sit fast oxidative (Type IIa) fibers, which are fast and reasonably fatigue-resistant — a versatile middle ground.

Aerobic versus anaerobic engines

The difference comes down to how each fiber makes its ATP. Oxidative fibers run on aerobic respiration: they are packed with mitochondria, richly supplied with capillaries, and loaded with myoglobin — an oxygen-binding pigment that makes them look red. That red, oxygen-fed machinery is slow but sustainable. Glycolytic fibers rely on anaerobic glycolysis: they have few mitochondria, little myoglobin (so they look pale), and large glycogen stores for quick bursts. They deliver explosive power but flood with lactate and fatigue fast.

Everybody is a mixture — and a nod to the other two muscles

Almost every real muscle contains all three fiber types, blended in a ratio set partly by your genes and partly by how you train. Endurance training coaxes fibers toward the oxidative, fatigue-resistant end; strength and sprint training push toward larger, more powerful fibers. It is why a marathoner’s calves and a sprinter’s thighs are built for different feats. And remember the two muscle types we set aside at the start: cardiac muscle behaves like a permanently endurance-tuned, self-rhythmic tissue joined by intercalated discs so the heart never truly rests, while smooth muscle has no sarcomeres at all and can hold tension for long stretches with very little energy — a “latch” state perfect for keeping a blood vessel or a sphincter squeezed shut all day.

White meat, dark meat. A chicken’s breast is pale because flight muscles fire in fast, glycolytic bursts; its legs are dark because standing and walking all day demand red, myoglobin-rich oxidative fibers. The color on your dinner plate is a fiber-type lesson.

Key points
  • Three fiber types trade speed for stamina: slow oxidative (I) = endurance; fast glycolytic (IIx) = power; fast oxidative (IIa) = in between.
  • Oxidative fibers are red (many mitochondria, myoglobin, capillaries) and aerobic; glycolytic fibers are pale, anaerobic, powerful, and quick to fatigue.

The chapter in one breath

  • Muscle comes in three types — skeletal (voluntary, striated), cardiac (involuntary, striated, self-rhythmic), and smooth (involuntary, unstriated) — sharing the same basic contraction machinery.
  • A skeletal muscle is bundles within bundles wrapped in epimysium, perimysium, and endomysium, all continuous with the tendon; inside each fiber are the sarcolemma, T-tubules, sarcoplasmic reticulum, and myofibrils.
  • The sarcomere (Z-disc to Z-disc) is the functional unit; overlapping thick (myosin) and thin (actin) filaments create the striations, guarded by tropomyosin and troponin.
  • In the sliding filament theory, filaments keep their length while the sarcomere shortens; the cross-bridge cycle runs on ATP, and Ca²⁺ released by the SR uncovers the binding sites.
  • The neuromuscular junction hands the signal from nerve to muscle using acetylcholine, then clears it with acetylcholinesterase; toxins here cause weakness or spasm.
  • A motor unit is one neuron and its fibers; tension is graded from a twitch to tetanus by recruitment and rate coding.
  • Three fiber types trade speed for endurance, and most muscles blend all three in a ratio shaped by genes and training.

ReferenceWords worth knowing

A quick reference for the terms in this chapter — skim it now, or circle back whenever a word trips you up.

A band
The dark region of a sarcomere spanning the full length of the thick filaments; it does not change length during contraction.
Acetylcholine (ACh)
The neurotransmitter released by a motor neuron at the neuromuscular junction to trigger a muscle action potential.
Actin
The protein of the thin filament, carrying the myosin-binding sites that cross-bridges attach to.
Cross-bridge
The connection formed when a myosin head binds an exposed site on actin; its cycle of binding, power stroke, and release drives filament sliding.
Endomysium
The delicate connective-tissue sheath wrapping each individual muscle fiber.
Epimysium
The connective-tissue sheath surrounding an entire skeletal muscle.
Excitation–contraction coupling
The sequence linking a muscle action potential to contraction: the signal travels down T-tubules and triggers Ca²⁺ release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum.
Fascicle
A bundle of muscle fibers wrapped by perimysium.
Motor unit
A single motor neuron together with all the muscle fibers it innervates; they contract as one.
Myofibril
A long, threadlike organelle inside a muscle fiber made of a chain of sarcomeres.
Myosin
The protein of the thick filament, whose hinged heads form cross-bridges and perform the power stroke.
Neuromuscular junction
The synapse between a motor neuron and a skeletal muscle fiber where acetylcholine passes the signal.
Recruitment
Increasing muscle force by activating more motor units, smallest and most fatigue-resistant first (the size principle).
Sarcolemma
The plasma membrane of a muscle fiber; its inward tunnels are the T-tubules.
Sarcomere
The functional contractile unit of striated muscle, running from one Z-disc to the next.
Sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR)
The specialized smooth ER of a muscle fiber that stores and releases the Ca²⁺ that triggers contraction.
Sliding filament theory
The model that muscle shortens because thick and thin filaments slide past one another, not because the filaments themselves shorten.
Tetanus
A smooth, sustained, maximal contraction produced when stimuli arrive too fast for the fiber to relax between them.
Tropomyosin
The thin-filament protein that covers the myosin-binding sites at rest and slides aside when calcium binds troponin.
Troponin
The thin-filament protein complex that binds Ca²⁺ and moves tropomyosin to expose the binding sites.
Twitch
A single brief contraction and relaxation of a muscle fiber in response to one stimulus, with latent, contraction, and relaxation phases.

Check yourself

1. Name the three types of muscle tissue and, for each, state whether it is voluntary or involuntary and whether it is striated.
Skeletal — voluntary, striated; cardiac — involuntary, striated; smooth — involuntary, not striated. Skeletal moves the skeleton, cardiac is found only in the heart wall (with intercalated discs), and smooth lines hollow organs and vessels.
2. Working from the outside in, name the three connective-tissue wrappings of a skeletal muscle and what each surrounds.
Epimysium (the whole muscle) → perimysium (each fascicle) → endomysium (each fiber). The three sheaths are continuous and merge at the muscle’s ends to form the tendon that anchors it to bone.
3. What are the sarcolemma, the sarcoplasmic reticulum, and the T-tubules, and what does each contribute to contraction?
The sarcolemma is the fiber’s membrane, the T-tubules are its inward tunnels, and the SR stores calcium. The action potential runs along the sarcolemma and down the T-tubules, prompting the SR to release Ca²⁺ that triggers the filaments to slide.
4. A sarcomere is defined as the region between two of which structures, and why is it called the “functional unit” of muscle?
Between two Z-discs; it is the smallest unit that actually performs contraction. When millions of sarcomeres shorten in series and in register, the whole muscle shortens.
5. During contraction, which sarcomere regions get narrower and which stays the same length? What does that tell you?
The I band and H zone narrow; the A band stays the same. Because the A band equals the length of the thick filaments, its constancy proves the filaments slide rather than shorten.
6. What do tropomyosin and troponin do at rest, and what changes when calcium arrives?
At rest, tropomyosin covers the myosin-binding sites on actin, held by troponin. When Ca²⁺ binds troponin, the complex shifts, tropomyosin slides aside, the binding sites open, and cross-bridges can form.
7. State the two distinct jobs ATP performs in the cross-bridge cycle.
ATP binding detaches the myosin head from actin; ATP hydrolysis then re-cocks (re-energizes) the head. Both release and re-setting cost energy — which is why a body with no ATP after death stiffens into rigor mortis.
8. Put the events of excitation–contraction coupling in order: Ca²⁺ binds troponin; SR releases calcium; action potential travels down T-tubules; binding sites open.
Action potential down T-tubules → SR releases Ca²⁺ → Ca²⁺ binds troponin → binding sites open. To relax, the SERCA pump returns calcium to the SR, tropomyosin re-covers the sites, and cross-bridges stop forming.
9. At the neuromuscular junction, which neurotransmitter is released, which ion enters the muscle to depolarize the end plate, and which enzyme ends the signal?
Acetylcholine is released; Na⁺ enters the muscle; acetylcholinesterase breaks down the ACh. ACh binds nicotinic receptors on the motor end plate, letting Na⁺ in to create an end-plate potential and then a muscle action potential.
10. Myasthenia gravis destroys ACh receptors, while a nerve agent blocks acetylcholinesterase. Predict the effect of each on the muscle, and explain the difference.
Myasthenia gravis → weakness/fatigue; a nerve agent → uncontrolled contraction and spasm. Removing ACh signaling weakens the muscle; preventing ACh breakdown lets it pile up and over-stimulate the fiber.
11. Define a motor unit, and explain why the muscles that move the eye have much smaller motor units than the quadriceps.
A motor unit is one motor neuron plus all the fibers it innervates. Small units (few fibers per neuron) allow the precise, fine control the eye needs, while large units in the quadriceps deliver powerful but coarse force.
12. How does a muscle progress from a single twitch to a smooth, forceful tetanus, and which two mechanisms grade the overall force a whole muscle produces?
Faster, repeated stimuli cause wave summation that builds into tetanus; force is graded by recruitment and rate coding. Recruitment adds more motor units (smallest first, the size principle) and rate coding fires each unit faster.
