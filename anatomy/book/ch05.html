Anatomy & Physiology · Chapter 5

The Integumentary System

Spread flat, your skin would cover about two square meters and weigh more than any other organ you own — and yet we rarely think of it as an organ at all. This chapter changes that. You will build the skin from the top down, layer by layer, then follow a single cell on its month-long journey from a living factory floor to a dead flake on the surface. You will learn where skin color comes from, meet the hair, nails, and glands that grow out of the skin, and see the five quiet jobs your covering does every second. We finish where the clinic begins — with burns and skin cancer, the two ways this remarkable barrier fails.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Name the two true layers of the skin — the epidermis and dermis — and the hypodermis beneath, and describe the tissue and job of each.
  • List the five epidermal strata in order and trace a keratinocyte's keratinization journey from birth to shedding.
  • Explain how melanocytes and melanin produce skin color and shield cell DNA from ultraviolet light.
  • Describe the structure of hair, nails, and the sebaceous and sweat glands, and state what each contributes.
  • Summarize the skin's five functions: protection, thermoregulation, sensation, vitamin D synthesis, and excretion.
  • Classify burns by depth, estimate burned area with the rule of nines, and distinguish the three skin cancers.
  • Apply the ABCDE warning signs to tell a harmless mole from a possible melanoma.

Section 1Layers of the skin

An organ you can see

The integumentary system is the skin plus everything that grows out of it — hair, nails, and glands. The word integument simply means "a covering," and that is the plainest way to think of the skin: it is the wrapper that separates the wet, carefully controlled world inside you from the dry, dangerous world outside. But calling it a wrapper undersells it. The skin is a true organ, built of every tissue type, and it is the largest organ in the body by both weight and surface area. Everything in this chapter hangs on one anatomical fact: the skin is made of two layers stacked on a third supporting layer that, strictly speaking, is not skin at all.

Two layers of skin, one layer beneath

Read the skin from the surface down and you pass through three named layers. The top two are the skin proper; the third anchors it to the body.

LayerTissueWhat lives there / what it does
EpidermisKeratinized stratified squamous epitheliumThe tough, waterproof outer sheet; no blood vessels — it is fed by diffusion from below
DermisDense irregular connective tissueThe living core — blood vessels, nerves, hair follicles, glands, and the collagen that gives skin its strength
Hypodermis (subcutaneous)Areolar and adipose tissueNot part of the skin; anchors skin to muscle and bone, insulates, and stores energy

The epidermis is pure epithelium and, importantly, is avascular — it contains no blood vessels of its own. Its living cells survive on oxygen and nutrients that seep up from the dermis below, which is why the outer epidermis, cut off from that supply, is entirely dead. The dermis is where the action is: a dense feltwork of collagen and elastic fibers packed with the blood vessels, sensory nerves, hair follicles, and glands that make skin do its jobs. It has two sublayers — a thin, bumpy papillary layer on top (its dermal papillae create your fingerprints) and a thick reticular layer below that carries most of the collagen. Beneath the dermis, the hypodermis (also called the subcutaneous layer or superficial fascia) is mostly fat; it pads and insulates the body and tethers the skin loosely to the structures underneath.

The epidermis eats by diffusion. Because it has no blood supply of its own, every nutrient the epidermis uses must travel up from dermal capillaries. That single fact explains why a superficial scrape doesn't bleed, why the surface cells are dead, and why the dermis — not the epidermis — bleeds and hurts when you're cut.

Thick skin and thin skin

Not all skin is the same thickness. Thick skin covers the palms of the hands and the soles of the feet — the surfaces that take the most abrasion — and it carries all five epidermal layers, including an extra clear one. Thin skin covers the rest of the body; it is more delicate, has only four epidermal layers, and is the only skin that grows hair. The names refer to the epidermis, not to total skin thickness, which is a common point of confusion.

Key points
  • The integumentary system = skin + hair, nails, and glands; the skin is the body's largest organ.
  • The skin has two true layers: the epithelial epidermis (avascular) over the connective-tissue dermis (vascular, holds the structures).
  • The hypodermis beneath is fat and areolar tissue — not skin — and anchors and insulates.
  • Thick skin (palms, soles) has five epidermal strata; thin skin has four and bears hair.

Section 2The five epidermal strata

A conveyor belt of cells

The epidermis is a factory that never stops. Its main cell, the keratinocyte, is born at the very bottom, gets pushed steadily upward as new cells form beneath it, fills with a tough protein called keratin, dies along the way, and finally flakes off the top. The whole trip takes about four weeks — roughly 25 to 45 days — which means the outermost surface of you is completely replaced every month or so. This slow upward march is called keratinization, and it is the single most important process in the epidermis.

The five layers, bottom to top

In thick skin the keratinocyte passes through five named strata on its way up. Learn them in order from the deepest (where cells are born) to the surface (where they are shed):

Stratum (deep → superficial)NicknameWhat happens here
Stratum basaleThe nurseryA single row of dividing stem cells; new keratinocytes are born here by mitosis. Also home to melanocytes and touch-sensing Merkel cells.
Stratum spinosumThe spiny layerSeveral rows of keratinocytes held together by strong desmosomes (which give a spiny look). Immune dendritic cells patrol here.
Stratum granulosumThe dying layerCells fill with granules of keratin and a waterproofing lipid; nuclei break down and the cells begin to die.
Stratum lucidumThe clear layerA thin, translucent band of dead cells — found only in thick skin (palms and soles).
Stratum corneumThe horny layer15–30 layers of flat, dead, fully keratinized cells — the tough, waterproof barrier you actually touch.

A memory hook helps: reading from the base up, Basale, Spinosum, Granulosum, Lucidum, Corneum — "Bad Students Get Lousy Classes." The bottom two strata (basale and spinosum) are living and are sometimes grouped as the stratum germinativum, the growth zone. From the granulosum up, the cells are dying or dead. By the time a cell reaches the stratum corneum it is nothing but a flattened bag of keratin, glued to its neighbors and coated in lipid — and that dead, waterproof crust is exactly what makes the skin such an effective barrier. We shed roughly a million of these cells every day.

Dead cells do the hard work. It feels backward, but the layer that protects you best is the one made entirely of dead cells. The stratum corneum's keratin-packed corpses, sealed with lipid, are what keep water in and microbes out. Much of household dust is shed corneum.

Key points
  • Keratinocytes are born in the stratum basale, pushed upward, and shed from the surface in about a month — the process of keratinization.
  • Order deep to superficial: basale → spinosum → granulosum → lucidum → corneum ("Bad Students Get Lousy Classes").
  • The stratum lucidum appears only in thick skin; the stratum corneum is the dead, keratinized barrier you touch.

Section 3Skin color & melanin

Three pigments, one dominant player

The color of skin comes from three pigments blended together. Melanin is the main one — a brown-to-black (or red-to-yellow) pigment made in the epidermis. Carotene is a yellow-orange pigment that comes from the diet and collects in the stratum corneum and the fatty hypodermis. And hemoglobin, the red pigment of blood, shows through where the epidermis is thin, giving lighter skin its pink flush. Of the three, melanin is by far the most important, and it is the one that varies most from person to person.

Melanocytes hand off their pigment

Melanin is made by a specialized cell called the melanocyte, which sits down in the stratum basale. Here is the surprising part: melanocytes do not keep their pigment. They package melanin into little sacs called melanosomes and hand them off to the surrounding keratinocytes. Once inside a keratinocyte, the melanin migrates to the sunny side of the cell and forms a little cap over the top of the nucleus — like a tiny umbrella held over the cell's DNA. There it absorbs incoming ultraviolet (UV) radiation before it can reach and damage the genetic material. This is the whole point of skin pigment: melanin is sunscreen your cells make for themselves.

Everyone has about the same number of melanocytes. Differences in skin color come almost entirely from how much melanin those cells make and what kind — not from how many melanocytes you have. A tan is simply melanocytes ramping up production after UV exposure, a repair-and-defend response to DNA-damaging light.

When pigment goes wrong — and reading skin color in the clinic

Two inherited conditions show what happens at the extremes. In albinism, melanocytes are present but cannot make melanin, so the skin, hair, and eyes have little or no pigment and burn easily. In vitiligo, the immune system destroys melanocytes in patches, leaving pale blotches of skin. Beyond pigment disorders, skin color is one of the fastest bedside signs of illness, because it reflects what is happening in the blood underneath.

Color changeAppearanceWhat it usually means
CyanosisBluish skin, lips, and nail bedsPoorly oxygenated blood (low O₂)
PallorPale, blanched skinLow blood flow or anemia (shock, fear, cold)
ErythemaRednessIncreased blood flow — fever, inflammation, embarrassment, sunburn
JaundiceYellow skin and scleraeBilirubin buildup — usually a liver problem
BronzingGolden-bronze toneAdrenal insufficiency (Addison disease)

Key points
  • Skin color blends three pigments: melanin (main), carotene, and blood hemoglobin.
  • Melanocytes in the stratum basale make melanin and hand it to keratinocytes, where it caps the nucleus and absorbs UV to protect DNA.
  • Everyone has similar melanocyte numbers; color differences come from melanin amount and type. A tan is a UV defense response.
  • Skin color reads the blood: cyanosis, pallor, erythema, jaundice, bronzing each signal a specific problem.

Section 4Hair, nails & glands

Hair: keratin threads with a living root

Every accessory structure of the skin is born from the epidermis but tucked down into the dermis, where the blood supply is. Hair is a flexible strand of dead, hard keratin. The part you see above the skin is the shaft; the part below is the root, sheathed in a tube called the hair follicle. At the very bottom the follicle swells into a bulb, and inside it a knot of connective tissue called the papilla delivers the blood vessels that feed growth. Just above the papilla, the matrix is the only living, dividing part of a hair — which is why a haircut doesn't hurt but a plucked hair does. Attached to each follicle is a tiny arrector pili muscle; when it contracts (from cold or fear) it pulls the hair upright and dimples the skin into "goosebumps."

Nails: a window over the fingertip

A nail is a plate of hard keratin that protects the tip of each finger and toe and gives us a tool for scratching and grasping. The visible nail body sits on the nail bed; the whitish crescent at its base, the lunula, marks the underlying nail matrix, which is where the nail actually grows. The cuticle (eponychium) is the fold of skin that seals the base against dirt and microbes. Nails are a genuinely useful clinical window: they turn blue with poor oxygenation, become curved and clubbed in chronic heart or lung disease, and spoon inward in iron-deficiency anemia.

Glands: oil and sweat

The skin holds two great families of glands. Sebaceous (oil) glands usually empty into a hair follicle and secrete an oily mixture called sebum that lubricates the hair and skin and slows bacterial growth. When a follicle clogs with sebum and dead cells, the result is acne. Sweat (sudoriferous) glands come in two types worth separating carefully:

GlandWhere / what it secretesJob
Sebaceous (oil)Into hair follicles; oily sebumLubricates skin and hair; mildly antibacterial
Eccrine (merocrine) sweatAll over the body, onto the surface; watery sweatCools the body by evaporation — the main thermoregulation gland
Apocrine sweatArmpits and groin, into hair follicles; thicker, protein-rich sweatActivates at puberty; feeds skin bacteria that cause body odor
Ceruminous & mammaryEar canal / breastModified sweat glands: earwax and milk
Eccrine cools, apocrine smells. The sweat that cools you on a hot day is watery eccrine sweat, found nearly everywhere on the body. The sweat behind body odor is thicker apocrine sweat from the armpits and groin — and it doesn't smell until skin bacteria break it down.

Key points
  • Hair is dead keratin; it grows only from the living matrix at the base, fed by the papilla, and stands up when the arrector pili contracts.
  • Nails are keratin plates that grow from the nail matrix under the lunula; their color and shape reveal systemic disease.
  • Sebaceous glands make oily sebum; eccrine sweat glands cool the body, while apocrine glands (armpit, groin) cause body odor after bacteria act on their sweat.

Section 5What the skin does

One organ, five jobs

It is tempting to think the skin just covers you, but covering is only the first of five real jobs it performs every moment. Hold onto all five — they come up again and again in later chapters.

FunctionHow the skin does it
1. ProtectionA physical barrier (keratin, tight cells), a chemical one (the acidic "acid mantle" of sweat and sebum), and a biological one (immune dendritic cells that catch invaders)
2. ThermoregulationTo cool: sweat evaporates and dermal blood vessels dilate to dump heat. To warm: vessels constrict to keep heat in
3. SensationPacked with receptors for touch, pressure, vibration, temperature, and pain — the body's largest sense organ
4. Vitamin D synthesisUV light converts a cholesterol relative in the skin into vitamin D₃, needed to absorb dietary calcium
5. Excretion & storageSweat sheds small amounts of water, salts, and wastes; the dermis stores a blood reservoir (up to ~5% of blood volume)

The barrier you rebuild every month

Protection is the headline act. The dead, keratin-packed stratum corneum keeps water in (you would dehydrate in hours without it) and keeps microbes, chemicals, and UV out. Sweat and sebum coat the surface in a slightly acidic film — the acid mantle — that discourages bacteria and fungi, while dendritic immune cells woven through the epidermis grab any invader that slips past. Because the whole barrier is rebuilt roughly every month, minor damage heals almost invisibly.

Temperature, sensation, and a vitamin the skin makes

The skin is also your master thermostat. When you overheat, eccrine glands pour out sweat that carries heat away as it evaporates, and dermal blood vessels widen to radiate heat from the surface — which is why you flush pink when hot. When you are cold, those same vessels clamp down to trap warmth in the core. Meanwhile the dermis is crowded with sensory receptors that make the skin the largest sense organ you own, feeding the nervous system a constant stream of touch, pressure, temperature, and pain. And in a job no other organ shares, the skin manufactures vitamin D: ultraviolet light strikes a cholesterol-like molecule in the epidermis and starts its conversion into vitamin D₃, without which you cannot absorb the calcium your bones and nerves depend on.

Sunlight is a nutrient — in moderation. The same UV that damages DNA also kick-starts vitamin D synthesis. That is the tension at the heart of this whole chapter: enough sun to make vitamin D and lift mood, but not so much that you overwhelm melanin's protection and court skin cancer.

Key points
  • The skin's five jobs: protection, thermoregulation, sensation, vitamin D synthesis, and excretion/storage.
  • Protection is physical (keratin), chemical (the acidic acid mantle), and biological (immune dendritic cells).
  • Cooling uses sweat + vessel dilation; warming uses vessel constriction. Vitamin D₃ is made in skin using UV and is needed to absorb calcium.

Section 6Burns & skin cancer

Burns: it's all about depth

A burn destroys skin with heat, electricity, chemicals, or radiation, and its severity is judged first by how deep it goes. Because the skin's barrier, water control, and temperature regulation all live in these layers, the deeper the burn, the more dangerous it becomes — not because it hurts more, but sometimes because it stops hurting at all.

Burn (depth)Layers destroyedLook & feel
First-degree (superficial)Epidermis onlyRed, dry, painful, no blisters — a typical sunburn; heals in days
Second-degree (partial thickness)Epidermis + part of dermisBlisters, red, very painful; heals but may scar
Third-degree (full thickness)Entire epidermis + dermisWhite, brown, or charred and leathery; often painless because nerves are destroyed; needs skin grafting

The two great dangers of a serious burn are fluid loss and infection: once the barrier is gone, water pours out and microbes pour in, and the body can no longer hold its temperature. To gauge how much skin is involved, clinicians use the rule of nines, which divides the adult body surface into regions worth about 9% each (or multiples of nine) — each arm 9%, each leg 18%, the front of the trunk 18%, and so on. A burn is generally considered critical when it covers more than 25% of the body, or involves the hands, face, or feet, or is third-degree over more than a few percent.

A painless burn is a worse burn. It sounds paradoxical, but a full-thickness (third-degree) burn is often numb — the sensory nerves in the dermis have been destroyed along with everything else. Never take absence of pain as a sign a burn is minor.

Skin cancer: three kinds, one that kills

Chronic UV exposure is the leading cause of skin cancer, which is the most common cancer of all. There are three main types, and telling them apart matters enormously because they range from a nuisance to a killer. Notice that each one arises from a cell you already met earlier in this chapter.

CancerCell of originBehavior
Basal cell carcinomaCells of the stratum basaleMost common, least dangerous; rarely spreads; usually cured by removal
Squamous cell carcinomaKeratinocytes of the stratum spinosumLess common; can spread if ignored, but usually curable if caught early
MelanomaMelanocytesLeast common but most deadly; spreads (metastasizes) early and fast — catching it early saves lives

The dangerous one is melanoma, a cancer of the pigment-making melanocytes. It often begins in or near a mole, and because it metastasizes early, spotting it early is everything. The ABCDE rule is the tool used to screen a suspicious mole: Asymmetry (one half unlike the other), Border (ragged or blurred edges), Color (uneven, several shades), Diameter (larger than about 6 mm, the size of a pencil eraser), and Evolving (any change in size, shape, or color over time). Any mole that checks these boxes deserves a professional look.

Key points
  • Burns are graded by depth: first-degree (epidermis), second-degree (into dermis, blisters), third-degree (full thickness, often painless, needs grafts).
  • Serious burns kill through fluid loss and infection; the rule of nines estimates the percent of body surface burned.
  • Three skin cancers: basal cell (common, mild), squamous cell (can spread), and melanoma (rare but deadly). Screen moles with ABCDE.

The chapter in one breath

  • The integumentary system is the skin plus hair, nails, and glands — the body's largest organ.
  • Skin has two true layers: the avascular epithelial epidermis over the vascular connective-tissue dermis, resting on the fatty hypodermis.
  • Keratinocytes ride an upward conveyor through five strata — basale → spinosum → granulosum → lucidum → corneum — filling with keratin, dying, and shedding in about a month.
  • Melanocytes make melanin and pass it to keratinocytes, where it caps the nucleus and absorbs UV to protect DNA; color differences reflect melanin amount, not melanocyte number.
  • Hair and nails are keratin structures grown from living matrices; sebaceous glands make oil, eccrine glands cool, and apocrine glands cause body odor.
  • The skin performs five jobs: protection, thermoregulation, sensation, vitamin D synthesis, and excretion/storage.
  • Burns are graded by depth (first- to third-degree) and area (rule of nines); the three skin cancers are basal cell, squamous cell, and the deadly melanoma, screened with ABCDE.

Check yourself

1. A patient has a shallow scrape that does not bleed at all. Which skin layer was damaged, and why is the lack of bleeding expected?
Only the epidermis was scraped, and the epidermis is avascular (has no blood vessels). Blood vessels live in the dermis below, so a wound has to reach the dermis before it bleeds or hurts much. The epidermis survives on nutrients diffusing up from the dermis.
2. Which layer sits directly beneath the skin, what tissue is it made of, and why is it not counted as part of the skin?
The hypodermis (subcutaneous layer), made mostly of adipose and areolar tissue. It anchors the skin, insulates, and stores fat, but because it is not epithelium-plus-dermis it is considered support tissue beneath the skin rather than skin itself.
3. Name the five epidermal strata from deepest to most superficial, and state which one is found only in thick skin.
Stratum basale → spinosum → granulosum → lucidum → corneum; the stratum lucidum is found only in thick skin (palms and soles). Remember "Bad Students Get Lousy Classes." Cells are born in the basale and shed from the corneum.
4. In which stratum are keratinocytes born, roughly how long does the journey to the surface take, and what is the outermost layer made of?
They are born in the stratum basale; the trip takes about a month (~25–45 days); the stratum corneum is made of dead, keratin-filled cells. This upward, dying march is called keratinization, and the dead corneum is what makes the skin a waterproof barrier.
5. Two people have very different skin colors. Is this because one has far more melanocytes than the other? Explain.
No — everyone has roughly the same number of melanocytes. The difference is in how much melanin those cells produce and what type. More active pigment production means darker skin; a tan is the same cells ramping up melanin after UV exposure.
6. A melanocyte makes melanin but hands it off. Where does the pigment end up inside a keratinocyte, and what protective job does it do there?
The melanin collects as a cap over the top of the keratinocyte's nucleus, like a tiny umbrella. There it absorbs incoming ultraviolet radiation before it can damage the cell's DNA — melanin is essentially sunscreen the cell makes for itself.
7. Why does a haircut not hurt, but plucking a hair does? Point to the structure responsible for hair growth.
The visible shaft is dead keratin, so cutting it is painless; the living, growing part is the matrix at the base of the follicle, fed by the papilla. Plucking disturbs that living root and its nerve supply, which is why it hurts.
8. Distinguish eccrine from apocrine sweat glands: where is each found, and which one is responsible for cooling versus body odor?
Eccrine glands are all over the body and secrete watery sweat that cools by evaporation; apocrine glands are in the armpits and groin and secrete a thick sweat linked to body odor. Apocrine sweat itself is odorless until skin bacteria break it down.
9. List the five functions of the skin, and name the one that is a nutrient-related job no other organ shares.
Protection, thermoregulation, sensation, vitamin D synthesis, and excretion/storage; vitamin D synthesis is the unique nutritional job. UV light starts the conversion of a cholesterol relative into vitamin D₃, which the body needs to absorb calcium.
10. On a hot day the skin does two things to shed heat. Name both, and describe how the same blood vessels respond when you are cold.
To cool: eccrine glands produce sweat that evaporates, and dermal blood vessels dilate to radiate heat. When cold, those vessels constrict to keep heat in the core. This makes the skin the body's main temperature-regulating organ.
11. A burn is charred, leathery, and surprisingly painless. What degree of burn is this, which layers are destroyed, and why is it painless?
A third-degree (full-thickness) burn, destroying the entire epidermis and dermis; it is painless because the sensory nerves in the dermis have been destroyed. These burns usually require skin grafting, and absence of pain never means a burn is minor.
12. Name the three skin cancers from least to most dangerous and their cell of origin, and give the ABCDE warning signs for the deadliest one.
Basal cell carcinoma (stratum basale, mildest), squamous cell carcinoma (stratum spinosum keratinocytes), and melanoma (melanocytes, deadliest). Screen a mole for melanoma with ABCDE: Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Color variation, Diameter over ~6 mm, and Evolving/changing over time.
