The Autonomic Nervous System

Right now, without a single deliberate thought, your heart is beating, your gut is churning breakfast, your pupils are adjusting to the light, and your blood pressure is being nudged up and down a dozen times a minute. All of that quiet housekeeping belongs to the autonomic nervous system — the part of you that runs the organs while your conscious mind is busy elsewhere. In this chapter you will meet its two divisions, the sympathetic "fight or flight" system and the parasympathetic "rest and digest" system, and see how they pull most organs in opposite directions to keep the body tuned. You will learn the two-neuron wiring that makes autonomic pathways different from every other motor route, the two chemicals that carry every autonomic message, and the elegant reflex that stops you from fainting each time you stand up.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • Define the autonomic nervous system and explain how it differs from the somatic (voluntary) motor system.
  • Contrast the sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions by their overall job, their outflow from the CNS, and their effects on target organs.
  • Describe the two-neuron autonomic pathway and locate the ganglia of each division.
  • Explain dual innervation and autonomic tone, and predict the net effect when one division dominates.
  • Trace the whole-body fight-or-flight response organ by organ and describe the parasympathetic return to calm.
  • Match the autonomic neurotransmitters (acetylcholine, norepinephrine) to their receptors and predict the effect at each target.
  • Diagram the baroreceptor reflex as a negative feedback loop that defends blood pressure.

Section 1Two divisions, opposite jobs

A motor system for the organs you never think about

The nervous system has two motor outputs. The somatic system carries your voluntary commands to skeletal muscle — the decision to lift a cup. The autonomic nervous system (ANS) does everything else: it is the involuntary, visceral motor system that controls smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands. It sets your heart rate, the width of your pupils and airways, the pace of your digestion, the tone of your blood vessels, and the activity of your sweat and salivary glands — all automatically, below the level of awareness. You feel its work only indirectly, as a pounding heart before a test or a dry mouth when you are nervous.

Two divisions that pull opposite ways

The ANS is built from two divisions that usually act as opponents. The sympathetic division is the body's accelerator — the "fight or flight" system that ramps you up to meet a threat. The parasympathetic division is the brake — the "rest and digest" system that runs the body during calm, restful conditions and handles the unglamorous business of digestion, waste elimination, and energy storage. Most organs receive a nerve supply from both divisions, an arrangement called dual innervation, and the two supplies push the organ in opposite directions. The heart is the clearest example: sympathetic input speeds it up, parasympathetic input slows it down, and your actual heart rate at any moment is the running balance between the two.

Where each division leaves the central nervous system

The two divisions even exit the CNS from different places, which is the easiest way to tell them apart. Sympathetic fibers all emerge from the thoracolumbar region — the lateral horns of spinal segments T1 through L2 — so the sympathetic division is often called the thoracolumbar outflow. Parasympathetic fibers emerge from the two ends of the CNS: from the brainstem in four cranial nerves (III, VII, IX, and the far-reaching vagus, X) and from the sacral spinal cord (segments S2–S4). That top-and-bottom pattern gives the parasympathetic division its nickname, the craniosacral outflow.

Tone means both are always on. Neither division is ever fully switched off. Both fire at a low background rate called autonomic tone, so the body regulates an organ simply by dialing one division up and the other down — like steering with a gas pedal and a brake pressed at the same time. Resting heart rate, for instance, is dominated by ongoing vagal tone; cut the vagus and the heart races.

  • Its two divisions are opponents: sympathetic = fight or flight (accelerator); parasympathetic = rest and digest (brake).
  • Sympathetic outflow is thoracolumbar (T1–L2); parasympathetic outflow is craniosacral (CN III, VII, IX, X and S2–S4).

Section 2The two-neuron pathway

Two neurons in a relay, not one

Here is the single structural fact that defines the whole autonomic system: it takes two neurons to carry a signal from the CNS to a visceral target, and they meet at a relay station called a ganglion. The first neuron, the preganglionic neuron, has its cell body inside the CNS; its axon runs out to a ganglion and synapses there. The second neuron, the postganglionic neuron, has its cell body in the ganglion and sends its axon the rest of the way to the organ. Compare this to the somatic system, where a single motor neuron reaches all the way from the spinal cord to the muscle with no relay in between. That extra synapse in the ganglion is where the two divisions do their most distinctive wiring.

Sympathetic wiring: short first, long second, and it spreads

In the sympathetic division, the ganglia sit close to the spinal cord. Most form the sympathetic chain ganglia (the paravertebral ganglia) — a beaded double column running alongside the vertebral column that you can see flanking the cord in the figure. A few others, the prevertebral (collateral) ganglia such as the celiac and mesenteric ganglia, sit out in front of the aorta and are reached by the splanchnic nerves. Because the relay is near the cord, sympathetic preganglionic fibers are short and postganglionic fibers are long. Crucially, each preganglionic fiber synapses on many postganglionic neurons — a fan-out called divergence — which is exactly why a sympathetic response is not local but sweeps the whole body at once.

Parasympathetic wiring: long first, short second, and it stays local

The parasympathetic division reverses the geometry. Its ganglia sit in or right beside the target organ (terminal or intramural ganglia), so preganglionic fibers are long and postganglionic fibers are very short. There is little divergence, so a parasympathetic signal produces a discrete, localized effect on one organ rather than a body-wide surge — you can slow the heart without also cramping the gut. This precision fits the calm, one-thing-at-a-time character of rest and digest.

FeatureSympatheticParasympathetic
CNS outflowThoracolumbar (T1–L2)Craniosacral (brainstem + S2–S4)
Ganglion locationNear the spinal cord (chain & prevertebral)Near or in the target organ
Preganglionic fiberShortLong
Postganglionic fiberLongShort
DivergenceHigh → widespread responseLow → localized response
The adrenal medulla is a modified ganglion. One sympathetic pathway breaks the two-neuron rule: preganglionic fibers run straight to the adrenal medulla and synapse on hormone-making chromaffin cells, which dump epinephrine (and some norepinephrine) directly into the bloodstream. Those cells are essentially postganglionic neurons that lost their axons. This is why fight or flight has a hormonal tail — the racing, jittery feeling that lingers minutes after the scare has passed.

Section 3Fight or flight, rest and digest

One switch, and the whole body answers

Because sympathetic fibers diverge so widely and the adrenal medulla adds a hormonal boost, the sympathetic division works as a coordinated, all-at-once package. Imagine the moment you step off a curb and a car horn blares. Before you can think, your heart pounds, your breathing deepens, your eyes go wide, your palms sweat, and your stomach seems to drop. That is the fight-or-flight response — the sympathetic division reprioritizing the entire body toward one goal: sudden, intense physical exertion to escape or confront a threat. Every change is purposeful, and each one shifts resources away from long-term maintenance and toward immediate muscle power.

The sympathetic surge, organ by organ

Follow the surge through the body and every effect earns its place. The heart beats faster and harder to move oxygen quickly. The airways widen so more air reaches the lungs. The pupils dilate to take in more of the scene. The liver pours stored glucose into the blood to fuel muscle. Blood vessels constrict in the gut and skin but dilate in skeletal muscle, shunting the blood where it is needed. Digestion and urination pause — no time for either. Sweat glands switch on to pre-cool the body for exertion. It is a beautifully logical package: everything that helps you run or fight is turned up, and everything that can wait is turned down.

Fight-or-flight changeWhy it helps survival
Heart rate and force riseDelivers oxygen and fuel to muscle faster
Bronchioles dilateMore airflow, more oxygen uptake
Pupils dilateSharper, wider vision of the threat
Liver releases glucoseReady fuel for a burst of effort
Blood shifts to skeletal musclePower where it is needed, not the gut
Digestion and urination haltSaves energy for the emergency
Sweating increasesCools the body before heavy exertion

Rest and digest: paying the debt back

When the threat is gone, the parasympathetic division takes the body back to baseline and then to the quiet work the emergency interrupted. The heart slows, the airways and pupils return to normal, blood flows back to the gut, and digestion, absorption, and elimination resume. This is the state you spend most of your life in — the body building and storing rather than spending. The two divisions are not enemies so much as a well-matched pair: the sympathetic system is the sprint, the parasympathetic system is the recovery, and health depends on being able to swing smoothly between them.

Why you stay shaky after a fright. Nerve signals stop the instant the danger passes, but the epinephrine the adrenal medulla poured into your blood keeps circulating for minutes until the liver and other tissues clear it. That lingering hormone is the trembling hands and thudding heart you feel after a near-miss — the nervous response is already over, but the hormonal one is still fading.

Section 4The chemistry: acetylcholine and norepinephrine

Only two chemicals do all the talking

For a system that controls so much, the ANS speaks a remarkably small vocabulary: nearly every autonomic message is carried by just two neurotransmitters. A neuron that releases acetylcholine (ACh) is called cholinergic; one that releases norepinephrine (NE) is called adrenergic. Where in the pathway each chemical is used follows a simple pattern. All preganglionic neurons of both divisions are cholinergic — they release ACh in the ganglion. The postganglionic neurons then split: parasympathetic postganglionic fibers release ACh onto the organ, while sympathetic postganglionic fibers release NE. (Two tidy exceptions: sympathetic fibers to sweat glands use ACh, and the adrenal medulla releases epinephrine into the blood.)

The receptor decides the effect, not the chemical

Here is the idea students most need to grasp: the same neurotransmitter can excite one organ and inhibit another, because the outcome depends on the receptor the chemical lands on. Cholinergic receptors come in two flavors — nicotinic receptors (found in all autonomic ganglia and at the skeletal neuromuscular junction) and muscarinic receptors (on the organs that parasympathetic fibers control). Adrenergic receptors also come in families — alpha and beta subtypes. Norepinephrine acting on alpha-1 receptors constricts blood vessels; the very same NE (and epinephrine) acting on beta-2 receptors relaxes the airways. One molecule, opposite effects — the target cell's receptor writes the instruction.

FiberNeurotransmitterReceptor on target
Preganglionic (both divisions)AcetylcholineNicotinic
Parasympathetic postganglionicAcetylcholineMuscarinic
Sympathetic postganglionic (most)NorepinephrineAdrenergic (α or β)

A loose spray, not a tight synapse

Notice in the figure that the postganglionic axon does not end in a single neat terminal. Instead it is studded with beads called varicosities that release neurotransmitter in a diffuse spray over a wide patch of smooth muscle. This is why one autonomic fiber can influence a broad sheet of tissue at once — ideal for setting the tone of an entire vessel wall or gut segment rather than commanding a single cell.

Receptor subtypes are drug targets. This chemistry is the whole reason autonomic drugs can be so selective. A beta-blocker shields the heart's β1 receptors to slow it; albuterol for asthma stimulates β2 receptors to open the airways without racing the heart; atropine blocks muscarinic receptors to speed a dangerously slow heart or dry secretions before surgery. Learn which receptor sits on which organ and the pharmacology almost writes itself.

Section 5The baroreceptor reflex

The body's fastest blood-pressure guard

The autonomic system does more than react to emergencies — it runs continuous, silent reflexes that keep vital numbers steady. The most important of these is the baroreceptor reflex, the second-by-second guardian of blood pressure. It is a textbook negative feedback loop, and once you see its three parts you will recognize the same machinery you met in Chapter 1: a sensor, a control center, and effectors.

Sense, compare, correct

The sensors are baroreceptors — stretch receptors packed into the walls of the carotid sinus (where blood heads to the brain) and the aortic arch. When blood pressure rises, these vessel walls stretch more and the receptors fire faster; when pressure falls, they fire less. Their reports travel along cranial nerves IX (glossopharyngeal) and X (vagus) to the cardiovascular center in the medulla of the brainstem — the control center that compares the signal to the desired pressure. The medulla then adjusts its two autonomic effectors: it dials parasympathetic (vagal) and sympathetic output up or down to change heart rate, contractility, and vessel diameter. Because the response always opposes the disturbance, pressure is pushed back toward its set point.

Blood pressureBaroreceptor firingAutonomic responseResult
Rises too highIncreasesParasympathetic up, sympathetic downHeart slows, vessels dilate → BP falls
Falls too lowDecreasesSympathetic up, parasympathetic downHeart speeds, vessels constrict → BP rises

Standing up without fainting

You depend on this reflex every time you stand. When you rise, gravity pools blood in your legs and pressure at the head momentarily drops. Baroreceptors instantly sense the fall, the medulla ramps up sympathetic drive, your heart quickens and your vessels tighten, and pressure is restored before the brain is ever short-changed — all in a single heartbeat or two. When this reflex is sluggish (from aging, dehydration, or certain drugs), the correction lags and you feel lightheaded on standing, a condition called orthostatic hypotension.

The dizzy-on-standing test. That brief wooziness when you jump up too fast is the baroreceptor reflex being caught a half-second behind. It is also why a clinician checks orthostatic vitals — blood pressure lying, sitting, and standing — to unmask a reflex or a blood volume that cannot keep up with a change in posture.

Section 6Somatic versus autonomic

Two ways the brain moves the body

It is worth putting the autonomic system side by side with its sibling, the somatic motor system, because the contrast makes both clearer. The somatic system carries voluntary commands to skeletal muscle. It uses a single motor neuron from the CNS straight to the muscle, that neuron releases acetylcholine onto nicotinic receptors, and its effect is always the same: excitation — a somatic motor neuron can only tell a muscle to contract, never to relax. To stop a movement, the CNS simply stops firing.

Where the autonomic system differs

The autonomic system differs on nearly every count. It is involuntary; it targets smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands; it uses a two-neuron chain relaying at a ganglion; it uses ACh or NE depending on the fiber; and it can be either excitatory or inhibitory at the target — the parasympathetic vagus can actively slow the heart, something no somatic neuron can do to a muscle. That ability to both stimulate and inhibit, division by division, is what lets the ANS fine-tune organs in both directions.

FeatureSomaticAutonomic
ControlVoluntaryInvoluntary
Effector (target)Skeletal muscleSmooth & cardiac muscle, glands
Neurons to targetOneTwo (relay at a ganglion)
NeurotransmitterAcetylcholine onlyAcetylcholine or norepinephrine
Effect on targetAlways excitatoryExcitatory or inhibitory

A third player: the gut's own brain

The figure also names a quieter third division, the enteric nervous system (ENS) — a vast web of neurons embedded in the wall of the digestive tract. So numerous that it is nicknamed the "second brain," the ENS can run local digestive reflexes on its own, coordinating the muscle contractions and secretions of the gut even when its links to the CNS are cut. The autonomic divisions still modulate it — parasympathetic input revs digestion, sympathetic input suppresses it — but the day-to-day churning of your intestines is largely self-governed.

Inhibition is the autonomic superpower. The single most useful line between the two systems: a somatic neuron can only excite its muscle, so relaxation means silence from the nerve. An autonomic target, by contrast, has two nerves that can each actively push it up or down. That is why your heart has a built-in brake (the vagus) and your gut has a built-in throttle.

Key points
  • The somatic system is voluntary, uses one neuron to skeletal muscle, releases only ACh, and is always excitatory.
  • The autonomic system is involuntary, uses a two-neuron chain to smooth/cardiac muscle and glands, uses ACh or NE, and can excite or inhibit.
  • The enteric nervous system is a self-governing "second brain" in the gut wall, modulated but not commanded by the ANS.

The chapter in one breath

  • The autonomic nervous system is the involuntary motor system that runs smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands.
  • Its two divisions oppose each other: sympathetic (fight or flight, thoracolumbar) and parasympathetic (rest and digest, craniosacral), with most organs under dual innervation and steered by shifting tone.
  • Every autonomic route is a two-neuron chain relaying at a ganglion — sympathetic ganglia sit near the cord and diverge widely; parasympathetic ganglia sit at the organ and stay local.
  • The fight-or-flight response is a coordinated, body-wide sympathetic surge; the parasympathetic division restores calm, aided by the slow clearance of adrenal epinephrine.
  • Just two neurotransmitters — acetylcholine and norepinephrine — run the system, and the receptor decides whether a target is excited or inhibited.
  • The baroreceptor reflex is a negative feedback loop that defends blood pressure by balancing sympathetic and parasympathetic output through the medulla.
  • Compared with the somatic system (voluntary, one neuron, ACh, always excitatory), the autonomic system is involuntary, two-neuron, and can both excite and inhibit; the enteric system runs the gut semi-independently.

Check yourself

1. What kind of tissue does the autonomic nervous system control, and how does that differ from what the somatic system controls?
The ANS controls smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands (involuntary); the somatic system controls skeletal muscle (voluntary). The autonomic system is the visceral motor system that runs the organs automatically, below conscious awareness.
2. Name the two divisions of the ANS, give each its nickname, and state which one you are mostly in while resting on the couch.
Sympathetic ("fight or flight") and parasympathetic ("rest and digest"); resting on the couch you are mostly parasympathetic. The parasympathetic division dominates during calm conditions, running digestion and energy storage.
3. From which parts of the CNS does each division emerge, and what are the two "outflow" nicknames those origins produce?
Sympathetic = thoracolumbar outflow (T1–L2); parasympathetic = craniosacral outflow (brainstem CN III, VII, IX, X and S2–S4). The exit point from the CNS is the quickest way to tell the two divisions apart.
4. A single organ receives nerves from both divisions that push it in opposite directions. What is this arrangement called, and how does the body use it to set, say, heart rate?
Dual innervation. Because both divisions fire at a background level (autonomic tone), the body sets heart rate by shifting the balance — dialing sympathetic up and vagal (parasympathetic) down speeds it, and the reverse slows it.
5. How many neurons carry a signal from the CNS to an autonomic target, and what is the relay point between them called?
Two — a preganglionic and a postganglionic neuron — relaying at a ganglion. This is a defining feature of the ANS; the somatic system uses only one neuron from the cord all the way to the muscle.
6. Compare where the ganglia sit in each division, and explain why a sympathetic response is body-wide while a parasympathetic one is localized.
Sympathetic ganglia sit near the spinal cord; parasympathetic ganglia sit at the target organ. Sympathetic preganglionic fibers diverge onto many postganglionic neurons, spreading the signal widely; parasympathetic pathways show little divergence, so effects stay discrete.
7. The adrenal medulla is described as a "modified sympathetic ganglion." What does it do that makes fight or flight outlast the nerve signals?
It releases epinephrine (and some NE) directly into the blood as a hormone. Circulating epinephrine keeps acting for minutes after the nerve firing stops, which is why you stay shaky and racing after a scare passes.
8. List four organ changes of the fight-or-flight response and, for each, say why it aids survival.
For example: heart rate rises (delivers oxygen faster), airways dilate (more airflow), pupils dilate (better vision), liver releases glucose (fuel), blood shifts to muscle, digestion halts (saves energy). Every change turns up resources for exertion and turns down anything that can wait.
9. Which two neurotransmitters run the ANS, and which fibers use each? Note the location of the two exceptions.
Acetylcholine and norepinephrine. All preganglionic fibers and all parasympathetic postganglionic fibers use ACh; most sympathetic postganglionic fibers use NE. The exceptions: sympathetic fibers to sweat glands use ACh, and the adrenal medulla releases epinephrine into the blood.
10. Norepinephrine constricts blood vessels but relaxes the airways. How can one chemical do opposite things?
The receptor decides the effect, not the chemical. NE on alpha-1 receptors constricts vessels; the same NE (and epinephrine) on beta-2 receptors relaxes bronchioles. This receptor logic is why drugs like beta-blockers and albuterol can be so selective.
11. Trace the baroreceptor reflex when blood pressure suddenly rises: sensor, pathway, control center, and the autonomic correction.
Baroreceptors in the carotid sinus and aortic arch fire faster → signal travels via CN IX and X to the cardiovascular center in the medulla → the medulla raises parasympathetic (vagal) output and lowers sympathetic output → heart slows and vessels dilate, so pressure falls back to normal. It is a classic negative feedback loop — the response opposes the change.
12. Give three ways the autonomic system differs from the somatic system, and name the self-governing "second brain" in the gut wall.
Autonomic: involuntary, two neurons, targets smooth/cardiac muscle and glands, uses ACh or NE, and can excite or inhibit. Somatic: voluntary, one neuron, targets skeletal muscle, uses ACh only, always excitatory. The gut's own network is the enteric nervous system. The autonomic ability to actively inhibit (for example, the vagus slowing the heart) is its key advantage over the always-excitatory somatic system.
