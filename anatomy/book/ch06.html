Bone Tissue & the Skeletal System

It is easy to picture the skeleton as a dry rack of scaffolding — the part of us that outlasts everything else. That picture is wrong in the most important way: bone is living tissue, laced with blood vessels and nerves, rebuilt every day of your life, and quietly running jobs that have nothing to do with holding you up. In this chapter you will meet the skeleton as an organ system — how it is built, what its cells do, how it keeps your blood calcium in a razor-thin range, and how it heals itself when it breaks. Learn bone as a tissue that is alive, and the rest of the skeletal course falls into place.

By the end of this chapter you can

  • List the major functions of the skeletal system and split the skeleton into its axial and appendicular divisions.
  • Classify bones by shape — long, short, flat, irregular, and sesamoid — and give an example of each.
  • Name the gross parts of a long bone and describe the difference between compact and spongy bone.
  • Identify the four bone cells and explain how remodeling balances bone deposition against resorption.
  • Describe the organic and inorganic components of the bone matrix and what each contributes.
  • Diagram how parathyroid hormone and calcitonin hold blood calcium near its set point.
  • Recognize common fracture patterns and place the four stages of bone repair in order.

Section 1What the skeleton is for

A frame that is also a factory

Every bone is an organ — it contains several tissue types (bone tissue, cartilage, dense connective tissue, blood, nervous tissue) working together, and it is very much alive. Snap a fresh bone and it bleeds; leave a broken one alone and it heals. The whole set of bones, cartilages, and joints makes up the skeletal system, and that system does far more than give you a shape. It works as a support beam, a suit of armor, a system of levers, a mineral bank, and a blood-cell factory — often all at the same moment.

The jobs bones do

It is worth memorizing the skeleton's core functions as a short list, because almost everything else in the chapter is one of these jobs seen up close. Bone stores fat and minerals, builds blood, moves you, protects you, and holds you upright.

FunctionHow the skeleton delivers it
SupportA rigid framework that holds the body up and gives soft organs and muscles a place to attach
ProtectionHard cases shield vital organs — the skull guards the brain, the vertebrae the spinal cord, the rib cage the heart and lungs
MovementBones act as levers that muscles pull on; where two bones meet at a joint, motion happens
Mineral & fat storageBone is a reservoir for calcium and phosphate, released or stored on demand; yellow marrow stores energy as fat
Blood-cell formationHematopoiesis — red marrow inside certain bones makes red cells, white cells, and platelets

Axial and appendicular: two halves of one set

An adult skeleton has 206 bones, and they split into two named divisions. The axial skeleton (about 80 bones) runs down the body's central axis — the skull, the vertebral column, and the thoracic cage — and its main job is protection and support. The appendicular skeleton (about 126 bones) is everything that hangs off that axis: the arms and legs plus the two girdles (pectoral and pelvic) that anchor the limbs to the trunk. Its main job is movement. In the reference figure, the axial bones are tinted one color and the appendicular bones another — a useful way to keep the two straight.

Bone is the body's calcium bank. Roughly 99% of the body's calcium is deposited in the skeleton. That single fact drives one of the chapter's biggest ideas: when blood calcium runs low, the body can withdraw it from bone — even at the skeleton's expense — because a steady blood level matters more moment to moment than any single bone.

Section 2Classifying bones by shape

Shape follows the job

Bones come in a handful of recognizable shapes, and — just as with the rest of the body — the shape is a clue to the function. A bone built for leverage looks nothing like a bone built to shield a soft organ. Sorting the skeleton into five shape classes gives you a quick vocabulary for describing any bone you meet.

The five shapes

ClassShape & roleExamples
Long boneLonger than wide, with a shaft and two ends; act as levers for movementFemur, humerus, phalanges (fingers & toes)
Short boneRoughly cube-shaped; provide stability with limited motionCarpals (wrist), tarsals (ankle)
Flat boneThin, often curved plates; protect organs and give broad muscle attachmentSkull bones, sternum, ribs, scapula
Irregular boneComplex shapes that fit no other class; varied protective and support rolesVertebrae, hip bones, many facial bones
Sesamoid boneSmall bones embedded inside a tendon; protect the tendon and improve its pullPatella (kneecap)

Notice that the phalanges of your fingers count as long bones even though they are tiny — the label is about proportion (longer than wide, with a shaft) and about function (leverage), not about absolute size. The reference figure also shows a sutural (Wormian) bone, a small extra bone that can appear within the seams of the skull; think of it as a special case of a flat bone rather than a sixth class.

The patella is the body's largest sesamoid. Because a sesamoid bone sits inside a tendon, it lifts that tendon away from the joint and increases the muscle's leverage — which is exactly why the kneecap makes the quadriceps a more powerful knee extender. Small sesamoids also hide in the tendons of the hands and feet.

Section 3Inside a long bone

The shaft and the ends

A long bone such as the femur has a clean architecture once you learn its parts. The long central tube is the diaphysis, or shaft. Its wall is a cylinder of dense compact bone, and its hollow center — the medullary cavity — is filled in adults with fatty yellow marrow. Each rounded end is an epiphysis, filled with lightweight spongy (trabecular) bone whose spaces hold red marrow, the site of blood-cell formation. Where the joint surface meets another bone, the epiphysis is capped with slick articular cartilage that cushions the joint and lets it glide. Between the shaft and each end lies the metaphysis, which in a growing child contains the epiphyseal (growth) plate — a disc of cartilage where the bone lengthens. When growth finishes, that plate ossifies into a thin epiphyseal line.

The living wrappers

Two thin membranes keep bone alive and connected. The periosteum is a tough sheath covering the outer surface everywhere except the joint caps; it carries the blood vessels and nerves, anchors tendons and ligaments, and holds bone-forming cells for growth and repair. Lining the inner surfaces — the medullary cavity and the spaces of spongy bone — is the delicate endosteum, also stocked with bone cells. A nutrient artery pierces the shaft through a small opening to supply the bone from within.

Zoom in: the osteon

Under the microscope, compact bone is not a solid block but a bundle of tiny weight-bearing pillars called osteons (Haversian systems). Each osteon is a set of concentric rings of bone matrix, the lamellae, wrapped around a central canal that carries a blood vessel and nerve. Trapped in small pockets called lacunae between the rings are the mature bone cells, the osteocytes, and they stay connected to one another and to the central vessel through hair-thin channels called canaliculi. This canal-and-channel plumbing is how a hard, mineralized tissue keeps its buried cells fed and alive.

PartWhat it is / holds
DiaphysisThe shaft; a tube of compact bone around the medullary cavity
EpiphysisAn expanded end filled with spongy bone and red marrow
MetaphysisThe region between shaft and end; site of the growth plate/line
Medullary cavityThe hollow center of the shaft; holds yellow (fatty) marrow in adults
Articular cartilageSmooth cartilage capping the joint surface of each epiphysis
PeriosteumOuter membrane; vessels, nerves, attachment, bone-forming cells
EndosteumInner lining of the marrow cavity and spongy spaces
The growth plate is a child's weak point. Because the epiphyseal plate is cartilage, it is softer than the bone around it — so in children a hard blow can crack straight through it. These growth-plate (Salter–Harris) fractures need careful attention, since damage there can disturb how the limb grows. Once the plate seals into the epiphyseal line in adulthood, that vulnerability disappears.

  • The metaphysis holds the epiphyseal plate (growth) that becomes the epiphyseal line when growth stops.

Section 4Bone cells & remodeling

What bone is made of

Bone tissue is a masterpiece of engineering because it combines two opposite qualities. Its organic part — mostly collagen fibers — gives bone flexibility and tensile strength, so it bends a little under load instead of snapping. Its inorganic part — mineral salts of calcium and phosphate that crystallize as hydroxyapatite — gives bone its hardness and compressive strength. Take the mineral away and bone becomes rubbery; take the collagen away and it becomes brittle as chalk. Healthy bone needs both, in balance.

Four cells, one crew

Four cell types build and maintain that matrix, and the easiest way to remember them is by their jobs. Stem-like osteogenic (osteoprogenitor) cells divide to produce the builders. Osteoblasts lay down new bone matrix — they build. Once an osteoblast walls itself into the matrix it has made, it matures into an osteocyte, the caretaker cell that lives in a lacuna and keeps the tissue healthy. And the demolition crew are the osteoclasts, large multinucleated cells that dissolve old bone and release its minerals back into the blood.

CellJobMemory hook
Osteogenic cellStem cell that divides into osteoblastsThe source
OsteoblastBuilds new bone matrix“b” for build
OsteocyteMature cell; maintains bone, senses stressRetired builder, walled in
OsteoclastBreaks down bone; frees calcium“c” for consume/carve

The tug-of-war called remodeling

Bone is never finished. Throughout life, osteoclasts dissolve old bone while osteoblasts lay down fresh bone right behind them — a continuous cycle called remodeling that replaces roughly 10% of the skeleton every year. When the two crews stay balanced, bone mass holds steady. Two things tune the balance. First, mechanical stress: bone thickens where it is loaded and thins where it is not — the principle known as Wolff's law, which is why weight-bearing exercise strengthens the skeleton and why bones weaken during bed rest or spaceflight. Second, hormones, especially the calcium-regulating pair you will meet next. When resorption outpaces formation for years — as often happens after menopause — the result is osteoporosis, porous bone that fractures easily.

Blasts build, clasts consume. The whole system is a balance between the two: osteoblasts build bone up, osteoclasts chew it down. Keep them even and the skeleton stays strong; tip the balance toward the clasts and bone thins toward osteoporosis.

Key points
  • Bone matrix = collagen (flexibility) + hydroxyapatite mineral (hardness); it needs both.
  • Four cells: osteogenic (source), osteoblast (build), osteocyte (maintain), osteoclast (break down).

  • Chronic imbalance toward resorption gives osteoporosis.

Section 5Calcium homeostasis

Why calcium is worth guarding

Blood calcium (Ca²⁺) must stay in a tight window, roughly 9–10 mg/dL, because so much depends on it: calcium triggers muscle contraction, lets nerves fire, is required for blood clotting, and acts as a messenger inside cells. Drift too low and nerves become jumpy, causing muscle spasms and cramps (tetany); drift too high and nerves and muscles go sluggish. This is a textbook example of the homeostasis idea from Chapter 1 — a value held near a set point by feedback — and the skeleton is the reservoir the body draws on to defend it.

PTH raises calcium

When blood calcium falls, the parathyroid glands (four small glands on the back of the thyroid) release parathyroid hormone (PTH). PTH pushes calcium up through three routes at once. In bone, it stimulates osteoclasts to dissolve matrix and release calcium into the blood. In the kidneys, it tells the tubules to reclaim calcium that would otherwise be lost in urine, and it activates vitamin D into calcitriol. And through calcitriol, it drives the intestines to absorb more calcium from food. Bone, kidney, and gut all pour calcium into the blood, and the level climbs back toward normal.

Calcitonin lowers calcium

When blood calcium runs high, the parafollicular (C) cells of the thyroid gland release calcitonin, which does the opposite of PTH. Calcitonin inhibits osteoclasts and favors osteoblasts, so calcium is pulled out of the blood and deposited back into bone, and the level settles down. The two hormones form a push–pull pair — PTH the accelerator, calcitonin the brake — that keeps blood calcium remarkably steady.

FeatureParathyroid hormone (PTH)Calcitonin
Released whenBlood calcium is lowBlood calcium is high
SourceParathyroid glandsThyroid C (parafollicular) cells
Effect on calciumRaises itLowers it
BoneStimulates osteoclasts (breakdown)Inhibits osteoclasts (favors buildup)
Kidney / gutReabsorbs Ca; activates vitamin D → more gut absorptionMinor role
The body defends blood calcium before the skeleton. If the diet is short on calcium, PTH will keep pulling it out of bone to protect the blood level — because a normal blood calcium is needed for the heart to beat and nerves to fire right now. Over years, that steady withdrawal is one road to weak, brittle bone, which is why dietary calcium and vitamin D matter so much.

Section 6Fractures & repair

How bones break

A fracture is simply a broken bone, but clinicians name the pattern of the break because the pattern hints at what caused it and how it should be treated. The first and most important distinction is whether the skin stayed intact. A closed (simple) fracture leaves the skin unbroken; an open (compound) fracture breaks through the skin, which is far more serious because it opens a door for infection. Beyond that, breaks are named for their line and severity.

TypeWhat it looks like
Closed vs openSkin intact (closed) vs bone piercing the skin (open) — open carries infection risk
TransverseBreak runs straight across the shaft
ObliqueBreak runs at an angle across the bone
SpiralTwisting break that winds around the shaft; suggests a rotational force
ComminutedBone shatters into three or more fragments
ImpactedOne broken end is driven into the other
GreenstickIncomplete break; bone bends and cracks on one side (mostly in children)

How a bone heals: four stages

A broken bone repairs itself in a predictable sequence that can take months to finish. First, torn vessels bleed and clot, forming a fracture hematoma that seals the gap and sets the stage. Next, cells build a soft fibrocartilaginous (soft) callus of collagen and cartilage that splints the ends together. Osteoblasts then replace that soft bridge with a bony (hard) callus of spongy bone, roughly six weeks after the injury. Finally, over months, the bone remodels — osteoclasts and osteoblasts reshape the callus back toward the bone's original form, guided by the same stresses that shape every bone.

StageWhat happens
1. Fracture hematomaBleeding forms a clot that fills and seals the break
2. Soft (fibrocartilaginous) callusCollagen and cartilage bridge the gap, splinting the ends
3. Hard (bony) callusOsteoblasts replace the soft callus with spongy bone (~6 weeks)
4. Bone remodelingThe callus is reshaped over months toward the original contour

To help this happen, a displaced fracture is first reduced — the ends are lined back up, either by manipulation from outside (closed reduction) or by surgery (open reduction) — and then immobilized in a cast or with hardware so the delicate callus is not disturbed while it matures.

Kids bend before they break. A child's bones contain more collagen and less mineral than an adult's, so they are springier — which is why children get greenstick fractures, where the bone bows and cracks partway through like a fresh twig instead of snapping clean. The same flexibility helps children heal faster than adults.

  • The skeleton splits into the axial (~80 bones) and appendicular (~126 bones) divisions, 206 in all.
  • Bones are classed by shape as long, short, flat, irregular, or sesamoid, and the shape follows the function.

ReferenceWords worth knowing

A quick reference for the terms in this chapter — skim it now, or circle back whenever a word trips you up.

Appendicular skeleton
The bones of the limbs plus the pectoral and pelvic girdles that attach them to the axis (~126 bones); specialized for movement.
Axial skeleton
The bones along the body's central axis — skull, vertebral column, and thoracic cage (~80 bones); specialized for support and protection.
Calcitonin

Dense, strong bone tissue built of osteons; forms the outer wall of bones and the shaft of long bones.

The tubular shaft of a long bone; compact bone surrounding the medullary cavity.
Endosteum
The thin membrane lining the internal surfaces of bone — the medullary cavity and spongy-bone spaces.
Epiphyseal plate
The cartilage growth plate in the metaphysis where a long bone lengthens; becomes the epiphyseal line when growth ends.
Epiphysis
The expanded end of a long bone, made of spongy bone with red marrow and capped by articular cartilage.
Hematopoiesis
The formation of blood cells (red cells, white cells, platelets) in the red marrow of bone.
Hydroxyapatite
The crystalline calcium-phosphate mineral of bone matrix that gives bone its hardness.
Medullary cavity
The hollow center of a long bone's shaft; stores fatty yellow marrow in adults.
Osteoblast
The bone-building cell that secretes new matrix; matures into an osteocyte once surrounded by bone.
Osteoclast
The large, multinucleated cell that dissolves bone matrix and releases its minerals into the blood.
Osteocyte
A mature bone cell housed in a lacuna that maintains the matrix and senses mechanical stress.
Osteon
The structural unit of compact bone — concentric lamellae of matrix around a central (Haversian) canal.
Parathyroid hormone (PTH)
The hormone from the parathyroid glands that raises blood calcium via bone, kidney, and intestine.
Periosteum
The tough outer membrane of a bone; carries vessels and nerves, anchors tendons, and holds bone-forming cells.

Lightweight, porous bone tissue of struts (trabeculae) found in the epiphyses; its spaces hold red marrow.

Check yourself

1. A friend says the skeleton is just “dead scaffolding.” Give two pieces of evidence that bone is living tissue.
Bone is richly supplied with blood vessels and nerves, it is continually remodeled by living cells, and it heals itself after a fracture. Any of these shows that bone is a living organ, not inert scaffolding — a broken bone even bleeds and repairs.
2. List the major functions of the skeletal system, and which one involves red bone marrow.
Support, protection, movement, mineral and fat storage, and blood-cell formation; the last one — hematopoiesis — occurs in red marrow. Red marrow in spongy bone makes red cells, white cells, and platelets.
3. Sort these into axial or appendicular skeleton: femur, sternum, a vertebra, the scapula, the skull.
Axial: sternum, vertebra, skull. Appendicular: femur, scapula. The axial skeleton is the central axis (skull, vertebral column, thoracic cage); the appendicular is the limbs and their girdles — the scapula is part of the pectoral girdle.
4. Classify each bone by shape: the femur, a carpal, a rib, a vertebra, the patella.
Femur = long; carpal = short; rib = flat; vertebra = irregular; patella = sesamoid. Shape follows function — long bones lever, short bones stabilize, flat bones protect, irregular bones are specialized, and sesamoids sit inside tendons.
5. Name the shaft of a long bone, the tissue that fills its hollow center in an adult, and the tissue found in the expanded ends.
The shaft is the diaphysis; the medullary cavity holds yellow (fatty) marrow; the epiphyses contain spongy bone with red marrow. Compact bone forms the shaft wall; spongy bone fills the ends.
6. What is the difference between the epiphyseal plate and the epiphyseal line, and why does it matter clinically in children?
The epiphyseal plate is the active cartilage growth plate in a growing bone; the epiphyseal line is the bony remnant left after growth stops. Because the plate is softer cartilage, a fracture through it in a child can disturb future limb growth.
7. Match the cell to its job: osteoblast, osteoclast, osteocyte. Which one raises blood calcium when it is active?
Osteoblast builds new bone; osteocyte maintains bone; osteoclast breaks down bone — and the osteoclast raises blood calcium by dissolving matrix. Remember: blasts build, clasts consume.
8. Bone matrix has an organic and an inorganic part. Name each and the property it provides.
Organic collagen gives flexibility and tensile strength; inorganic hydroxyapatite (calcium phosphate) gives hardness and compressive strength. Bone needs both — without mineral it is rubbery, without collagen it is brittle.
9. What is bone remodeling, and how does Wolff's law explain why weight-bearing exercise strengthens bone?
Remodeling is the ongoing balance of resorption by osteoclasts and deposition by osteoblasts; Wolff's law says bone remodels in response to the stress placed on it. Loading the skeleton signals osteoblasts to add bone where it is needed, so weight-bearing exercise builds density while disuse thins it.
10. Blood calcium drops below normal. Which hormone responds, where is it made, and what are its three targets?
Parathyroid hormone (PTH), made by the parathyroid glands, acts on bone (stimulates osteoclasts), the kidneys (reabsorb calcium and activate vitamin D), and the intestine (absorb more calcium via calcitriol). All three raise blood calcium back toward its 9–10 mg/dL set point.
11. Contrast an open fracture with a closed fracture, and explain why one is more dangerous.
In a closed (simple) fracture the skin stays intact; in an open (compound) fracture the broken bone pierces the skin. The open fracture is more dangerous because the wound exposes bone to the outside and carries a high risk of infection.
12. Put the four stages of bone repair in order, starting right after the break.
Fracture hematoma → soft (fibrocartilaginous) callus → hard (bony) callus → bone remodeling. A clot fills the gap, cartilage and collagen splint it, spongy bone replaces that bridge, and finally the callus is reshaped toward the original bone.
